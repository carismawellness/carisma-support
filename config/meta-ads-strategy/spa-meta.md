## Customer Archetypes

Five core personas for Spa Meta Ads. Each requires a different emotional hook and different creative approach.

### Archetype 1: The Stressed Professional (Age 28–45)
**Profile:** She works hard, achieves a lot, and has no downtime. Stress has become her default state. She feels guilty taking time for herself but secretly craves it.
- **Trigger:** Reached breaking point. Exhausted after a long project. Friend recommended a spa day.
- **Primary desire:** Permission to switch off. Complete mental disconnection. To feel human again.
- **Main fear:** Wasting time. Not "deserving" it. The spa feeling rushed or transactional.
- **Trust needs:** Genuine luxury experience, not a beauty salon. The world actually stops here.
- **Messaging:** "The world can wait." "You don't have to earn rest." "Give yourself permission."
- **Best hook:** "When was the last time you truly switched off?"

### Archetype 2: The Mother Needing Escape (Age 30–52)
**Profile:** She's poured herself into her family. She loves her life but she's running on empty. A spa visit is not indulgence — it's survival.
- **Trigger:** Partner or friend gifted a voucher. Mother's Day. She booked it herself after a particularly hard week.
- **Primary desire:** Silence. Stillness. Being taken care of for once.
- **Main fear:** Feeling guilty about leaving. The experience not being worth it.
- **Trust needs:** Quality that validates the "selfish" decision. She needs to return home knowing it was worth every minute.
- **Messaging:** "You give everything. Take a moment." "This is what restoration feels like." "You come back better."
- **Best hook:** "You've been giving all year. Take a day."

### Archetype 3: The Couple Seeking Shared Ritual (Age 28–55)
**Profile:** Two people who want to do something meaningful together. Not dinner, not a movie — an experience. Could be a birthday, anniversary, Valentine's, or just "we need this."
- **Trigger:** Anniversary approaching. Valentine's. Looking for something different to do.
- **Primary desire:** Shared memories. Intimacy without distraction. Feeling connected.
- **Main fear:** One partner not enjoying it. Awkwardness. Feeling out of place.
- **Trust needs:** "This is for couples" framing — normalise it for him and her.
- **Messaging:** "Give each other the gift of time together." "The couple that unwinds together, stays together."
- **Best hook:** "Your best date night doesn't need a restaurant."

### Archetype 4: The Wellness Enthusiast (Age 25–45)
**Profile:** She reads about wellness. She knows her sauna protocols. She tracks HRV. She wants a scientifically-grounded, premium wellness experience — not just a nice bath and a candle.
- **Trigger:** Read about contrast therapy, infrared sauna, or new treatment. Wants to try it.
- **Primary desire:** Optimise her recovery and wellbeing. Access to premium facilities and expertise.
- **Main fear:** Gimmicky or low-quality experience. Not worth her knowledge level.
- **Trust needs:** Credentials, equipment quality, evidence-based treatments, knowledgeable staff.
- **Messaging:** "Built for people who take wellness seriously." "The science behind the experience."
- **Best hook:** "Your sauna protocol, elevated."

### Archetype 5: The Gift Buyer (Age 25–65)
**Profile:** She's looking for the perfect gift — something thoughtful, premium, personal. Not a generic voucher. A real experience that says "I know you."
- **Trigger:** Birthday, Christmas, Mother's Day, thank-you, milestone.
- **Primary desire:** The gift to feel special and personal. The recipient to feel truly seen.
- **Main fear:** Gift not being used or appreciated.
- **Trust needs:** Beautiful presentation. Easy booking process. Flexible validity.
- **Messaging:** "The gift they'll actually remember." "Give them what no one else thinks of." "A gift that says everything words can't."
- **Best hook:** "She deserves more than flowers. Give her this."

- **Physical tension** — "My shoulders are up at my ears. I carry the whole week in my back."

## Creative Rules for Spa Ads

- Tone is peaceful, soothing, and elegant — NEVER aggressive or urgency-heavy in the visual layer
- Use real spa environments — real treatment rooms, real therapists, real clients (with consent)
- Soft lighting, warm neutrals, natural textures — never clinical white or harsh lighting
- Avoid stock imagery from non-Malta environments — Maltese light and architecture is recognisable and builds trust
- For video: slow pan of treatment room, hands-on treatment close-ups, relaxed client expressions (eyes closed, serene smile)
- Urgency lives in the COPY (15 spots, limited time) — not in the visual tone
- Gifting ads: show the gift card, the moment of giving, the reaction — emotion sells the gift
- Always end with "Peacefully, Sarah" in the sign-off for brand voice consistency
