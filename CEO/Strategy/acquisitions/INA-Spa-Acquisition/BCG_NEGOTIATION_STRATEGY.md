## SECTION 1: TIMING STRATEGY — DAY-BY-DAY PLAYBOOK

### The Psychology of Time in Small-Market M&A

In large transactions, advisors move fast because both sides have teams, deadlines, and institutional pressure. In a EUR 200-300K owner-operated deal in Malta, time works completely differently. The seller is a private individual with emotional stake. Every day that passes without resolution creates a slow anxiety in him: "Is the deal slipping? Did I push too hard? Should I have been more flexible?"

Silence is not absence of negotiation. It IS negotiation. Every hour you do not contact the seller, the conversation from the meeting replays in his head. The negative framing you deployed, the anchors you set, the questions you raised about his operational decisions — all of that continues working for you after you leave the room.

The buyer who calls the next morning signals desperation. The buyer who takes 72 hours signals strength, a full calendar, and genuine alternatives.

### Timeline: Now Through First Formal Offer

**Day 0 (Today, Day of Meeting)**
Do nothing. No email, no WhatsApp, no call. Do not send the NDA today. If you have any automated read receipts on your email, disable them. Visible silence communicates: "I have other things to do and this is one of several options I'm evaluating."

**Day 1 (Tomorrow)**
Do not send the NDA in the morning. If the seller's advisor contacts you proactively — which would be a highly positive signal — respond briefly and warmly, but commit to nothing. Morning contact from them means anxiety has already set in.

Send the NDA tomorrow afternoon, between 3:00 PM and 4:30 PM. This timing is deliberate:
- Not morning: Would imply you were waiting by the computer
- Not evening: Would imply you stayed late working on this
- Mid-afternoon: Implies this was one of several items on your agenda that day

The NDA cover message (exact language provided in Section 2).

**Days 2-4 (After Sending NDA)**
Wait for the seller to execute the NDA and transmit the data room. Do not follow up. They will send it — they want the process to move. If you have not heard by Day 4 (end of business), a single brief WhatsApp to the M&A advisor is appropriate (language in Section 2).

**Days 5-9 (After Receiving Financials)**
When the data room arrives, acknowledge receipt immediately with a brief message (language in Section 2). Then go silent for 3-5 business days. Do not respond within 24 hours. This communicates: you are a sophisticated buyer who reviews documents carefully; you are busy; you have other transactions; the data is being analyzed by your team (real or implied). You are not sitting at home refreshing your email.

During this window, conduct your actual analysis. The data review playbook is in Section 4.

**Days 10-14 (Follow-Up Call / Questions Phase)**
Do not make an offer yet. Come back with a list of 5-7 clarifying questions (Section 4 has the exact questions). Schedule a follow-up call with the advisor. Questions serve three purposes:
1. They demonstrate analytical rigor and signal sophisticated buyer
2. They expose additional weaknesses you can reference in your offer framing
3. They delay the timeline, which continues to work in your favor

**Days 15-21 (First Formal Offer)**
Make your first offer in writing, approximately 10-14 days after receiving the data room. The optimal total elapsed time from NDA send to first offer is 14-18 days. This is long enough to signal you are deliberate, not so long that momentum dies. First offer mechanics are in Section 5.

### Summary Timeline

| Day | Action | Purpose |
|-----|--------|---------|
| 0 | Nothing | Signal lack of eagerness |
| 1 (afternoon) | Send NDA | Controlled re-engagement |
| 2-4 | Wait for execution | Let them manage the process |
| 5 (upon receipt) | Acknowledge data room | Brief, professional |
| 5-9 | Silent analysis period | Signal competence, buy time |
| 10-14 | Questions call with advisor | Expose weaknesses, delay |
| 15-21 | Written first offer | Formal anchoring |
| 22-35 | Counter-offer cycle | Close the deal |

Total elapsed time from today to signed term sheet: approximately 4-5 weeks. This is appropriate for a deal of this size and complexity. Moving faster signals desperation. Moving slower risks losing momentum or giving the seller time to re-engage any real competing buyers.

## SECTION 2: COMMUNICATION CHANNEL STRATEGY

### Channel Architecture

This deal has three communication nodes:

1. **You (Buyer)** — final decision authority
2. **Seller's M&A Advisor** — the deal-maker, commission-motivated, wants this to close
3. **Seller (Owner)** — emotional principal, wants maximum recovery, may self-sabotage if over-pressured

The optimal channel architecture for the remainder of this negotiation is:

**Primary channel: You → Advisor → Owner**
**Secondary channel: You → Owner (only for relationship maintenance)**
**Never: Advisor → You → Owner (don't let them triangulate you)**

This is the standard principal-agent structure used in M&A deals for good reason: it insulates the emotional principals from each other and lets professional intermediaries manage the temperature. You are not a professional acquirer yet, and the seller is not a professional seller. The advisor is the professional in the room. Use that.

### The M&A Advisor: How to Handle Him

The advisor's commission is only earned at close. His incentive is perfectly aligned with getting a deal done — at any price above zero. He is not the seller's loyal ally; he is a transaction professional who wants his fee.

This creates a useful dynamic. The advisor will:
- Tell you things the seller won't (his real floor, his emotional state, whether he has real alternative interest)
- Manage seller expectations downward when needed
- Push the seller to be reasonable in ways you cannot
- Signal when the seller is genuinely close to walking vs. posturing

How to leverage the advisor without being manipulated by him:

**Step 1: Build personal rapport immediately.** Call him directly, before sending the NDA. A brief call: "I wanted to touch base before sending the NDA over. I appreciated your professionalism in the meeting. I want to make sure we approach this process properly." This establishes a peer-to-peer channel outside the formal transaction.

**Step 2: Give him information to take back.** You are not negotiating with him. You are giving him things to say to the seller. For example: "Honestly, my parents are quite skeptical of this deal. I think there's a transaction here, but I'm going to have to show them numbers that make sense. I'd hate to get excited about this and then have to pull back." He will relay this. It plants seeds before you even submit an offer.

**Step 3: Ask him questions you'd never ask the seller.** "Is he emotionally ready to move on from this, or is he still attached?" "Do you have a sense of where his floor really is?" "Is there a number that would make this a straightforward decision for him?" You will not always get direct answers, but the answers you do get are valuable.

**Step 4: Use him to test reactions before making formal moves.** Before you submit a written offer, you can tell the advisor verbally: "I'm thinking about coming in around EUR 230K. How do you think that lands?" His reaction tells you whether you're in range or about to create a blowup. This is called a "trial balloon" and it is standard M&A practice.

**Step 5: Watch for the advisor playing both sides.** He may share your private reasoning with the seller, quote your walk-away price, or push you upward unnecessarily to protect his fee (since a higher deal price means a higher commission in many fee structures, but also more risk of deal failure). Never tell the advisor your true walk-away number. If he asks "how high would you go?", the answer is always: "I'm constrained by my parents and by my analysis. I have to be able to defend any number I bring to them."

### Communication Scripts

**NDA Cover Message (Day 1, Afternoon) — Email to Advisor, CC Seller if He Insists**

Subject: INA Spa — NDA for Review

> Hi [Advisor name],
>
> Good to meet yesterday. I've attached the NDA for review and execution. Once signed, I'd appreciate access to the materials we discussed — the lease terms, historical revenue breakdown, and cost structure.
>
> I'll take a few days to review carefully before we reconnect. Looking forward to the process.
>
> [Your name]

Note the tone: businesslike, not warm, not eager. "A few days" explicitly signals you will not be responding in 24 hours. This sets their expectations before they even start waiting.

**Acknowledging Data Room Receipt (Day 5, Same Day as Receipt)**

WhatsApp to advisor (informal channel signals comfort):

> "Received the materials. Will review this week and come back to you with a few questions. Talk soon."

Twelve words. Nothing more.

**No-Response Follow-Up (Only if Silence Exceeds 4 Business Days)**

WhatsApp to advisor:

> "Hey [name] — just checking in. Did the NDA come through on your end? I'm still putting together my questions from the data room, should be back to you early next week."

This accomplishes three things: confirms the NDA process is moving, signals you are still in analysis mode (not ready to offer), and sets a timeline for the questions call without committing to a specific day.

**Questions Call Request (Day 10-12)**

Phone call to advisor:

> "I've been through the materials and I have a few questions — not major issues, just things I want to understand before I put a number together. Would [Owner's name] be available for a 30-minute call later this week? Or if easier, I can go through these with you first."

The offer to go through questions with the advisor first is deliberate. It lets you preview his reactions, and it keeps the owner off balance — he doesn't know what you're finding until the advisor tells him (at which point the advisor has already framed it).

**Contacting the Owner Directly**

Do not initiate direct contact with the seller during the data room analysis phase. The one exception: if the seller texts or calls you personally (a positive signal of anxiety), respond warmly and briefly, deflect to process:

> "Great to hear from you. I'm still working through the numbers — your advisor and I have a call coming up. I'll make sure to loop you in once I have a clearer picture."

This keeps the owner engaged without moving the process outside the advisor channel.

## SECTION 3: THE PARENTS GAMBIT — DEEP ANALYSIS

### What This Tactic Is

The buyer's instinct to use "my parents aren't positive on this deal" is correct. This is a well-established negotiation technique known variously as:

- The **Higher Authority tactic** (Herb Cohen's framing in "You Can Negotiate Anything")
- The **Good Cop / Bad Cop variant** (buyer is willing, parents are skeptical)
- The **Reluctant Buyer signal** (I want this deal more than they do; help me sell it to them)

It is powerful precisely because it is mostly true. The buyer's parents are involved in the business. They are a real audience for any deal. The tactic works because it:

1. Creates a third party the seller cannot negotiate with directly
2. Shifts the seller's frustration away from the buyer (you are sympathetic, the parents are the obstacle)
3. Gives the buyer an escape valve without destroying the relationship ("the decision isn't mine alone")
4. Creates pressure on the seller to make the deal attractive enough to "convince the parents"
5. Anchors the conversation around what the parents need to see, not what the buyer wants to pay

### When It Works and When It Backfires

**It works when:**
- Deployed after initial relationship is established (not in the first meeting)
- The "parents" concern is specific, not vague ("they're concerned about the lease risk" not "they just don't like it")
- It is used to explain a low number, not to refuse a counter entirely
- The buyer's enthusiasm is visible ("I want to make this work, but...")
- It is used once or twice, not as a constant refrain

**It backfires when:**
- The seller has already met the parents and knows they are enthusiastic about deals
- It is used so early in the relationship that it feels like an excuse
- It is used to avoid all accountability ("I can't make any decisions without them")
- The "parents" concern keeps changing — first it's price, then it's the lease, then it's timing
- Malta's small business community is tight: if the seller or advisor knows your parents and knows they are generally supportive of your ventures, the bluff dissolves instantly

**The specific cultural risk in Malta:** Malta has a small business community where everyone knows everyone. Before deploying this tactic, assess: does the seller or his advisor have any relationship with your parents through the hospitality industry, chambers of commerce, or professional networks? If yes, the tactic is risky and should be modified.

### How to Execute It

Do not announce the parents' skepticism as a statement. Reveal it conversationally, as if the buyer is slightly embarrassed to admit it. The most effective framing is vulnerability, not strategy.

**Wrong framing:**
"My parents don't think this is a good deal at this price." (adversarial, feels tactical)

**Right framing:**
"Look, I'll be honest with you — and I hope this stays between us — my parents are not as excited about this as I am. They've seen me get enthusiastic about deals before. They're asking me to show them the math. I want to make this work. But I need to show them a number they can get comfortable with."

This version:
- Positions you as the seller's ally against the parents
- Creates genuine human connection ("I hope this stays between us")
- Explains a low offer without making the seller feel insulted
- Frames the negotiation as "help me convince them" rather than "I don't want to pay more"

### The Good Cop / Bad Cop Variant

In this structure, you (the buyer) are the Good Cop. The parents are the Bad Cop. You use them as follows:

- When making a low offer: "I want to come in higher. But my parents reviewed the EBITDA and the lease and they're anchored at a number I have to respect."
- When rejecting a counter: "I took your counter to my parents. They said they'd need to see more comfort on [specific issue] before they move."
- When making a final concession: "I pushed my parents. They agreed to move to EUR 270K but that is their final number and I've spent significant political capital getting there."

The Bad Cop is always the parents. You remain empathetic and deal-oriented. This keeps the seller's relationship with you intact even as the negotiation gets tense.

### Optimal Deployment Timing

Do not use the parents in the NDA phase. That is too early.

Deploy it in two moments:

**Moment 1: When transmitting the questions about the data room.** In your follow-up call: "I'm going through these with my parents as well, and they've raised a few questions..." This introduces the parents as involved decision-makers before the number is even on the table.

**Moment 2: When making the first offer.** The offer cover note (Section 5) references parents explicitly: "This number reflects where my family's investment committee has landed after reviewing the financials."

From that point forward, "the parents" is the ceiling. Your personal enthusiasm is the floor. The seller needs to close the gap.

## SECTION 4: DATA ROOM ANALYSIS PLAYBOOK

### How Long to Take

The data room will likely contain: audited or management accounts, the lease agreement, payroll records, and possibly equipment schedules. It is not a complex data room.

You will review it in 2-3 hours. You will not tell anyone this.

Take 3-5 business days before your questions call. Use this time to:
1. Actually read everything carefully
2. Prepare your question list
3. Run the numbers through the financial model
4. Identify every inconsistency, risk, or weakness
5. Decide whether the actual financials are materially better or worse than the EUR 400K revenue / breakeven EBITDA picture painted in the meeting

The 3-5 day delay signals: this is a serious review, not a rubber stamp.

### What to Look For in the Data Room

**Priority 1: Verify the revenue number.**
The seller claims EUR 400K annual revenue. Verify this against actual bank statements or POS system records, not just management accounts he prepared. If the number is EUR 350K or below, you have a new weapon. If it is above EUR 400K, you need to know.

**Priority 2: Understand the real cost structure.**
He has 14 staff for EUR 400K revenue — that is roughly EUR 29K revenue per staff member, which is extremely low for a spa. Understand the payroll number specifically. In a Maltese spa of this size, payroll for 14 staff likely runs EUR 280-340K+ annually. That alone destroys profitability.

**Priority 3: The hotel lease terms in full.**
5% revenue share is the headline. But what are the assignment provisions? Can this lease be assigned to you? Under what conditions? Does the hotel have right of approval over the buyer? This is a potential deal-killer (as noted in your prior analysis) and the full text of the lease is more important than any financial figure.

**Priority 4: Capex detail.**
If he claims EUR 800K in capex, the actual invoices or depreciation schedule will tell you what was spent and when. Equipment from 2018-2020 is worth 10-20 cents on the dollar today. If EUR 500K of that EUR 800K was spent in the first year and equipment is now 6-7 years old, the replacement cost argument falls apart entirely.

**Priority 5: Hidden liabilities.**
Any outstanding loans against the business. Any personal guarantees the seller made related to the business. Any employment disputes or pending claims. Any arrears on the revenue share to the hotel.

### Questions to Bring Back (Exact Language)

Deploy these in the follow-up call with the advisor (and optionally, the seller). Each question is designed to either extract useful information or expose a weakness the seller will struggle to answer cleanly.

**Question 1: Revenue verification**
"The management accounts show EUR 400K in revenue. I'd like to see the underlying booking system or POS data — just so I can reconcile the monthly figures and understand seasonality. Can that be included in the data room?"

Why this works: It signals you are not taking numbers at face value. If he resists providing POS data, that is itself a signal. If the actual number is lower, you now have it in writing.

**Question 2: Payroll structure**
"I want to understand the staff cost in detail. I see 14 employees. Can I get a breakdown of FTEs vs. part-time, contract vs. employment, and the total payroll line? I'm trying to model what happens to cost structure if we right-size the team post-acquisition."

Why this works: Openly signals you plan to reduce headcount. Creates psychological pressure on the seller, who likely has loyalty to his staff. Also exposes whether payroll is as high as suspected.

**Question 3: Lease assignment**
"This is critical for me — I need to understand the exact language in the lease on assignment. Has the hotel been informed that a sale process is underway? And has there been any preliminary indication of whether they'd approve an assignment? Because if there's a risk the hotel can block this, it materially changes my underwriting."

Why this works: Exposes the biggest structural risk in the deal. If the hotel has veto rights and has not been consulted, this is a genuine complication you can use to justify a lower offer or a longer timeline.

**Question 4: Other buyer credibility test**
"I want to be respectful of your time and mine. I've heard there may be other parties at various stages of interest. I'm not asking for details — but can you tell me whether anyone else has received the data room? If there's an active competing process, I want to make sure I'm allocating the right amount of time to structuring my offer."

Why this works: Forces the advisor to either confirm there are real competing buyers (in which case you now know and can adjust) or implicitly confirm there are none. If the "only two buyers" slip from the meeting was accurate, this question will be answered with deflection or with something that confirms your suspicion.

**Question 5: Seller timeline pressure**
"Can you help me understand what's driving the timing on your side? I want to make sure I structure something that actually works for [seller's name] — whether that's a quick close, structured payments, a specific transition period. Understanding his priorities helps me put together a better offer."

Why this works: Sounds collaborative. But it extracts information about whether the seller is under time or cash pressure — both of which compress his floor. If he has any debt, personal cash flow issues, or reasons to want a fast close, this opens that door.

### How to Frame Data Findings When Presenting

Do not send a memo or analysis document. In small deals, written analysis comes across as aggressive or lawyerly and puts the seller on the defensive.

Use verbal framing in the follow-up call:

"I've been through the materials. There's a lot here I respect — the revenue is real, the location is strong. But there are a few things that have given my parents pause, and honestly, I wanted to be upfront with you rather than just send a low number and not explain it. The payroll at EUR [X] is significantly higher than what I'd carry. The lease assignment question is an open issue. And the equipment, which I know represents a lot of your investment, has depreciated considerably. Those three things, in combination, are what have driven my parents to a more conservative number than I would have liked to bring."

This framing:
- Leads with acknowledgment (not adversarial)
- Attributes conservatism to parents, not buyer
- Gives specific reasons for the low number (data-driven, not arbitrary)
- Keeps the buyer in the "wants to make this work" camp
- Does not make promises about where the number can go

## SECTION 5: FIRST OFFER STRATEGY

### Timing

Submit the first offer in writing 12-16 days after receiving the data room. Not before. If the data room arrives and you submit an offer within 3 days, you have signaled that either you didn't look carefully or you are eager. Neither helps you.

Optimal day of week: Tuesday or Wednesday. Avoid Monday (feels rushed) and Friday (seller's weekend is now consumed by a low offer and he has no outlet).

Time of day: Send between 10:00 AM and 12:00 PM. Sends a "work hours professional" signal.

### Written vs. Verbal First Offer

**Written. Always. In this deal.**

A verbal offer cannot be misquoted to you later. But in a small deal where the seller is emotional and the advisor is commission-motivated, a verbal offer gets inflated in the retelling. The seller hears "EUR 250K" and tells his wife "they offered EUR 230K" and now his emotional anchor has shifted against you.

A written offer also forces the seller to sit with the number, read it, sleep on it. It prevents an emotional knee-jerk rejection in the room. Many deals die verbally that would have survived in writing.

The offer should be a 1-page letter (not a term sheet yet — that comes after acceptance in principle). Keep it brief.

### Who Receives It

Send to the advisor. CC the seller only if the advisor specifically requests it, or if you have established a strong direct relationship with the seller. Letting the advisor receive it first gives him the opportunity to frame it before the seller sees it. He has every incentive to frame it positively ("this is a serious buyer, this is a starting point, let's counter").

If you send directly to the seller without the advisor, you risk: an emotionally driven rejection that the advisor cannot temper, the advisor feeling cut out (which reduces his cooperation), and losing the buffer the advisor provides.

### The Offer Letter — Exact Language

Subject: INA Spa — Indicative Offer

---

Dear [Seller name],

Following our recent meeting and review of the materials shared, I would like to submit the following indicative offer for the acquisition of INA Spa & Wellness.

**Indicative Purchase Price: EUR 230,000 (cash at close)**

This reflects:
- The current EBITDA profile of the business as presented
- The capital investment required to right-size the team and upgrade operational structure post-acquisition
- The lease assignment risk and associated uncertainty
- Equipment values at current market depreciation
- Our family's conservative approach to this type of investment

I want to be transparent that this process has involved significant discussion within my family's investment group. My parents, who are experienced in the hospitality and wellness sector, have reviewed the financials and believe this offer fairly reflects the business as it stands today.

I remain genuinely interested in this acquisition and in building on what you have created. I see real potential here, and I believe a transaction structured at this level protects both parties appropriately.

I am open to a conversation about structure — whether that includes a short transition period, any seller financing component, or other terms that work for you. I would welcome a call with you and [advisor name] to discuss.

[Your name]

---

### Why EUR 230K as the Opening Formal Offer

Your meeting anchor was EUR 200K (build cost). Your opening offer target is EUR 220-240K. EUR 230K:
- Is EUR 30K above your stated anchor, which shows you have "moved" already
- Creates room to negotiate toward EUR 260-280K (your target settlement)
- Is below your walk-away by EUR 90K, which is ample maneuvering room
- Is high enough to not feel insulting (it is more than the seller's current annual revenue)
- Implies the parents are the ceiling, not the buyer

Do not show your work in the offer letter. No spreadsheets, no EBITDA multiples, no depreciation analysis. That can come in the counter-offer phase if needed. A number with three clean justifications is more powerful than a spreadsheet. Spreadsheets invite the seller to pick apart your assumptions. Three sentences do not.

### The Anchoring Sequence

Your goal over the next 3-4 exchanges is:

| Round | Your Position | Seller's Expected Position |
|-------|--------------|---------------------------|
| First offer | EUR 230K | EUR 230K (shock, rejection) |
| After counter | EUR 250K (+20K) | EUR 420-450K (fantasy counter) |
| After second counter | EUR 265K (+15K) | EUR 380-400K (coming down) |
| Settlement zone | EUR 275-285K | EUR 280-300K |

The key discipline: your concessions must be asymmetric and decreasing. Move EUR 20K on the second step, then EUR 15K, then EUR 10K, then EUR 5K. Never make a large concession on the third or fourth move. Decreasing concessions signal you are approaching your limit. Steady concessions signal there is always more room.

Never, under any circumstances, increase your offer by more than EUR 20K in any single move. A EUR 30K jump reads as "I had more room all along" and invites the seller to test for more.

## SECTION 6: COUNTER-OFFER PLAYBOOK

### Receiving the Seller's Counter

Expect the seller's counter to be in the EUR 400-500K range. This is not a real number — it is a positioning response. Do not react emotionally. Do not respond immediately.

**Timing of your response to their counter:**
- If counter arrives Monday-Wednesday: Respond Thursday or Friday
- If counter arrives Thursday-Friday: Respond the following Tuesday or Wednesday
- Minimum response lag: 48 hours. Ideally 72-96 hours.

During this window, contact the advisor by phone (not text): "I received the counter. I appreciate them responding. I'll need a few days to take this back to my parents. I'll be honest — EUR [their number] is a long way from where we are. But let me think about whether there's a path. Can we plan to talk Thursday?"

This call accomplishes:
- Signals you are not walking
- Expresses genuine surprise at the number without anger
- Gives the advisor a chance to tell you how committed the seller is to that number (he will often say something like "he knows it's high" which tells you everything)
- Creates a specific next contact point

### Reading the Seller's Real Position

Watch for these signals from the advisor that the seller is ready to deal:

- "He wants to understand what you're really prepared to do" (translation: he's ready to move, tell us your real number)
- "He feels EUR X is the minimum he can justify to himself" (translation: there is a specific psychological floor, not just posturing)
- "He's asking about timing — would you be able to close by [date]?" (translation: he is motivated by a specific event and speed is valuable to him)
- "He's open to structure" (translation: total number matters more than cash vs. other terms)

Signals the seller is still posturing:
- Counter is round number (EUR 500K exactly) with no supporting rationale
- Advisor does not follow up proactively within 3-4 days after your non-response
- Seller goes quiet for extended periods after your offer

### The Escalation to "Final Offer"

Use the "final offer" gambit exactly once, and only when you believe you are within EUR 20-30K of a deal. Deploy it as follows:

Make your final offer verbally first through the advisor: "Look, I want to be respectful of your time. I've pushed my parents as hard as I can. EUR 275K is where they've landed and that's the ceiling I can work with. If that doesn't work for [seller], I genuinely understand — and I think he's built something real here — but I'd need to know before I spend more time on due diligence."

Then send it in writing with the same number. Make no further moves.

If they counter, do not move. If they threaten to go to the other buyer, say: "I understand. If that option becomes something they pursue, I'd wish them well. But I can't go higher than EUR 275K."

**Important:** The "final offer" gambit only works if you can actually walk away. If the seller calls the bluff and you cave, you have destroyed your credibility and the seller knows there is always more room. Only use "final offer" language when you genuinely mean it.

### When to Walk

Walk immediately if:
- The lease assignment is blocked by the hotel without a process to challenge it
- Actual revenue (verified) is materially below EUR 400K (below EUR 330K)
- Significant hidden liabilities are discovered in due diligence
- Seller refuses to reduce staff count as a precondition (you should not assume that cost yourself)
- Seller counter never moves below EUR 380K after 3 rounds

Walk without drama: "I've valued this carefully and I can't go higher than EUR 275K. That's where the math closes for us. If something changes on your end, I'd be open to picking this up. I hope we find a way to work together." Then actually stop following up. You have the Marion Mizzi deal and the build-from-scratch option. Both are real. Both are better at prices above EUR 320K.

## SECTION 7: RISK FACTORS AND CONTINGENCIES

### Risk 1: Seller Is Offended by EUR 230K Offer

**Probability:** Moderate (30-40%). The seller spent EUR 800K. EUR 230K is less than 30 cents on his dollar.

**Response strategy:** This is why you use the parents as the named reason for the number, not your own analysis. The seller cannot be angry at your parents. He can be angry at you. Your framing gives him somewhere to direct his frustration that is not the relationship.

If the seller becomes emotional and calls you directly: "I completely understand. I want to be transparent that this number comes from my parents' analysis, not my own. I believe in what you've built. Can we give [advisor name] a chance to work through this with us? I'd hate to lose this over the gap between where we started."

Never apologize for a low offer. Empathize, but do not apologize. An apology signals you know the offer was wrong.

### Risk 2: Advisor Inflates Seller Expectations

**Probability:** High (50-60%). Advisors often tell sellers what they want to hear in order to maintain the client relationship while simultaneously telling buyers the seller will move. You are being played if the advisor tells you the seller is flexible while telling the seller you are eager.

**Detection method:** Track consistency. If the advisor tells you "he's open to EUR 350K" and then the formal counter comes in at EUR 500K, the advisor is not managing expectations — he is managing you.

**Response:** If this happens, go direct to the seller with a friendly framing: "I feel like [advisor name] and I might be talking past each other. Can we grab a coffee and make sure we're actually aligned on the parameters before we waste more time on formal offers?" A direct conversation between principals often cuts through advisor-driven complexity.

### Risk 3: The Other Buyer Actually Exists

**Probability:** Low (15-20%), given the seller's "only two buyers" slip. But not zero.

**Response strategy:** Do not panic and do not accelerate your timeline beyond what is reasonable. If a competing buyer exists and is serious, the seller will tell you — not because he wants you to know, but because he will want to use it. The moment the advisor says "we've received another offer" or "there's a competing bid," ask immediately: "Can you tell me the structure? I want to understand if it's comparable." If they cannot or will not tell you the structure, it is almost certainly not a real competing offer.

If a real competing offer exists at a price above EUR 280K, evaluate carefully: Is the offer at a price where the deal still works for you? If yes, match it with better terms (faster close, simpler structure). If no, walk gracefully. You have better uses for EUR 350K+ than acquiring this specific business.

**Under no circumstances** should you make a pre-emptive bid above your target simply because of alleged competition. This is the oldest play in small-market M&A and it is designed to extract exactly that reaction.

### Risk 4: Seller Tries to Rush the Timeline

**Possible scenario:** Seller says he needs a decision by a specific date (end of month, next week, etc.) because of a lease payment, a personal event, or a claimed competing offer deadline.

**Response:** "I want to move forward thoughtfully. Rushing the process doesn't serve either of us well. If there's a specific milestone driving your timeline, help me understand it and I'll do my best to accommodate it. But I won't compromise my diligence for speed." Then do not change your timeline materially.

The exception: if the advisor privately tells you the seller needs cash by a specific date for a specific reason (debt, personal obligation, partner buyout), that is information you want to understand. A seller under time pressure has a lower effective floor. That is not something to exploit ruthlessly — it is something to price accurately.

### Risk 5: Data Room Reveals Better-Than-Expected Numbers

**Possible scenario:** Actual monthly revenue is EUR 45K, not EUR 30K (your anchor). EBITDA is EUR 120-150K, not breakeven. The business is materially stronger than the seller presented in the meeting (or than you implied with your pessimistic framing).

**Response strategy:** Do not immediately revise your offer upward. The seller shared the data in an NDA-protected context. Take time to model the revised numbers. Your offer changes, but not proportionally. The reasoning:

- Better numbers reduce your execution risk, but do not change your BATNA (you can still build for EUR 200K)
- Better numbers may justify moving to EUR 250-260K as an opening offer instead of EUR 230K
- Better numbers do not change the lease risk, the overstaffing problem, or the hotel contract — those are structural, not financial

If the numbers are significantly better (EUR 150K+ EBITDA), your target settlement moves to EUR 290-310K and your walk-away moves to EUR 360K. The framework stays the same; the inputs change.

Do not tell the advisor or seller that you found better numbers than expected. Play it neutral: "I've reviewed the materials. There are some positives and some areas of concern. I'd like to discuss both."

## SECTION 9: THE DEAL PSYCHOLOGY PLAYBOOK

### Understanding the Seller's Emotional State

The seller spent EUR 800K. He is sitting on a business he knows, at some level, has not worked as planned. He is in a conflicted emotional state that is common in founder sellers:

- **Pride:** He built something real. He does not want to admit it was a poor investment.
- **Relief:** At some level, he wants out. The "won't work unless part of a bigger system" comment is telling — that is a man who has accepted the thesis of the sale even if not the price.
- **Anxiety:** He had 4-5 interested parties in his head. Now it is 2. One of those is you.
- **Dependency:** His advisor wants a deal. The seller's ability to hold a high price is in tension with his advisor's incentive.

You are not trying to win an argument with him. You are trying to make it emotionally easy for him to say yes to a number that feels fair. That means:
- Acknowledging what he built without validating his price
- Giving him a narrative he can tell himself and others ("I sold to a serious operator who will take this to the next level")
- Making the process feel dignified, not adversarial
- Letting him hold his face by crediting your parents for the low number, not you

### The Narrative He Needs to Close

The seller needs to believe three things before he can accept EUR 275K:
1. That you are the right buyer — someone who will take care of what he built
2. That he got the best price available from a real, capable buyer
3. That EUR 275K is a good outcome given the alternative (holding a declining business)

Your job from here is to confirm all three, not just negotiate the number.

On narrative point 1: Bring your operational story. What you have done at Carisma. The 14 years in Malta. The brand trust. The future you see for INA inside Carisma.

On narrative point 2: Let the advisor tell him. You are serious, you are funded, you are the best buyer available. The advisor knows this and should say it.

On narrative point 3: Reference, gently, the cost inflation trajectory and the overstaffing — not as weapons, but as shared context. "I think you know that holding this another 3-5 years without restructuring is going to get harder, not easier."

**Questions call opener:**
"I've been through everything carefully. Before I put a number together, I have a few things I want to understand. Some of it is straightforward verification, some of it relates to questions my parents specifically raised. Mind if I walk through them?"

**After submitting EUR 230K offer and receiving a high counter:**
"I got the counter. I appreciate them responding. EUR [X] is meaningfully above where we are, and I'll be honest — I expected we'd be closer. Let me take a few days. I'll need to take this back to my parents and have a real conversation about whether the gap is closeable."

**Approaching the final offer:**
"Look, I want to stop going back and forth. I've pushed my parents as far as I can. EUR 275K is where they've landed and I genuinely believe that is a fair number for what the business generates today. If that doesn't work for [seller], I understand — I really do. But I'd rather we both know now than keep investing time in a process that doesn't close."

**Graceful walk-away (if needed):**
"I've valued this carefully and I've reached my ceiling. EUR 275K is as far as I can go. If the business doesn't sell at that level, or if something changes in the future, I'd be open to revisiting. I respect what [seller] has built and I hope it finds the right home."
