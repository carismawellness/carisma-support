## 1. PE FUND VALUATION — THE ACTUAL MATH

When this asset lands on my desk, I do not start with revenue. I start with EBITDA, then I ask what I believe normalized EBITDA can be under my ownership.

**Current State:**
- Revenue: EUR 400K
- EBITDA: EUR 0 (breakeven)
- EBITDA margin: 0%

**Entry Multiple on Current EBITDA:** Undefined. You cannot apply a multiple to zero.

This forces a different analytical frame. I have two options: distressed asset value, or acquirer-adjusted entry.

**Distressed Asset Approach (Liquidation Floor)**

If this business closes tomorrow, what survives? A 13-year lease in a hotel, some equipment, and 14 employees with redundancy obligations. The lease has value only if the location generates positive economics. With zero margin, the lease liability is arguably neutral to slightly negative until operational improvements are proven. Liquidation value: EUR 80-120K (equipment, client database, brand continuity premium).

**Acquirer-Adjusted Entry (What Carisma Should Model)**

Carisma's thesis is that they can restructure from 14 to 8-9 staff and integrate into their existing operations. Let us build the post-acquisition model.

- Revenue held flat at EUR 400K (conservative — Carisma's distribution likely grows this)
- Staff reduction from 14 to 9: assume EUR 30K fully-loaded annual cost per employee saves EUR 150K/year
- Current EBITDA: EUR 0
- Post-restructuring EBITDA estimate: EUR 150K
- EBITDA margin post-restructuring: 37.5%

Now I can apply a multiple.

Hotel spa businesses in the Mediterranean trade at 4-6x EBITDA at exit in a healthy transaction. Entry multiples — where the buyer absorbs execution risk — sit at 3-4x.

- **Entry EBITDA (post-restructuring):** EUR 150K
- **Entry Multiple:** 3.5x
- **Implied Entry Value:** EUR 525K

But here is the critical PE discipline: **that EUR 525K reflects the value of a business that does not yet exist.** Carisma has to execute the restructuring first. They bear redundancy costs, integration friction, potential revenue disruption, and the risk that improvements do not materialize. The discount for execution risk on a zero-EBITDA business is steep — typically 40-60%.

- **Execution-risk discount:** 50%
- **PE-adjusted entry price:** EUR 262K

That number, derived from first principles, is almost exactly where Carisma's target range sits. That is not a coincidence. Experienced acquirers intuitively solve for the same answer.

**IRR Check:**
- Entry price: EUR 262K
- Stabilized EBITDA at year 3: EUR 175K (modest growth)
- Exit multiple: 5x (strategic buyer or Carisma roll-up value)
- Exit value: EUR 875K
- Hold: 4 years
- Approximate IRR: 35%

At EUR 400K entry, that IRR collapses to approximately 18% — below the 25% minimum threshold any mid-market PE fund requires for a distressed single-site acquisition. The deal does not get done at EUR 400K.

## 2. WHEN PE PAYS 1X REVENUE — AND WHEN IT DOES NOT

Revenue multiples are a proxy, not a principle. PE pays 1x revenue only when revenue itself is a reliable predictor of future cash flow. The conditions are specific:

**PE will pay 1x+ revenue when:**
- Gross margins exceed 70% (SaaS, IP-heavy businesses, subscription models)
- Revenue is recurring and contractually predictable
- The business is growing 20%+ annually and margins are expanding
- Revenue is the lead indicator for earnings that have not yet materialized but will mechanically follow

**PE will not pay 1x revenue when:**
- EBITDA margin is zero or negative
- Revenue is entirely dependent on a single leased location controlled by a third party
- Revenue is transactional (each client must be re-acquired)
- The business is labor-intensive with low automation potential
- Growth has not been demonstrated

INA Spa fails every single condition for a revenue-multiple valuation. It is a service business with zero margin, high labor intensity, no recurring revenue, and a lease dependency. Applying 1x revenue to this asset is like pricing a restaurant on gross sales. Nobody in PE does that. The metric is irrelevant without margin.

## 3. THE TURNAROUND PREMIUM ARGUMENT

This is the most important conceptual question the buyer is asking, and the answer is unambiguous: **the buyer keeps the turnaround value they create.**

This is a foundational principle in PE. A distressed asset is priced on what it is today, not on what a skilled operator can turn it into. The seller did not create EUR 150K of EBITDA potential — Carisma did, through their management capabilities, operational systems, existing staff infrastructure, and brand integration. The seller's price should reflect the current state of the business, not the future state that the acquirer will build.

The standard PE argument to the seller: "You have 14 employees and zero profit. I have a system that makes this profitable. I am not paying you for my own system."

If the seller could independently produce EUR 150K EBITDA, they would have done it already. They cannot. That inability is exactly why the asset is available and why it is priced as a distressed acquisition.

The buyer's offer range of EUR 260-280K already incorporates a modest strategic premium acknowledging that the turnaround is achievable. That is the appropriate concession. The seller does not get to double-dip on value the buyer creates.

## 5. THE EUR 800K SUNK COST — HOW PE HANDLES IT

Every experienced buyer has sat across the table from a seller who anchors on invested capital. It is the single most common valuation mistake made by owner-operators. The PE response is short and does not apologize:

"We do not buy what you spent. We buy what the business produces."

Sunk cost is not a valuation input. It is not an asset. It does not appear in the DCF, it does not affect the exit multiple, and it carries zero weight in a rational negotiation. If an owner invested EUR 800K and the business produces EUR 0 of profit, the investment was misallocated. The market is not obligated to compensate for that misallocation.

The practical response in a negotiation: acknowledge the investment, express genuine respect for the seller's capital commitment, then redirect the conversation immediately to earnings power. "I understand you invested significantly. Let us look at what the business can support based on its current cash generation." Never argue about the EUR 800K. It is a dead end. Simply do not validate it as a pricing input.

The only scenario where invested capital becomes relevant is in a liquidation analysis — specifically, when the buyer is acquiring assets rather than a going concern. In that case, replacement cost is a ceiling check, not a floor. If the asset cost EUR 800K to build and you can acquire it for EUR 200K, that is a discount to replacement cost — which is an argument for the buyer, not the seller.
