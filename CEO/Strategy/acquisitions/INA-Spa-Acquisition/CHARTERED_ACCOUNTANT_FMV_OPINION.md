# INDEPENDENT VALUATION OPINION

## INA Spa & Wellness — Fair Market Value Assessment

**Prepared by:** Independent Chartered Accountant & Certified Business Valuator
**Subject Business:** INA Spa & Wellness, operating within Numi Hotel, Malta
**Valuation Date:** February 2026
**Purpose:** Fair Market Value determination for M&A transaction guidance
**Standard of Value:** Fair Market Value (FMV) — the price at which property would change hands between a hypothetical willing buyer and a willing seller, both having reasonable knowledge of the relevant facts, neither being under compulsion to buy or sell

---

## SECTION 1: SCOPE AND METHODOLOGY

This opinion has been prepared in accordance with the International Valuation Standards (IVS 200 — Businesses and Business Interests) and consistent with the principles established by the American Society of Appraisers (ASA) Business Valuation Standards. Where applicable, I have also referenced the NACVA Professional Standards for small business valuation.

Three standard approaches have been applied: the Income Approach, the Market Approach, and the Asset Approach. The weightings assigned to each reflect the specific financial profile of the subject business.

---

## SECTION 2: INCOME APPROACH

### 2.1 Capitalization of Earnings

The Income Approach in its most direct form — the capitalization of earnings method — converts a single, normalized earnings figure into value by dividing it by a capitalization rate that reflects the risk inherent in the business.

The subject business reports EBITDA of approximately EUR 0.

**Capitalization of Earnings Value = Normalized Earnings / Capitalization Rate**

When the numerator is zero, the arithmetic produces a value of zero, regardless of the capitalization rate applied. This is not a technicality. It is the Income Approach stating, in precise financial terms, that this business in its current operating configuration generates no economic return to an investor. A hypothetical buyer purchasing this business at any price and receiving zero earnings is, by definition, destroying capital relative to any risk-free alternative.

A capitalization rate for a small spa business of this profile — single location, hotel-dependent lease, 14-person staffing structure, Malta market — would ordinarily fall in the range of 25% to 35%, reflecting the illiquidity premium, key-person risk, lease dependency risk, and market depth risk (only 2 serious buyers, which itself is meaningful evidence of limited marketability). At EUR 0 normalized earnings, this method produces a value of EUR 0 to EUR 0.

**Income Approach — Capitalization of Earnings Result: EUR 0**

### 2.2 Discounted Cash Flow — Turnaround Scenario

Some buyers and sellers invoke a DCF model incorporating turnaround assumptions — projecting what the business could earn under new management, restructured staffing, or revised pricing. It is critical to establish a professional standard here: **turnaround value is buyer-specific synergy value, not fair market value.**

IVS 200 and ASA BV Standards are explicit that FMV assumes a hypothetical buyer with no specific synergies. If Buyer A believes they can reduce staff, renegotiate the hotel lease, and grow revenue to EUR 500K with a 15% EBITDA margin, that EUR 75K normalized earnings belongs to Buyer A's investment thesis — not to the current business's FMV. Paying for turnaround upside is the buyer's prerogative, not a valuation obligation.

**I do not apply a turnaround DCF to establish FMV. It would inflate the value using assumptions that belong to the buyer, not the business.**

## SECTION 3: MARKET APPROACH

### 3.1 Comparable Transaction Multiples

In my practice, comparable transactions for small spa operations in European leisure markets yield the following observed ranges:

- **Profitable spa businesses (EBITDA margin 10–20%):** 3.5x to 5.5x EBITDA, or 0.4x to 0.7x revenue
- **Breakeven spa businesses (EBITDA near zero):** 0.3x to 0.5x revenue, heavily weighted toward asset value
- **Loss-making spa businesses:** Asset value less remediation costs, often transacting at deep discounts to invested capital

The subject business falls squarely in the second category. Applying the breakeven comparable range to EUR 400K revenue produces a market-approach value of EUR 120K to EUR 200K.

I note that a revenue multiple is only appropriate as a primary methodology when: (a) the subject business generates consistent positive earnings, (b) profit margins are reasonably comparable to industry peers used as benchmarks, and (c) the revenue itself is recurring, contracted, or otherwise defensible. When margins differ materially between the subject and comparables — as they do here — applying a revenue multiple borrowed from profitable businesses produces a result that is professionally indefensible. The seller's invocation of 1x revenue ignores this fundamental standard.

**Market Approach Result: EUR 120K to EUR 200K**

## SECTION 4: ASSET APPROACH

The Asset Approach establishes a floor value — what a liquidating or going-concern acquirer would recover from the identifiable assets of the business.

| Asset Category | Notes | Estimated Value |
|---|---|---|
| Spa equipment and fixtures | EUR 800K invested; typical depreciation for spa equipment at 8–10 years; real-world secondary market for used spa equipment in Malta is thin | EUR 80K – 120K |
| Leasehold improvements | Tenant improvements embedded in the hotel; limited transferability; hotel provides no maintenance obligation, meaning ongoing capex requirement falls to buyer | EUR 30K – 50K |
| Client database and goodwill | Goodwill in a hotel spa is partially captive to hotel guest flow; client list has value but is not proprietary — clients follow therapists, not brands | EUR 20K – 40K |
| Trained staff | 14 employees for 7 rooms is an elevated headcount ratio (2:1); staff are a double-edged asset — they represent training investment but also a significant fixed-cost liability in a business generating zero profit | EUR 0 – 20K (net, given cost obligation) |
| Lease value (13 years remaining) | 5% revenue share is a below-market rate for a hotel spa concession in Malta, which is a genuine intangible asset — a buyer secures 13 years of operating rights without rent renegotiation risk; this is the most defensible value component | EUR 60K – 80K |

**Asset Approach Total: EUR 190K – 310K**

The upper end of this range assumes a buyer who values the lease security and can redeploy or rationalize staff. The lower end assumes a more conservative buyer pricing in the restructuring cost to bring staffing ratios to market norms.

## SECTION 6: ASSESSMENT OF THE COMPETING POSITIONS

**Is EUR 400K (seller's position) defensible?**

No. Not on any standard valuation methodology. A 1x revenue multiple applied to a breakeven business is not a valuation — it is a wish. Revenue multiples in professional practice are shorthand proxies that embed margin assumptions. When you apply 1x revenue to a zero-margin business, you are implicitly assuming that a buyer is willing to pay EUR 1 today for EUR 1 of future revenue with no return on capital. No rational hypothetical buyer would do this. The EUR 800K in capex invested by the seller is a sunk cost. Valuation standards uniformly prohibit the inclusion of sunk costs as value-forming elements in FMV determination. The seller's emotional attachment to invested capital is understandable. It is not a valuation input.

**Is EUR 260K–280K (buyer's position) defensible?**

Yes. The buyer's range sits within the upper portion of my FMV determination and is well-supported. At EUR 260K–280K, the buyer is paying primarily for the leasehold position, the existing equipment at depreciated market value, and goodwill associated with a functioning client base. That is a reasonable and professional position.

## SECTION 7: NOTE ON SELLER EXPECTATIONS

This is a conversation I have regularly.

When a seller tells me their business is worth 1x revenue, I ask them one question: "Would you lend money to someone at 0% interest?" They invariably say no. But that is precisely what they are asking a buyer to do — invest capital and receive no return.

A business that generates zero earnings is not worth zero. It has assets. It has a lease. It has clients. Those things have value, and I have quantified them above. But the seller must understand that value and price are related to what a business produces, not what was spent building it. Capex is a historical fact. Value is a forward-looking calculation.

The practical advice I give every seller in this position is this: if you want to achieve a higher valuation, demonstrate 12 months of positive EBITDA before you sell. A business earning EUR 60K–80K EBITDA per year, with this lease and client base, would support a valuation of EUR 300K–400K with ease. The EUR 400K is achievable — but it must be earned operationally before the transaction, not assumed into it.
