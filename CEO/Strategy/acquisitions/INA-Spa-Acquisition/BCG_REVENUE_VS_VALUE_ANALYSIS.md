## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The seller's implied ask of EUR 400K (1x revenue) is not defensible under any standard valuation methodology for a breakeven hospitality-adjacent business. The buyer's current range of EUR 220-280K is well-supported by fundamentals. This memo explains why, provides the arithmetic the buyer needs to make this case, and identifies whether any deal structure can bridge a EUR 120-180K gap.

## 1. WHEN DOES 1X REVENUE MAKE SENSE IN SPA/WELLNESS M&A?

Revenue multiples are shorthand. They are only used when margin structure is consistent and predictable across comparable transactions. A 1x revenue multiple is occasionally defensible in the following conditions:

**Condition A — Strong, stable EBITDA margins.** SaaS businesses trade at high revenue multiples because they carry 70-80% gross margins. Premium spa businesses with disciplined operations can achieve 20-30% EBITDA margins. At EUR 400K revenue with a 25% EBITDA margin, that is EUR 100K EBITDA — which, at a 4x EBITDA multiple, would yield a EUR 400K valuation. The math works only if the EBITDA is there. It is not here.

**Condition B — Proprietary brand equity or IP.** A business with a recognizable brand, loyal membership base, or unique method that travels with the brand commands a premium on revenue. INA Spa is a hotel-embedded operation. The brand does not travel. The customer relationship belongs to the hotel, not the spa operator.

**Condition C — Growth trajectory justifying forward multiples.** Revenue multiples are sometimes applied to fast-growing businesses where current EBITDA is suppressed by reinvestment. That is not this business. This is a mature, stable-revenue operation at a 7-room, hotel-captive facility with no visible growth lever.

**Conclusion:** None of the three conditions that justify 1x revenue are present in this deal. The seller is applying a rule of thumb that only makes sense in a completely different type of business.

## 2. THE FUNDAMENTAL DISCONNECT — THE MATH

The seller hears "EUR 400K revenue" and translates it directly to enterprise value. This is the mistake. Buyers buy cash flow, not revenue.

Work through the numbers:

**Starting point: EUR 400K gross revenue**

Deduct the hotel lease (5% revenue share): EUR 20K annually paid to the hotel with no maintenance obligation returned. This is a cost with zero reciprocal benefit beyond tenancy.

**Remaining gross revenue after lease: EUR 380K**

The business is at breakeven EBITDA. That means after staff costs, consumables, utilities, marketing, insurance, and the lease, the business earns approximately EUR 0. Fourteen employees in a 7-room spa is a significant payroll. Assuming EUR 300K in total operating costs (staff, consumables, utilities, marketing) plus EUR 20K lease, the cost base is roughly EUR 320-340K. EBITDA of zero means there is nothing left for the buyer after covering operations.

**What does a buyer actually receive?**

- Year 1 cash return on EUR 400K investment: EUR 0
- Return on invested capital: 0%
- 10-year undiscounted cumulative return (assuming breakeven): EUR 0
- Payback period: Never, at current EBITDA

At EUR 200K, the buyer still earns 0% return on capital because EBITDA is zero. The logic for the buyer to pay anything is entirely predicated on their ability to extract value the current operator cannot — through operational improvement, integration synergies, or cost reduction.

**The seller's EUR 800K capex figure is irrelevant to value.** This is a common seller error. Historical capex is a sunk cost. The market does not compensate sellers for poor historical returns on investment. What the seller spent building the business has no bearing on what a rational buyer will pay today.

## 3. WHAT THE BUYER IS ACTUALLY ACQUIRING

At the asking price of EUR 400K, here is a precise breakdown of what changes hands:

- **A lease with 13 years remaining** inside a hotel, with 5% revenue leakage to the landlord and zero maintenance support. This is a liability as much as an asset. The hotel can choose not to renew at expiry.
- **Equipment and fit-out** with an unknown remaining useful life. The EUR 800K invested by the seller is now depreciated. Current replacement value of the physical plant is what matters — likely EUR 150-250K depending on condition.
- **14 staff positions** — a cost center that needs to be renegotiated or restructured immediately. This headcount is bloated for a 7-room operation. Industry benchmark is 1.2-1.5 staff per treatment room; 14 staff for 7 rooms is nearly double the efficient ratio.
- **An existing client base** — the primary asset with genuine intangible value, but captive to a hotel environment. Client retention post-acquisition of a hotel spa is historically unreliable.
- **Zero working capital buffer** — the business is at breakeven, meaning there is no operational cushion for the buyer in the transition period.

The honest asset-by-asset valuation does not reach EUR 400K.

## 4. HOW TO FRAME THIS TO THE SELLER — EXACT LANGUAGE

The buyer should not argue the seller's logic directly. Instead, anchor the conversation in what the business must produce to justify any price.

Suggested framing:

> "We understand what you have built here, and we respect the capital and time you have invested. Our offer is not a reflection of that effort. Here is the constraint we are working within: we are acquiring the cash flow this business generates, not its history. Today, the business is at breakeven. That means at any price — EUR 200K, EUR 400K, or EUR 800K — the return to us in Year 1 is zero. Our price reflects the cost of rebuilding that cash flow ourselves plus a premium for the client base and the operational runway your lease provides. At EUR 260K, we are paying above replacement cost for a business that currently earns nothing. That is a fair premium for what you have built, and it reflects real risk on our side."

Separately, address the 1x revenue logic directly:

> "If this business were earning EUR 80-100K EBITDA, we would be having a very different conversation. At that level, EUR 400K would be a reasonable starting point. The gap between us is not about disagreeing on your revenue — it is about the cost structure beneath it. We need to be able to service the acquisition and still have a business worth operating."

## 5. COMPARABLE BENCHMARKS — WHAT SIMILAR BUSINESSES ACTUALLY TRADE AT

In European small-to-mid-cap spa and wellness transactions, standard valuation parameters are as follows:

| Scenario | EBITDA Margin | Typical Multiple | Implied Value (EUR 400K Rev) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Breakeven operations (this deal) | 0% | 0.5-0.7x Revenue | EUR 200-280K |
| Moderate margin | 10-15% | 3-4x EBITDA | EUR 120-240K |
| Strong operations | 20-25% | 4-5x EBITDA | EUR 320-500K |
| Premium branded asset | 25-30% | 5-6x EBITDA | EUR 500-720K |

Hotel-embedded spas with no proprietary brand, no membership model, and operational lease dependency trade at the bottom of this range — 0.5-0.6x revenue, maximum. That puts fair value at EUR 200-240K, consistent with the four expert reports already commissioned.

The buyer's target range of EUR 260-280K already represents a 10-17% premium to fundamental value in recognition of the client base and lease remaining.

## 6. THE HONEST ANSWER — CAN THE GAP BE BRIDGED?

A EUR 120-180K gap between EUR 280K and EUR 400K is not automatically fatal, but it requires deal structure creativity rather than price movement.

**Option A — Earnout on EBITDA recovery.** Pay EUR 260K at close. Offer an additional EUR 80-120K contingent on the business achieving a defined EBITDA threshold (e.g., EUR 60K) within 24 months post-acquisition. If the seller believes in the value, they accept the earnout. If they do not, the earnout reveals the gap in confidence.

**Option B — Deferred payment with operational milestones.** EUR 260K at close, EUR 40K deferred over 18 months tied to staff transition and client retention metrics. Total EUR 300K if milestones are met.

**Option C — Seller financing.** EUR 260K cash at close, EUR 80K seller note at 4-5% over 3 years, payable from operating cash flow. Seller gets closer to their number; buyer manages cash exposure; note is only viable if operations improve.

**If the seller refuses all structured alternatives and holds at EUR 400K cash at close:** this deal does not work. A EUR 400K purchase price for a breakeven business requires the buyer to fund the acquisition entirely from synergies and operational improvement — with no margin for error, no income to service debt, and no payback horizon. That is not an investment; it is a speculative bet on a premise the seller himself cannot demonstrate with his own operating history.

The seller spent EUR 800K and built a EUR 0 EBITDA business. He is asking the buyer to pay EUR 400K for the privilege of trying harder.

The buyer should hold at EUR 280K as the ceiling, offer a structured earnout above that level, and be prepared to walk at EUR 320K. With only two serious buyers, urgency is on the seller's side, not the buyer's.
