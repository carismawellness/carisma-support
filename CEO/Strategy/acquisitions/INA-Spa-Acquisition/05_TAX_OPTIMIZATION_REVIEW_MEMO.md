## 1. QUESTIONS EACH EXPERT SHOULD ADDRESS

### 1.1 For the Malta Tax Lawyer

**Anti-Avoidance Provisions (Article 51, Income Tax Act).** Any aggressive purchase price allocation -- loading value onto depreciable equipment rather than non-depreciable goodwill -- must be tested against Article 51. The Commissioner for Revenue has broad discretion to disregard arrangements whose primary purpose is obtaining a tax advantage. If 70% of the purchase price is allocated to equipment (depreciable) and only 5% to goodwill (non-depreciable), the Commissioner could challenge this as lacking commercial substance. The lawyer must confirm that the allocation is defensible with an independent equipment appraisal and that the allocation rationale is documented in the APA. This is not theoretical: Malta's Inland Revenue has become more aggressive on purchase price allocations in asset deals since 2023.

**Lease Premium vs. Goodwill Treatment.** The transaction involves a 13-year commercial lease currently at EUR 5K/month (below market). A payment attributable to the lease assignment -- the "lease premium" -- may be treated differently from goodwill under Maltese tax law. Under the Income Tax Act, a premium paid for the assignment of a lease can be treated as capital expenditure on the acquisition of a right, potentially depreciable under the Sixth Schedule if it qualifies as "expenditure on know-how or patents" or analogous rights. Alternatively, it may fall under the general rules for capital expenditure on an interest in immovable property. The lawyer must clarify: (a) how the lease premium should be characterized for income tax purposes, (b) whether it qualifies for capital allowances, and (c) whether the 5% stamp duty on immovable property interests applies to the lease component specifically (versus the 2% rate on business goodwill). Getting this wrong could cost EUR 3,000-9,000 in unnecessary stamp duty.

**Notional Interest Deduction (NID).** Malta introduced the NID as part of its fiscal consolidation measures. If Carisma funds this acquisition with equity (which is likely for a EUR 300K+ deal), the NID allows a deduction for a notional return on risk capital. At the current reference rate (roughly 1.5-2.5% on the ECB benchmark plus a premium), the NID on EUR 300K-500K of equity could generate an annual deduction of EUR 6,000-15,000, reducing taxable income meaningfully in early years when EBITDA is thin. The lawyer must confirm: (a) whether Carisma's existing corporate structure qualifies for NID, (b) what the current applicable rate is, and (c) whether the equity used for this acquisition can be ring-fenced as "risk capital" for NID purposes.

**Full Imputation Refund Mechanism.** If Carisma has non-Maltese shareholders (the Turkish founding connection suggests this is possible), the 6/7ths refund mechanism under Malta's imputation system reduces the effective corporate tax rate from 35% to 5%. However, the refund requires strict procedural compliance: the shareholder must be a "registered shareholder" on the date the dividend is declared, the company must have elected for the refund to apply, and the refund claim must be filed within the statutory deadline. If any procedural step is missed, the full 35% applies with no recovery. The lawyer must map out the exact refund mechanics for Carisma's ownership chain and confirm that all procedural requirements are being met for existing operations (because if they are not, this acquisition compounds the exposure).

**ATAD Implementation.** Malta transposed the EU Anti-Tax Avoidance Directive in 2019. The interest limitation rule (capping net borrowing costs at 30% of EBITDA or EUR 3M, whichever is higher) is unlikely to bite on a deal this size, but the General Anti-Abuse Rule (GAAR) under ATAD is broader than Article 51 and applies to "non-genuine arrangements." If the deal involves any intercompany financing or holding company layers, the lawyer should confirm that the ATAD GAAR has been considered.

**Double Tax Treaty Implications.** Malta has an extensive treaty network (70+ treaties). If any part of Carisma's ownership or financing chain runs through a treaty jurisdiction (Turkey, UK, other), the lawyer must confirm that treaty benefits are available and that the Principal Purpose Test (PPT) under BEPS Action 6 (now incorporated into most of Malta's treaties via the MLI) is satisfied. This is especially relevant if the Turkish founders use a holding structure to claim the imputation refund.

### 1.2 For the Malta Tax Accountant

**Capital Allowances: Initial vs. Wear-and-Tear.** Malta's Sixth Schedule to the Income Tax Act provides two types of capital allowances: (a) an initial allowance (a one-off deduction in the year of acquisition), and (b) annual wear-and-tear allowances (straight-line depreciation). For plant and machinery, the typical initial allowance is 20% and the annual wear-and-tear is 20% (reducing balance or straight-line depending on the asset class). The accountant must confirm: (a) whether the CURRENT rates apply (there have been amendments -- verify against the 2025/2026 Budget measures), (b) whether medical devices (Candela laser, T-Shape) qualify under the "plant and machinery" category or under a separate "scientific/medical equipment" category with potentially different rates, and (c) whether the initial allowance is still available for second-hand equipment acquired as part of a business transfer (some jurisdictions limit initial allowances to new assets).

**Accelerated Depreciation for Medical Equipment.** Malta has periodically introduced accelerated depreciation incentives for specific investment categories, including medical and scientific equipment. The accountant must check: (a) whether any Malta Enterprise incentive or Budget 2025/2026 measure provides enhanced capital allowances for medical devices in the healthcare/wellness sector, (b) whether the Candela laser and T-Shape device qualify for immediate expensing under any small-business or SME capital investment scheme, and (c) whether the future EUR 150K clinic investment could qualify for enhanced first-year allowances if structured correctly.

**Loss Carry-Forward Rules.** If the spa business runs at a loss in Year 1 (which is plausible after restructuring costs, transition friction, and accelerated depreciation claims), unused capital allowances and trading losses can generally be carried forward indefinitely in Malta against future profits of the same trade. However, the accountant must confirm: (a) whether a change of ownership triggers any loss restriction (Malta does not have a UK-style change of ownership rule for losses, but this should be verified), (b) whether the losses from the spa and clinic are treated as one trade or two (if treated as separate trades, losses from one cannot shelter profits from the other), and (c) whether carried-forward losses can shelter profits that are then eligible for the imputation refund (the interaction between loss utilization and the refund system needs to be mapped).

**35% Headline vs. 5% Effective Rate.** The 5-year tax projection must show BOTH the headline 35% tax charge AND the effective rate after the 6/7ths refund. This matters for two reasons: first, the 35% is the rate that determines Provisional Tax (PT) payments, creating a cash flow mismatch (you pay 35% in advance but recover 30/35ths as a refund months later). Second, the imputation refund is paid to the shareholder, not the company -- so the company-level cash flow shows the full 35% outflow, and the shareholder must fund the gap until the refund arrives. The accountant must model this cash flow timing explicitly, because on a EUR 72K EBITDA business, the PT payment could be EUR 15-25K annually, with the refund arriving 6-14 months later. That is EUR 15-25K of dead capital sitting with the tax authority.

**Provisional Tax Timing.** Malta's PT system requires three payments: April 30, August 31, and December 21. For a newly acquired business, the first year PT basis may default to the estimated income for the year. If the accountant can establish a credible low estimate (citing restructuring costs, transition losses), the PT payments can be minimized legitimately. Overpaying PT and then waiting months for a refund is a cash flow trap that many first-time acquirers in Malta fall into.

**Purchase Price Allocation and Stamp Duty Interaction.** This is a critical cross-discipline issue. The allocation that minimizes income tax (load value onto depreciable equipment) may INCREASE stamp duty (if the equipment is valued higher, the total transfer duty base is larger). Conversely, allocating more to non-depreciable goodwill reduces the depreciable base but may attract a lower stamp duty rate (2% vs. 5%). The accountant must run a sensitivity analysis showing the NET tax benefit of different allocations across both income tax savings (5-year NPV of capital allowances) and stamp duty cost (one-time). The optimal allocation is not obvious and requires actual numbers.

### 1.3 For the M&A Deal Specialist

**Provisional Withholding Tax on Property Transfers.** Under Maltese law, a 15% provisional withholding tax may apply to transfers of immovable property. If the lease assignment is treated as a transfer of an interest in immovable property (which a 13-year commercial lease might be), the buyer may be required to withhold 15% of the consideration attributable to the lease and pay it to the Inland Revenue, with the seller receiving a credit. The deal specialist must confirm whether this applies and, if so, factor it into the payment mechanics. At EUR 100K allocated to the lease, this is EUR 15K of cash tied up until the seller's tax return is processed.

**TOGC (Transfer of a Going Concern) for VAT.** If the transaction qualifies as a Transfer of a Going Concern under Article 13(2) of the VAT Act (Cap. 406), no VAT applies on the transfer. However, TOGC treatment requires that: (a) the assets are transferred as a going concern, (b) the buyer continues the same business activity, and (c) the transfer includes all essential assets for the business to continue. If TOGC fails -- for example, because the buyer does not take over all employees or because the lease is treated separately from the business -- VAT at 18% on the asset value could apply. On a EUR 300K deal, that is EUR 54K of unexpected VAT. While the buyer may recover this as input VAT, the cash flow impact is severe (EUR 54K paid upfront, recovered over subsequent VAT periods). The deal specialist must structure the APA to maximize TOGC eligibility and obtain written confirmation from the VAT Department that TOGC applies.

**Medical Device Regulatory Transfer.** The transaction roadmap mentions the Malta Medicines Authority and device registration, but the question is more nuanced. The Candela laser is a Class IIb medical device under EU MDR 2017/745. The operator must have a designated "Responsible Person" (RP) and must be registered with the MMA. The deal specialist must confirm: (a) whether the DEVICE registration transfers automatically with the business or requires new registration by the buyer, (b) whether Carisma needs to designate a new RP (a named individual with appropriate qualifications), and (c) whether there is any operational downtime during the registration transfer (devices cannot be used without valid registration). If the laser generates EUR 5-10K/month in revenue, even 30 days of downtime costs EUR 5-10K in lost revenue.

**GDPR and Client Database Transfer.** The client database is a commercial asset being transferred. Under GDPR (and Malta's Data Protection Act, Cap. 586), transferring personal data as part of a business sale requires: (a) a lawful basis for processing (legitimate interest is typically relied upon, but it must be documented), (b) notification to data subjects (clients must be informed that their data is being transferred to a new controller), and (c) potentially a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) if the transfer involves sensitive health data (which spa/aesthetics client records almost certainly do -- treatment histories, medical questionnaires, before/after photos). The deal specialist should factor EUR 2,000-5,000 for GDPR compliance work and ensure the APA includes a warranty that the seller's data collection was GDPR-compliant (because if it was not, the buyer inherits tainted data).

**Prepaid Treatment Packages.** The transaction roadmap correctly identifies prepaid packages as a hidden liability, but the tax treatment deserves attention. If clients have prepaid for treatments (say EUR 15-20K outstanding), the seller has already collected the cash and (presumably) declared the VAT and income tax on receipt. When the buyer delivers the treatments, the buyer incurs costs without corresponding revenue. The deal specialist must ensure that: (a) a complete schedule of prepaid obligations is obtained, (b) the purchase price is reduced by the full outstanding prepaid amount, and (c) the tax treatment is clarified -- can the buyer claim a deduction for the cost of delivering prepaid treatments, or is it considered part of the purchase price (non-deductible)?

## 2. GAPS AND BLIND SPOTS

### 2.1 The Stamp Duty vs. Capital Allowances Tug-of-War

This is the single most important cross-discipline issue that risks falling through the cracks. The tax lawyer advises on stamp duty rates. The accountant advises on capital allowances. The deal specialist structures the APA. But the optimal purchase price allocation requires all three to coordinate simultaneously, because:

- Allocating EUR 200K to equipment (depreciable at 20% p.a.) saves approximately EUR 40K over 5 years in income tax (at 5% effective rate: EUR 200K x 20% x 5 years x 5% = EUR 10K; at 35% headline: EUR 70K). But it may attract 5% stamp duty on EUR 200K = EUR 10K.
- Allocating EUR 200K to goodwill (non-depreciable) produces zero capital allowances but attracts only 2% stamp duty = EUR 4K.
- The NET difference is material: the optimal allocation could save EUR 5,000-15,000 depending on the actual rates and the time value of the capital allowance stream.

None of the three experts will see this interaction unless they are explicitly asked to model it jointly.

### 2.2 Timing of Redundancies vs. TUPE vs. Tax Deductibility

Post-acquisition redundancy costs (EUR 15-35K per the deal specialist's estimate) are deductible as trading expenses. BUT the timing matters. If redundancies occur before the business transfer date, they are the seller's expense. If they occur after, they are the buyer's expense. The deal specialist correctly notes that restructuring should happen AFTER transfer (Weeks 12-18), but the tax accountant should confirm that redundancy payments made during this period are fully deductible as revenue expenses (not capital expenses related to the acquisition, which would be non-deductible). There is a grey area in Maltese tax law about whether restructuring costs incurred "in connection with" an acquisition are capital or revenue in nature. The safe harbor is to wait at least 3-6 months post-close before restructuring and to document the business rationale independently of the acquisition.

### 2.3 Cash Flow Timing: When Do Tax Costs Hit vs. When Do Savings Arrive?

None of the three experts are likely to produce a month-by-month CASH tax flow model. But this deal has a severe cash flow timing problem:

- **Month 0:** Stamp duty due at closing (EUR 6,000-15,000 -- immediate cash outflow)
- **Months 1-4:** Provisional Tax payments begin based on estimated income (EUR 5,000-8,000 per installment if the business is profitable)
- **Month 12:** First-year capital allowances reduce taxable income, but the TAX SAVING only materializes when the tax return is filed (18 months after year-end)
- **Month 14-24:** Imputation refund for Year 1 arrives (6-14 months after dividend declaration)

Net result: the buyer is cash-flow negative on taxes for the first 18-24 months, even though the long-term tax position is favorable. On a EUR 72K EBITDA business, the timing mismatch could consume EUR 20,000-30,000 of working capital. This must be factored into the working capital reserve.

### 2.4 The "Day After" Compliance Burden

After the acquisition closes, Carisma will need to:
- File PT returns three times per year for the new business unit
- File annual income tax returns (potentially separate from Carisma's main return if the business is in a different entity)
- File monthly/quarterly VAT returns incorporating the new revenue stream
- Maintain separate capital allowance schedules for the acquired assets
- Track the imputation credit balance and refund claims
- File Jobsplus notifications for all transferred and subsequently terminated employees
- Potentially file CRS/FATCA returns if any foreign shareholders are involved

The ongoing compliance cost is approximately EUR 3,000-5,000/year on top of Carisma's existing compliance burden. This should be in the accountant's 5-year projection but often is not.

### 2.5 Pillar Two (Global Minimum Tax)

The OECD Pillar Two rules (15% global minimum tax) apply to MNE groups with consolidated revenue exceeding EUR 750M. Carisma Wellness Group, with approximately EUR 400K revenue from this acquisition and even assuming EUR 5-10M consolidated group revenue, is well below the threshold. Pillar Two is IRRELEVANT to this deal. The experts should confirm this in one sentence and move on -- but they should not ignore it entirely, because if Carisma has ambitions to grow through further acquisitions and eventually exceeds EUR 750M, the Malta imputation structure would need to be revisited. A note in the memo is sufficient.

### 2.6 EU State Aid Risk

Malta's full imputation system with the 6/7ths refund has faced periodic EU State Aid scrutiny (the European Commission has examined Malta's tax system multiple times). While the system has been found compliant to date, there is a non-zero risk that future EU action could restrict or modify the refund mechanism. Any tax projection relying on the 5% effective rate should include a sensitivity showing the impact of a hypothetical increase to 10-15% effective rate. This is a tail risk but one worth acknowledging.

## 3. CREATIVE TAX STRATEGIES

### 3.1 Transition/Consulting Fee Structure

Could part of the EUR 300K+ payment be structured as a consulting fee to the seller for transition services, rather than as part of the purchase price? A EUR 20-30K "transition consultancy agreement" paid over 12 months would be:
- **For the buyer:** Deductible as a revenue expense (reduces taxable income immediately, unlike capitalized goodwill which is non-depreciable)
- **For the seller:** Taxable as income (but the seller is exiting and may have losses or personal allowances to offset)

The risk: the Commissioner could recharacterize the consulting fee as part of the purchase price if it lacks commercial substance. Mitigation: the consulting agreement must have genuine deliverables (client introductions, staff training, supplier transitions), a defined scope, and be at arm's length rates. EUR 2,000-2,500/month for 12 months is defensible for a genuine transition consultancy.

Estimated saving: EUR 20-30K x 5% effective rate = EUR 1,000-1,500. Small, but essentially free if the transition period is happening anyway.

### 3.2 Separate Entity for Clinic Investment

The future EUR 150K clinic investment could be housed in a separate Maltese company, with Carisma providing management services to the clinic entity (and charging a management fee). This creates:
- A separate depreciation pool for the clinic assets (potentially with enhanced first-year allowances if Malta Enterprise incentives apply)
- Intercompany management charges that are deductible by the clinic entity and taxable to Carisma (but potentially at a lower effective rate through the imputation system)
- Flexibility for future structuring: if the clinic becomes very profitable, it could be sold separately, potentially qualifying for the participation exemption on a share sale

The risk: Transfer pricing scrutiny. Intercompany charges must be at arm's length. The Commissioner could challenge above-market management fees. Mitigation: benchmark management fees against comparable Malta wellness management contracts (typically 5-10% of revenue).

Estimated saving: Difficult to quantify without a full model, but the structural optionality alone is worth EUR 10,000-20,000 over the life of the investment. The clinic entity also isolates liability.

### 3.3 Malta's IP Regime (Patent Box)

Does Carisma own any proprietary treatment protocols, training methodologies, or brand IP that could be licensed to the acquired business? Malta's Patent Box regime provides a reduced tax rate (effectively 0-5%) on qualifying IP income. If Carisma develops a proprietary treatment protocol (e.g., a branded wellness program combining spa and clinical treatments), the IP could be registered and licensed to the operating entity at an arm's length royalty. Revenue shifted from trading income (5-35% tax) to IP income (0-5% tax) creates a meaningful saving.

The risk: The IP must be genuine, registered, and the royalty rate must be arm's length. Creating artificial IP purely for tax purposes is exactly the type of arrangement that Article 51 and the ATAD GAAR target. Mitigation: only pursue this if Carisma genuinely develops proprietary IP (treatment protocols, branded programs, training systems) that has commercial value independent of the tax benefit.

Estimated saving: If EUR 30-50K/year of revenue can be characterized as IP royalties, the saving is EUR 1,500-2,500/year (marginal, given the already-low 5% effective rate). Worth pursuing only if the IP is genuine.

### 3.4 Lease Restructuring for Tax Advantage

Could Carisma negotiate with the Numi Hotel to restructure the lease terms in a way that creates tax benefits? Options include:
- **Front-loading rent:** Paying a lump-sum lease premium upfront (deductible over the lease term) in exchange for reduced monthly rent. This accelerates the deduction into early years when it shelters clinic ramp-up losses.
- **Revenue-share restructuring:** If the current 5% hotel revenue share can be renegotiated to a higher percentage with a lower base rent, the variable component may be fully deductible as a cost of sales rather than a fixed overhead. This changes the P&L profile without changing the economics.
- **Improvement allowance:** Negotiate with the landlord for a rent-free period or contribution to fit-out costs in exchange for the lease commitment. Landlord contributions to fit-out are not taxable income if structured correctly (they are treated as a reduction in the cost of the leasehold improvements, reducing the depreciable base).

Estimated saving: Highly dependent on the landlord's willingness. A 6-month rent-free period alone is worth EUR 30,000.

### 3.5 Malta Enterprise Grants and EU Structural Funds

Malta Enterprise offers several incentive schemes relevant to this acquisition:
- **Investment Aid Tax Credits (IATC):** Tax credits of 25-50% (depending on company size) on qualifying capital expenditure for initial investments. The EUR 150K clinic investment could qualify, generating EUR 37,500-75,000 in tax credits.
- **Business Development and Continuity Scheme:** Grants for business expansion in tourism and healthcare sectors.
- **Micro Invest Scheme:** Tax credits for micro enterprises (fewer than 30 employees) -- Carisma may qualify at the single-location level.
- **EU Structural Funds:** Malta receives EU cohesion funding, with allocations for SME investment in tourism and healthcare infrastructure.

The risk: Application processes are bureaucratic and competitive. No guarantee of approval. Malta Enterprise incentives have specific conditions (employment maintenance, sector restrictions, de minimis State Aid limits).

Estimated saving: If the IATC applies to the EUR 150K clinic investment at 35% (mid-range), the saving is EUR 52,500 -- the single largest potential tax optimization in the deal.

## 4. PRIORITY RECOMMENDATIONS (Ranked by EUR Value)

### Rank 1: Malta Enterprise Investment Aid Tax Credits (IATC)
- **What:** Apply for IATC on the EUR 150K clinic capital investment
- **Estimated saving:** EUR 37,500-75,000 (one-time tax credit, 25-50% of qualifying expenditure)
- **Complexity:** Medium (application required, conditions attached, 3-6 month approval process)
- **Risk:** Medium (approval not guaranteed; must maintain employment levels and investment for minimum period; de minimis limits apply)
- **Action:** Engage Malta Enterprise before committing clinic capex. Structure the investment to maximize qualifying expenditure.

### Rank 2: Optimal Purchase Price Allocation (Stamp Duty vs. Capital Allowances)
- **What:** Model the NET tax-optimal allocation across equipment, goodwill, lease premium, and client base
- **Estimated saving:** EUR 5,000-15,000 over 5 years (net of stamp duty vs. capital allowance trade-off)
- **Complexity:** Medium (requires coordinated analysis by accountant, lawyer, and notary)
- **Risk:** Low-Medium (must be supported by independent equipment appraisal; allocation must have commercial substance)
- **Action:** Commission the equipment appraisal FIRST, then build the allocation around the appraisal value. Do not reverse-engineer the appraisal to fit the allocation.

### Rank 3: Notional Interest Deduction on Equity Funding
- **What:** Claim NID on the equity capital used to fund the acquisition
- **Estimated saving:** EUR 3,000-10,000/year (depending on the NID rate and capital base, recurring annually)
- **Complexity:** Easy (largely administrative; requires equity injection to be documented as risk capital)
- **Risk:** Low (the NID is a standard provision of Maltese tax law, widely used)
- **Action:** Ensure the equity injection is documented correctly and that the NID election is made in the first tax return.

### Rank 4: Provisional Tax Minimization in Year 1
- **What:** File conservative PT estimates based on post-acquisition restructuring costs and transition losses
- **Estimated saving:** EUR 10,000-20,000 cash flow benefit (timing, not permanent -- but capital has a time value)
- **Complexity:** Easy (administrative filing with credible supporting estimates)
- **Risk:** Low (PT estimates must be "reasonable" -- underestimation by more than 20% triggers penalties, but a genuine first-year loss estimate is defensible)
- **Action:** Accountant prepares PT estimates before the first PT installment date.

### Rank 5: Transition Consulting Fee Structure
- **What:** Structure EUR 20-30K of the consideration as a genuine transition consultancy fee (deductible by buyer)
- **Estimated saving:** EUR 1,000-1,500 (income tax saving at 5% effective rate); EUR 4,000-6,000 if stamp duty on the reduced purchase price also decreases
- **Complexity:** Easy (requires a separate consulting agreement with genuine deliverables)
- **Risk:** Low-Medium (must have commercial substance; Commissioner could recharacterize if the agreement is a sham)
- **Action:** Draft the transition consulting agreement alongside the APA, with clear scope, deliverables, and payment schedule.

## 5. RED FLAGS

### 5.1 Aggressive Purchase Price Allocation Without Appraisal Support
If the APA allocates 60-70% of the purchase price to depreciable equipment without an independent appraisal, the Commissioner for Revenue may challenge the allocation under Article 51. The penalty is not just a reassessment of the allocation -- it is potential interest and penalties on the understated tax for all years the incorrect allowances were claimed. Always obtain the appraisal BEFORE finalizing the allocation, and attach it to the APA as a schedule.

### 5.2 Imputation Refund Procedural Failures
If Carisma's shareholders miss a deadline or fail to register properly, the entire refund mechanism fails for that year. On EUR 72K EBITDA, the difference between 5% and 35% effective tax is approximately EUR 21,600. This is not a "nice to have" -- it is the foundation of Malta's attractiveness as a tax jurisdiction. The tax team must create a compliance calendar with all refund-related deadlines and assign clear responsibility.

### 5.3 TOGC Failure Creating Unexpected VAT Liability
If the transaction is structured in a way that fails TOGC (for example, if the lease is treated as a separate transaction from the business assets), 18% VAT could apply on the full consideration. On EUR 300K, that is EUR 54K. While recoverable as input VAT, the cash flow hit is immediate and the recovery takes months. Obtain advance confirmation from the VAT Department.

### 5.4 Restructuring Costs Treated as Capital
If the Commissioner treats post-acquisition redundancy costs as capital expenditure (on the basis that they were incurred to "put the business in a position to generate profits" rather than as ordinary trading expenses), they become non-deductible. This is a EUR 15-35K exposure. Mitigate by waiting at least 3-6 months post-acquisition before restructuring, documenting the business rationale independently, and taking advice from the tax lawyer specifically on this point.

### 5.5 Transfer Pricing on Intercompany Charges
If Carisma charges management fees, brand royalties, or shared service allocations to the acquired business, these must be at arm's length. Malta's transfer pricing rules (introduced more formally in recent years, aligned with OECD guidelines) give the Commissioner authority to adjust non-arm's-length transactions. Over-charging the spa to strip profits is a classic audit trigger. Keep intercompany charges benchmarked and documented.

### 5.6 Stamp Duty Understatement
If the notary or accountant calculates stamp duty on a value that the Commissioner considers below fair market value, the Commissioner can reassess based on the FMV. The penalty is the underpaid duty plus 10% and interest. Ensure the value declared for stamp duty purposes is consistent with the independent equipment appraisal and the APA price -- do not declare a lower value for stamp duty than for capital allowance purposes. This inconsistency is an immediate audit trigger.

## CLOSING NOTE

This deal, at EUR 300-340K, is small enough that aggressive structuring is unlikely to attract serious scrutiny on its own. But Carisma Wellness Group operates 50+ hotel spas, which means the Commissioner likely already knows the business. Anything done here establishes a precedent for future acquisitions. The smartest approach is to be methodical and compliant rather than aggressive: capture the legitimate savings (Malta Enterprise credits, NID, optimal allocation, PT management) and leave the marginal strategies (IP boxes, intercompany fee structures) for a later stage when the clinic is profitable and the economics justify the structuring cost.

The three experts should coordinate their memos explicitly on the stamp duty vs. capital allowances interaction, the TOGC confirmation, and the cash flow timing model. These are the areas where siloed analysis produces the wrong answer.
