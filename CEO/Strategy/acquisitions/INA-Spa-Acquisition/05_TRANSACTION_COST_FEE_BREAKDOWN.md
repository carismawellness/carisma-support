## 2. STAMP DUTY DEEP DIVE

This is the single most important tax analysis in the transaction. Getting this right can save EUR 5,000-10,000.

### 2.1 Legal Framework

Stamp duty in Malta is governed by the **Duty on Documents and Transfers Act (Chapter 364 of the Laws of Malta)**. The Act imposes duty on the transfer of certain property, including immovable property, marketable securities, and certain rights and interests.

### 2.2 Applicable Rate

For **transfers of immovable property** (including assignments of leases and transfers of commercial premises), the standard rate is **5% of the market value** of the property transferred.

For **transfers of business goodwill, trade names, and intellectual property**, Chapter 364 provides that duty is assessed on the value of the goodwill. In practice, goodwill is calculated by the Commissioner for Revenue as **two years' average profit based on the last three to five years of operation**.

For **transfers of movable property** (equipment, furniture, fixtures), Chapter 364 does not impose stamp duty. Equipment sold in an asset deal is generally treated as a sale of movable goods, subject to VAT rather than stamp duty.

### 2.3 How This Applies to the INA Deal

The critical question is: **what portion of the EUR 300K purchase price is allocable to what category of asset?**

**Asset allocation breakdown (negotiated between buyer and seller in the APA):**

| Asset Category | Estimated FMV (EUR) | Stamp Duty Treatment |
|---------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Tangible equipment (beds, sauna, hydro, reception furniture) | 80,000 - 120,000 | NO stamp duty -- movable property; subject to VAT at 18% (but VAT-registered buyer reclaims input VAT) |
| Medical devices (Candela laser, T-Shape) | 20,000 - 30,000 | NO stamp duty -- movable property; same VAT treatment |
| Lease assignment (13-year commercial lease) | 50,000 - 80,000 | 5% stamp duty applies if treated as transfer of interest in immovable property |
| Goodwill / going concern value | 70,000 - 120,000 | Stamp duty applies on goodwill portion; rate depends on CfR assessment |
| Client database | 10,000 - 20,000 | May be treated as goodwill (dutiable) or as movable intangible (not dutiable) -- depends on structuring |
| Inventory / consumables | 5,000 - 10,000 | NO stamp duty -- movable property |

### 2.4 Stamp Duty Calculation -- Three Scenarios

**Scenario A: Aggressive allocation (minimise stamp duty)**

Allocate maximum value to equipment (movable property, no duty) and minimum to goodwill/lease.

- Equipment: EUR 150,000
- Lease: EUR 50,000
- Goodwill: EUR 80,000
- Client database: EUR 10,000 (treated as movable)
- Inventory: EUR 10,000
- **Dutiable base:** EUR 130,000 (lease + goodwill)
- **Stamp duty at 5%: EUR 6,500**
- Risk: CfR may challenge the allocation if equipment values appear inflated. An independent equipment appraisal protects this position.

**Scenario B: Moderate allocation (defensible middle ground)**

- Equipment: EUR 120,000
- Lease: EUR 60,000
- Goodwill: EUR 100,000
- Client database: EUR 10,000 (treated as goodwill)
- Inventory: EUR 10,000
- **Dutiable base:** EUR 170,000 (lease + goodwill + client database)
- **Stamp duty at 5%: EUR 8,500**
- This is the most likely outcome with competent professional advice.

**Scenario C: Conservative allocation (CfR-assessed worst case)**

If the CfR treats the entire purchase price as a transfer of a going concern (including lease rights as immovable property interest):

- **Dutiable base:** EUR 300,000
- **Stamp duty at 5%: EUR 15,000**
- This is unlikely if the APA clearly separates the asset categories and is supported by independent valuations.

### 2.5 Important Stamp Duty Rules

**When due:** Stamp duty must be paid within 21 days of the execution of the deed/agreement. In practice, the notary collects the duty at signing and remits it to the CfR.

**Who pays:** By law, duty is payable by the buyer unless the agreement specifies otherwise. In practice, it is always the buyer's obligation.

**Late payment:** Penalty of 10% of the duty amount, plus interest at the rate published by the CfR (currently approximately 0.75% per month). Late payment also delays registration of the transfer.

**CfR challenge:** The Commissioner for Revenue has the right to reassess the market value of transferred assets. If the CfR determines that the allocated values in the APA do not reflect market value, the duty is recalculated on the CfR's assessed values. The taxpayer can appeal to the Administrative Review Tribunal. Having an independent valuation report significantly strengthens the buyer's position in a CfR challenge.

**Goodwill calculation by CfR:** When assessing goodwill independently, the CfR typically uses a formula of two years' average net profit based on the last three to five years of operation. If the business has been at or near breakeven (as the Chartered Accountant's FMV opinion suggests), the CfR-assessed goodwill could be EUR 0 to EUR 50,000, which would significantly reduce the duty. This is a material argument for paying for an independent FMV opinion.

### 2.6 Stamp Duty Budget Recommendation

**Budget EUR 8,500 - 10,000 for stamp duty.** With competent structuring and an independent valuation, the actual duty should fall in the EUR 6,500-10,000 range. Budget conservatively at EUR 10,000 for financing purposes.

### 2.7 VAT Implications on Equipment

Equipment transferred as part of an asset deal is subject to VAT at the standard rate of **18%**. However, if both buyer and seller are VAT-registered (which they will be), the transaction may qualify as a **Transfer of a Going Concern (TOGC)** under Maltese VAT law (transposing Article 19 of the EU VAT Directive). A TOGC is treated as a supply outside the scope of VAT, meaning no VAT is charged.

To qualify as a TOGC:
- The assets transferred must constitute a business capable of being operated independently
- The buyer must be (or become) VAT-registered
- The buyer must intend to carry on the same type of business

This deal almost certainly qualifies as a TOGC. If it does, no VAT is payable on the asset transfer. Your accountant must confirm TOGC treatment with the CfR before closing. If TOGC does not apply, VAT of 18% on the equipment portion (EUR 100-150K) would add EUR 18,000-27,000 to the transaction cost, though the buyer would reclaim this as input VAT in the next return period. The cash flow impact is 2-3 months.

### 3.2 Post-Transfer Redundancy Costs

If Carisma proceeds with restructuring from 14 to 8-9 employees within 3-6 months post-closing, the following costs apply.

**Malta's redundancy framework:**

Under the Employment and Industrial Relations Act (EIRA, Chapter 452), redundancy in Malta is governed by these key rules:

1. **Redundancy must be genuine** -- based on elimination of roles, not individual performance.
2. **LIFO (Last In, First Out)** applies strictly within each job class. You must terminate the most recently hired employee in each affected position first.
3. **Notice periods** are mandatory and depend on length of service:

| Length of Continuous Employment | Notice Period |
|-------------------------------|---------------|
| Up to 6 months | 1 week |
| 6 months to 2 years | 2 weeks |
| 2 to 4 years | 4 weeks |
| 4 to 7 years | 8 weeks |
| 7 years and above | 8 weeks (plus additional per collective agreement if applicable) |

4. **Statutory redundancy/severance pay:** Malta law does **not** mandate statutory redundancy payments beyond payment in lieu of notice period. This is a critical distinction from UK law. The employee is entitled to notice (or payment in lieu) and all accrued entitlements (leave, bonuses), but there is no statutory formula-based redundancy payment.

5. **Re-employment rights:** Redundant employees have a right of first refusal for 12 months if their position re-opens.

### 3.4 Ex-Gratia Payments (Practical Reality)

While Malta law does not mandate severance beyond notice, the practical reality -- particularly in a small market where reputational considerations matter -- is that buyers often offer ex-gratia payments of EUR 1,000-3,000 per redundant employee to:

- Avoid Industrial Tribunal claims (even frivolous claims cost EUR 2,000-5,000 to defend)
- Maintain goodwill among remaining staff (they will be watching how departing colleagues are treated)
- Secure clean separation agreements (waiver of all claims)

**Budget EUR 5,000-15,000 for ex-gratia payments** (EUR 1,000-2,500 per employee x 5-6 employees). These should be paid conditional on the employee signing a full settlement agreement drafted by your employment lawyer.

### 3.5 DIER Notification Requirements

The Department of Industrial and Employment Relations (DIER) must be notified of collective redundancies (defined as 10 or more within 30 days for companies with 21-99 employees). With 14 employees and 5-6 redundancies, you are likely below the collective redundancy threshold, but this depends on timing.

If redundancies are staggered across 2-3 months (recommended), no collective redundancy notification is required. If executed simultaneously, consult your employment lawyer on whether the notification threshold applies.

### 3.6 Social Security Implications

Employer social security contributions (National Insurance -- Class 2) must be paid for all employees up to their last day of employment. The employer's NI rate in Malta is 10% of the employee's gross salary, capped at a maximum weekly contribution. For employees earning EUR 1,400-1,800/month, the employer NI contribution is approximately EUR 140-180/month per employee.

During the notice period (whether worked or paid in lieu), employer NI contributions continue. Budget EUR 800-1,200 for employer NI on the redundant employees during their notice periods.
