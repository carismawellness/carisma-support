## 2. LEASE COMMENCEMENT INVESTIGATION

This is, as the IC Chair stated, "the single most important factual question that none of the three analysts resolved."

### 2.1 What the Contract Says

The Fortina lease contract (signed January 10, 2020) contains two critical definitions:

**"Rent Commencement Date"** (page 3 of the contract, Definitions section):
> "Shall be the 1st July 2020 **or any date after the said commencement date, whereby the Hotel opens for business.**"

**"Term"** (page 4 of the contract, Definitions section):
> "Shall be that of **15 (fifteen) years** that shall commence to run from the Rent Commencement Date."

### 2.2 The Critical Ambiguity: "Whereby the Hotel Opens for Business"

The contract provides a default Rent Commencement Date of July 1, 2020. But it includes a conditional clause: "or any date after... whereby the Hotel opens for business." This is significant because:

1. **The lease was signed in January 2020.** COVID-19 lockdowns hit Malta in March 2020.
2. **The Fortina Complex was undergoing major redevelopment.** Schedule Part 2 of the contract describes "construction currently in full swing" with "a completion date set for January 2021." The redevelopment included demolishing and replacing the existing "Spa Block" with a 13-storey extension, plus complete interior renovation of the 18-storey hotel tower.
3. **The premises were to be delivered in "shell form state"** (Schedule Part 1). The lessee then had to complete a EUR 738K+ fit-out before the spa could operate.
4. **The hotel opening post-COVID is unknown.** If the hotel did not "open for business" until mid-2022 or later (due to COVID + construction delays on a project that was targeting January 2021 completion), the Rent Commencement Date would have been delayed correspondingly.

### 2.3 Evidence from the Financial Data

The BCG report provides crucial corroboration. In Appendix C (Rent Verification Math), BCG writes:

> "Confirming we are in Year 2 of the lease... **Confirmed: Year 2 of lease. Rent commencement was approximately January 2024 (spa opening).**"

BCG's rent reconciliation math confirms this:
- 2025 P&L shows EUR 71,689 in rent
- Year 2 rent formula: EUR 50,000 fixed + 5% x EUR 426,018 = EUR 71,301
- Match confirmed (delta of EUR 388 is immaterial rounding)

If 2025 is Year 2, then Year 1 commenced approximately January 2024. This makes the Rent Commencement Date approximately January 2024.

### 2.4 Why Year 1 Rent Was EUR 5,000 (Not EUR 50,000)

The rent schedule in Clause 3.1 states Year 1 fixed rent is EUR 5,000. This is a discounted introductory rate -- common in hotel-spa concessions where the lessee invests heavily in fit-out. The jump from EUR 5,000 (Year 1) to EUR 50,000 (Year 2) is dramatic but contractual. It suggests Year 1 was designed as a grace period during which the lessee was still ramping up after the fit-out.

**Scenario C is the most likely.** The financial evidence is compelling: 2025 rent matches Year 2 rates exactly. The Offer Summary's claim of "~13 years remaining" is consistent with a January 2024 commencement (13 years from early 2026 = expiry in early 2039).

**My first report's "9-10 years" claim was directionally incorrect.** The remaining term is most likely approximately 12.5 years (from mid-2026), not 9-10. This is actually better for the deal -- more years of cash flow.

### 3.1 Exact Contract Language (Clause 9.3)

> And
> ii) The Lessor shall reimburse the Lessee for **any investment made within the Premises** upon receiving all **fiscal receipts**. The amount to be reimbursed for the **investment made within the premises** shall be at a rate of depreciation which sees a deduction in value of the invested amount, of zero per cent (0%) in the first two (2) years, and from then on a depreciation rate of ten percent (10%) per annum on the investment."

### 3.2 IC Chair Question (a): What Qualifies as "Investment Made Within the Premises"?

The clause uses the phrase **"investment made within the Premises"** twice, and requires **"fiscal receipts"** as evidence. Let me parse this carefully:

**"Investment made within the Premises"** -- The natural reading is investment IN the physical premises. This would include:
- Fit-out works (EUR 737,884) -- Yes, unambiguously. These are permanent improvements to the leased space.
- Portable equipment installed and operating within the premises -- This is where it gets ambiguous.

**The Candela laser (EUR 65,000) and T-Shape (EUR 55,000):**
- These are physically located "within the Premises" and are used to generate revenue there.
- However, they are portable -- they can be removed without damaging the premises.
- The contract says "investment made within the Premises," not "investment made TO the Premises" or "investment affixed to the Premises."
- The broader reading ("within") would include portable equipment. The narrower reading (improvements to the physical space) would exclude it.

**My assessment:** The language "investment made within the Premises" is broad enough to include all equipment operating within the spa, including portable devices. The requirement for "fiscal receipts" (invoices) suggests the drafters contemplated a documented inventory approach. A Maltese court would likely interpret this broadly in favor of the lessee, as termination clauses with compensation are typically construed to protect the displaced party.

**Conclusion:** The investment base is most likely EUR 857,884 (total investment including portable equipment), not just EUR 737,884 (fit-out only). However, this is a legal interpretation question that should be confirmed with Maltese counsel.

### 3.3 IC Chair Question (b): When Was the Investment Completed?

The CAPEX records show the fit-out was completed before the spa opened. Based on the BCG data confirming the spa opened approximately January 2024, the investment was completed in late 2023 or early 2024.

The contract's depreciation schedule runs from "the invested amount" with 0% in "the first two (2) years." But the contract does not specify whether the two years run from:

**Most logical interpretation:** The depreciation clock starts from the Rent Commencement Date (when the lease term begins), because the clause is part of the lease termination provisions. The "investment" is the capital deployed to establish the business under the lease.

## 7. TOGC RISK ANALYSIS

### 7.1 What Is TOGC?

Transfer of Going Concern (TOGC) is a VAT relief mechanism under Malta's VAT Act that exempts the transfer of a business as a going concern from the 18% VAT that would otherwise apply to the sale of individual assets. If TOGC applies, the EUR 200-220K purchase price is VAT-free. If it fails, VAT of 18% on the asset value (approximately EUR 36-40K) becomes payable by the buyer.

### 7.2 When TOGC Fails -- Specific Circumstances

Under Malta Revenue's guidelines (consistent with EU VAT Directive Article 19), TOGC treatment requires that:

1. **The buyer must carry on the same kind of business.** Carisma operating a spa/wellness business satisfies this. Adding medical aesthetics is an expansion, not a change. **Risk: LOW.**

2. **The business must be transferred as a going concern -- i.e., as a functioning business, not just assets.** If the APA is structured to transfer the lease, staff (under TUPE), client database, equipment, and goodwill, this is satisfied. **Risk: LOW if properly structured.**

3. **The buyer must be VAT-registered (or become registered).** Carisma is already VAT-registered. **Risk: NONE.**

4. **There must be no significant gap in business operations.** If the spa closes for more than a brief period during the transition (e.g., 2+ weeks for rebranding), Malta Revenue may argue the business was interrupted and therefore not transferred "as a going concern." **Risk: MODERATE** -- transition disruption could trigger this.

5. **The transfer must include all assets necessary to continue the business.** If key assets are excluded (e.g., the seller retains the Candela laser), Malta Revenue may argue this is a partial asset sale, not a TOGC. **Risk: LOW if all operating assets transfer.**

6. **Lease assignment must be completed.** If the lease does not transfer (landlord refuses), the business cannot continue at the same location. Without the lease, TOGC fails. **Risk: HIGH -- this is the primary TOGC risk factor.**

### 7.3 The Specific Scenario Where TOGC Fails

**Scenario 1 (Highest probability): Lease assignment delayed or refused.**
If the landlord consent process takes months and the business temporarily operates under an interim arrangement (management agreement, license), Malta Revenue may argue the TOGC conditions were not met at the date of asset transfer. Probability: 20-25%. Impact: EUR 36-40K.

**Scenario 2: Selective asset purchase.**
If Carisma cherry-picks assets (takes equipment but not certain contracts or liabilities), Malta Revenue may challenge TOGC treatment. Probability: 5-10%. Impact: EUR 36-40K.

**Scenario 3: Business interruption during transition.**
If the spa closes for renovation/rebranding for more than 2-3 weeks, TOGC could be challenged. Probability: 10-15%. Impact: EUR 36-40K.

### 7.4 Probability and Mitigation

**Overall probability of TOGC failure: 15-25%** (primarily driven by lease assignment timing).

**Expected cost: EUR 5.4-10K** (15-25% probability x EUR 36-40K).

**Mitigation:**
1. Structure the APA to transfer ALL assets and liabilities needed to continue the business
2. Ensure lease assignment is completed simultaneously with closing (or as close as possible)
3. Ensure no operational gap -- keep the spa open throughout the transition
4. Obtain advance ruling from Malta Revenue if lease timing is uncertain
5. Include a TOGC warranty in the APA -- seller warrants the business qualifies as a going concern
6. Budget EUR 40K as a contingency in case TOGC fails

## 8. EXIT OPTION ANALYSIS -- "DI RISPETTO" TERMINATION

### 8.1 The Clause

Clause 2.A.2 states:
> "The first five (5) years of this Lease are 'di fermo', whereas the rest of the term is 'di rispetto'."
> "Provided that at any time in the period of 'di rispetto', the Lessee may terminate this lease by giving a six (6) month prior notice to the Lessor."

### 8.2 What This Means for Carisma

**Under Maltese commercial lease law:**
- **"Di fermo"** (binding/fixed): Neither party can terminate. The first 5 years are irrevocable.
- **"Di rispetto"** (at will): The lessee can terminate with 6 months' notice. The lessor cannot terminate (except for cause under Clause 9.1 or Complex sale under 9.3).

**Current status:** If the Rent Commencement Date was January 2024, we are currently in Year 3. The "di fermo" period expires approximately January 2029. After that, Carisma can walk away with 6 months' notice at any time.

**If the Rent Commencement Date was July 2020:** The "di fermo" period has already expired (July 2025). Carisma could terminate with 6 months' notice at any point after taking over.

### 8.3 Downside Implications -- This Limits Maximum Loss

My first report's downside analysis assumed Carisma was locked in for the full remaining term. **This is incorrect.** The "di rispetto" option means Carisma's maximum commitment is:

**Scenario A (Commencement January 2024):**
- Locked in through January 2029 (Year 5)
- After January 2029: Can exit with 6 months' notice
- Maximum locked-in rent commitment from mid-2026: approximately EUR 245K (Years 3-5)
- After Year 5: voluntary -- each additional year's rent is a choice, not an obligation

**Scenario B (Commencement July 2020, already in "di rispetto"):**
- Carisma could exit with 6 months' notice from Day 1
- Maximum locked-in rent commitment: approximately EUR 45K (6 months of Year 7 rent)

### 8.4 Value of the Exit Option

The "di rispetto" option is essentially a put option on the business. If things go wrong -- clinic fails, revenue collapses, landlord relationship deteriorates -- Carisma can terminate and walk away. The sunk cost is:
- Purchase price (EUR 200-220K)
- Clinic capex (EUR 80K) -- but portable equipment (Candela, T-Shape, new devices) can be removed
- Remaining lease rent through the 6-month notice period

**Recoverable on exit:**
- Portable equipment: EUR 65-120K at auction/resale
- Client database and goodwill: some value if sold to another operator

**Maximum unrecoverable loss if Carisma terminates after 2 years:**
- Purchase price: EUR 200-220K
- Net clinic capex after equipment recovery: EUR 20-40K
- Total: EUR 220-260K

**This is NOT a EUR 450-500K downside.** The "di rispetto" option cuts the worst-case loss by roughly 40% compared to being locked in for 10+ years. My first report's risk analysis should have reflected this.

## 10. BLIND SPOTS ADDRESSED

### 10.1 Staff Non-Compete / Non-Solicitation Clauses

**The question:** Can departing therapists open competing practices?

**Analysis:** Malta's employment law (Employment and Industrial Relations Act, Chapter 452) permits non-compete clauses in employment contracts but subjects them to reasonableness tests. Key principles:

- Non-competes must be limited in scope, geography, and duration
- Typical enforceable range in Malta: 6-12 months, within a 5-10km radius
- Courts will not enforce overly broad restrictions
- For spa therapists earning near minimum wage (EUR 16K average), courts are more likely to void non-competes as disproportionate

**What we do NOT know:** Whether the 13 current employment contracts contain non-compete or non-solicitation clauses. This is DD item 3.13 (Outstanding).

**Risk if no non-competes exist:** A departing senior therapist could open a competing spa in Sliema within weeks. Given that the top 2 therapists likely drive 30-40% of rebookings, this is a material revenue risk.

**Recommendation:**
1. Review all 13 employment contracts for existing restrictive covenants (DD Priority 1)
2. If no non-competes exist, include new non-compete/non-solicitation clauses in the TUPE transfer -- but note that under Maltese TUPE law, terms cannot be changed to the detriment of the employee at the point of transfer
3. Consider retention bonuses (EUR 3-5K vesting over 12-18 months) for the 2-3 key therapists, conditional on signing updated contracts that include reasonable non-compete provisions
4. Budget EUR 10-15K for retention packages

### 10.2 POS System Compatibility (Clause 5.11)

**The clause:** "To install a Point of Sale system that is compatible with the system of the Lessor."

**Analysis:** This is a straightforward operational requirement. The Fortina Complex likely has a central POS/property management system (e.g., Oracle Opera, MEWS, or similar). The spa's POS must integrate with or at minimum be compatible with the hotel's system, likely for:
- Hotel guest billing (charges to room)
- Revenue reporting to the landlord (for the 5% turnover fee calculation)
- Consolidated guest experience management

**Cost estimate:** POS system implementation or migration: EUR 3-8K depending on whether Carisma's existing system (referenced as EUR 4,018/yr in IT costs) is compatible. If a full replacement is needed, expect EUR 5-10K one-time plus EUR 3-5K annual licensing.

**Action:** During the landlord meeting, ask what POS system the hotel uses and what "compatibility" means in practice. During DD, verify whether the current INA POS system meets this requirement and whether Carisma's preferred system is compatible.

### 10.3 Client Database GDPR Compliance

**The risk:** Spa client data includes sensitive health information under GDPR/EU regulation:
- Treatment histories (including medical aesthetic treatments)
- Medical questionnaires (required for laser treatments)
- Before/after photographs
- Payment details
- Contact information with marketing consent records

**In an asset purchase:** The client database is being transferred from one data controller (Well Being Services Ltd) to another (Carisma entity). This requires:

1. **Legal basis for transfer:** Legitimate interest or consent. Health data requires explicit consent under GDPR Article 9.
2. **Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA):** Required for large-scale processing of health data.
3. **Client notification:** All clients must be informed that their data is being transferred to a new controller, with the opportunity to object.
4. **Data processing agreement:** Between the parties during the transition period.

**Cost:** EUR 3-5K for GDPR compliance work (lawyer + data protection officer consultation).

**Timeline impact:** The client notification process should begin at least 30 days before the transfer date. This can run in parallel with APA negotiation.

**Critical risk:** If a significant number of clients object to the data transfer, Carisma loses the ability to contact them for rebooking. Mitigation: Frame the notification as a positive ("exciting new chapter") rather than a cold data transfer notice.

The lease was signed in January 2020, but the premises were delivered in shell form and required a EUR 738K fit-out.
