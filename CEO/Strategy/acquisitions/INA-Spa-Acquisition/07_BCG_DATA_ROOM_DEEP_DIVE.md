### Revised Key Findings

1. **EBITDA of EUR 36.5K cannot support a EUR 300K+ purchase price on standalone fundamentals.** At 4x EBITDA, the spa is worth EUR 146K. At 5x, EUR 183K. Even at a generous 6x (reserved for high-growth businesses), the spa is worth EUR 219K.

2. **The cost structure has two structural problems: wages (49.2%) and rent (16.8%).** Combined, labor and occupancy consume 66% of revenue. The industry benchmark for a well-run spa is 35-40% labor and 10-12% occupancy = 45-52% combined. INA is 14-21 percentage points above benchmark.

3. **Growth is real but misleading.** 32.6% YoY growth from 2024 to 2025 reflects a Year 1-to-Year 2 ramp, not organic steady-state growth. The spa opened January 2024. First-year revenue is always depressed by launch inefficiencies. Projecting 32% growth forward would be a serious analytical error.

4. **The rent structure is worse than we assumed.** The 5% turnover component means rent grows automatically with revenue. As you grow revenue through clinic expansion, you pay more rent. At EUR 600K revenue, total rent = EUR 60K base + EUR 30K turnover = EUR 90K. At EUR 800K, it is EUR 105K+. This is a meaningful drag on returns from any revenue optimization strategy.

5. **The lease assignment at absolute discretion is a genuine deal risk.** The landlord can block the transfer entirely or extract concessions. The "change of control = deemed assignment" clause means acquiring the company (not just assets) still triggers this provision.

6. **The clinic is partially in-place -- this is better than assumed.** Laser treatments already represent 16.4% and T-Shape 7.2% of treatment mix. The Candela laser (EUR 65K) and T-Shape (EUR 55K) machines are operational. The clinic "opportunity" is not purely greenfield -- it is an expansion of an existing service line.

7. **78% local clientele is a significant positive.** This dramatically reduces dependency on the Barcelo Hotel partnership and provides year-round revenue stability. It also means the customer base is more defensible post-acquisition.

## 3. LEASE RISK DEEP DIVE

### 3.1 Lease Assignment Risk

**The lease contains these critical provisions:**

1. **Assignment requires landlord consent at absolute discretion.** This is the highest standard of consent -- the landlord can refuse for any reason or no reason at all. In many jurisdictions, courts will not second-guess "absolute discretion" even if refusal seems unreasonable.

2. **Change of Control = deemed assignment.** Acquiring the shares of Well Being Services Ltd (the lessee) triggers the assignment clause. This means a share purchase does not avoid the landlord consent requirement.

3. **Risk mitigation strategies:**
   - **Pre-signing landlord engagement:** Before committing to any purchase, obtain written confirmation from Fortina Developments that they will consent to the transfer. Do NOT proceed without this.
   - **Asset purchase structure:** If acquiring assets rather than shares, negotiate a new lease directly with Fortina. This gives cleaner legal standing but may result in different terms.
   - **Relationship approach:** Fortina's incentive is a reliable tenant who pays rent and maintains the property. Carisma should present itself as a stronger tenant than the current operator (larger group, better capitalized, professional management).

4. **Probability assessment:**
   - Landlord consents without conditions: 50%
   - Landlord consents with rent increase or modified terms: 30%
   - Landlord refuses or demands unacceptable conditions: 20%

**If landlord demands a rent increase:** Even a EUR 5K/year increase to the fixed base compounds to EUR 65K over 13 years. This should be factored into pricing.

### 3.2 Complex Sale Termination Risk

**If the Fortina Hotel Complex is sold, the lessor can terminate the lease with:**
- EUR 150,000 cash payment to the lessee
- Plus reimbursement of the lessee's investment, depreciated at 0% for the first 2 years, then 10% per year

**Termination payment calculation (if triggered today, Year 2):**
- Cash: EUR 150,000
- Investment reimbursement: EUR 857,884 (fit-out + equipment) at 0% depreciation (still in first 2 years) = EUR 857,884
- **Total termination payment: EUR 1,007,884**

**Termination payment calculation (if triggered in Year 5):**
- Cash: EUR 150,000
- Investment: EUR 857,884 x (1 - 30% depreciation for 3 years past Year 2) = EUR 600,519
- **Total: EUR 750,519**

**Termination payment calculation (if triggered in Year 10):**
- Cash: EUR 150,000
- Investment: EUR 857,884 x (1 - 80% depreciation) = EUR 171,577
- **Total: EUR 321,577**

**Assessment:** The termination provisions are actually protective for the lessee in the early years. If the complex were sold tomorrow, the lessee would receive over EUR 1M -- far more than the acquisition cost. The risk increases over time as the depreciation erodes the investment reimbursement. By Year 10+, the termination payment barely covers the purchase price.

**Probability of complex sale in next 5 years:** Low (10-15%). The Barcelo Hotel is a major operating asset for Fortina. Malta hotel assets rarely change hands. But if Fortina is under financial pressure or a major hospitality group makes an offer, this clause becomes relevant.

**Net assessment:** The termination clause is net-positive for the buyer in the near term (forced buyout at premium) but becomes a risk in Years 8+.

### 8.5 Is EUR 340K Still Defensible?

**No. The data does not support it.**

Our previous EUR 340K target assumed EUR 72K EBITDA. The actual EBITDA is EUR 36.5K. To justify EUR 340K at the same 4.7x multiple, you would need EUR 72K of EBITDA -- which requires:
- Revenue at EUR 426K (actual), AND
- Wages reduced from 49% to ~38% of revenue, AND
- All other costs held constant

This is not impossible, but it requires the buyer to execute a significant restructuring before the business justifies the purchase price. **Paying EUR 340K means paying for value you have not yet created.** That is fundamentally different from our prior analysis, which assumed the business already generated EUR 72K EBITDA.

The Red Team memo warned us: "You cannot simultaneously argue (a) the business will generate EUR 150K+ EBITDA once we fix it, and (b) the business is worth EUR 260K because EBITDA is currently zero." The Red Team was right about the tension. But the answer is not to pay for turnaround value -- the answer is to price the business on actuals and capture the turnaround upside as return on invested effort.

### 8.6 What About the Seller's Perspective?

The seller invested EUR 857K in fit-out and equipment. They are running the business at a loss (negative EUR 19K pre-tax). Their annual rent is EUR 71.7K and rising. They are burning cash.

**Seller's BATNA (Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement):**
- Continue operating: Negative cash flow after depreciation, rising rent, no clear path to profitability without restructuring they have not executed in 2 years
- Liquidation: Equipment resale EUR 80-120K (distressed), lease walk-away possible after Year 5 (with 6 months notice), but forfeits all fit-out investment
- Find another buyer: Limited market (only 2 serious buyers per prior analysis), lengthy process

**Seller's rational price floor:** EUR 120-150K (equipment value + transition costs)
**Seller's emotional anchor:** EUR 400K+ (sunk cost fallacy on EUR 857K investment)

At EUR 200-250K, we are offering the seller 1.7-2.1x their liquidation value and a clean exit from a loss-making operation. This is a fair deal for both parties.
