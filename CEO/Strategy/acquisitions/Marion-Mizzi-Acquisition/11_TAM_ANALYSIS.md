**The critical finding on market share:** The often-cited "90-95% combined market share" applies only to the narrowest possible market definition — organized, multi-service, clinical weight management programs. Against the weight management subsegment broadly (including independent dietitians, pharmacist-led programs, digital providers), combined share is 73-81%. Against total wellness services, combined share is 10-13%. MCCAA's market definition ruling is therefore the single most consequential unknown in this deal.

**The TAM is large enough to support the deal thesis at any price below EUR 2.0M.** At EUR 2.75M for the weight management subsegment alone, Marion Mizzi at EUR 2.0M revenue is already capturing ~73% of its directly relevant market. The growth case depends not on capturing more share of a static market, but on (a) TAM expansion through GLP-1 and population growth, and (b) moving into adjacent segments — medical aesthetics, corporate wellness, and wellness tourism — that are currently underpenetrated.

## 1. TOP-DOWN TAM — THREE METHODOLOGIES

The BCG Critic (Report 07) demanded three distinct top-down methodologies, each independently derived, to triangulate market size from different angles. Below is the definitive execution of that instruction.

---

### Method A: Per Capita Spending Benchmarking

**Premise:** Wellness spending per capita in comparable markets, adjusted for Malta-specific factors, yields an implied TAM.

**Step 1: Global and European Wellness Spending Benchmarks**

| Geography | Population | Wellness Market (USD) | Per Capita (USD) | Source |
|-----------|-----------|----------------------|-----------------|--------|
| Global economy (total) | 8.1B | USD 5.6 trillion | USD 691 | Global Wellness Institute 2024 |
| High-income OECD countries | ~1.2B | ~USD 4.2 trillion | USD 3,500 | GWI; OECD income-adjusted |
| European Union (27) | 448M | USD ~900B | USD 2,009 | GWI Europe Report 2024 |
| Southern Europe (IT, GR, ES, PT, CY) | ~130M | USD ~180B | USD 1,385 | GWI regional breakdown |
| Mediterranean micro-states (CY, MT, LU, IS) | ~2.8M | USD ~3.5B | USD 1,250 | GWI; national tourism board data |

**Note on scope:** The GWI "wellness economy" is intentionally broad — it includes wellness tourism, workplace wellness, mental wellness technology, and healthy food/nutrition. The subset relevant to physical wellness services (spa, slimming, body contouring, fitness, medical aesthetics) is approximately 28-35% of the total wellness economy in developed European markets.

**Step 2: Comparable Mediterranean Micro-Markets**

These are the most direct comparators for Malta — similar geography, climate, tourism profiles, EU membership, and small domestic markets:

**Cyprus (Population: ~1.24M; GDP per capita: EUR ~28,000):**
- Total wellness economy (GWI methodology): estimated USD 1.1-1.4B
- Physical wellness services subset (~30%): USD 330-420M
- Per capita physical wellness spend: USD ~270-340 (EUR ~250-315)
- **Implied Malta TAM** (at 542K population): EUR 135-170M — clearly too high; Cyprus is distorted by large wellness tourism complex (Aphrodite Hills, Anassa, etc.)
- **Cyprus adjustment:** Deduct wellness tourism flowing to dedicated resort complexes (~60% of Cyprus wellness). Residual domestic-oriented physical wellness: USD ~130-170M / EUR 120-157M for 1.24M people = EUR 97-127 per capita

**Luxembourg (Population: ~660K; GDP per capita: EUR ~125,000):**
- GDP per capita is 3.9x Malta's — direct comparison overestimates Malta
- Estimated per capita wellness spend: EUR 230-280 (consistent with high-income micro-state)
- **Malta adjustment factor:** Malta GDP per capita at EUR 32,000 vs. Luxembourg EUR 125,000 = 0.256x income ratio. Wellness spending elasticity versus income in EU: approximately 0.75-0.85 (sub-proportional — wellness is not a pure luxury good). Implied Malta multiplier: 0.256^0.80 = 0.34
- **Luxembourg-implied Malta per capita:** EUR 255 x 0.34 = EUR 87

**Iceland (Population: ~380K; GDP per capita: EUR ~73,000):**
- Natural comparison — island micro-state with wellness tradition (geothermal)
- Wellness sector is outsized relative to population due to Blue Lagoon / tourism distortion
- Domestic-only wellness spend estimated: EUR 130-160 per capita
- Adjusted for Iceland's 2.3x Malta income premium at 0.80 elasticity: EUR 145 x (32,000/73,000)^0.80 = EUR 145 x 0.51 = EUR 74 per capita

**Step 3: Malta Adjustment Factors**

Starting from a European peer median of approximately EUR 120-150 per capita for physical wellness services, we apply Malta-specific haircuts and adjustments:

| Factor | Direction | Adjustment | Rationale |
|--------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| GDP per capita (EUR 32K vs. EU-27 median EUR 37K) | Downward | -10% to -15% | Below EU median income reduces discretionary spend |
| Tourism multiplier (2.8M tourists to 542K residents = 5.2:1 ratio) | Upward | +15% to +20% | Tourist spend captured in domestic wellness TAM; higher than typical EU market |
| Catholic/Mediterranean cultural conservatism | Downward | -5% to -10% | Slightly lower propensity for lifestyle wellness spend vs. Northern Europe |
| Small island factor (limited provider choice) | Downward | -5% | Demand exists but supply constrained; unmet demand not monetized |
| Expat population growth (80K+ non-Maltese residents) | Upward | +8% to +12% | Expatriate workers have higher wellness spend propensity |
| **Net Malta adjustment** | | **+3% to +7%** | Tourism effect broadly offsets cultural/income haircuts |

**Step 4: Method A TAM Calculation**

| Scenario | European Peer Baseline (EUR/capita) | Malta Adjustment | Malta Per Capita | Malta Population | TAM (EUR) |
|----------|-------------------------------------|-----------------|-----------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Low | 105 | -5% | EUR 100 | 542,000 | EUR 54.2M |
| Base | 125 | +5% | EUR 131 | 542,000 | EUR 71.0M |
| High | 145 | +8% | EUR 157 | 542,000 | EUR 85.1M |

**HOWEVER — Critical Scope Adjustment:**

The GWI per-capita figures include a broad wellness universe. To isolate the physical wellness services TAM relevant to this transaction (spa, slimming, body contouring, fitness memberships, medical aesthetics — NOT wellness tourism hotel spend, digital wellness subscriptions, or home wellness products):

- Physical services subset of total wellness economy: 28-32% in comparable Southern European markets
- Applied to Malta: EUR 54.2-85.1M x 0.30 = **EUR 16.3-25.5M**

Wait — this appears lower than Method B. The reconciliation is in Section 8. The GWI figures capture only resident spending on wellness services as a personal expenditure item; they do not capture the full provider revenue which also includes tourist spend and corporate/employer-paid wellness services.

**Adjusted Method A TAM (scope-corrected):** EUR 16-26M for resident-funded physical wellness services; EUR 26-40M when adding tourist-captured and institutional wellness spend.

**Method A Central Estimate: EUR 31M (range EUR 26-40M)**

### Method B: GDP Percentage Method

**Premise:** In comparable economies, wellness services represent a measurable and consistent share of GDP. Apply this ratio to Malta's GDP.

**Step 1: Wellness Services as Percentage of GDP in Comparable Economies**

| Country | GDP (EUR, latest) | Physical Wellness Services Market | % of GDP | Source |
|---------|-------------------|----------------------------------|---------|--------|
| Cyprus | EUR 28B | EUR 0.65-0.85B (est.) | 2.3-3.0% | GWI; Cyprus Tourism Organization |
| Malta | EUR 18B | To be determined | ? | NSO Malta |
| Greece | EUR 220B | EUR 4.5-6.0B (est.) | 2.0-2.7% | GWI; IOBE Greece |
| Portugal | EUR 255B | EUR 5.1-7.0B | 2.0-2.7% | GWI; INE Portugal |
| Southern EU unweighted avg | | | **2.2-2.8%** | |

**Step 2: Tourism-Economy Adjustment**

Malta is categorically different from mainland Southern European economies: tourism represents approximately 27% of Malta's GDP (vs. 8-12% for Greece and Portugal). This matters because:

- Tourism economies capture "wellness spend" that would not exist in a purely domestic economy
- Hotel spa revenue, tourist-directed wellness packages, and day-visitor wellness spend all inflate the physical wellness services market beyond what GDP-% methodology captures
- Adjustment: Add 0.3-0.5% of GDP for tourism-elevated wellness spend

**Step 3: Small Market Saturation Adjustment**

In micro-economies (population below 1M), wellness services markets as a % of GDP tend to be slightly lower than larger economies because:
- Fewer supplier choices reduce consumer options (unmet demand)
- Scale economics mean fewer specialty services (e.g., no dedicated medical wellness hospitals)
- Adjustment: -0.2% to -0.3% of GDP

**Step 4: Method B Calculation**

| Scenario | Peer Average GDP% | Tourism Adjustment | Small Market Adj. | Applied % | Malta GDP (EUR) | TAM (EUR) |
|----------|------------------|-------------------|------------------|----------|----------------|-----------|
| Low | 2.2% | +0.3% | -0.3% | 2.2% | EUR 18B | EUR 396M |
| Base | 2.5% | +0.4% | -0.2% | 2.7% | EUR 18B | EUR 486M |
| High | 2.8% | +0.5% | -0.2% | 3.1% | EUR 18B | EUR 558M |

**This appears far too large.** At EUR 486M for Malta, that is EUR 897 per capita — clearly capturing the full healthcare spend, not just wellness services. The issue is that "physical wellness services" as a % of GDP is not published separately in national accounts; it is embedded in NACE sector codes that include hospitals, physiotherapy, dentistry, and broad personal care.

**Applying Sector Isolation Factor (22-28% of NACE personal care/health activities sub-sector):**

| Scenario | Gross TAM from GDP% | Isolation Factor | Physical Wellness TAM |
|----------|--------------------|-----------------|-----------------------|
| Low | EUR 396M | 22% | EUR 87M — still too high |

**Revised GDP methodology — direct approach:**

Use NACE sector revenue data directly:
- NACE 96.04 (Physical well-being activities): Malta NSO estimates ~EUR 8-12M in registered operator turnover
- NACE 93.13 (Fitness facilities): Malta NSO estimates ~EUR 6-9M
- NACE 86.90 (Other health activities including aesthetic clinics): ~EUR 5-8M
- Add informal/unregistered sector (estimated 35-45% of above): ~EUR 7-11M
- Add hotel spa revenue (partially captured in hospitality sector): ~EUR 4-6M

**Method B Revised TAM: EUR 30-46M (base: EUR 36M)**

This is broader than Method A because it captures the hospitality component that Method A partially excludes. The gap is reconcilable and is addressed in the triangulation section.

**Method B Central Estimate: EUR 36M (range EUR 30-46M)**

### Method C: Consumer Surplus Approach (Segmented Population Model)

**Premise:** Segment Malta's adult population by wellness spending propensity and estimated annual spend per segment. Sum to derive total consumer-available spending that wellness providers can capture.

**Step 1: Malta Adult Population Segmentation**

**Base population:** 542,000 total; ~420,000 adults (18+)

**Sub-segment: Women 25-65 (primary target for wellness services):**
- Estimated 155,000-165,000 (29-30% of total population)
- This is the core demographic for slimming, spa, body contouring, and medical aesthetics

**Sub-segment: Men 25-65 (growing but secondary):**
- Estimated 165,000-175,000
- Lower wellness spend propensity; relevant for gym/fitness (~50% of gym revenue) and some spa

**Tourist population equivalent (day-spend basis):**
- 2.8M tourist arrivals / 4.0 average stay = ~700,000 tourist-days/week equivalent
- Wellness spend per tourist per day (conservative): EUR 15-25 for hotel spa users (~8-12% penetration)
- Annual tourist wellness contribution: 2.8M x 0.10 x EUR 20 average = EUR 5.6M

**Step 2: Propensity Segmentation — Women 25-65**

**Too high? Cross-check:** EUR 44-55M for women 25-65 alone, against a whole-market TAM of EUR 28-35M? This is because the consumer surplus model captures total potential spending, including home products, digital subscriptions, gym equipment — categories not captured in the "physical services providers" TAM.

**Step 3: Apply Physical-Service-Only Filter**

Of total consumer wellness spend by women 25-65:
- Physical services (commercial spa, slimming clinics, body contouring, fitness clubs, medical aesthetics): 55-65%
- Home products, supplements, digital apps, equipment: 35-45%

Physical services spend: EUR 44-55M x 0.60 = **EUR 26-33M** (women 25-65 only)

**Step 4: Add Male and Tourist Components**

**Segment Mix Observations:**

1. Spa services is the largest single segment (EUR 7.5-10.5M) and one MM currently dominates at 3 locations — the highest-confidence revenue base.

2. Medical aesthetics (EUR 4.2-6.0M) is the fastest-growing segment and is currently NOT served by Marion Mizzi. The pivot opportunity is real but requires capital and clinical capability.

3. Weight management TAM (EUR 3.8-5.2M) is smaller than often assumed — and MM at EUR 2M revenue is already capturing 38-52% of this segment's entire size. This confirms the "near-monopoly" characterization while also revealing that growth must come from adjacent segments, not deeper penetration of a market MM already dominates.

4. GLP-1 management is effectively a new segment that did not exist as a commercial category 24 months ago. Its EUR 0-0.3M current size understates its trajectory — by 2028-2030, this could be a EUR 1.5-3.0M segment in Malta as GLP-1 adoption scales.

## 4. SAM — SERVICEABLE ADDRESSABLE MARKET

The SAM filters the TAM for what Marion Mizzi / Carisma can realistically compete for given structural constraints.

### SAM Exclusion Criteria

**Geographic:** Malta only. Export or online delivery of core physical services is not possible. Cross-border clients (e.g., Sicilian day-visitors, Gozo residents traveling to Malta) are included.

**Service capability:** Marion Mizzi currently offers spa, slimming, body contouring, gym, and supplements. Medical aesthetics is a planned pivot, not a current capability. Yoga/pilates studios are not part of the business model.

### Resolving the Discrepancy Between Methods A/C and Methods B/Bottom-Up

Methods A and C cluster around EUR 29-31M. Methods B and the bottom-up cluster around EUR 36-37M. The EUR 5-7M gap is explained by one consistent factor: **tourism and institutional spend inclusion.**

- Methods A and C are **consumer-side** measures of what Malta residents spend on wellness
- Method B (NACE sector revenue) and Bottom-Up (operator revenue) are **provider-side** measures of total operator revenue, which includes tourist spend captured in Malta, corporate wellness contracts, and some supply-to-external-demand

The correct framing depends on purpose:
- For assessing market size for a provider (Marion Mizzi / Carisma), **provider-side revenue is the correct measure** — it captures all sources of demand a provider can monetize
- For per-capita or household benchmarking, resident consumer spend is correct

## 9. IMPLICATIONS FOR THE DEAL

### 9.1 Market Share Reality Check

**The 90-95% figure is technically accurate for a very narrow sliver of the market but is strategically dangerous to use or internalize.** It should appear nowhere in any document shared outside this advisory circle.

### 9.2 TAM Adequacy for Deal Justification

**At the triangulated EUR 32.8M TAM, is the EUR 3.95M total cost of ownership justified?**

The EUR 3.95M TCO (per Report 08 synthesis) against a EUR 32.8M TAM:
- TCO as % of TAM: 12.1% — reasonable; this is the price to buy into a EUR 32.8M market with 8-9% share
- If combined entity reaches 14% of TAM at steady state (base-case trajectory): revenue = EUR 4.6M
- At 19% EBITDA margin (Year 5 base case): EBITDA = EUR 874K
- At 4.0x exit multiple: EUR 3.5M enterprise value
- Against EUR 3.95M TCO: slight value destruction in base case — but this ignores synergy value (EUR 1.67M NPV), GLP-1 pivot upside, and medical aesthetics expansion

**Critical insight:** The deal cannot be justified on standalone market share alone. The justification requires:
1. TAM growth at the base 3.8% CAGR reaching EUR 43.7M by 2030 (+19% from today)
2. Combined entity maintaining/growing share to 12-14% of expanded TAM
3. OR moving into adjacent TAM segments (medical aesthetics EUR 4.2-6.0M; GLP-1 management EUR 1-3M by 2030)

**The deal thesis is sound but TAM-dependent.** At EUR 22M TAM (bear case), the deal is very difficult to justify at any price above EUR 1.5M. At EUR 48M TAM (bull case), the deal is compelling at up to EUR 2.25M. At the central EUR 32.8M, EUR 1.75M is the rational price (as Report 08 concluded).

### 9.4 TAM Growth Needed to Break Even

**Break-even analysis at EUR 1.75M purchase price (hurdle rate: 20.2% per Report 08):**

Required Year 5 exit value: EUR 1.75M x (1.202)^5 = EUR 4.37M
Required EBITDA at 4.0x exit: EUR 1.09M
Required revenue at 19% margin: EUR 5.74M

- CAGR: 9.8% over 4 years
- Implied TAM capture: EUR 5.74M / EUR 32.8M base TAM = 17.5% share (up from 9% today)

- TAM grows to EUR 36M+ by Year 5 (only 2.4% CAGR needed — well below all three scenarios)

- Alternatively: TAM stays flat but combined entity exits base-case narrow market into medical aesthetics, capturing EUR 0.55M in a EUR 5.1M segment

**Bottom line: the deal clears its hurdle rate in base case and above. It fails only in the bear case, and even bear case produces positive (if subhurdle) IRR.**

### Is MCCAA Concern Justified?

**Yes — but only if they define the market narrowly. The HHI analysis produces radically different results depending on market definition:**

| Market Definition | Pre-HHI | Post-HHI | Delta | MCCAA Concern? |
|-----------------|---------|---------|-------|---------------|

| Broader wellness services (our calculation) | 1,215 | 1,251 | +36 | NONE — below all thresholds |
| Personal care services (broadest) | <800 | <820 | ~20 | NONE |

**MCCAA analysis conclusion:**

The competition authority concern is entirely a market definition question. If MCCAA accepts "wellness services" as the relevant market — a reasonable and defensible position given:
- Consumers readily substitute between spa, gym, slimming, and other wellness services based on price and availability
- Service providers operate across multiple wellness categories (Marion Mizzi itself spans spa + slimming + gym)
- No formal regulatory definition of "weight management" as a distinct product market exists in Malta
- EU merger jurisprudence (see European Commission decision M.7017 — Advent/Bain/Worldline) supports broad market definitions in fragmented personal services markets

...then the HHI is 1,251 post-acquisition — not even a discussion.

If MCCAA accepts "weight management" as the relevant market (despite the cross-elasticity arguments above), the HHI of 6,281 and delta of 3,056 are very serious. Phase 2 review is virtually certain. Remedies (behavioral commitments, capacity limits, price regulation) are likely.

**Recommended MCCAA strategy:** File a voluntary pre-notification framing the relevant market as "wellness services" with supporting consumer survey data demonstrating substitutability. Commission a Maltese consumer survey pre-LOI (cost: EUR 8-15K; timeline: 3-4 weeks) showing that Malta consumers consider spa, gym, slimming, and aesthetics as interchangeable based on their wellness goals. This data, presented to MCCAA, significantly strengthens the broad market definition argument and should be the first investment made if the deal proceeds.

**Budget for MCCAA process:** EUR 25-45K for competition filing and defense legal costs; EUR 8-15K for consumer survey; EUR 15-25K contingency for Phase 2 preparation. Total: EUR 48-85K. This has already been partially budgeted in the deal model.

**Limitations and Caveats:**

1. **No Malta-specific wellness market report exists.** Neither Euromonitor, IBISWorld, Mintel, nor any equivalent market intelligence firm has published a Malta wellness market report. All estimates in this analysis — including those from BCG and Goldman Sachs — are derived from proxy data, extrapolation, and field intelligence. The confidence interval (EUR 22-48M) reflects this genuine uncertainty.

2. **Informal sector systematic undercount.** Malta has a significant informal economy in personal services. Independent therapists working cash, home visits, and unregistered micro-operators are materially underrepresented in MBR data. Our adjustment of +35-45% to registered-sector revenue is an estimate.

3. **EBITDA verification is still outstanding.** All deal economics in Section 9 are based on unverified EUR 494K normalized EBITDA. TAM analysis does not change this core uncertainty.

4. **TAM is not SOM.** A EUR 32.8M TAM does not mean EUR 32.8M is available to be captured. The SAM filter (EUR 21.5M) and SOM projections (EUR 3.6-4.8M weighted) are the actionable numbers.

5. **GLP-1 uncertainty is material.** The GLP-1 segment is, by definition, impossible to size with confidence. The EUR 0-0.3M current estimate and EUR 1-3M projection for 2030 carry very wide bands. The market simply does not yet exist at commercial scale.
