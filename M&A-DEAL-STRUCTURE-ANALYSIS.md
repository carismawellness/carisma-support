**Status:** CONFIDENTIAL — Strategic Planning

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The INA Spa acquisition presents an attractive cash-generative add-on opportunity with strong risk-adjusted returns. The business generates EUR 123K baseline EBITDA (1.0x multiple) with meaningful upside from clinic integration (clinic module adds EUR 90K potential EBITDA in Year 1).

**Current pricing debate centers on a flawed anchor:** The seller's EUR 250K opening demand is anchored to sunk cost (EUR 1M historical investment), not market multiples. Real market value is EUR 120-150K at 1.0-1.2x current EBITDA.

**Critical insight:** The optimal deal structure is not purely about purchase price—it's about **aligning execution risk, cash flow uncertainty, and buyer optionality**. A lower upfront cash commitment with performance-based contingencies may be superior to a higher all-cash deal, because it:

1. Reduces immediate capital deployment (preserves optionality for clinic expansion)
2. Ties seller economics to actual clinic success (aligns incentives)
3. Creates contingency leverage if due diligence uncovers red flags
4. Reduces carry cost if clinic underperforms

**Recommendation (Preview):** **Scenario 5 (Maximum Leverage with Performance Earnout)** is the optimal structure, with modifications. A hybrid approach—EUR 250K cash + EUR 100K performance earnout (tied to clinic EBITDA targets, not time)—maximizes your leverage while improving seller acceptance probability and managing execution risk.

- EUR 50K earnout typically capitalized on balance sheet (contingent liability)
- If earnout triggers, it becomes expense on P&L

- **Incentive to help:** None (earnout is guaranteed)

2. **Customer continuity:** Seller may not actively support customer transition if earnout is time-based
3. **Documentation:** Earnout definition must be crystal clear (€123K EBITDA = payment? €120K? €115K?)

#### Verdict
**RANK: #5 (Poor)**

- **Leverage ratio:** 1.67x (EUR 500K total debt / EUR 123K EBITDA)
- **Seller carries EUR 200K note:** This is NOT a conventional bank loan. Risk of default if clinic underperforms

#### Risk Assessment
**Execution Risk:** CRITICAL
- **Problem 1 — Clinic ramp failure:**
  - If clinic underperforms and you generate only EUR 90K combined EBITDA (instead of EUR 123K)
  - EUR 18K annual debt service becomes unmanageable
  - You risk default on seller note, triggering renegotiation or litigation

- **Problem 2 — Seller position becomes creditor, not ally:**
  - Seller transforms from "former owner" to "creditor with leverage"
  - If you struggle, seller can threaten enforcement, creating operational stress
  - Seller may demand right to audit books monthly (common in seller financing)

- **Problem 3 — Prepayment risk:**
  - If seller becomes concerned about business viability, he may accelerate note payment
  - Typical seller note includes acceleration clause (all remaining balance due if breached)
  - Leaves you illiquid

#### Financial Leverage Analysis
- **Debt / EBITDA:** 1.67x (at 123K base case)
- **Debt / EBITDA (if clinic underperforms to EUR 100K):** 2.0x (distressed territory)
- **Interest coverage (EBITDA / Interest Expense):** 6.8x (acceptable, but thin if EBITDA drops)

#### Seller Acceptance Probability
- **Likelihood seller accepts:** LOW-MEDIUM (25-35%)
- **Why seller might reject:**
  - "I want all-cash at close, not a 3-year note"
  - "I don't trust you'll make the EUR 200K payments"
  - "I want to be done with this business, not tied to it financially"

- **Why seller might accept:**
  - Tax advantage: Can recognize sale over 3 years instead of lump sum
  - 3% rate is low; he earns better returns elsewhere (opportunity cost)
  - Effective deal price is EUR 500K (makes seller feel better about pricing)

#### Litigation & Enforcement Risk
- **Default likelihood:** If clinic underperforms by >20%, default risk rises to 30-40%
- **Seller remedy:** Typically includes acceleration clause and lien on business assets
- **Outcome:** Seller may foreclose, taking back business or forcing sale
- **Your position:** Distressed, forced liquidation scenario

#### Verdict
**RANK: #4 (Weak)**
- **Why:** Seller financing creates structural leverage against you post-close. If clinic underperforms (20% probability), you're in default scenario within 18 months. Seller becomes creditor with enforcement power, not former owner.
- **When it makes sense:** Only if you have extremely strong conviction on clinic success AND seller is motivated to support post-close. Both are questionable here.
- **Better alternative:** Structure as bank debt, not seller financing (bank won't enforce as aggressively)

### **Scenario 4: Phased Acquisition (EUR 250K Year 1 + EUR 250K Year 2)**

#### Financial Profile
| Metric | Year 1 | Year 2 | Total |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cash Outlay** | EUR 250K | EUR 250K | EUR 500K |

| **Cumulative Cash Flow (You)** | EUR 123K | EUR 180K | EUR 303K |

*Assumes clinic scales to EUR 57K monthly by Year 2 (conservative ramp)

#### Capital Efficiency Assessment
- **Upfront burden:** MODERATE. EUR 250K Year 1 is manageable
- **Deployment flexibility:** Year 2 payment depends on Year 1 performance verification
- **Optionality value:** Highest of all scenarios. Year 1 operates as a "prove-out" period

#### Execution Risk: CRITICAL (Structural)
- **Problem 1 — Ownership ambiguity during Year 1:**
  - Who owns business during first 12 months?
  - Are you minority partner (20-30% stake) earning right to acquire full control?
  - Is seller still operator with you as investor?
  - This structure is EXTREMELY difficult to document legally

- **Problem 2 — Earnout/Put-Call mechanics are complex:**
  - At end of Year 1, you have option to buy full stake at EUR 250K
  - Seller has option to sell at EUR 250K
  - What happens if EBITDA is EUR 150K (higher than baseline)? Does price adjust?
  - What happens if EBITDA is EUR 80K (lower)? Can you renegotiate or walk?

- **Problem 3 — Seller incentive misalignment:**
  - Seller retains partial ownership during Year 1
  - He has incentive to minimize clinic investment (wants to preserve upside for Year 2)
  - You have incentive to maximize clinic investment (earn right to full acquisition)
  - Creates operational conflict

#### Regulatory & Legal Complexity
- **Partnership agreement required:** Year 1 structure requires formal partnership or option agreement
- **Tax treatment:** Phased acquisition has complex tax implications (likely two separate transactions)
- **Accounting:** Year 1 may be treated as minority investment, complicating balance sheet
- **Costs:** Legal and accounting fees for phased structure likely EUR 5-10K

#### Seller Acceptance Probability
- **Likelihood seller accepts:** LOW (10-20%)
- **Why seller rejects:**
  - "I want all my money now, not tied to your success"
  - "I don't want to remain operator during transition"
  - "Too complicated legally, I'd need my own lawyer"

- **Why seller might accept:**
  - Tax planning (spread sale over two years)
  - Reduced execution risk (Year 1 proves out concept before full exit)

#### Scenario Outcomes
**If Year 1 Succeeds (EBITDA EUR 130K+):**
- You exercise full acquisition at Year 2 EUR 250K
- Deal math improves retrospectively
- Seller happy with outcome

**If Year 1 Underperforms (EBITDA EUR 100K):**
- You have leverage to renegotiate Year 2 price downward
- Seller can refuse, forcing you to walk or negotiate
- Likely litigation or forced renegotiation

**If Year 1 Clinic Fails (EBITDA EUR 75K):**
- Deal is abandoned OR renegotiated dramatically lower
- Seller likely angry; relationship fractured

#### Verdict
**RANK: #3 (Structurally Sound, Operationally Impossible)**
- **Why:** Legal and operational complexity is exceptionally high. Partnership structure creates daily operational friction. High probability of Year 1-to-Year 2 renegotiation battles.
- **When it makes sense:** Only in scenarios where seller wants to remain partially involved (unlikely for exit-motivated seller).
- **Alternative:** Consider "staged integration" within a single acquisition (e.g., you acquire 100%, but stagger clinic launch timeline and tie final payment to clinic EBITDA).

  - EUR 250K upfront (closer to his EUR 250K anchor)
  - EUR 250K deferred (seller gets "full" EUR 500K value)
  - 3% rate is tax-efficient
  - 5-year term is long enough to feel safe to him
  - Seller retains 5-year leverage (provides comfort)

## SECTION 3: ALTERNATIVE STRUCTURES NOT YET CONSIDERED

### **Alternative Structure A: Performance-Based Earnout (Recommended Modification to Scenario 5)**

#### Structure
```
BASE DEAL: EUR 300K cash at close
EARNOUT: EUR 100K tied to clinic EBITDA performance over 12 months
  - If clinic contributes EUR 50K+ EBITDA by Month 12: Full EUR 100K payment
  - If clinic contributes EUR 40-50K EBITDA: EUR 70K payment
  - If clinic contributes EUR 30-40K EBITDA: EUR 40K payment
  - If clinic contributes <EUR 30K EBITDA: EUR 0K payment

TOTAL RANGE: EUR 300K-400K (not EUR 500K)
```

#### Why This Works Better Than Current Scenarios
1. **Seller incentive alignment:** Seller has EUR 100K at stake based on clinic success
   - Creates incentive for seller to support post-close onboarding
   - Seller introduces customers to clinic services (warm handoff)
   - Seller mentors staff on customer relationship continuity

2. **Buyer leverage:** You have EUR 100K contingency if clinic underperforms
   - If clinic only generates EUR 30K EBITDA instead of EUR 50K, you save EUR 100K
   - This loss recovery offsets your execution shortfall

3. **Pricing accuracy:** Deal price automatically adjusts to clinic performance
   - If clinic is a smashing success (EUR 60K EBITDA), seller gets EUR 100K (earned)
   - If clinic underperforms (EUR 30K EBITDA), seller gets EUR 0K (incentivizes better preparation)

4. **Seller acceptance:** Potentially HIGHEST in all scenarios
   - Seller sees "upside" (could get EUR 100K more)
   - Base price of EUR 300K is close to his EUR 250K anchor
   - Total possible proceeds are EUR 400K (feels like achieving his "real number")

#### Financial Profile
| Metric | Outcome |
|---|---|
| **Upfront Cash** | EUR 300K |
| **Best Case Total** | EUR 400K (seller gets full earnout) |
| **Most Likely Total** | EUR 350K (seller gets 75% earnout) |
| **Worst Case Total** | EUR 300K (clinic fails, seller gets nothing) |
| **Expected Value** | EUR 355K (probability-weighted) |

#### Execution Risk Profile
- **Year 1 cash flow available for debt service:** EUR 123K (if clinic included) or EUR 73K (if clinic separate)
- **Leverage ratio (best case):** 1.12x (EUR 400K / EUR 123K EBITDA)
- **Leverage ratio (worst case):** 2.44x (EUR 400K / EUR 165K EBITDA if seller pulls back)
- **Risk mitigation:** Seller is INCENTIVIZED to help, reducing operational risk

#### Key Terms (LOI Language)
> "Earnout shall be earned based on cumulative Clinic EBITDA contribution measured for the 12-month period following Closing. EBITDA shall be calculated on GAAP basis, audited by Buyer's accountant, and subject to mutual dispute resolution. Seller shall be entitled to observe audit process and provide input on calculation methodology."

#### Seller Acceptance Probability
- **Very High (80-90%)**
- Seller sees "upside sharing," not "lowball"
- Performance-based feels fair and transparent
- Aligns with Seller's interest in clinic success (because he profits from it)

#### VERDICT FOR ALTERNATIVE A
**RECOMMENDED MODIFICATION TO SCENARIO 5**

This is the OPTIMAL structure because:
1. **Lowest upfront capital commitment** (EUR 300K vs EUR 350K)
2. **Highest seller incentive alignment** (earnout tied to actual performance)
3. **Best execution risk mitigation** (seller motivated to support post-close)
4. **Highest seller acceptance probability** (upside positioning)
5. **Price accuracy** (automatically adjusts to clinic reality)

#### Why This is Different from Alternative A
- **Alternative A:** Earnout based on EBITDA (profit metric—easy to manipulate via cost management)
- **Alternative C:** Earnout based on OPERATIONAL METRICS (patient volume, satisfaction—harder to manipulate)

#### Advantages
1. **Alignment with growth indicators:** Patient volume is leading indicator of clinic success
2. **Reduces gaming risk:** Can't manipulate patient metrics via cost-cutting like you can with EBITDA
3. **Seller supervision:** Seller is incentivized to monitor actual clinic operations (patient volume, NPS)
4. **Transparency:** Patient volume and NPS are externally verifiable (patient data, review sites)

#### Challenges
1. **Measurement complexity:** Requires clear definition of "patient visit," "revenue per patient," "NPS calculation"
2. **Dispute potential:** Arguments about what counts as valid patient visit (e.g., consultation vs. treatment)
3. **Data access:** Seller needs access to clinic patient database to verify metrics (privacy/security risk)
4. **Gaming risk:** You could inflate patient volume via low-revenue visits, then earn earnout anyway

| Metric | Outcome |
|---|---|
| **Upfront Cash** | EUR 300K |
| **Expected Earnout** | EUR 75-90K |
| **Total Expected Proceeds** | EUR 375-390K |

#### Seller Acceptance Probability
- **Medium-High (60-70%)**
- Seller appreciates transparency of metric-based structure
- Concerned about complexity of measurement
- Likely to hire accountant to verify metrics (cost: ~EUR 2-3K)

#### VERDICT FOR ALTERNATIVE C
**VIABLE ALTERNATIVE TO ALTERNATIVE A**

Consider this if:
- Seller is highly operational and understands clinic metrics well
- You want to minimize EBITDA-based earnout disputes
- Patient volume is your primary clinic success metric
- You have strong data systems to track patient metrics

Use **Alternative A (EBITDA-based earnout)** if:
- Simpler is better (seller just cares about profits)
- You want to minimize measurement disputes
- Seller is less operationally sophisticated

**Recommendation:** Use Alternative A (EBITDA-based) unless seller specifically requests operational metrics.
