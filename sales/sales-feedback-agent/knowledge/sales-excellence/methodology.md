# Sales Excellence Methodology

> Synthesized from: Astra Culture Hospitality Program (all folders), GBB Playbook, Advertising Principles, Steps of Service Guide, Verbiage Swap, CHEF Method, Friendly Bust, AOA System, Open House Presentation Framework, SDR Scripts

This document organizes all hospitality program training materials into a unified sales excellence framework with 8 sections. Each section defines principles, specific techniques, example language, and scoring criteria for the Sales Feedback Agent.

---

## 1. Greeting

### Principles
- The greeting sets the emotional tone for the entire interaction
- First impressions are formed in the first 7 seconds — warmth, confidence, and professionalism must be immediate
- Every patient should feel expected, welcomed, and valued from the very first word
- Use the patient's name whenever possible — it signals preparation and personal attention

### Specific Techniques
- **Answer within 3 rings** on phone calls
- **5ft/10ft Rule (in-person):** Eye contact and smile at 10 feet; verbal greeting at 5 feet
- **Name confirmation:** Always confirm you're speaking with the right person
- **Brand identification:** State your name and the brand clearly
- **Warm tone:** Conversational, not scripted-sounding
- **Refreshment offer (in-person):** Immediately upon arrival

### Example Language
- Phone: "Hi, this is [Name] calling from Carisma [Brand]. Is this [Patient Name]?"
- In-person: "Welcome to Carisma! You must be [Name] — we've been expecting you. Can I get you something to drink?"
- Follow-up call: "Hi [Name], this is [Agent] from Carisma [Brand] — I'm just following up on your visit with us."

### Scoring Criteria
- [ ] Answered/initiated contact promptly (within 3 rings for calls)
- [ ] Used patient's name correctly
- [ ] Identified themselves by name and brand
- [ ] Tone was warm, professional, and welcoming
- [ ] Made the patient feel expected, not like an interruption
- [ ] Used hospitality language (no "Hey" or overly casual openings)

## 2. Discovery

### Principles
- Discovery is about listening, not pitching — the patient must feel heard before they'll trust your recommendation
- Ask open-ended questions that explore emotional and practical dimensions
- Identify the patient's awareness level (unaware, problem-aware, solution-aware, product-aware, most-aware) and match language accordingly
- Never assume the concern — let the patient articulate it in their own words
- Discovery information drives the entire consultation and recommendation

### Specific Techniques
- **Open-ended questions:** "What specific concerns have you noticed lately?" not "Do you want Botox?"
- **Treatment history check:** "Have you had any aesthetic treatments before? Even something simple like a facial?"
- **Concern exploration:** "What bothers you most about [area]?" / "How does that make you feel?"
- **Timeline awareness:** "How long has this been a concern for you?"
- **Avatar matching:** Listen for story patterns that map to specific GBB packages
- **Active listening signals:** Verbal acknowledgments, paraphrasing back, asking follow-up questions

### Example Language
- "What specific concerns have you noticed lately — perhaps around [target area] — that you'd like to improve?"
- "Have you had any aesthetic treatments before? Even something simple like a facial or Botox?"
- "What brought you to us today?"
- "How long have you been thinking about addressing this?"
- "In a perfect world, what would your ideal result look like?"

### Scoring Criteria
- [ ] Asked at least 2-3 open-ended discovery questions
- [ ] Explored the emotional dimension of the concern (how it makes them feel)
- [ ] Checked treatment history / experience level
- [ ] Let the patient speak without interrupting
- [ ] Paraphrased or acknowledged the patient's concerns back to them
- [ ] Identified the primary concern before presenting any solution
- [ ] Noted key details in CRM / took notes

## 3. Consultation

### Principles
- The consultation bridges discovery to recommendation — it's where trust converts to commitment
- Start with them (their story, concerns, goals), not with your technology or pricing
- Show proof before asking for belief — diagnostics, before/after photos, patient stories
- Use the Investment Frame: treatments are investments in themselves, not costs
- Present options using the Good/Better/Best framework — never a single take-it-or-leave-it offer
- Explain the mechanism simply — why this treatment works, in language they understand

### Specific Techniques
- **Good/Better/Best Presentation:**
  - Good = Anchor Driver (one proven treatment that stands alone)
  - Better = Anchor + Supportive Boosters (amplifies results, creates completeness)
  - Best = Anchor + Boosters + Elite Layer + Status (transformation + exclusivity)
- **PROOF Visuals:** Before/after photos, diagnostic scans, patient testimonials
- **Investment Frame:** "[Total perceived value] is [X], but we're going to give it to you for [Y]"
- **Credential the provider:** "You'll be seeing Dr. [Name], who specializes in..."
- **Avatar-to-package mapping:** Match the patient's story to the right tier
- **Lab/Proof tie-in:** Connect diagnostic results directly to package recommendation

### Example Language
- "I completely get it! Many of our patients visit us with [reiterate their concern]. This is perfect timing because the [Package Name] seems like it could be really beneficial for you."
- "The entire package has a total value of [perceived value] but what we're going to do is give it to you for [actual price]."
- "Your experience will first begin with a personalized consultation with our doctor; they will assess your [area], and overall goals closely with you."
- "Your scan shows [finding] — that's why our [Package] is right for you."

### Scoring Criteria
- [ ] Reiterated the patient's concern before presenting the solution
- [ ] Presented treatment as a solution to their specific concern, not a generic pitch
- [ ] Used Investment Frame (value vs. price anchoring)
- [ ] Offered Good/Better/Best tiers (not just one option)
- [ ] Used proof elements (before/after, scans, testimonials)
- [ ] Explained the mechanism in simple, jargon-free language
- [ ] Credentialed the provider or clinic
- [ ] Connected the recommendation to the patient's specific situation

## 4. Objection Handling

### Principles
- Objections are signs of interest, not rejection — they mean the patient is thinking about it
- Never argue or become defensive — acknowledge, empathize, redirect
- Use stories and social proof to address concerns indirectly
- Price objections are usually value objections — if the value isn't clear enough, re-establish it
- The Verbiage Swap is critical here: reframe negative language into positive alternatives
- Timing matters: some objections are best addressed preemptively during consultation

### Specific Techniques
- **Acknowledge + Empathize + Redirect:** "I completely understand. Many of our patients felt the same way before they started. What we found is..."
- **Story Framework:** Address objections through patient success stories rather than direct counter-arguments
- **Preemptive addressing:** Raise common concerns before the patient does ("A lot of people wonder about...")
- **Value re-establishment:** When price is the objection, break down what's included and re-anchor the perceived value
- **Verbiage Reframing:**
  - "Expensive" becomes "Premium" / "Elevated"
  - "Pain" becomes "Discomfort" / "Sensation"
  - "Side effects" becomes "What to expect" / "Normal response"
  - "Downtime" becomes "Recovery period" / "Healing time"

### Example Language
- "I completely understand — that's actually one of the most common questions we get. The reason we structure it this way is..."
- "Many of our patients had the same thought before they started. [Patient story with similar concern and positive outcome]"
- "What to expect is minimal discomfort — most patients describe it as a warm sensation."
- "The recovery period is typically [timeframe], and we'll be checking in with you throughout."
- When re-anchoring value: "Remember, the total value of everything you're getting is [perceived value] — the [treatments], the [bonuses], and the [follow-ups]."

### Scoring Criteria
- [ ] Acknowledged the objection without being dismissive
- [ ] Showed empathy before redirecting
- [ ] Used hospitality language (Verbiage Swap standards)
- [ ] Did not become defensive or argumentative
- [ ] Addressed the underlying concern, not just the surface objection
- [ ] Used social proof or patient stories when appropriate
- [ ] Reframed negative language into positive alternatives
- [ ] Maintained warmth and confidence throughout

## 5. Closing

### Principles
- Every interaction should have a clear next step — never end without an ask
- The close should feel natural, not pressured — it's an invitation, not a demand
- Collect all necessary details in one smooth sequence (booking, deposit, contact info)
- Confirm everything before ending the conversation
- Send confirmation immediately — don't let momentum fade

### Specific Techniques
- **Assumptive close:** "What day and time usually works best for you?" (assumes they're booking)
- **Deposit collection:** "You'll receive a deposit request through Fresha to confirm and secure your appointment."
- **Detail collection:** Seamlessly gather email, DOB, address in the booking flow
- **Confirmation send:** Immediate text with appointment details, address, and contact info
- **Warm close:** End on a positive, forward-looking note
- **Schedule next visit before they leave** (in-person)

### Example Language
- "We're based in St Julians and we're open Monday-Saturday 9am till 7pm. What day and time usually works best for you?"
- "Great — I've booked you for [Day, Time]. I'll send over a confirmation text shortly. Just need to take your email and date of birth."
- "Perfect! You'll receive a confirmation text now with your appointment, our address, and contact info. Feel free to reach out anytime!"
- "You're in great hands — we're excited to meet you and get you started on your journey with us!"
- "Is there anything else I can assist you with?"

### Scoring Criteria
- [ ] Asked for the booking / next step explicitly
- [ ] Collected deposit or explained deposit process
- [ ] Gathered all required patient details (email, DOB, etc.)
- [ ] Confirmed appointment details (date, time, location)
- [ ] Sent confirmation text/message promptly
- [ ] Closed warmly with enthusiasm and reassurance
- [ ] Left the door open for questions ("Is there anything else?")
- [ ] Added complete call notes to CRM

## 6. Follow-Up

### Principles
- Follow-up is where retention begins — it demonstrates that care extends beyond the transaction
- Timing matters: 24-hour post-treatment check-in is the gold standard
- Every follow-up should have a purpose (check-in, rebook, referral, feedback)
- Follow-up is not just about the patient — it's about gathering intelligence for better service
- Nurture sequences keep the relationship warm between visits

### Specific Techniques
- **24-hour text:** Simple check-in after any treatment or consultation
- **Referral introduction:** Plant the seed for referrals in the first follow-up
- **Rebook prompt:** Suggest the next appointment while satisfaction is high
- **Review request:** Ask for Google/social media reviews when the experience is fresh
- **Nurture sequences:** Ongoing email/text that provides value (tips, offers, education)
- **Community events:** Invite patients to open houses, appreciation events, educational sessions
- **No-show/cancellation recovery:** Express understanding, rebook immediately, don't guilt

### Example Language
- 24-hour check-in: "Hi [Name]! Just checking in after your visit yesterday. How are you feeling? Any questions about your aftercare?"
- Referral: "We'd love to welcome anyone you know who might benefit from the same experience. Here's a special offer for friends and family."
- Rebook: "Based on your treatment plan, your next session would ideally be in [timeframe]. Shall I reserve your preferred slot?"
- No-show recovery: "Hi [Name], we missed you today! No worries at all — life happens. Would you like to reschedule? We'd love to see you soon."

### Scoring Criteria
- [ ] Followed up within 24 hours of treatment/consultation
- [ ] Check-in was genuine and caring, not transactional
- [ ] Included a clear purpose (check-in, rebook, referral, review)
- [ ] Used hospitality language throughout
- [ ] Offered to rebook or suggested next steps
- [ ] Handled no-shows/cancellations with grace, not guilt
- [ ] Personalized the message (referenced specific treatment/concern)
- [ ] Recorded follow-up outcome in CRM

## 7. Upselling

### Principles
- Upselling is enhancing the patient's experience, not extracting more money
- Use "Enhance" and "Complement" instead of "Upsell" or "Add-on"
- The GBB framework IS the upsell mechanism — present all three tiers and let the patient choose
- Upselling works best when tied to the patient's stated goals, not generic promotions
- Proof and diagnostics make upselling feel like medical recommendation, not sales pressure
- VIP memberships and maintenance plans are the highest-value upsell

### Specific Techniques
- **GBB tier presentation:** The natural movement from Good to Better to Best
- **Booster positioning:** "To amplify your results, we can add [treatment] which works synergistically with..."
- **VIP/membership introduction:** Present at checkout when satisfaction is highest
- **Maintenance plan:** "To maintain your results long-term, most of our patients do..."
- **Skincare/product recommendation:** Complement treatments with take-home products
- **Appointment confirmation upsell:** Add value when confirming an upcoming appointment
- **Event-based upsell:** Special packages available only during open houses or events

### Example Language
- "To get the most out of your [treatment], many of our patients also add [booster] which really amplifies the results."
- "Based on your goals, the Better package would give you [additional benefit] on top of the foundation treatment."
- "We also have a VIP membership that gives you priority booking, special rates, and exclusive access to new treatments."
- "To keep your results looking amazing long-term, I'd recommend scheduling a maintenance session every [timeframe]."

### Scoring Criteria
- [ ] Presented upsell as enhancement to the patient's goals, not a generic add-on
- [ ] Used hospitality language ("enhance," "complement," not "upsell," "add-on")
- [ ] Tied recommendation to the patient's specific concern or stated goals
- [ ] Used the GBB framework (presented tiers, not just one option)
- [ ] Included proof or rationale for the recommendation
- [ ] Did not pressure — respected the patient's choice at any tier
- [ ] Mentioned VIP/membership where appropriate
- [ ] Timing was natural (not forced into the conversation)

## 8. Referrals

### Principles
- Referrals are the highest-quality leads — they come with built-in trust
- The ask should happen when satisfaction and emotional connection are at their peak
- Make it easy: provide a clear mechanism (link, card, code) for referring
- Reward both the referrer and the referred — mutual value strengthens the referral
- Referrals extend naturally from exceptional experience — if the experience isn't right, fix that first
- Community building (events, social media engagement) creates organic referral opportunities

### Specific Techniques
- **Post-treatment ask:** Plant the referral seed during the 24-hour follow-up or at checkout
- **Referral incentive program:** Clear, simple benefit for both parties
- **Community events:** Open houses, appreciation nights, and educational sessions where patients bring friends
- **Review-to-referral pipeline:** Ask for a review first (lower commitment), then mention the referral program
- **Social media sharing:** Create shareable moments during the visit (photo ops, results celebrations)
- **Hero's journey testimonials:** Turn patient success stories into referral content (with permission)

### Example Language
- "We'd love to welcome anyone you know who might benefit from the same experience. Here's a referral card with a special offer for them — and a thank-you for you too."
- "If you know anyone who's been struggling with [similar concern], we'd love to help them the way we helped you."
- "We're hosting an open house next [date] — it's a great chance to bring a friend and show them what we do here."
- "Would you be open to sharing your experience with us? Your story could really help someone who's going through the same thing."

### Scoring Criteria
- [ ] Mentioned the referral program or opportunity naturally in conversation
- [ ] Timing was appropriate (after positive experience, not during a complaint)
- [ ] Made the referral process clear and easy (provided mechanism)
- [ ] Mentioned mutual benefit (value for referrer and referred)
- [ ] Used patient's own positive language/experience as the foundation
- [ ] Invited to community events where appropriate
- [ ] Asked for reviews as a stepping stone to referrals
- [ ] Did not pressure — framed as an invitation, not an obligation

## Appendix: Cross-Cutting Standards

### Hospitality Language (applies to ALL 8 sections)
Every interaction must use elevated, hospitality-grade language. Key swaps:
- "No problem" → "My pleasure" / "Absolutely"
- "Cost" → "Investment" / "Value"
- "Procedure" → "Treatment" / "Experience"
- "Problem" → "Challenge" / "Concern"
- "Pain" → "Discomfort" / "Sensation"
- "Sell" → "Recommend" / "Suggest"
- "Cheap" → "Accessible" / "Entry-level"
- "Contract" → "Agreement" / "Membership"

### Team Accountability (Friendly Bust + AOA)
Sales excellence requires team-wide consistency:
- **AOA system:** Immediate verbal acknowledgment of every task or handoff
- **Friendly Bust:** Quick, respectful corrections in real time — anyone to anyone
- **CHEF leadership:** Coach (develop people), Host (set the tone), Execute (hold standards), Follow Up (reinforce growth)

### Awareness-Level Matching
Match communication style to the patient's awareness level:
- **Unaware:** Lead with story and identity
- **Problem-aware:** Lead with empathy and agitation
- **Solution-aware:** Lead with mechanism and differentiation
- **Product-aware:** Lead with proof and social validation
- **Most aware:** Lead with offer and urgency

### Scoring Weight Guide

> **NOTE: These importance levels are for training reference only. Actual scoring weights are defined in rubric/scoring-rubric.md and rubric/brand-criteria.md. Do not use these weights for scoring.**

When evaluating a sales interaction, weight these areas based on their impact:
1. **Discovery** (25%) — The foundation; poor discovery leads to mismatched recommendations
2. **Consultation/Presentation** (20%) — Where value is established and belief is built
3. **Objection Handling** (15%) — Critical for conversion; reveals skill under pressure
4. **Closing** (15%) — Where commitment happens; must be confident but not pushy
5. **Greeting** (10%) — Sets the tone; hard to recover from a poor first impression
6. **Follow-Up** (5%) — Retention driver; shows care beyond the transaction
7. **Upselling** (5%) — Revenue optimization; must feel natural, not forced
8. **Referrals** (5%) — Growth driver; highest-quality lead generation
