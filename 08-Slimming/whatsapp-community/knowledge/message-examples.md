EXAMPLE T-1 (Theme: Protein hacks — easy wins)

💪 Tip Tuesday

Here's the single easiest way to improve your day without changing what you eat.

Add protein to whatever you're already having.

Greek yoghurt on the side of your lunch. An egg on top of your salad. A handful of leftover chicken added to last night's pasta.

You don't need a new meal. You need a stronger version of the one you're already making.

The goal: 1 to 2 palm-sized portions of protein at each main meal. That amount keeps hunger calm for hours.

Try it today and see how different your 4pm feels.

With you every step,

EXAMPLE T-3 (Theme: Hydration — the quiet tool)

💪 Tip Tuesday

If you're feeling low energy by mid-morning, the first question to ask isn't "am I hungry?"

It's: "have I had enough water today?"

Mild dehydration mimics hunger. It also makes you feel tired, foggy, and low — which makes food feel more appealing than it actually needs to be.

The simple fix: a big glass of water before you do anything else. Then another mid-morning. Sparkling water works even better if you're doing the morning fasting window — it takes the edge off and makes the time feel shorter.

Two glasses before noon. See what changes.

With you every step,
Katya

EXAMPLE W-1 (Recipe: Baked Lemon Herb Chicken Thighs)

💪 What's Cooking Wednesday

This one goes in the oven and then you forget about it. Done in 35 minutes, works for the whole family, and genuinely tastes like you tried harder than you did.

Lemon Herb Baked Chicken Thighs
Serves 4 — approx 300 points per serving (without sides)

You'll need:
• 4 bone-in chicken thighs (skin on or off, your choice)
• 2 cloves garlic, minced
• Juice of 1 lemon
• 1 tsp dried oregano
• 1 tsp paprika
• Salt and pepper
• 1 tbsp olive oil
• Fresh parsley to serve (optional)

How to make it:
1. Preheat your oven to 200°C.
2. Mix the garlic, lemon juice, oregano, paprika, olive oil, salt, and pepper in a small bowl.
3. Coat the chicken thighs in the marinade — if you have time, leave them 30 minutes. If not, go straight in.
4. Place in a baking dish and roast for 35-40 minutes until golden and cooked through.
5. Serve with a big salad (free points) and a small portion of roasted potatoes or rice on the side.

The protein is there. The fat is intentional. The vegetables are free. This is the formula in real food.

With you every step,
Katya

EXAMPLE W-2 (Recipe: Tuna and Chickpea Salad)

💪 What's Cooking Wednesday

No cooking. Seven minutes. High protein. And the kind of lunch you'd actually order if you saw it on a menu.

Tuna and Chickpea Salad
Serves 1 — approx 450 points

You'll need:
• 1 can tuna in water, drained (about 120g)
• Half a can of chickpeas, rinsed (about 120g drained)
• 1 large handful of rocket or mixed leaves
• 1 tomato, chopped
• Half a cucumber, sliced
• 4-5 olives
• Juice of half a lemon
• 1 tbsp olive oil
• Salt, pepper, dried herbs to taste

How to make it:
1. Drain the tuna and chickpeas well.
2. Add everything to a bowl — no cooking needed.
3. Drizzle with lemon juice and olive oil.
4. Season and toss.
5. Eat immediately or pack for work.

The chickpeas give you protein AND fibre, so this one keeps you full for a long time. The vegetables are free. The olive oil is your one intentional fat.

Quick, filling, and genuinely satisfying.

With you every step,
Katya

EXAMPLE W-3 (Recipe: Greek Yoghurt Overnight Oats)

💪 What's Cooking Wednesday

This takes four minutes to make tonight and gives you a breakfast that's actually filling tomorrow morning — if you're on the non-fasting track.

Greek Yoghurt Overnight Oats
Serves 1 — approx 400 points

You'll need:
• 50g rolled oats
• 150g plain Greek yoghurt (full fat or low fat, both work)
• 100ml milk (any type)
• 1 tsp honey or a few drops of vanilla extract
• A handful of berries (strawberries, blueberries — they count as points so keep it moderate)
• Pinch of cinnamon

How to make it:
1. Mix the oats, yoghurt, milk, and honey in a jar or container.
2. Stir well. The oats will absorb the liquid overnight.
3. Top with berries and cinnamon before eating.
4. Store in the fridge — stays good for up to 2 days.

Protein from the yoghurt. Fibre from the oats. A small carb hit from the honey and fruit. This keeps blood sugar steady and hunger calm through the morning.

Make it tonight. Thank yourself tomorrow.

With you every step,
Katya

EXAMPLE W-4 (Recipe: One-Pan Salmon and Roasted Vegetables)

💪 What's Cooking Wednesday

One pan, one oven, almost no washing up. This is the dinner for the nights when you have twenty minutes and nothing planned.

One-Pan Salmon with Roasted Vegetables
Serves 2 — approx 500 points per serving (salmon + vegetables, before extra carbs)

You'll need:
• 2 salmon fillets (about 150g each)
• 1 courgette, sliced into rounds
• 1 red pepper, cut into strips
• 1 red onion, cut into wedges
• 10 cherry tomatoes
• 1 tbsp olive oil
• Salt, pepper, garlic powder, dried thyme
• Lemon wedges to serve

How to make it:
1. Preheat oven to 200°C.
2. Toss the vegetables in olive oil, salt, pepper, and garlic powder. Spread on a baking tray.
3. Roast for 15 minutes.
4. Push vegetables to the edges. Place salmon fillets in the centre.
5. Season the salmon with thyme, salt, and pepper.
6. Roast for another 12-15 minutes until salmon is cooked through.
7. Serve with lemon wedges. Add rice or bread if you need carbs — that's your one carb choice for the meal.

The vegetables are zero points and cover most of the tray. The salmon is your protein anchor. Everything else is colour.

With you every step,
Katya

EXAMPLE SC-1 (Theme: The breakfast metabolism myth)

💪 Science Thursday

Let's clear something up that most people believe but isn't actually true.

"Breakfast starts your metabolism."

It doesn't. Your metabolism runs continuously. It does not switch on when you eat your first meal and switch off when you skip it. Metabolic rate is determined by your body composition, your activity level, and your total intake — not by whether you ate at 7am.

The reason breakfast timing matters for some people is habit and appetite, not metabolic activation. If eating breakfast helps you control hunger later in the day, great. If you do better without it, that's equally valid.

What actually matters: total protein intake across the day. That's the lever that preserves muscle and keeps your metabolism healthy during weight loss.

The plan isn't built around myths. It's built around what the evidence actually shows.

With you every step,
Katya

---

EXAMPLE SC-2 (Theme: Why sleep affects weight loss)

💪 Science Thursday

If you're doing everything right and still feeling like progress is slow, this is worth knowing.

Sleep and weight loss are connected more directly than most people realise.

When you sleep poorly, your body produces more ghrelin — the hormone that makes you hungry — and less leptin — the hormone that tells you you're full. The result the next day: you feel hungrier than usual and less satisfied after eating. Your brain also craves higher-calorie, higher-sugar foods specifically.

On top of that, poor sleep raises cortisol, which encourages fat storage — particularly around the abdomen.

This isn't willpower. This is biology.

If your nights are rough, you're fighting a harder battle the next day through no fault of your own. Getting sleep isn't a luxury. For weight loss, it's infrastructure.

With you every step,
Katya

---

EXAMPLE SC-3 (Theme: What protein actually does)

💪 Science Thursday

Protein gets a lot of marketing attention. Here's what it's actually doing in your body.

When you eat protein, it triggers the release of satiety hormones — particularly GLP-1 and peptide YY. These are the signals that tell your brain "you've had enough." Carbohydrates and fats don't do this as efficiently or for as long.

Protein also has something called a thermic effect: your body burns more calories digesting it than it does digesting carbs or fats. Roughly 20-30% of protein calories are used in digestion itself.

And critically for weight loss: protein preserves lean muscle. When you eat less, your body can break down muscle for energy. High protein intake protects against that, which means your metabolism stays healthier as you lose weight.

The 1-2 palm-portion rule at each meal isn't arbitrary. It's the simplest way to hit the range that makes the science work.

With you every step,
Katya

---

EXAMPLE SC-4 (Theme: Cortisol and stress eating)

💪 Science Thursday

Ever noticed that stressful weeks are the hardest weeks on the plan?

That's not coincidence. That's cortisol.

When you're stressed — really stressed, chronically — your body releases cortisol. Cortisol is a survival hormone. Its job is to make sure you have enough energy to deal with the threat. So it increases appetite, encourages your body to hold onto fat, and specifically drives cravings for dense, calorie-rich foods.

Your body cannot tell the difference between a work deadline and a lion. It responds the same way.

This is why "just eat less" advice falls apart during high-stress periods. You're not weak. You're wired.

What actually helps: protein keeps blood sugar steadier, which reduces cortisol spikes from hunger. Sleep helps cortisol regulate overnight. And knowing this is happening means you can plan around it instead of feeling like you're failing.

Stress eating isn't a moral failure. It's a hormone. And it's manageable.

With you every step,
Katya

# SATURDAY — Saturday Strategy
Theme: Meal prep, navigating restaurants/social events, weekend planning, managing alcohol, batch cooking
Length: Medium (5-8 lines)
Target emotion: "I know how to handle today without throwing the week away"

---

EXAMPLE SS-1 (Theme: Simple batch cooking approach)

💪 Saturday Strategy

You don't need to spend your Saturday meal prepping like a professional chef.

Here's a simpler version that takes about 40 minutes and sets up most of your week.

Cook one protein in bulk. Chicken thighs, a piece of salmon, a batch of boiled eggs — whatever you'll actually eat. Cook enough for 3-4 lunches.

Wash and cut any vegetables you'll use raw this week. Put them in containers at the front of the fridge where you can see them.

That's it. Two things. One cooking session.

The rest of the week, lunch is protein from the fridge plus whatever vegetables you have. Add your one carb — bread, rice, a potato. Five minutes. Not a production.

Preparation doesn't have to be elaborate to be useful.

With you every step,
Katya

💪 Saturday Strategy

Going out tonight? Here's what actually works.

At the restaurant: find the protein on the menu first and build around it. Order a salad starter if there is one — that's vegetables handled. For your main, pick one carb (pasta OR bread OR potatoes — not all three).

If you're having wine, count it. It's not off-limits; it just has points. One or two glasses with dinner fits into most evenings without a problem.

You don't need to explain the plan to anyone at the table. You don't need to order differently from everyone else. You're just being intentional about where you start.

Enjoy the evening. Really. That's part of this too.

With you every step,

EXAMPLE SS-3 (Theme: Managing alcohol at weekend events)

💪 Saturday Strategy

If you're drinking this weekend, you don't need to choose between the plan and enjoying yourself.

You need a strategy.

Alcohol has points — roughly 70 points per unit, more if there are mixers. A glass of wine is around 120-150 points. A gin and slimline tonic is around 60. A beer is around 150-200 depending on size.

A simple approach: decide before you go how many drinks you're having. One decision, made in advance, from a calm place. Much easier than making that decision at the bar at 11pm.

Eat your protein and vegetables before or with the drinks. Food slows alcohol absorption and keeps blood sugar steadier — which means fewer late-night cravings.

And the next morning: return to your normal structure. No punishment. No skipping meals to "compensate." Just back to rhythm.

One evening doesn't undo a week. The return does.

With you every step,
Katya

# HOW TO USE THESE EXAMPLES

These are not templates to copy word for word. They are benchmarks for:

Voice check: Does the generated message sound like Katya — warm, honest, never preachy, never hollow?
Length check: Does it match the target length for that day?
Emotional arc check: Does it validate before advising? Does it end with agency, not guilt?
Format check: Is it WhatsApp-ready? No markdown. No links. Breathable spacing. Single 💪 at the top.

When generating a new message, ask: "Would this fit in this file without looking out of place?"

If the answer is no, revise until it does.
