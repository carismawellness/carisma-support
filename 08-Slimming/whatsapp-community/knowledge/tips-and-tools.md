## MEAL PREP & PLANNING

---

### SUNDAY PROTEIN BATCH — The One Habit That Changes Everything

**Best for:** Tip Tuesday
**Speaks to:** Busy Professional, Young Mum, All

The Challenge: The reason most people go off plan mid-week isn't lack of motivation — it's that there's nothing ready when hunger hits.

The Strategy: On Sunday, cook three protein sources in bulk. A tray of chicken thighs in the oven (salt, olive oil, garlic, 40 minutes at 200°C). A pot of hard-boiled eggs (12 at a time, they keep 5 days in the fridge). A pan of mince with onion and herbs. Divide into containers. You now have protein available for every meal Monday through Thursday without a single decision required.

Why It Works: Protein is the most effort-intensive part of every meal. When it's already cooked, meals assemble in 2 minutes instead of 20. You remove the friction that makes takeaway feel easier than cooking.

Katya's Note: You don't need Pinterest-worthy meal prep — you just need protein in the fridge.

### THE THREE-CONTAINER LUNCH METHOD

**Best for:** Tip Tuesday / Either
**Speaks to:** Busy Professional, All

The Challenge: Lunch is often the meal people abandon first, because mornings are chaotic and there's no time to think about food at 7am.

The Strategy: Buy three matching containers with lids (IKEA, Pavi, or any euro store). The night before — not the morning — fill Container 1 with your protein (leftover chicken, tuna, boiled eggs, chickpeas). Container 2 gets raw or roasted vegetables. Container 3 holds your optional carb or fat (rice, cheese, olives, hummus). Stack them. Grab and go. This works even if you're running 10 minutes late.

Why It Works: Decision fatigue is real. Three containers with one job each removes every choice from your morning. The structure builds the habit — after two weeks, you do it without thinking.

Katya's Note: The nights you don't feel like prepping are exactly the nights you'll thank yourself the next day.

### BUILD A WEEK OF MEALS IN 30 MINUTES

**Best for:** Saturday Strategy
**Speaks to:** Busy Professional, Young Mum, Serial Dieter

The Challenge: "I don't have time to meal plan" — said by almost everyone who's started and stopped before.

The Strategy: Spend 15 minutes on Saturday writing out your 5 dinners for the week using this formula: Protein + Vegetable + Optional carb. Monday: salmon + roasted courgette + rice. Tuesday: mince bolognese + salad + no pasta (or a little pasta). Keep it boring. Boring is sustainable. Then write your shopping list from that plan. Spend the other 15 minutes ordering click-and-collect from PG or Pavi so it's ready Sunday morning. That's it. The week is planned.

Why It Works: When dinner is decided in advance, you don't arrive home hungry and make a last-minute call that derails the whole day. Planning removes the 6pm chaos decision.

Katya's Note: You don't need 30 recipe ideas — you need 5 reliable ones you rotate.

### THE ANCHOR MEAL STRATEGY

**Best for:** Tip Tuesday
**Speaks to:** Serial Dieter, Menopausal Woman, All

The Challenge: Trying to get every meal right every day is exhausting. It sets you up to feel like a failure when one meal isn't perfect.

The Strategy: Choose ONE meal to get right consistently, every single day — this is your anchor meal. Most people find dinner easiest to control because they're home. Make that meal non-negotiable: protein first, vegetables, small carb if you want one. The rest of the day, do your best. But the anchor holds. Over time, as the anchor becomes automatic, the other meals follow naturally.

Why It Works: Consistency in one area builds the identity of someone who eats well. That identity then extends to other meals — not through discipline, but through self-concept.

Katya's Note: One meal done well, every day, will outperform perfect eating for three days followed by nothing.

### PRE-PORTIONING SNACKS — END THE GRAZING LOOP

**Best for:** Tip Tuesday
**Speaks to:** Young Mum, Busy Professional, Menopausal Woman

The Challenge: You're not bingeing — you're grazing. A handful here, a biscuit there, a few crackers at 4pm. By evening you've eaten more than a full meal without ever sitting down to eat.

The Strategy: On Sunday, portion your week's snacks into small zip-lock bags or containers. A portion of nuts (a small handful — about 20 almonds). A portion of cheese. A few squares of dark chocolate. A hard-boiled egg. When the 4pm hunger hits, you reach for the pre-portioned bag — not the open pack you'll mindlessly eat from. Put the open packets at the back of the cupboard or in an inconvenient drawer.

Why It Works: The barrier between you and a reasonable portion is the container. Without it, the default is eating until the packet is empty or until you feel too full to stop. Pre-portioning removes the decision at the moment of hunger — the worst possible time to decide anything.

Katya's Note: This is boring. It's also one of the most effective things you can do this week.

### THE SHOPPING LIST STARTER KIT

**Best for:** Saturday Strategy
**Speaks to:** All, especially those just starting

The Challenge: You go to PG with good intentions and leave with things that don't add up to meals.

The Strategy: Keep this list on your phone as a template. Protein: chicken thighs, mince, eggs, tinned tuna, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese. Vegetables: whatever is cheapest and in season — frozen is fine (Lidl frozen spinach, peas, and mixed veg are excellent). Fats: olive oil, avocado, cheese in small amounts. Optional carbs: rice, oats, wraps, sweet potato. Flavour: garlic, lemon, paprika, cumin, mixed herbs. That list will build a week of meals. Add your specific recipes on top, but never shop without the base.

Why It Works: A structured shopping list means you cook from a stocked kitchen rather than improvising from a half-empty one. The food environment drives the food choices.

Katya's Note: If it's in your fridge, you'll eat it. If it's not, you won't. The shop is where the week is decided.

### FREEZER-FIRST THINKING

**Best for:** Tip Tuesday
**Speaks to:** Young Mum, Busy Professional

The Challenge: You batch-cook with great intentions, then midweek something happens and you need a meal fast.

The Strategy: Whatever you cook in bulk, freeze half of it. Bolognese, soup, chili, chicken curry — all freeze perfectly. Label with masking tape and a marker (Lidl sell both for under €2). Date it. When life collapses on a Wednesday, you pull a container from the freezer, defrost by morning, and you still have a real meal. Build a rotation: cook, freeze half, rotate in the frozen meals.

Why It Works: The freezer is your emergency plan. Without one, the emergency plan is takeaway. With one, it's a meal you already cooked in a better moment.

Katya's Note: Future-you is always grateful. Past-you just needs to make a slightly bigger batch.

## EATING OUT & SOCIAL SITUATIONS

---

### THE RESTAURANT ORDERING STRATEGY

**Best for:** Saturday Strategy
**Speaks to:** Busy Professional, Event-Driven, All

The Challenge: Restaurants feel like they work against you — menus designed around carbs and sauces, portions sized for two people, bread arriving before you've even decided what you want.

The Strategy: Before you open the menu, decide: I'm ordering protein and vegetables. Scan for those two things first. A grilled fish, a chicken dish, a steak — choose the protein, then see what vegetables come with it or can be swapped in. Ask the server: "Can I have extra salad instead of the chips?" Most places say yes. Eat the protein first. If bread arrives, leave it or pass it to someone else. If you want something from the carb section, have it after — but get the protein in first.

Why It Works: The eating order matters. Protein first slows digestion and reduces how much you eat of everything else. You leave feeling satisfied rather than stuffed on bread and pasta.

Katya's Note: You're not the difficult one at the table. You're just someone who knows what they want.

---

### HOW TO HANDLE A BUFFET WITHOUT SPIRALING

**Best for:** Saturday Strategy
**Speaks to:** Event-Driven, Serial Dieter, All

The Challenge: Buffets are designed to overwhelm. Every option looks appealing. The abundance triggers something primal. Most people leave buffets having eaten double what they would at a normal meal.

The Strategy: Do one full walk around before you take a plate. Look at everything. Then go back with a plate and choose: a protein first (anything grilled, roasted, or baked — skip the fried), then fill half the plate with salad or vegetables, then add one thing you really want from the rest. Sit down. Eat slowly. If you're still hungry after 15 minutes, go back — but you probably won't be.

Why It Works: The first walk removes the "I need to try everything" anxiety. You've seen it all. Now you can choose intentionally rather than reactively.

Katya's Note: One plate, chosen with intention, is always better than three plates chosen in a panic.

### THE "DECIDE BEFORE YOU ARRIVE" RULE

**Best for:** Tip Tuesday
**Speaks to:** Event-Driven, Serial Dieter, Menopausal Woman

The Challenge: Social events feel unpredictable. You don't know what food will be there, and by the time you see it, you're already making decisions while hungry and distracted.

The Strategy: Before you go to any social event — a family dinner, a birthday, a work lunch — spend 2 minutes deciding your plan. Will you eat beforehand so food is less of a focus? Will you have protein first whatever is served? Will you allow one drink? Make the decision when you're calm and at home, not when you're standing next to a table of pastizzi and someone is pushing a plate toward you.

Why It Works: Pre-commitment works. When the decision is already made, willpower isn't in play. You're just executing a plan, not fighting an impulse.

Katya's Note: The plan doesn't need to be perfect. It just needs to exist before you walk through the door.

### NAVIGATING MALTESE FEASTS AND FAMILY DINNERS

**Best for:** Saturday Strategy
**Speaks to:** Young Mum, Menopausal Woman, All (Malta-specific)

The Challenge: Maltese food culture is generous, communal, and abundant. Feasts, family Sundays, and village festivals are beautiful — but they're built around bread, pastizzi, rabbit stew, and desserts. Saying no feels rude. Saying yes feels like giving up.

The Strategy: You don't have to say no to everything. Have the rabbit stew — it's protein-rich, actually a decent choice. Eat the salad first if there is one. Skip or minimise the bread and ftira (or have one piece, not four). Have a small amount of the dessert if you want it — sitting and sharing it is part of connection. The goal is not to be the person making it awkward at the table. The goal is to choose the best options within what's available, eat slowly, and stop when you're satisfied.

Why It Works: Rigid restriction at social meals leads to the binge-restrict cycle. Flexible, intentional participation keeps you in control while staying connected to your life.

Katya's Note: A feast is not a failure. How you eat the rest of the week is what matters most.

### THE WINE STRATEGY — INCLUDING ALCOHOL WITHOUT DERAILING

**Best for:** Saturday Strategy
**Speaks to:** Busy Professional, Event-Driven, All

The Challenge: You want to have a drink. You feel like you can't. So you either don't and feel resentful, or you do and feel guilty and then eat more than you intended.

The Strategy: Alcohol is not forbidden — it's accounted for. One or two glasses of wine, a G&T, or a beer is manageable. Here's how: eat protein first before you start drinking — alcohol on an empty stomach leads to worse decisions. Alternate one glass of water with every alcoholic drink. Choose dry wine over sweet, spirits with sugar-free mixers over cocktails. Limit to two drinks. Plan for it rather than letting it happen by default.

Why It Works: Accounting for alcohol removes the guilt that triggers overeating. When you've planned for two drinks, you don't feel like you've "broken the rules" and you don't spiral into the rest of the evening.

Katya's Note: The goal is a life you can live in, not a plan you white-knuckle through.

### HOW TO HANDLE FOOD PUSHERS

**Best for:** Tip Tuesday
**Speaks to:** Young Mum, Menopausal Woman, All

The Challenge: Someone always offers more, insists you try something, or comments on what you're eating. Sometimes it's meant with love. Sometimes it's intrusive. Either way, it's uncomfortable.

The Strategy: You don't owe anyone an explanation about your food. A few phrases that work: "It looks amazing, I'm just pacing myself." "I've already eaten, thank you." "I'm good with this, thank you." Say it warmly, smile, move the conversation forward. If someone persists, it's fine to be slightly more direct: "I'm good, really — but tell me about [change subject]." You don't need to explain your health journey to anyone at the table.

Why It Works: Food pushers usually aren't malicious — they're just used to food being the medium of care. Redirecting warmly satisfies the social need without derailing your meal.

Katya's Note: You're allowed to eat exactly as much as you want to eat. No justification needed.

## KITCHEN & COOKING HACKS

---

### THE ONE-PAN RULE FOR BUSY WEEKNIGHTS

**Best for:** Tip Tuesday
**Speaks to:** Young Mum, Busy Professional

The Challenge: After a full day, cooking a proper meal feels like too many steps, too much washing up, too much effort.

The Strategy: One pan, one method — sheet pan dinners. Lay your protein on a baking tray (chicken thighs, salmon fillets, sausages if you're in a rush). Chop whatever vegetables are in the fridge — courgette, pepper, onion, cherry tomatoes, broccoli — and spread them around the protein. Drizzle with olive oil, salt, garlic, a squeeze of lemon, whatever spices you have. 200°C for 30-40 minutes. One pan. Almost no prep. Almost no washing up. A complete Carisma-compliant meal.

Why It Works: The simpler the cooking process, the more likely you are to do it. A one-pan dinner at home beats a takeaway every single time — in cost, in nutrition, and in how you feel the next morning.

Katya's Note: On the days you have no energy for cooking, this is the move.

### PROTEIN POWDER IN UNEXPECTED PLACES

**Best for:** Tip Tuesday
**Speaks to:** Busy Professional, Young Mum, All

The Challenge: Getting enough protein is the thing most women struggle with, especially at breakfast when there's no time to cook.

The Strategy: Add an unflavoured or vanilla protein powder to: your porridge (stir it in before adding hot water), Greek yogurt (adds 15-20g of protein to an already protein-rich base), pancake mix (one egg, one scoop protein, one mashed banana — cook like normal pancakes), and smoothies of course. One scoop typically adds 20-25g of protein with minimal impact on taste when mixed into food. Scott's in Valletta, Lidl, or online — pick whichever is cheapest and has minimal ingredients.

Why It Works: Protein at breakfast sets the tone for the whole day. It reduces hunger at lunch, improves concentration, and makes the Carisma points system easier to navigate because you're not spending points playing catch-up.

Katya's Note: You don't have to love the taste of protein powder. You just have to get it in.

### GREEK YOGURT AS A SUBSTITUTE FOR EVERYTHING

**Best for:** Tip Tuesday
**Speaks to:** All

The Challenge: Recipes call for cream, mayonnaise, sour cream, or heavy dressings — all high in points with very little protein return.

The Strategy: Full-fat plain Greek yogurt (Lidl, PG, or Pavi — the 500g tubs are good value) can replace: mayonnaise in coleslaw and dressings (same creaminess, 10x the protein), sour cream on baked potato or tacos, cream in creamy pasta sauces (stir in off the heat so it doesn't split), and as the base for any dip — add garlic and cucumber for a tzatziki that takes 2 minutes. Also works as a dessert base with a drizzle of honey and some fruit.

Why It Works: Greek yogurt gives you the creaminess and satiety of high-fat options but with a much better protein-to-point ratio. Your meals feel indulgent without being expensive in points.

Katya's Note: This one swap, done consistently, makes a meaningful difference.

### HOW TO MAKE VEGETABLES ACTUALLY ENJOYABLE

**Best for:** Tip Tuesday
**Speaks to:** All, especially those who say they don't like vegetables

The Challenge: Raw or steamed vegetables feel like punishment. The reason most people don't eat enough vegetables isn't lack of knowledge — it's that they're not making them taste good.

The Strategy: Roast them. Everything tastes better roasted. Cut broccoli, courgette, peppers, or cauliflower into pieces, toss with olive oil, garlic, and salt, roast at 200°C until the edges are slightly brown. That caramelisation changes the flavour entirely. Other methods that work: stir-fry with soy sauce and sesame oil, roast cherry tomatoes until they collapse (they become almost sweet), cook onions low and slow in olive oil until soft and golden. Season aggressively. Vegetables are free in the Carisma system — you should be eating them in quantity, but only if they taste good.

Why It Works: Palatability drives behaviour. If the food is enjoyable, you eat more of it. The goal is to make your free foods so good that they crowd out the things you're trying to eat less of.

Katya's Note: "I don't like vegetables" usually means "I've only had them boiled." Try roasting. Report back.

### THE SPICE SHELF THAT CHANGES EVERYTHING

**Best for:** Saturday Strategy
**Speaks to:** Busy Professional, All

The Challenge: Healthy food tastes bland. The answer isn't more sauce — it's more flavour.

The Strategy: Build this starter spice shelf and you can make the same five protein-and-veg meals taste completely different every day. Essential: smoked paprika (adds depth to anything), cumin (earthy and warm — works on chicken, chickpeas, eggs), garlic powder (faster than fresh garlic when you're tired), mixed Italian herbs (for any Mediterranean-style cooking), cinnamon (for porridge, yogurt, and sweet potato). Add: chili flakes, turmeric, za'atar. All available at PG, Pavi, or Lidl at low cost. A chicken thigh with garlic powder and smoked paprika is a completely different meal from one with cumin and coriander.

Why It Works: Variety prevents boredom. Boredom triggers cravings and the sense that you're deprived. When your food tastes genuinely good, you stop looking for something else.

Katya's Note: A €2 jar of smoked paprika might be the best investment you make this week.

## HYDRATION & TIMING

---

### THE MORNING WATER RITUAL

**Best for:** Tip Tuesday
**Speaks to:** All

The Challenge: Most people start their day dehydrated without knowing it, then misread dehydration as hunger by mid-morning.

The Strategy: Before your first coffee, before your phone, before anything — drink a large glass of water. 300-500ml. Room temperature or warm with a slice of lemon if you like. Make it the first automatic act of your morning. It takes 30 seconds. If you struggle to remember, put a glass next to your kettle or on your bedside table the night before. During the rest of your 4-hour morning delay, drink two more glasses. Aim for 1.5-2 litres before your first meal.

Why It Works: Dehydration mimics hunger. By front-loading water in the morning, you reduce false hunger signals, support digestion, and arrive at your first meal actually hungry rather than anxious about whether you're hungry.

Katya's Note: If you do nothing else new this week, do this one thing. The simplest habits compound fastest.

---

### HOW TO USE THE 4-HOUR MORNING DELAY EFFECTIVELY

**Best for:** Saturday Strategy
**Speaks to:** Serial Dieter, Busy Professional, Menopausal Woman

The Challenge: Delaying your first meal until 11am-12pm sounds straightforward until you've woken up at 7am and the hunger hits at 8:30am.

The Strategy: The 4-hour delay works best when you're occupied and hydrated. As soon as you wake up, drink a large glass of water. Keep yourself busy — a shower, getting children ready, getting into work tasks. If hunger arrives early, drink another glass of water and black coffee if you drink it (coffee suppresses hunger without breaking the fast). Most people find the hunger wave passes within 20-30 minutes if they don't engage with it. If your first meal is at 12pm, your eating window naturally closes earlier in the evening — making the 3-hour dinner cutoff easier too.

Why It Works: The morning delay shifts your eating window to allow your body a longer rest from digestion overnight. For the Carisma fasting track, this is the primary lever — it doesn't require extreme restriction, just a consistent shift in timing.

Katya's Note: The delay gets easier by week two. Give it that long before you decide whether it works for you.

### TIMING YOUR MEALS FOR CONSISTENT ENERGY

**Best for:** Tip Tuesday
**Speaks to:** Busy Professional, Menopausal Woman, All

The Challenge: Blood sugar crashes, the 3pm slump, evening hunger that arrives after a light day — these are often timing problems, not willpower problems.

The Strategy: Aim for a meal rhythm with roughly 4-5 hours between meals. For the fasting track: First meal at 11-12pm. Second meal at 3-4pm (lighter — Greek yogurt, eggs, a salad). Dinner at 6-7pm. For the non-fasting track: Breakfast at 8am, lunch at 12-1pm, dinner at 6pm. The spacing matters because it allows a proper hunger and fullness cycle — you arrive at each meal actually hungry rather than eating out of habit or boredom.

Why It Works: Eating on a consistent schedule trains your hunger signals to become predictable. You stop thinking about food between meals because your body knows when food is coming.

Katya's Note: When you stop grazing, you start genuinely enjoying your meals again. That shift is worth the adjustment period.

### THE 3-HOUR DINNER CUTOFF — WHY AND HOW TO MAKE IT EASY

**Best for:** Saturday Strategy
**Speaks to:** Menopausal Woman, Serial Dieter, All

The Challenge: Eating late is a cultural habit in Malta. Dinner at 8pm or later is normal. But for weight management and sleep quality, it works against you.

The Strategy: Aim to finish your last meal at least 3 hours before bed. If you sleep at 11pm, dinner is done by 8pm. If 8pm feels late to cook, consider shifting your day slightly earlier — or making dinner simple enough to eat at 7pm (the one-pan method works perfectly here). If genuine hunger arrives after dinner, a small serving of Greek yogurt or a warm herbal tea is acceptable. What to avoid: grazing on bread, crackers, nuts, or chocolate in front of the TV after dinner. That's rarely hunger — it's a habit.

Why It Works: The body digests and processes food better earlier in the evening. Eating late disrupts sleep quality (food processing raises core body temperature), increases the likelihood of reflux, and gives the body less time in a fasted state overnight.

Katya's Note: This one is non-negotiable if you want the programme to work. Shift dinner earlier by even 30 minutes this week.

### THE RETURN AT THE NEXT MEAL PRACTICE

**Best for:** Tip Tuesday
**Speaks to:** Serial Dieter, All

The Challenge: One off meal turns into an off day, which turns into an off week. The spiral starts with a single moment of thinking "I've already ruined it."

The Strategy: The Carisma method has one recovery rule: return at the next meal. Not tomorrow. Not Monday. The next meal. A lunch that went wrong means a solid dinner. A dinner that went wrong means a solid breakfast (or first meal, if you're on the fasting track). Nothing is ruined. Nothing needs to be compensated for — no extra restriction, no punishment, no extra exercise to "make up for it." Just the next meal, done well.

Why It Works: The spiral is a learned behaviour — and it can be unlearned. When "returning at the next meal" becomes your default, you break the all-or-nothing thinking that has derailed previous attempts. Progress is measured over weeks, not individual meals.

Katya's Note: This is the most important mindset shift in the whole programme. Everything else is secondary.

---

### THE 2-MINUTE RULE FOR NEW HABITS

**Best for:** Tip Tuesday
**Speaks to:** Busy Professional, Young Mum, All

The Challenge: New habits feel like a big commitment. Meal prep, food journaling, morning water — each one sounds like another thing to add to an already full day.

The Strategy: Make the new habit take 2 minutes or attach it to something you already do. Drinking water in the morning: it's a glass of water. It takes 30 seconds. Food journaling: enter it into your phone while your coffee brews. Meal prep: put chicken thighs in the oven while you're already in the kitchen making dinner. The 2-minute rule says: if you can make the habit start in under 2 minutes, you'll almost always follow through. The full behaviour follows the start.

Why It Works: The biggest barrier to a new habit is starting. Not sustaining — starting. Reducing the start cost to near zero makes the habit happen.

Katya's Note: You don't need more motivation. You need fewer friction points.

### USING PHONE ALARMS FOR MEAL TIMING

**Best for:** Tip Tuesday
**Speaks to:** Busy Professional, Young Mum

The Challenge: When you're in back-to-back meetings, looking after children, or absorbed in work, you forget to eat — then you're starving and grab whatever is fast.

The Strategy: Set three recurring alarms in your phone. First meal alarm (11am-12pm if on fasting track, 8am if not). Second meal alarm (3-4pm). Dinner alarm (6-6:30pm). Name them whatever you like — "protein time," "lunch," "dinner soon." When the alarm goes off, you have a 15-minute window to eat. This is not about rigidity — it's about not skipping meals and arriving at dinner desperate.

Why It Works: External cues remove reliance on internal signals that get drowned out by a busy day. The alarm is the trigger, and triggers drive behaviour. After a few weeks, your hunger will sync with the timing and you won't need the alarms anymore.

Katya's Note: Set the alarms tonight. It takes 3 minutes and might be the most useful thing you do this week.

### THE FOOD JOURNAL — WHAT TO TRACK AND WHY IT WORKS

**Best for:** Saturday Strategy
**Speaks to:** Serial Dieter, Menopausal Woman, All

The Challenge: "I'm eating well but nothing is changing." This is one of the most common things I hear. Usually, there's a gap between what people think they're eating and what they're actually eating.

The Strategy: Track for 7 days — not forever, just 7 days. Use the Carisma points system (1 cal = 1 point, vegetables free) and log everything. Not to judge yourself, but to see the data. The goal is awareness. Most people discover 2-3 things: a habitual grazing pattern they didn't notice, a meal that's much higher in points than expected, or that they're not eating enough protein at meals and compensating with snacks later. That data tells you exactly where to put your focus.

Why It Works: What gets measured gets managed. Not because tracking is motivating, but because it makes invisible patterns visible. Once you see the pattern, you can change it.

Katya's Note: Track with curiosity, not judgment. You're a scientist, not a defendant.

### THE "JUST PROTEIN" EMERGENCY DEFAULT

**Best for:** Tip Tuesday
**Speaks to:** All

The Challenge: There are days when you genuinely don't know what to eat, you're tired of making decisions, or you're in a situation where options are limited.

The Strategy: When in doubt, just get protein in. A boiled egg. A tin of tuna. A handful of chicken from the fridge. A Greek yogurt. That's the baseline. Protein satisfies hunger more effectively than anything else, keeps you full longer, and is the building block of every Carisma meal. If you can get protein, you haven't lost the meal. Add vegetables if you have them. Add a carb if you need one. But if the only decision you can make is "protein" — that is enough.

Why It Works: Having a default removes the paralysis that leads to bad choices. "I don't know what to eat" becomes "I eat protein when I don't know what to eat." The habit forms around the decision, not around perfect planning.

Katya's Note: The person who always finds a way to get protein in is the person who makes consistent progress.

## WEEKEND & TRAVEL

### WEEKEND BRUNCH STRATEGY — PROTEIN-FIRST ORDERING

**Best for:** Saturday Strategy
**Speaks to:** Busy Professional, Event-Driven, All

The Challenge: Weekend brunch is a Malta ritual. Eggs Benedict, pastries, toast stacks, bottomless coffee — it's meant to be indulgent. And it can be, mostly.

The Strategy: Order eggs first — any style (scrambled, poached, omelette). Ask for extra eggs if portions are small, or a side of smoked salmon or bacon. Eat the eggs before you touch the bread or toast. If avocado comes with it, eat that too. Then, if you still want the toast, have one slice. Skip pastries as a main — but if you want one alongside eggs, have it after. The structure is: eggs (protein) first, everything else after. Most brunch menus make this easy.

Why It Works: Protein first at brunch means you arrive at the rest of your day satisfied rather than on a carb-and-sugar spike that crashes by early afternoon and sends you reaching for snacks.

Katya's Note: Brunch is one of life's genuine pleasures. The goal is to enjoy it, not endure it.

### STAYING ON TRACK WHILE TRAVELLING

**Best for:** Saturday Strategy
**Speaks to:** Busy Professional, Event-Driven

The Challenge: Airports, hotels, and unfamiliar cities make it nearly impossible to eat the way you normally would at home. The structure collapses and the whole trip can feel like a write-off.

The Strategy: At the airport: a protein bar (check the label — at least 15g protein, under 250 calories), a boiled egg pack if available, or a Greek yogurt from WHSmith. Skip the croissant and the meal deal unless the meal deal has a protein in it. At the hotel: breakfast is usually manageable — eggs, smoked salmon, Greek yogurt if they have it. Lunch and dinner: use the restaurant strategy (protein first, ask for extra salad). Carry protein bars in your bag as insurance. The goal while travelling is not perfect — it's damage limitation and arriving home without feeling like you've undone three weeks of progress.

Why It Works: Travel is a context collapse — all your normal cues and defaults are gone. The strategies that work on the road are pre-made decisions and portable protein.

Katya's Note: Business trips and holidays are part of life. Plan for them instead of hoping for the best.

### THE 80/20 WEEKEND FRAMEWORK

**Best for:** Saturday Strategy
**Speaks to:** Serial Dieter, All

The Challenge: Weekends either become too rigid (miserable, not sustainable) or completely off plan (Monday guilt, starting over). Both extremes are working against you.

The Strategy: Think of your weekend as 80% on-plan, 20% flexible. That means: Saturday breakfast, lunch, and most of dinner are business as usual. Saturday evening — you're at dinner with friends, you enjoy the meal, you have a glass of wine, you don't count every point. Sunday brunch is a treat. Sunday dinner is back to normal. You're not tracking every point across the weekend, but you're also not treating Saturday as the start of a two-day free-for-all. One or two flexible moments within a mostly structured weekend keeps progress moving.

Why It Works: The 80/20 approach prevents the all-or-nothing spiral. It also keeps you social and human. Perfect adherence is not the goal — consistent progress over weeks and months is.

Katya's Note: The weekend is not the enemy. How you think about the weekend is.

### THE MONDAY MORNING RECOVERY ROUTINE

**Best for:** Tip Tuesday (post-weekend)
**Speaks to:** Serial Dieter, All

The Challenge: Monday arrives with guilt, bloating, and the urge to do something drastic to compensate for the weekend.

The Strategy: Do nothing drastic. No extreme restriction. No skipping meals to "make up for it." Monday morning is just a morning. Drink a large glass of water when you wake up. Delay your first meal as usual. Make your first meal protein-rich — eggs, chicken, Greek yogurt. Eat your vegetables. Return to your points tracking. That's it. By Wednesday you'll have forgotten last weekend entirely and by Friday you'll have made real progress. The people who recover fastest from off weekends are the ones who return to normal immediately, without drama.

Why It Works: Guilt-driven restriction on Monday causes rebound hunger and overeating by Tuesday. Normal eating resumed immediately after an off weekend restores routine fastest and avoids the compensatory binge.

Katya's Note: Every Monday is a fresh page. Put the weekend down. Write something new.

### THE 15-MINUTE MEAL LIBRARY (5 DEFAULTS TO KNOW BY HEART)

**Best for:** Saturday Strategy
**Speaks to:** Busy Professional, Young Mum, All

The Challenge: Decision fatigue at mealtimes leads to defaults that aren't on plan. If you don't know what your healthy defaults are, you'll always fall back on your old defaults.

The Strategy: Memorise 5 meals you can make in 15 minutes or less. Suggested defaults: (1) Scrambled eggs with smoked salmon and cherry tomatoes. (2) Tinned tuna on a bed of spinach with olive oil and lemon. (3) Pan-fried chicken thigh with frozen peas and garlic. (4) Mince sauteed with canned tomatoes and spices, eaten as a bowl or with a small amount of rice. (5) Greek yogurt with a scoop of protein powder, berries, and a drizzle of honey. These five cover breakfast, lunch, and dinner situations. When you don't know what to cook, you go to the list.

Why It Works: Default meals work because they're automatic. The goal isn't to eat an exciting variety every day — it's to have reliable options that keep you on track when creativity and energy are low.

Katya's Note: Know your five. Rotate them. Add new ones when you find something that works.

### THE HUNGER SCALE CHECK-IN

**Best for:** Tip Tuesday
**Speaks to:** Menopausal Woman, Serial Dieter, All

The Challenge: Years of dieting often disconnect people from natural hunger and fullness cues. You eat because it's time, or because food is there, or because you're emotional — not because you're genuinely hungry.

The Strategy: Before every meal and midway through, rate your hunger on a simple 1-10 scale. 1 is completely empty and dizzy. 10 is so full it's uncomfortable. The ideal is to start eating at a 3-4 (genuinely hungry, but not desperate) and stop at a 6-7 (satisfied, comfortably full, not stuffed). If you're at a 6 midway through your plate and you're not hungry, you can stop. You don't have to finish it. If you're at a 3 between meals, drink water first and check again in 20 minutes.

Why It Works: Reconnecting to physical hunger reduces eating driven by boredom, habit, or emotion. Over time, the scale becomes internal — you stop needing to rate consciously and start eating intuitively within a healthy range.

Katya's Note: You were born knowing how to do this. Diet culture disconnected you from it. This is how you get it back.
