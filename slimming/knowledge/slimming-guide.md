## THE CARISMA SLIMMING METHOD

### WHEN TO EAT

**Quote: "When the day has rhythm, hunger loses its urgency."**

#### Compendium

**Default:** Delay your first meal until your usual time (for most people, this means starting with lunch).

**Morning tools:** Water, sparkling water, black coffee, or plain tea.

**Rule:** If hunger hits, wait 10 minutes first, then decide.

**Anchor:** Save more of your points for the evening.

### Key Insights

Most plans do not stray apart at breakfast. They fall apart later, when hunger is higher and life is busier. **That is why we build your day around one clear idea: save your points for the hours that matter most.**

This is why our default approach is morning fasting, when safe. Not as punishment, and not as restriction, but as structure. Structure makes the day calmer, reduces decision fatigue, and protects the part of the day where most people struggle.

### Why We Fast in the Morning (When Safe)

Even if fasting is not right for you, this section explains the logic behind the main program.

**1) Mornings are naturally easier.**
For many people, appetite is lower in the morning once the habit is built. Delaying the first meal can feel simpler than managing hunger all day.

**2) Evenings need more room.**
Willpower drops as the day goes on, and most people want more food at night than early in the morning. We shift more points later, where hunger is higher.

**3) Maltese life happens in the evenings.**
Family meals, dinners out, and social plans are part of normal life. Restaurants often mean pasta, pizza, and shared plates. **We do not fight culture — we design around it.**

**4) A clean morning stabilises hunger.**
Calm mornings reduce grazing for many people and set a steadier rhythm for the day.

**Timing matters not because of discipline, but because structure reduces reactive eating.**

### Myth vs Truth

| Myth | Truth |
|------|-------|
| Breakfast "starts" your metabolism. | You burn energy either way. Appetite and total intake matter more. |
| Skipping breakfast always leads to binge eating. | Sometimes. Structure and safety rules prevent it. |
| Fasting means suffering. | If it feels awful or unsafe, we adjust. |
| "I've always eaten breakfast, I can't change." | Most people adapt in 7–14 days if it's safe, with simple supports. |
| "It only counts if I fast for very long." | A consistent, doable window works best. |

### Safety First

Morning fasting is not for everyone. Use the non-fasting option if you:
- Are pregnant or breastfeeding
- Have type 1 diabetes
- Use insulin or certain diabetes medications
- Have a history of disordered eating
- Often feel dizzy, faint, or unwell without breakfast
- Are under 18
- Have been advised not to fast by your doctor

**If you ever feel unwell, eat. Safety always comes first.**

### Making Morning Fasting Easier

**1) Pattern recognition, without judgement.**

Most of us grew up with one rule: wake up, then eat. When you delay breakfast, your brain may say, "this feels wrong." That is just habit, not a problem. You are not skipping food; you are delaying it. For many people, the first few days are the hardest, and then it gets easier.

**2) The two best morning tools.**

**Sparkling water is the biggest help.** It can make the transition easier by reducing urgency around food and creating a feeling of fullness. One glass after waking, one mid-morning, and one when hunger appears is often enough.

**Coffee can also help reduce hunger and improve focus.** Black coffee is best; if you add milk, keep it small, and avoid sugar in the morning as it can trigger more hunger.

**3) The "same time" rule.**

Your body loves rhythm. Pick a time you usually break your fast and aim to keep it consistent most days. This helps your body expect food later and makes fasting easier.

**4) What to do when hunger hits.**

Hunger comes in waves and often passes. Try sparkling water, wait 10 minutes, and move a little, even a few minutes of walking. If you are still hungry, eat a proper meal rather than grazing on snacks.

### Late Dinner or Late-Night Eating

Late dinners are common, especially in Maltese life. They do not mean you have failed. **If you ate late: return to your normal plan the next day.** Keep the morning clean if you feel okay, and break your fast at your usual time if you can.

**The goal is rhythm, not perfection.**

### When to Stop Eating

This is one of the most underestimated parts of when to eat. Eating too close to sleep can keep glucose higher overnight, increase digestion work when your body should be resting, and make sleep lighter and less refreshing. Poor sleep often leads to more hunger and cravings the next day.

**Default rule:** Aim to finish dinner about **3 hours before sleep**, as often as you can.

**Examples:**
- Sleep 22:30 → finish dinner by 19:30
- Sleep 23:30 → finish dinner by 20:30
- Sleep 00:30 → finish dinner by 21:30

If dinner is late, do not panic. Keep it simpler: eat slowly, start with protein, and avoid late-night grazing.

### When to Start Eating

This is the second half of the rhythm. The goal is not to fast perfectly, but to make your day predictable and your evenings easier.

**Default rule (simple and sustainable):** Delay your first meal by **4 hours after waking.**

**Examples:**
- Wake 07:00 → first meal 11:00
- Wake 08:00 → first meal 12:00
- Wake 09:00 → first meal 13:00

Try to keep your first meal time consistent most days. Your body learns rhythm, and rhythm makes hunger calmer.

### Your Two Tracks: Fasting and Non-Fasting

This program offers **two ways to structure your day: the fasting track and the non-fasting track.** Both work and can lead to strong results.

Most clients use the fasting track because it often fits appetite rhythms and evening social life in Malta. Others use the non-fasting track for valid reasons such as pregnancy, breastfeeding, medication needs, medical conditions, or simply feeling unwell without breakfast.

**Both tracks follow the same method:**
- The same daily targets
- The same protein focus
- The same dinner flexibility
- The same return-to-structure rule

**The only thing that changes is timing.**

### How to choose

**Choose the track that matches your body and your life, not the one that sounds more disciplined.**
Your consultant will help you choose the safest and most effective option.

### Why This Works (the Science)

Morning fasting is not a magic trick.

**1) It often lowers total intake without feeling strict.**
Fewer eating hours naturally reduce intake for many people.

**2) It can support steadier insulin patterns.**
Fewer eating events mean fewer insulin peaks for some people.

**3) It reduces the crash that drives cravings.**
A steadier rhythm can reduce urgent hunger later in the day.

**4) It protects your evening points budget.**
Most people struggle in the evening, not the morning. Keeping mornings simple protects that time and leaves more points for the evening hours where social eating typically leads to higher points consumed.

### The Honest Bottom Line

This approach helps many people, but not everyone. Results depend on your body, sleep, stress, activity, and medical picture. **That is why this method is personalised in clinic.**

**Structure matters more than rules. Safety matters more than fasting.**

### Fasting in real life: Maria's week that changed everything

Maria Camilleri didn't think she was the "fasting type." She was 38, lived in Birkirkara, worked an office job in Msida, and ran her life on tight timing. Wake up, get ready, drop the kids, fight traffic, work, groceries, dinner, tidy up, repeat.

Breakfast was her **safety blanket.** Not because she loved it, but because it felt like the rule. It used to be cereal, then sweet coffee, then "something quick" from the bakery because there wasn't time to think. If she was honest, it wasn't really breakfast. **It was a small panic in food form.**

By 10:30am she was hungry again. By noon she was tired. By 4pm she wanted sugar. And by the evening she felt like she had already **used up her control.** The part that hurt most was the evening, because evenings are where Maltese life happens: dinner out, birthdays, family meals, plates that keep refilling, pasta, pizza, bread, potatoes, shared platters.

**Maria didn't want to hide from her life. She wanted a plan that could live inside it.**

Still, when the clinic mentioned morning fasting, her face tightened. "You mean skipping breakfast?" she said, like it was a bad word. Her mind went straight to the worries: *Won't my metabolism slow down? Won't I get dizzy? Won't I binge later? Isn't breakfast the most important meal? And she had one private fear she didn't say out loud: What if I can't do it, and I prove again that I'm the problem?*

Her consultant didn't argue. She simply said, **"We're not removing food. We're delaying the first meal. And we'll make mornings easier, not harder."** A clean morning. A consistent first meal. Dinner with room. And one rule: **return at the next meal.**

Maria tried day one. She made the coffee black and poured sparkling water, cold, with ice. At 9:30am the familiar feeling arrived, not even true hunger, more like habit. She almost stood up. Instead, **sparkling water and ten minutes.** The feeling eased.

Lunch came: **protein first, vegetables, then carbs.** She felt calm, not stuffed. At 4pm there was **no sugar panic.** She still wanted food, but it wasn't urgent. Dinner came and she had **points left.** That night she ate with her family and didn't feel like she was **using up the whole day.**

Day three is when she almost quit. Bad sleep, stress, chaos; hunger felt louder. She opened the cupboard and saw the biscuits and stared at them like they were talking. Then she remembered: **hunger comes in waves.** Sparkling water. Coffee. Ten minutes. The wave softened.

By the end of the week, her evenings had changed, **not because she became disciplined, but because she had room.** On Friday night, her sister wanted pasta. Maria chose pasta too. She ate slowly, started with protein, had a glass of wine, and laughed.

The next morning the scale was up a little. Old Maria would have panicked. This time she **returned to the structure: clean morning, lunch on rhythm, dinner calm.**

After a few weeks, she stopped calling it fasting. She started saying, **"I just eat later."** Her cravings were quieter, her afternoons steadier, and her evenings felt less dangerous. She lost weight, yes, but the bigger change was how food felt in her mind: **less noise, more peace.**

One day she said, **"I thought the problem was me. But it was the structure."** That is the point. **Not perfection. A rhythm you can live with.**

### The One Thing That Makes This Easy: Protein

This is not a rule. It is a reference.

When most of your meals follow this structure, hunger gets quieter, energy stays steadier, and fat loss becomes easier to stick to.

**If people are constantly hungry, the plan will fail.**

**That is why appetite stability is treated as non-negotiable in the Carisma Slimming Method.**

Our three practical tools for appetite stability are:
- **Protein (the main lever)**
- **Sparkling water (for a clean, comfortable morning)**
- **Coffee or tea (to reduce appetite noise and support focus for many people)**

### Protein comes first.

Every main meal is anchored around a strong protein source to support satiety, lean tissue retention, and metabolic health.

**Lunch is intentionally very high in protein** because it prevents the afternoon slide: grazing, sweet cravings, and unplanned snacking. This also supports what you learned in the previous chapter: **when lunch is structured, dinner can stay flexible in real Maltese life.**

To keep decisions simple, we use a visual rule as a default guideline: **one to two palm-sized portions of protein at each main meal.** Protein targets are personalised, but the principle is always the same: **protein first.**

As a general clinical starting range, many adults do well around **1.4 to 2.0 grams of protein per kilogram of target body weight per day,** adjusted based on appetite, body composition, training, age, and medical context. Your clinician or consultant will tailor this for you.

If you have kidney disease, advanced liver disease, are pregnant or breastfeeding, or have been advised to limit protein, your clinician will set your target.

### Points: How We Track Food Without Stress

In Carisma Slimming, we do not track calories. **We track points.**

**Points are simply a way to budget energy without making eating feel like homework.**
Your consultant will give you a daily points target based on your plan and your pace.

### The rule and the one exception

- **Rule:** For most foods, **1 calorie equals 1 point.**
- **Exception:** **Non-starchy vegetables are zero points.**

We treat their calories as "free" because they help you feel full and satisfied, without tightening your day.

**So yes, 1 calorie equals 1 point, but we do not count vegetable calories as points.**

### What counts as points

These foods and extras count toward your daily points target:
- **Protein foods** (meat, fish, eggs, dairy, protein yogurts, protein powders, legumes)
- **Carbohydrates** (bread, rice, pasta, cereals, potatoes, sweets, fruit)
- **Fats** (nuts, seeds, avocado, butter, cheese, oils)
- **Sauces, oils, spreads, dressings**
- **Drinks with sugar or milk** (including "just a little milk")

### What does not count as points

- **Most non-starchy vegetables are zero points.**
  Examples: leafy greens, cucumber, tomato, peppers, mushrooms, broccoli, cauliflower, courgette, aubergine, cabbage.

**This is intentional.**

Vegetables give you:
- **Volume** so your plate looks and feels like "enough"
- **Fibre** to support appetite control and digestion
- **Satisfaction** so the plan feels easier to follow

**Vegetables are your free support tool. Use them to make meals bigger, calmer, and more filling.**

**Quick note:** Starchy vegetables count as points, for example potatoes, corn, peas, and large portions of beans and lentils. When in doubt, treat it as points, or follow your consultant's guide.

### Two quick examples so it's crystal clear

**Example 1: A normal lunch at home**

You have chicken, a big salad, and a simple dressing.
- Chicken counts as points
- Dressing counts as points
- Salad vegetables are zero points

**This is why vegetables are powerful. They make the plate feel bigger without making the plan feel tighter.**

**Example 2: A social dinner in Maltese life**

You are out for pasta. You want to enjoy it without stress. Here is what keeps it calm:
- Start with protein first, even a few bites
- Add vegetables if they are there
- Then enjoy the pasta as your main carb
- Avoid stacking extras mindlessly on top (bread basket, creamy sauces, dessert on autopilot)

**That is the difference between "I ruined it" and "I enjoyed it and stayed on plan."**

### Fibre Floor: The Missing Piece That Makes You Feel "Fed"

**Protein is the anchor. Fibre is what makes meals feel complete.**

Fibre slows things down in the body. It helps food stay satisfying for longer. It also supports digestion and helps reduce the "empty again" feeling that can show up even after a decent meal.

**A simple default for many people is a fibre floor of around 25 to 30 grams per day.**

You do not need to count this perfectly. You just need to build it into your day.

### Two easy ways to hit your fibre floor

**1) Build a vegetable base at lunch and dinner**

Aim for two fists of vegetables at most main meals.
Cooked or raw both count. This is the easiest fibre win.

**2) Use fibre smart carbs a few times per week**

When carbs fit your points, choose options that bring fibre with them, like:
- Beans and lentils
- Chickpeas
- Oats
- Potatoes with the skin
- Whole grains when tolerated

If fibre causes bloating at first, increase it slowly and drink more water. Your body adapts. Your consultant can guide you based on tolerance.

### Liquid Points: The Fastest Way People Go Over Without Noticing

**Liquid points do not feel like food, but they still count.**

They can push you over your daily points target without giving you the fullness you expected. That is why people often feel confused: **"I didn't eat that much,"** but progress slows down.

### Common liquid points that quietly add up

- Sweet coffees, flavoured lattes, syrups
- Juices and smoothies that are not part of the plan
- Soft drinks with sugar
- Alcohol and mixers
- Milk added generously to coffee
- Creamy "healthy" drinks that look light but carry a lot of points

### Simple swaps that keep you on plan

- Water or sparkling water
- Black coffee or plain tea
- Coffee with a measured splash of milk if needed
- Zero-sugar soft drinks occasionally if they help you stay consistent
- If drinking alcohol: keep it simple, avoid sugary mixers, and treat it as part of your dinner points

**This is not about being strict. It is about removing the sneaky things that make you feel like the plan "isn't working" when the plan is actually fine.**

### How to Build a Plate (So It Works Anywhere)

**Recipes are great. Real life is messier. This is the bridge.**

### The Plate Formula (memorise this)

1. **Protein first**
2. **Vegetables next**
3. **Carbs last**
4. **Fats purposeful**

### Protein first

**Default rule:** 1 to 2 palm-sized portions at main meals.

**If you do nothing else, do this: increase protein before you cut food.**

### Vegetables next

**Default rule:** aim for 2 fists of vegetables at most main meals.
Cooked or raw, both work.

### Carbs last

**Carbs are flexible. They just need structure.**

**Simple rule:** choose one main carb source per meal when you include carbs.

### Fats purposeful

Fats add flavour and satisfaction. Keep them intentional, not accidental.

### Clarifier: priority vs eating order

To be clear, the plate formula below is **not the order you must eat foods in.** It is the order we use to **prioritise what to put on your plate,** so the meal works anywhere, even in real Maltese life.

**Think of it as your selection order, not your chewing order.**

In the chapter *Which Order to Eat*, we go deeper into the actual eating sequence (what to start with first, what to leave for later) and why that matters for appetite and blood sugar.
