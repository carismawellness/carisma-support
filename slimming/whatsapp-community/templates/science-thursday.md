# Template: Science Thursday

Theme: Science Thursday
Sent: Every Thursday morning (recommended: 8:00–9:00 AM)
Purpose: Bust a common myth or share a surprising fact about nutrition, metabolism, or weight loss. Make members feel smarter and more in control — not overwhelmed.
Length: Medium — 5–8 lines of body text. Enough to explain nuance, not so much it feels like a lecture.
Tone: Curious, evidence-based, accessible. A trusted expert who loves their subject and wants you to love it too.
Source: knowledge/science-facts.md

LINE 1: Header

LINE 2: Blank line (mandatory)

SECTION A: Hook line (1 line)
State the myth or pose the question. Make them want the answer. The hook should be something they have heard before, believed, or wondered about. It should create a moment of "oh, I've thought that too."

BLANK LINE

What the evidence actually says. Plain language — no jargon without translation. If a number or threshold is relevant, include it. If the truth is more nuanced than "myth busted," honour that nuance briefly.

    [Line 3 — optional: the reason the myth persists (brief)]

Connect the science to their actual day-to-day experience. Make it personal. What does this mean for how they feel, what they eat, how they move, or how they think about their body?

BLANK LINE

SECTION D: Practical Takeaway (1–2 lines)
One thing they can do differently based on this. Or one belief they can release. Either is valid. The takeaway should feel like a small liberation, not a new obligation.

    [Practical takeaway — one action or one belief to let go of]

SIGNATURE (mandatory, exact format)

    Katya

## Character and Length Guidelines

Total body text (excluding header and signature): 8–14 lines including blank lines
One myth or fact only — do not combine two topics in one message
The science should be accurate — verify all facts against knowledge/science-facts.md
Jargon is allowed if immediately translated into plain language on the same line
No study citations needed — but avoid "studies show" without any specificity; say what the finding is instead

## Tone Calibration Notes

This is the most intellectually engaged message of the week. Katya is not dumbing it down — she is translating it. There is a difference. She respects her members' intelligence while making the science genuinely accessible.

She does not:
- Cite studies in a way that feels like a textbook or academic paper
- Use jargon without translating it in the same sentence
- Oversimplify to the point of inaccuracy
- Moralize about what they should have known
- Combine two myths or facts in one message

She does:
- State the myth in the way members actually think about it
- Give the truth with enough nuance to be accurate
- Connect the science back to something real in their life
- End with permission, not obligation

## Avoid These Phrases

"Most people don't know this"
"Here's the shocking truth"
"Science has proven..."
"You've been lied to"
"This is why diets don't work" (too broad)
"Experts say..." (vague)
"Research suggests..." without stating what it actually suggests

## Handling Nuance

Not every myth is completely false. Some are half-truths or context-dependent. When that is the case, honour the nuance:

Instead of: "That's a myth — carbs don't make you fat"
Say: "Carbs alone don't cause weight gain — context matters a great deal. What and when you eat them matters more than the carb itself."

This approach builds genuine trust. Members will feel that Katya is honest with them, not selling a simplified narrative.

## Complete Example

💪 Science Thursday

"Eating late at night makes you gain more weight."

It's one of the most repeated rules in weight loss — and it's more complicated than that.

Your body doesn't have a metabolic cut-off time. A calorie eaten at 9pm is the same calorie eaten at noon, as far as your body's energy balance is concerned. What actually drives late-night weight gain is what tends to happen at night — lower-quality food choices, eating past hunger, stress eating after a long day.

The time isn't the problem. The pattern around it is.

So if you have dinner at 8pm because that's when your family eats — that's fine. What's worth examining is whether you're eating from hunger or habit after that.

With you every step,
Katya
