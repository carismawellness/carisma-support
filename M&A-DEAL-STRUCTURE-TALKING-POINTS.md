## PART 1: OPENING POSITION — HOW TO PRESENT THE RECOMMENDED STRUCTURE

### Your Framing (Verbatim Script)

> "Here's how I'm thinking about this deal. The business today generates roughly EUR 123K EBITDA from the spa operation—that's proven, stable revenue. Market multiples for wellness businesses like this typically range 0.8 to 1.2x EBITDA. That puts fair value somewhere in the EUR 98K to EUR 148K range.
>
> Now, I see additional upside here. The clinic opportunity could add EUR 50K EBITDA on top of that baseline—that's meaningful value creation. But that clinic success isn't guaranteed; it's execution-dependent.
>
> **Here's the structure I'm proposing:**
>
> **EUR 300,000 cash at closing.** That's your money, 100% certain, Day 1. You get your principal back, clean exit.
>
> **Plus, EUR 100,000 earnout if the clinic hits EUR 50K EBITDA in Year 1.** Here's why I'm structuring it this way: If the clinic succeeds—which I'm confident it will—you participate in that success. EUR 100K more. Total deal is EUR 400K.
>
> If for some reason the clinic underperforms, we both take the hit together. But at least we're aligned in wanting it to succeed. That seems like the fairest way to split this opportunity.
>
> **What do you think about that structure?**"

## PART 2: WHY THIS IS BETTER — SUPPORTING ARGUMENTS

### Argument 1: Market Valuation Anchor (Use If Seller Asks "Why EUR 300K?")

> "Let me walk you through the valuation logic. Your spa today generates EUR 123K annual EBITDA. That's cash earnings, verified business performance. Market prices businesses on EBITDA multiples, not on what was historically invested.
>
> At 1.0x EBITDA—which is conservative for a stable spa business—fair value is EUR 123K. I'm offering EUR 300K upfront, which is 2.44x current spa EBITDA alone. That's a premium. Plus, you get EUR 100K more if the clinic succeeds.
>
> So the deal range is EUR 300K to EUR 400K, depending on clinic execution. That's fair market value for what you've built here."

---

### Argument 2: Risk-Sharing Rationale (Use If Seller Asks "Why an Earnout?")

> "An earnout isn't a lowball tactic. It's actually the fairest way to structure this. Here's why:
>
> The spa is proven and stable—EUR 123K EBITDA is locked in. You should get paid for that certainty. That's your EUR 300K upfront.
>
> The clinic is opportunity, not guarantee. You've built the foundation, but I'm taking the execution risk of launching the clinic module and scaling it. If I can hit EUR 50K clinic EBITDA, that's real value creation. You should participate in that success because you laid the groundwork.
>
> If the clinic underperforms—say, only hits EUR 30K EBITDA—neither of us should get rewarded for that. The earnout captures that reality.
>
> So: Certain revenue (spa) = certain payment. Opportunity revenue (clinic) = contingent payment. That's fair risk allocation."

---

### Argument 3: Seller Post-Close Incentive Alignment (Use If You Want Seller Support)

> "Here's the thing about the earnout structure: It means you have EUR 100K at stake based on clinic success. That creates a powerful incentive for both of us to make sure the clinic launches well.
>
> What I'd love from you during transition is support on a few fronts: Introducing me to key customers, helping with staff continuity, maybe some 60-day consulting where I can tap your knowledge if questions come up.
>
> With a time-based earnout or all-cash deal, you're incentivized to minimize post-close involvement. You've already been paid, so you're done. With a performance earnout, you have USD 100K on the line, so you're motivated to see this succeed.
>
> That alignment is valuable for both of us."

---

### Argument 4: Price Comparison Frame (Use If Seller Compares to Other Buyers)

> "If another buyer is offering EUR 350K or EUR 400K all-cash, that's a different structure. Here's the comparison:
>
> **Scenario A (Other buyer, EUR 400K all-cash):**
> - You get EUR 400K upfront
> - That buyer is assuming all clinic execution risk solo
> - In 18 months, if clinic fails, that buyer is underwater on the deal
> - You're not involved post-close, so the clinic success isn't your concern
>
> **Scenario B (Our proposal, EUR 300K + EUR 100K earnout):**
> - You get EUR 300K upfront (certain)
> - Plus EUR 100K if clinic succeeds (which we're confident about)
> - You're aligned with me on clinic success, so you'll help during transition
> - Total proceeds could be EUR 400K, same as other buyer
> - Plus, you have upside participation if clinic exceeds plan
>
> I'm not trying to lowball you. I'm trying to structure this fairly and make sure we're both betting on the same outcome."

## PART 3: HANDLING OBJECTIONS

### OBJECTION 1: "I want all my money at closing. No earnout."

#### Response A (If you're flexible on price):
> "I understand. You want certainty and clean exit. I respect that. Here's what I can do:
>
> If you want all-cash at close, I can move to EUR 350K all-cash. But then I'm taking all clinic execution risk solo, and the earnout goes away. We're paying for the proven spa business (EUR 300K value) plus a EUR 50K premium for the opportunity I see.
>
> At EUR 350K all-cash, you're exiting clean, but you're leaving clinic upside on the table. At EUR 300K + EUR 100K earnout, you keep some skin in the game.
>
> Which matters more to you: Getting maximum upfront cash, or having some participation in the clinic success?"

#### Response B (If you're not flexible):
> "I hear you. EUR 300K at close is your certain cash. That's real money, zero contingency. The EUR 100K earnout is the clincher—it's upside sharing.
>
> Here's my thinking: If I'm confident the clinic will hit EUR 50K EBITDA, why wouldn't you want to participate in that success? That EUR 100K could be yours if the clinic performs.
>
> If you're concerned about whether the clinic will succeed, then we're not aligned on the deal to begin with. But if you believe in the clinic opportunity—which you should, because you built the foundation—then the earnout is free money. You don't have to do anything except support the transition.
>
> So: Do you believe the clinic can hit EUR 50K EBITDA in Year 1?"

---

### OBJECTION 2: "EUR 300K upfront is too low. I'm looking for closer to my original EUR 250K... wait, I mean EUR 250K minimum, plus more."

**Note:** This is seller anchoring confusion. He originally asked EUR 250K, then increased to higher number. Use this to your advantage.

#### Response:
> "Let me clarify. When we started, you mentioned around EUR 250K as a number in your head. I'm offering EUR 300K upfront, which is already higher than that, plus EUR 100K potential earnout on top.
>
> So the total deal range is EUR 300K to EUR 400K. That's a meaningful jump from your original EUR 250K ask.
>
> Now, if you're looking for something higher than EUR 400K total, I need to understand the rationale. Are you pricing based on the spa business alone (which is EUR 123K EBITDA), or are you pricing as if you've already built and proven the clinic?
>
> Because the clinic hasn't been launched yet. You've created the opportunity; I'm taking the execution risk. The EUR 300K reflects the proven spa. The EUR 100K earnout is your share of the clinic upside.
>
> What would move you to say yes?"

---

### OBJECTION 3: "How do I know the clinic will hit EUR 50K EBITDA? That's your projection. What if it doesn't?"

#### Response A (Build confidence):
> "Great question. That's exactly why I'm confident enough to structure an earnout around it. Here's my clinic track record:
>
> [Share relevant experience launching clinic modules at other properties, patient acquisition timelines, revenue ramps, NPS scores, patient lifetime value.]
>
> EUR 50K EBITDA in Year 1 is conservative based on that track record. I'm not making a wild projection; this is based on proven execution from similar launches.
>
> But I hear your concern. That's why I'm not asking you to take risk on this. I'm taking the risk. I'm saying: 'Launch the clinic, hit the targets, you get EUR 100K.' If I can't execute, you don't get the full earnout.
>
> That's my skin in the game. I'm betting my deal math on clinic success, not betting your exit proceeds on it."

#### Response B (If you're less confident):
> "You're right to be skeptical. Clinic launches are execution-dependent. That's exactly why it's an earnout, not a guarantee.
>
> Look, I believe I can hit EUR 50K EBITDA. But I'm also realistic about risks. That's why I've built the EUR 300K upfront as your certainty. You're getting paid for the proven spa business, locked in. The earnout is extra if execution goes well.
>
> If I fall short on the clinic—say, I only hit EUR 30K EBITDA—then we both adjust. You get a reduced earnout, I eat the shortfall. Fair allocation of who controlled what risk."

---

### OBJECTION 4: "I don't trust your accounting. How will the clinic EBITDA be measured?"

#### Response:
> "Smart question. You shouldn't just trust my accounting. Here's how we'll handle this:
>
> **In the LOI, we'll specify:**
> 1. **Definition of Clinic EBITDA** — Revenue minus direct COGS (supplies, commission), not allocated overhead. Clear chart of accounts.
> 2. **Measurement period** — January to December Year 1 (or another agreed 12-month period)
> 3. **Third-party verification** — My accountant AND your accountant review the calculation
> 4. **Dispute resolution** — If we disagree on the number, we hire a neutral auditor to determine it
> 5. **Your right to audit** — You can have your CPA observe the entire measurement process and review supporting documents
> 6. **Payment timing** — EUR 100K paid within 30 days of final certification
>
> I'm not asking you to trust me on faith. I'm asking you to trust a documented process with independent verification. That's standard in any professional acquisition."

---

### OBJECTION 5: "This feels like I'm subsidizing your clinic launch. Why should I take risk on something new?"

#### Response A (Reframe as opportunity, not subsidy):
> "You're not subsidizing the clinic. You're participating in the upside you created. Here's the distinction:
>
> **You subsidize a deal when:** You take financial risk for something someone else controls. Example: If I said 'Give me EUR 100K discount on price, and maybe I'll share clinic profits with you later if I feel like it.'
>
> **You participate in upside when:** You maintain a financial stake in something you helped create, with clear terms and measurement. Example: 'You built the clinic foundation. If it succeeds, you share in that success via earnout tied to clear metrics.'
>
> This is the second one. You're not subsidizing my launch. You're keeping a financial interest in the opportunity you built.
>
> And here's the key: You don't have to do anything for that EUR 100K to be earned except support the transition. Introduce customers, help staff understand the new module, maybe take a few consulting calls. That's not risk on your part; that's light effort.
>
> If the clinic succeeds (and I'm betting it will), you participated in that upside. If it fails, you already got EUR 300K for building the spa. That's the risk split."

#### Response B (Reframe cost of capital):
> "Let me put this another way. EUR 100K earnout at risk is also EUR 100K of capital I'm deploying into the clinic on my dime. Equipment, staff training, systems, marketing ramp.
>
> You could ask for EUR 400K all-cash upfront. But then I'd need to cut EUR 100K from my clinic capex budget. That'd slow launch and reduce success probability.
>
> The earnout structure lets me invest more aggressively in clinic launch because I'm not over-committing upfront cash to your buyout. That capital goes into clinic success, which benefits both of us.
>
> So the earnout isn't a risk you're taking for me. It's capital efficiency that benefits you indirectly through higher clinic success probability."

---

### OBJECTION 6: "What if I need the money sooner? EUR 100K in Month 13 doesn't help me if I need cash in Month 6?"

#### Response:
> "Fair point. If you need cash urgently, we have options:
>
> **Option 1:** Increase upfront cash. EUR 350K upfront instead of EUR 300K. Then earnout drops to EUR 50K (contingent on EUR 60K clinic EBITDA). Your total is still EUR 400K if clinic hits, but more cash is upfront.
>
> **Option 2:** I can arrange a bridge loan for you against the earnout. You don't get cash now, but you get a loan against the expected EUR 100K earnout. Banks typically offer 75-80% of contingent proceeds as bridge financing.
>
> **Option 3:** Bring in an investor. If you need immediate capital, a third-party investor can buy your earnout right (pay you EUR 80K in Month 1 for the right to EUR 100K in Month 13). Not ideal, but it's an option.
>
> Which matters more: Maximum upfront cash, or participation in upside?"

## PART 4: CLOSING FRAMES

### Close 1: Trial Close (Mid-Negotiation)

> "So let me see where we are. You want certainty at close. I want to align on clinic success. It sounds like we both agree the spa business is solid and the clinic is opportunity.
>
> What if we settle on this: EUR 320K cash at close—that's higher than my opening EUR 300K—plus EUR 80K earnout if clinic hits EUR 45K EBITDA. Total could be EUR 400K.
>
> Does that get us closer?"

**Why this works:** Splits the difference without conceding on total deal value. Shows movement. Offers seller more upfront certainty (EUR 320K vs. EUR 300K).

---

### Close 2: Enthusiasm Validation (If Seller is Warm)

> "I can tell you care about this business and you want to see it succeed post-close. That's exactly the kind of alignment I need. Here's what I'm hearing from you: You want certainty at close, but you also want to make sure the clinic is done right.
>
> This earnout structure delivers both. EUR 300K locked in at close. EUR 100K if I execute the clinic successfully. And we're aligned on wanting that success.
>
> I think we have a deal here. Should we shake on EUR 300K plus EUR 100K clinic earnout?"

**Why this works:** Validates his emotions. Shows you've been listening. Proposes closure.

---

### Close 3: Market Reality Anchor (If Seller is Anchored High)

> "Look, I understand you want more. I also understand you want to exit. Let me be straight with you: I could try to get you EUR 400K all-cash. But here's what would happen:
>
> **Option A:** I pay EUR 400K all-cash. I have EUR 100K less capital for clinic launch. Clinic scales slower. Your post-close involvement is minimal (you're already paid, why would you help?). Eighteen months later, I'm struggling with clinic, you're out of the picture, and the business underperforms.
>
> **Option B:** I pay EUR 300K cash + EUR 100K earnout. I have EUR 100K more for clinic capex. I move faster on clinic launch. You're incentivized to support transition. Eighteen months later, clinic is thriving, you got your EUR 100K earnout, and I'm scaling the business.
>
> Which scenario is better for you long-term?"

**Why this works:** Reframes higher price as worse for him (slower clinic growth). Positions earnout as beneficial.

## PART 5: WHAT TO SAY IF SELLER WALKS

### If Seller Says "This Doesn't Work For Me"

> "I understand. This is a big decision. Here's what I want you to know: I believe in this deal and I believe in this business. If your concerns are about the structure—earnout, upfront cash, timeline—I'm open to adjusting. We don't have to end here.
>
> What's the core issue that's making you hesitant? Is it the price, the earnout mechanics, the cash split, something else? Let's see if we can solve it."

**Follow-up if he repeats "No":**

> "Okay, I respect that. I'm confident this is a good opportunity for both of us. If you change your mind, reach out. I'm interested in making this work. But I also respect if you need to explore other options. Best of luck."

**Then don't chase. Wait 5-7 days. Send brief email. If no response in 10 days, move on.**

## PART 6: ADVANCED TACTICS — WHEN SELLER IS PARTICULARLY DIFFICULT

### Tactic 1: Reframe Earnout as "Upside Bonus"

**Don't say:** "The earnout is contingent on clinic success. If you underperform, you don't get it."

**Say:** "The earnout is a bonus pool. If the clinic exceeds EUR 50K EBITDA, you get a EUR 100K bonus for helping lay the foundation."

**Why:** "Bonus" sounds like reward; "contingent" sounds like penalty.

---

### Tactic 2: Use Seller's Own Language

If seller says: "I want to make sure the business is taken care of..."

**Reflect back:** "You want to make sure the business is taken care of. That's why the earnout makes sense. If clinic succeeds, the business thrives, and you participate in that success."

**Why:** You're using his values (stewardship) to justify earnout.

---

### Tactic 3: Appeal to Seller's Rational Self

If seller is being emotional about price:

> "I know this is personal. You built this from nothing. But let's look at it rationally. Your options are:
>
> **A) Liquidate:** Sell equipment for EUR 100-120K, close the business. Done in 60 days.
>
> **B) Stay and operate:** Keep running it yourself. But you'd need to invest time and capital in clinic launch to make it work.
>
> **C) Sell to me:** EUR 300K cash at close (certain). Plus EUR 100K potential earnout if clinic succeeds. Someone else takes the execution risk. You move on.
>
> Which option is best for you? I'd argue C. You get the highest proceeds, the lowest effort, and some upside participation.
>
> What's the obstacle from your end?"

**Why:** Reframes as rational choice, not emotional negotiation.

---

### Tactic 4: Introduce Legitimate Constraints

If seller won't budge from EUR 400K:

> "I appreciate where you're coming from. EUR 400K is not unreasonable. Here's my constraint: I have investors backing this deal. They've approved a EUR 350K commitment maximum. I'm using that to get to EUR 300K upfront + EUR 100K earnout.
>
> If you want all EUR 400K cash, I need to go back to my investors for approval. That'll take 2-3 weeks, and they'll probably say no based on risk metrics.
>
> So realistically, our deal is EUR 300K cash + EUR 100K earnout, or no deal. I can't force my investors to approve EUR 400K all-cash."

**Why:** Creates external legitimacy. Seller can't negotiate around investor constraints.

## PART 7: SCRIPTS FOR SPECIFIC SCENARIOS

### If Seller Mentions Another Buyer

**Seller:** "I have another buyer offering EUR 380K all-cash."

**Response:**

> "That's helpful to know. A few questions: Is that offer in writing? What's their timeline? Do they have financing contingencies? Are they familiar with the business, or still due diligencing?
>
> Because here's my honest take: I'm confident in my EUR 300K + EUR 100K earnout offer. It's real, it's documented, and I can close in 30-45 days. If your other buyer is offering EUR 380K, ask them when they can close and whether that price is conditional on any due diligence findings.
>
> My experience: EUR 380K all-cash offers often have contingencies that drop the price in due diligence. My EUR 300K upfront is non-contingent. The EUR 100K earnout is clean and simple.
>
> You should compare not just price, but certainty and timing. I'm comfortable with your other buyer as backup; I'm just confident my deal is better structured."

**Why:** Doesn't challenge the other buyer. Acknowledges it. Makes you seem more credible and less desperate.

---

### If Seller Asks About Earnout Scenarios

**Seller:** "What if the clinic only hits EUR 40K EBITDA? Do I get anything?"

**Response:**

> "Great question. Yes, you get something. The earnout isn't binary (all-or-nothing). It's tiered based on actual performance.
>
> **Here's the payout scale:**
> - EUR 50K+ EBITDA = EUR 100K earned
> - EUR 45K-49K = EUR 85K earned
> - EUR 40K-44K = EUR 70K earned
> - EUR 35K-39K = EUR 50K earned
> - EUR 30K-34K = EUR 20K earned
> - <EUR 30K = EUR 0K earned
>
> So at EUR 40K clinic EBITDA, you'd get EUR 70K earnout. You're never in a position where you get 'nothing' if there's any clinic performance. You participate proportionally.
>
> Does that make the structure feel fairer?"

---

### If Seller Asks About Using Earnout for Personal Goals

**Seller:** "I want to use the proceeds to retire. Will the earnout come through in time?"

**Response:**

> "Absolutely. The earnout is paid in Month 13, which is roughly 12 months after we close. So if we close in March 2026, you'd have the earnout (if earned) by April 2027. Is that timeline workable for your retirement plans?
>
> If you need cash earlier, we can explore bridge financing against the earnout. You get the cash sooner, and we transfer the earnout right to the lender. It's an extra step, but it's an option.
>
> What's your target timeline for retirement?"

## PART 8: FINAL REMINDERS

### What NOT to Say

❌ **"I don't have much room to move"** — Signals weakness. Seller will push.

❌ **"This is my final offer"** — Creates ultimatum, kills negotiation momentum.

❌ **"I could do all-cash, but..."** — Never hint at flexibility you don't intend to deploy.

❌ **"This is what the accountant recommended"** — Transfers your judgment to third party. Own your logic.

❌ **"You're being unreasonable"** — Personal attack. Kills deal.

❌ **"Trust me on the earnout"** — You don't have trust yet. Use documentation instead.

---

### What TO Say

✓ **"That's a fair point. Let me think about that."** — Buys you time without conceding.

✓ **"Here's my thinking on that..."** — Explains rationale without defensiveness.

✓ **"I respect that perspective. Here's how I see it..."** — Validates his view while presenting yours.

✓ **"Let me understand what matters most to you..."** — Shows curiosity, not attack.

✓ **"We both want this to succeed. How do we get aligned?"** — Refocuses on shared goal.

✓ **"Let me run that by my advisors and get back to you."** — Creates deliberation, not reactive negotiation.

---

### Tone Reminders

- **Confident, not arrogant:** You know your market value. Seller should sense it.
- **Curious, not interrogating:** Ask questions to understand, not to prove him wrong.
- **Patient, not rushed:** Take time to think before responding.
- **Direct, not evasive:** Give straight answers. Vagueness erodes trust.
- **Collaborative, not adversarial:** This is solving a problem together, not winning at his expense.

## SECTION 9: POST-NEGOTIATION — WHAT TO DO AFTER SELLER AGREES

### If Seller Says "Yes, I Can Work With EUR 300K + EUR 100K Earnout"

**Immediate Next Steps:**

1. **Don't show excitement.** Say: "I'm glad we found structure that works. I'm excited to make this work together."

2. **Lock it in writing TODAY.** Say: "Let me send you a one-page term sheet. Just the key terms: EUR 300K at close, EUR 100K earnout if clinic hits EUR 50K EBITDA, 45-day close timeline. Once you sign that, we'll have solicitors draft the full LOI."

3. **Start due diligence immediately.** Say: "My team will send you a due diligence request list today. Nothing invasive—just 12 months P&L, equipment appraisals, lease copy, payroll records. Standard stuff."

4. **Set a second meeting.** Say: "Once I get that documentation and run it through my accountant, I'd like to meet with you again in 7-10 days to review findings and confirm we're ready for LOI."

---

### If Seller Says "I Need to Think About It"

**Don't push. Say:**

> "That's totally fair. This is a big decision. Here's what I'll do: I'll send you a summary email tonight with the key terms and my thinking. You take a few days to let it settle. No pressure. I'll follow up early next week to see if you want to move forward."

**Then:**
- Send brief email same day (not pushy, just recap)
- Wait 5-7 days
- Call once to check in
- If still hesitant, don't pursue further. Let him come back to you.

---

### If Seller Counters With Modified Terms

**Seller:** "I can work with EUR 320K cash + EUR 80K earnout."

**Your response:**

> "That's closer. EUR 320K + EUR 80K is still EUR 400K total, which is what we're aiming for. Let me think about the split. My concern is that EUR 320K upfront is pushing my leverage ratio higher. But I can move there if the earnout trigger is reasonable. If clinic hits EUR 45K EBITDA (instead of EUR 50K), you get the full EUR 80K. Does that work?"

**Why:** You're adjusting the split without changing total value. Shows flexibility, locks in higher upfront, lowers earnout trigger slightly.
