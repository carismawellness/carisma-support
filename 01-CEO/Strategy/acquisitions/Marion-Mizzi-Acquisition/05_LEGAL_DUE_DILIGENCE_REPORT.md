## 1. EXECUTIVE LEGAL SUMMARY

### Overall Legal Risk Assessment: MODERATE

This transaction presents a legally manageable but structurally complex acquisition in a small EU jurisdiction. The deal is not high-risk in the sense of regulatory prohibition or fundamental legal barriers, but it carries meaningful concentration risk under competition law, requires careful structuring to avoid legacy liability exposure, and involves significant intellectual property challenges tied to the personal-name brand of the founder. None of these risks are deal-breakers, but each requires deliberate mitigation.

### Top 3 Legal Risks

1. **Competition Law Concentration (MODERATE-HIGH).** Post-acquisition, Carisma would control an estimated 90-95% of Malta's organized weight management market. While Malta's small market may keep the transaction below mandatory notification thresholds depending on combined turnover, the dominance created across slimming, body contouring, and spa services could attract scrutiny from the Malta Competition and Consumer Affairs Authority (MCCAA) under the Control of Concentrations Regulations (S.L. 379.08). Voluntary notification is strongly recommended to eliminate the risk of post-closing challenge.

2. **Personal-Name Brand Transfer (MODERATE).** The "Marion Mizzi" brand is a personal name. Under Maltese and EU trademark law, personal name marks carry specific transferability complications. If Marion Mizzi (the individual) or her heirs retain the right to use the name commercially, the brand's exclusivity — and therefore a significant portion of the acquisition's goodwill — is at risk. A comprehensive name-rights assignment, restrictive covenant, and transitional licensing arrangement are essential.

3. **Lease Assignment Risk Across Three Locations (MODERATE).** Two of three locations operate inside hotel properties (AX Palace Hotel, Sliema and Maritim Antonine Hotel, Mellieha). Hotel lease assignments require landlord consent, and hotels may use the change-of-control event to renegotiate terms, impose higher rents, or decline assignment entirely. Loss of even one hotel location would materially impact the transaction's value proposition.

### Top 3 Legal Opportunities

1. **Asset Deal Structure Eliminates Legacy Liability.** If structured as an asset purchase (strongly recommended — see Section 2), the buyer acquires only specifically identified assets and avoids all historical liabilities: unknown tax obligations, employment claims, customer disputes, supplier debts, and any pending or threatened litigation. This is the single most important structural decision in the transaction.

2. **Competition Approval as Defensive Moat.** Paradoxically, once MCCAA clears the concentration, the resulting market position becomes virtually unassailable. No future competitor could replicate the combined Carisma + Marion Mizzi infrastructure without building from scratch against a 50-year-old brand and Malta's dominant wellness operator.

3. **TUPE Provisions Enable Selective Workforce Retention.** Malta's Transfer of Undertakings regulations (implementing EU Directive 2001/23/EC) require automatic transfer of employees, but the post-transfer restructuring framework provides lawful pathways to optimize the workforce within 6-12 months, provided proper procedures are followed.

### Recommended Deal Structure

**Asset Purchase Agreement (APA)** — not a share purchase. The buyer should acquire identified tangible assets (equipment, inventory), intangible assets (brand/trademark, client database, proprietary methods, website/digital assets), and assignable contracts (leases, supplier agreements, Biologique Recherche partnership). All historical liabilities remain with the seller entity. The seller should provide comprehensive representations, warranties, and indemnities for pre-closing matters. See Section 2B for detailed analysis.

### B. Share Deal vs Asset Deal — Detailed Legal Analysis

This is the most consequential structural decision in the transaction. The analysis below is Malta-specific.

#### Share Deal (Not Recommended)

In a share deal, Carisma would acquire 100% of the shares in Marion Mizzi Wellbeing Ltd (assuming it is incorporated). The company continues to exist as a going concern, with all its assets, liabilities, contracts, employees, permits, and obligations transferring automatically because ownership of the entity — not the individual assets — changes hands.

**Legal consequences of a share deal:**

*Liability inheritance:* The buyer assumes ALL liabilities of the company — known and unknown, disclosed and undisclosed. This includes:
- Historical tax obligations: Any underpayment of income tax, VAT, social security contributions, or other levies going back to the statute of limitations period (typically 6-8 years in Malta for tax matters under the Income Tax Act, Chapter 123)
- Employment claims: Any unfair dismissal claims, discrimination complaints, unpaid wages, holiday accruals, or social security contribution shortfalls for current and former employees
- Customer claims: Product liability, personal injury from treatments, breach of contract, or consumer protection claims
- Supplier obligations: Outstanding payables, disputed invoices, or contractual commitments
- Regulatory exposures: Non-compliance with health and safety regulations, data protection breaches, licensing deficiencies
- Environmental and premises obligations: Any remediation costs or building code compliance issues
- Pending or threatened litigation: Any legal proceedings, arbitrations, or regulatory investigations

*Contractual continuity:* All contracts remain in place with the existing company. However, many material contracts (leases, supplier agreements, financing arrangements) contain change-of-control provisions that may be triggered even in a share deal — requiring consent, providing termination rights, or allowing renegotiation.

*Tax position transfer:* Any tax losses carried forward by the company may, in theory, survive a share transfer. However, Malta's Commissioner for Revenue may challenge the use of pre-acquisition losses by a new controlling shareholder under anti-avoidance provisions. The buyer inherits any open tax years and the risk of retrospective assessments.

*Due diligence burden:* The due diligence required for a share deal is significantly more extensive than for an asset deal. The buyer must examine every aspect of the company's history, not just the assets it wishes to acquire.

#### Asset Deal (Strongly Recommended)

In an asset deal, Carisma would acquire specifically identified assets from the seller (whether a company, sole trader, or partnership). The seller entity retains all liabilities unless explicitly assumed by the buyer.

**Assets to acquire:**
- Tangible assets: All spa, gym, slimming, and body contouring equipment across all three locations; furniture, fixtures, and fittings; inventory of products and supplies; IT hardware and systems
- Intangible assets: The "Marion Mizzi" and "Marion Mizzi Wellbeing" trademarks and trade names (if registered); the 7-Step Diet intellectual property; proprietary methods, formulations, and protocols; the client database (subject to GDPR compliance — see Section 7); website, domain names, and social media accounts; goodwill
- Contracts (by assignment): Commercial leases for all three locations (subject to landlord consent); Biologique Recherche partnership/distributorship; key supplier contracts; customer program memberships (if any)

**Assets to exclude:**
- All debts and payables
- Any financing obligations (bank loans, equipment finance, overdraft facilities)
- Historical tax liabilities (income tax, VAT, social security)
- Pending or threatened litigation
- Pre-existing employment claims
- Any contracts the buyer does not wish to assume (e.g., unfavorable supplier terms, personal contracts of the owner)
- Personal assets of the owner that may be commingled with business assets

**Malta-specific advantages of an asset deal:**
- Clean acquisition: No historical liability risk
- Cherry-picking: Buyer selects only valuable assets and contracts
- Fresh start: New entity, new compliance baseline, new employment terms (subject to TUPE)
- Capital allowances: Buyer can claim wear and tear allowances on the purchase price allocated to depreciable assets, based on the asset values agreed in the APA, rather than the seller's historical book values
- VAT: The transfer of a going concern (TOGC) is outside the scope of VAT under Maltese VAT law (implementing Article 19 of the EU VAT Directive), meaning no VAT should be charged on the asset transfer if it constitutes a transfer of a totality of assets capable of carrying on an independent economic activity

**Malta-specific disadvantages of an asset deal:**
- Lease assignment requires landlord consent — if any landlord refuses, that location is lost
- Permits and licenses may need to be re-applied for in the buyer's name (not transferable)
- Change-of-control provisions in third-party contracts may be triggered
- Stamp duty: Transfer of immovable property (if any) attracts 5% stamp duty. Transfer of movable property and goodwill may attract stamp duty at 2% on certain instruments. However, the transfer of a going concern structured properly may benefit from exemptions or reduced rates
- Employment: TUPE provisions still apply — employees transfer automatically with their existing terms

**Recommendation:** An asset purchase is the clear optimal structure for this transaction. The target has operated for approximately 50 years, the current owner is disengaged, the financials are unverified, and the operational history is long enough to harbor unknown liabilities. A share purchase would expose Carisma to decades of potential claims that no amount of seller representations and warranties could adequately protect against — particularly given the seller's apparent desire to exit completely. Asset purchase eliminates this entire category of risk.

## 3. COMPETITION LAW ANALYSIS

### A. Malta Competition Authority (MCCAA)

Malta's competition law framework is primarily governed by the Competition Act (Chapter 379 of the Laws of Malta) and the Control of Concentrations Regulations (Subsidiary Legislation 379.08), which implement EU merger control principles at the national level.

**Merger Control Thresholds**

Under S.L. 379.08, a concentration must be notified to the Director General of the MCCAA's Office for Competition if:

- The combined aggregate turnover in Malta of all undertakings concerned exceeds EUR 2,329,373 (this threshold is periodically adjusted); AND
- Each of at least two of the undertakings concerned has a turnover in Malta exceeding EUR 232,937.

Based on available information:
- Carisma Wellness Group: revenue figures not specified in the acquisition memoranda, but as a multi-brand wellness operator in Malta with operations across spa, aesthetics, and slimming verticals, Carisma's Maltese turnover likely exceeds the individual threshold
- Marion Mizzi Wellbeing: estimated revenue of approximately EUR 2M/year
- Combined turnover: likely to exceed the combined threshold

**Assessment:** This transaction will very likely trigger a mandatory notification obligation under S.L. 379.08. Even if the combined turnover narrowly misses the threshold (which must be verified against the most recent threshold figures published in the Government Gazette), a voluntary notification is strongly advisable given the extreme market concentration that will result.

**Post-Acquisition Market Concentration**

The competitive analysis raises significant concerns across multiple relevant markets:

*Weight management / slimming services:*
- Marion Mizzi Wellbeing is Malta's dominant provider, with an estimated 60-70% market share in organized weight management
- Carisma Slimming is the second-largest organized provider
- Combined market share: estimated 90-95% — this constitutes near-monopoly
- Remaining competitors: small independent nutritionists, dietitians, and unorganized providers
- HHI (Herfindahl-Hirschman Index): would far exceed the 2,500 threshold that indicates a highly concentrated market

*Body contouring services:*
- Both entities offer cryolipolysis, radiofrequency, and related treatments
- Combined market share: estimated 70-80% of organized body contouring in Malta
- Competition exists from medical aesthetics clinics, but these are fragmented

*Spa and wellness services:*
- Lower concentration concern — hotel spas, independent spas, and wellness centers provide alternatives
- Combined share likely 15-25% — less problematic
- However, the MCCAA may define a narrower market (e.g., "standalone spa and slimming services" vs. "hotel spa services")

**Risk of Regulatory Challenge**

The risk is MODERATE-HIGH for the slimming segment and MODERATE for body contouring.

The MCCAA's analysis will depend critically on how the relevant market is defined:
- If the market is defined broadly as "wellness services" or "health and fitness," the concentration is diluted by gyms, yoga studios, physiotherapists, and hotel spas — approval is more likely
- If the market is defined narrowly as "organized weight management programs" or "commercial slimming services," the near-monopoly position is problematic
- The MCCAA will also consider barriers to entry: in Malta's small market, the investment, brand recognition, and operational expertise required to launch a competing slimming business are significant barriers

**Potential Remedies**

If the MCCAA raises competition concerns, the following remedies may be considered:

*Structural remedies (divestiture):*
- The MCCAA could require Carisma to divest one of its existing slimming operations or one of the acquired Marion Mizzi locations to a third-party operator
- This would be commercially damaging and should be resisted with market-definition arguments

*Behavioral remedies:*
- Commitments on pricing (e.g., no price increases above CPI for a defined period)
- Commitments to maintain service availability at all current locations
- Commitments not to restrict consumer choice or tie services across brands

**Timeline for MCCAA Review**

The MCCAA typically completes Phase 1 review within 25 working days of notification. If the transaction raises serious competition concerns, a Phase 2 in-depth investigation may be initiated, extending the review period by an additional 90 working days. Total potential review period: 4-6 months.

### B. EU Merger Control

**Does EU Merger Regulation (EUMR) apply?**

Almost certainly not. The EUMR (Council Regulation (EC) No 139/2004) applies only where the undertakings concerned have:
- A combined worldwide turnover exceeding EUR 5 billion, with each of at least two undertakings having EU-wide turnover exceeding EUR 250 million; OR
- Under the alternative thresholds: combined worldwide turnover exceeding EUR 2.5 billion, with each of at least two having EU-wide turnover exceeding EUR 100 million, and combined turnover in at least three Member States exceeding EUR 100 million in each, with individual turnover of at least EUR 25 million in each of those states

This transaction falls far below all EU thresholds. The buyer and target are both Maltese-focused operations with combined revenue well under EUR 10 million. There is no cross-border dimension.

**Small Market Exception:** Malta may, under Article 22 of the EUMR, refer a concentration to the European Commission even if it does not meet EU thresholds, if the concentration affects trade between Member States and threatens to significantly affect competition within Malta. However, this mechanism is rarely used by small Member States for local wellness transactions and is practically irrelevant here.

### C. Strategic Response to Competition Concerns

**How to structure the deal to minimize competition concerns:**

1. **Market definition arguments:** Argue for the broadest reasonable market definition — "health, wellness, and weight management services" rather than "commercial slimming programs." Include gyms, nutritionists, dietitians, online weight management platforms (e.g., Noom, WeightWatchers), GLP-1 prescribers, and unorganized providers in the relevant market.

2. **Counterfactual argument:** Argue that without the acquisition, Marion Mizzi Wellbeing would likely decline and potentially close (disengaged owner, declining performance, aging equipment, no investment). The counterfactual is not "status quo" but "gradual deterioration and eventual exit." The acquisition preserves employment, service availability, and consumer choice relative to the realistic alternative.

3. **Pro-competitive efficiencies:** Investment in modernization (technology, equipment, CRM), expansion of services (GLP-1 management), improved customer experience, and lower prices through operational synergies.

4. **Commitment to maintain the Marion Mizzi brand:** Operating two distinct brands (Carisma Slimming and Marion Mizzi Wellbeing) preserves the appearance and substance of consumer choice, even under common ownership.

5. **Phase the acquisition:** Consider acquiring a majority stake initially (e.g., 70%) with an option to acquire the remainder over 2-3 years, which may reduce the immediate concentration impact and allow the MCCAA to observe competitive behavior.

**Worst case — MCCAA blocks the transaction:**

If the MCCAA prohibits the concentration (unlikely but possible), options include:
- Appeal to the Competition and Consumer Appeals Tribunal
- Offer binding commitments (behavioral remedies) sufficient to address concerns
- Restructure the deal (e.g., exclude the slimming operations and acquire only the spa and body contouring assets)
- Abandon the deal and pursue organic growth in the slimming vertical

## 4. EMPLOYMENT LAW

### A. Transfer of Undertaking (TUPE)

Malta's Transfer of Undertakings regulations are found in the Transfer of Business (Protection of Employment) Regulations, 2003 (S.L. 452.85, as amended), which implement EU Directive 2001/23/EC on the safeguarding of employees' rights in the event of transfers of undertakings.

**Applicability**

The regulations apply to any transfer of an economic entity that retains its identity, meaning an organized grouping of resources that has the objective of pursuing an economic activity. An asset purchase that constitutes a transfer of a going concern — which this transaction clearly does — triggers TUPE protections.

**Key obligations:**

*Automatic transfer of employees:*
- All employees employed by the transferor (Marion Mizzi Wellbeing) at the date of transfer automatically become employees of the transferee (Carisma)
- The transfer covers all employees assigned to the part of the business being transferred — if Carisma acquires all three locations, all staff at all locations transfer
- Employment contracts transfer on their existing terms and conditions — the buyer cannot unilaterally change terms on transfer
- Continuity of employment is preserved — years of service with the transferor count for purposes of notice periods, redundancy entitlements, and unfair dismissal protection

*What transfers:*
- All contractual terms: salary, working hours, holidays, benefits, bonuses, commission structures
- Accrued holiday entitlement (paid or unpaid)
- Seniority and continuous service
- Collective agreements (if any)
- Custom and practice terms (informal but established benefits)

*What does NOT automatically transfer:*
- Occupational pension rights (under certain conditions) — Malta does not have a widespread private occupational pension system, so this is less relevant
- Personal relationships with the old employer — the new employer is not bound by personal arrangements that do not constitute contractual terms

**Can the buyer select which employees to keep?**

Not at the point of transfer. All employees assigned to the transferred undertaking transfer automatically. However:
- Employees who object to the transfer may resign — but this is their choice, not the buyer's
- Post-transfer, the buyer may restructure for genuine economic, technical, or organizational (ETO) reasons that entail changes in the workforce — this is the lawful pathway to workforce optimization (see Section 4C)
- Any dismissal connected to the transfer is automatically unfair unless it is for an ETO reason

**Consultation requirements:**

The transferor (seller) and transferee (buyer) must inform and consult with employee representatives (or the employees directly if no representatives exist) about:
- The fact that a transfer is taking place and the date of transfer
- The reasons for the transfer
- The legal, economic, and social implications for the employees
- Any measures the buyer envisages taking in relation to the employees (restructuring, changes to terms, etc.)
- This consultation must take place "in good time" before the transfer — practically, at least 2-4 weeks prior

**Liability for pre-transfer claims:**

In an asset deal:
- The buyer and seller are jointly and severally liable for pre-transfer employment obligations for a period following the transfer
- The APA should include comprehensive seller indemnities for all pre-transfer employment liabilities
- The buyer should require the seller to warrant that there are no pending employment claims, tribunal proceedings, or threatened disputes
- A retention from the purchase price (e.g., 10-15%) held in escrow for 12 months post-closing is advisable to cover any emerging pre-transfer employment claims

### B. Key Employee Agreements

**Restrictive covenants:**

The buyer must investigate whether any employees (particularly senior therapists, the nutritionist/dietitian, or any manager) are subject to:
- Non-compete clauses that would prevent them from working for a competitor (relevant if any key staff consider leaving rather than transferring)
- Non-solicitation clauses preventing them from taking clients
- Confidentiality agreements protecting proprietary methods, client information, and business practices

In Malta, restrictive covenants are enforceable but subject to reasonableness tests. Courts will not enforce covenants that are excessively broad in scope, duration, or geographic area. A 12-month non-compete limited to Malta's wellness industry would likely be enforceable; a 3-year global restriction would not.

**Outstanding accruals:**

Due diligence must quantify:
- Accrued but untaken holiday entitlement for all staff (this liability transfers to the buyer)
- Any outstanding sick leave or special leave balances
- Commission or bonus payments that are due or accruing
- Social security contribution compliance (National Insurance contributions) — any arrears become the buyer's problem in a share deal but remain with the seller in an asset deal (though joint liability provisions may apply during a transitional period)

### C. Workforce Optimization

**Legal framework for post-acquisition restructuring:**

Malta's Employment and Industrial Relations Act (Chapter 452) and subsidiary legislation provide the following framework:

*Redundancy:*
- After the TUPE-protected transfer, the buyer may implement redundancies for genuine ETO reasons — such as elimination of duplicate roles, operational restructuring, or location consolidation
- Redundancy selection must be based on objective criteria (last-in-first-out, skills assessment, performance) and must not discriminate
- Minimum notice periods under Maltese law: 1 week for each year of service, up to a maximum of 12 weeks (for employees with 8+ years of service). Given Marion Mizzi's 50-year history, long-serving employees may be entitled to the maximum
- Redundancy compensation: Malta does not mandate statutory redundancy payments in the private sector beyond the notice period, but collective agreements or company policy may provide for them
- The employer must notify the Director of Employment and Industrial Relations of collective redundancies (10+ employees in a 30-day period for businesses with 20-99 employees; 10% or 30+ for larger businesses)

*Recommended approach:*

A gradual transition strategy is legally safer and commercially preferable:
1. **Months 1-3 post-closing:** Assess all staff, identify key talent, document performance
2. **Months 3-6:** Implement voluntary separation schemes for surplus staff (offer enhanced terms in exchange for clean exits and mutual releases)
3. **Months 6-12:** If voluntary measures are insufficient, proceed with formal redundancy process for remaining surplus positions, citing genuine ETO reasons (operational restructuring, location rationalization, elimination of duplicate functions)
4. **Ongoing:** Retrain and redeploy suitable staff into Carisma's broader operations

This phased approach minimizes unfair dismissal risk, demonstrates good faith, and allows the buyer to identify valuable employees who may not be immediately apparent.

## 5. INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY

### A. Brand and Trademark

**"Marion Mizzi" and "Marion Mizzi Wellbeing" — Trademark Analysis**

The most significant IP question in this transaction is the status and transferability of the "Marion Mizzi" brand.

**Registration status:**

A search of the Malta Industrial Property Registrations Directorate (within the Commerce Department) and the EUIPO (European Union Intellectual Property Office) database is required to determine:
- Whether "Marion Mizzi" and/or "Marion Mizzi Wellbeing" are registered as trademarks in Malta
- The classes of registration (likely Class 44 — Medical and beauty services; Class 41 — Education/training; Class 5 — Dietary supplements; Class 3 — Cosmetics)
- Whether the marks are in force or have lapsed due to non-renewal or non-use
- Whether any third parties hold competing or confusingly similar marks

**Personal name trademark complications:**

Under both Maltese and EU trademark law, a personal name can function as a trademark, but the registration and transfer of personal name marks raises specific issues:

1. **Right to use one's own name:** Even after transferring the "Marion Mizzi" trademark, the individual Marion Mizzi (or her daughter Petra, if she shares the surname) retains a residual right to use their own name in commerce under the "own name defense" (Article 14(1)(a) of the EU Trade Mark Directive). This means Marion/Petra could potentially open a new business using their own name, provided they do so in accordance with honest practices. This risk must be contractually addressed through a comprehensive non-compete and name-use restriction.

2. **Assignability:** A registered trademark is personal property that can be assigned by written agreement. If the mark is registered, transfer requires:
   - A written assignment agreement
   - Recording the assignment at the Malta Industrial Property Registrations Directorate (and EUIPO if EU-registered)
   - Payment of applicable fees (modest — typically EUR 50-150 per mark per class)

3. **Goodwill transfer:** In Malta, a trademark can be assigned with or without the goodwill of the business. For this acquisition, the trademark should be assigned together with the goodwill to ensure the buyer receives the full commercial value of the brand.

**Critical contractual protections:**

The APA must include:
- Full assignment of all registered and unregistered trademark rights in "Marion Mizzi," "Marion Mizzi Wellbeing," and any variations
- A comprehensive non-compete covenant preventing Marion Mizzi (the individual), Petra, and any family members from using the name in any wellness, health, nutrition, beauty, spa, or related business — for a period of at least 5 years, within Malta and ideally across the EU
- A name-use restriction preventing the seller from authorizing any third party to use the name
- Seller's warranty that no licenses, assignments, or encumbrances exist over the brand
- A transitional licensing arrangement if the buyer intends to phase out the brand over time (operating under "Marion Mizzi Wellbeing by Carisma" or similar during a transition period)

### B. Other Intellectual Property

The 7-Step Diet program is a core asset. Its IP protection status must be assessed:

Copyright in works created by employees in the course of employment vests in the employer. Copyright in works created by the founder would vest in her personally unless assigned to the company.
- **Trademark:** "7-Step Diet" may be registrable as a trademark if it is not merely descriptive. Registration would provide stronger protection than copyright alone.
- **Trade secret:** The specific methodology, dietary protocols, and proprietary formulations may qualify as trade secrets/know-how. The APA should include assignment of all know-how and confidential information related to the program.
- The buyer must obtain a comprehensive assignment of all IP rights in the 7-Step Diet from whichever legal or natural person owns them.

**Biologique Recherche Partnership:**

- Biologique Recherche is a premium French skincare brand distributed through authorized partner spas
- The partnership/distributorship agreement must be reviewed to determine:
  - Whether it is held by the company or personally by Marion/Petra

  - Whether Biologique Recherche has approval rights over new partners

  - Territory exclusivity for Malta (if any)

## 6. LEASE AND PROPERTY LAW

### A. General Malta Lease Law

Commercial leases in Malta are governed by a combination of the Civil Code (Chapter 16 of the Laws of Malta), the Reletting of Urban Property (Regulation) Ordinance (Chapter 69) — though this primarily applies to older leases — and the general law of contract.

**Key principles for commercial lease assignments:**

1. **Assignment requires landlord consent** unless the lease explicitly permits assignment without consent. Most modern commercial leases require prior written consent of the landlord, with the standard formulation being "such consent not to be unreasonably withheld."

2. **"Reasonableness" of withholding consent** is assessed objectively. A landlord cannot refuse consent merely because they prefer a different tenant or want to renegotiate terms. However, legitimate reasons for refusal include: the proposed assignee's financial standing, the nature of the proposed use, references, and the proposed assignee's track record.

3. **Rent review on assignment:** Many commercial leases include rent review provisions triggered by assignment. The landlord may be entitled to adjust the rent to current market rates upon consenting to assignment. This could increase operating costs at any or all locations.

4. **Personal guarantees:** If the original tenant (Marion Mizzi / her company) provided personal guarantees, the landlord will likely require equivalent guarantees from the new tenant. Carisma's corporate standing and hotel industry track record should satisfy this requirement.

5. **Change of use:** If the buyer intends to modify the use of any premises (e.g., adding medical aesthetics, changing the service mix), the lease must be reviewed for permitted use restrictions.

## 7. DATA PROTECTION (GDPR)

### A. Client Data Transfer

The transfer of the Marion Mizzi Wellbeing client database (4,900+ records) to Carisma is subject to the General Data Protection Regulation (EU Regulation 2016/679), directly applicable in Malta, and the Data Protection Act (Chapter 586 of the Laws of Malta).

**Lawful basis for data transfer:**

In an asset deal, the client database is an asset being transferred. The lawful basis for this transfer depends on the specific circumstances:

1. **Legitimate interest (Article 6(1)(f) GDPR):** The buyer's legitimate interest in acquiring and using the client data to continue providing services to existing clients is a recognized lawful basis for data transfers in the context of business acquisitions. A legitimate interest assessment (LIA) must be documented, balancing the buyer's interest against the data subjects' rights and expectations.

2. **Performance of a contract (Article 6(1)(b) GDPR):** For clients with active service agreements or program memberships, the transfer may be necessary for the continued performance of those contracts.

3. **Consent (Article 6(1)(a) GDPR):** Original client consent may not have contemplated a change of operator. If the original privacy notice did not include a provision for data transfer in the event of a business sale, reliance on the original consent is problematic. However, seeking fresh consent from 4,900+ clients is impractical and risks data loss (clients who do not respond).

**Recommended approach:**

- Conduct a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) as required under Article 35 GDPR, given the large-scale processing of client data (including potentially special category data such as health information related to slimming programs, body measurements, and dietary requirements)
- Rely on legitimate interest as the primary lawful basis, supported by a documented LIA
- Issue a transparent notification to all clients informing them of the change of data controller, the identity and contact details of the new controller (Carisma), the purposes of processing, and their rights (including the right to object and the right to erasure)
- Provide an easy opt-out mechanism for clients who do not wish their data to be held by the new operator
- The notification should be sent before or immediately after the transfer — not months later

**Special category data (Article 9 GDPR):**

Marion Mizzi Wellbeing collects health-related data through its slimming programs:
- Body weight, measurements, BMI
- Dietary information, food intolerances, allergies
- Medical history relevant to weight management
- Before/after photographs
- Body contouring treatment records

This data qualifies as special category data (health data) under Article 9 GDPR, requiring explicit consent or another Article 9(2) derogation for processing. The buyer must:
- Verify that the seller obtained valid explicit consent for processing health data
- Ensure that the consent (or alternative lawful basis) covers transfer to a successor operator
- If not, obtain fresh explicit consent from clients for the continued processing of their health data — this is legally mandatory and cannot be avoided through legitimate interest alone

**Data audit checklist:**
- Inventory of all personal data held (categories, volume, storage location, format)
- Review of all privacy notices and consent forms used by Marion Mizzi
- Assessment of data quality and accuracy (4,900+ records accumulated over years — data minimization assessment needed)
- Review of data security measures (encryption, access controls, physical security)
- Assessment of data retention periods (how old is the oldest record? Is retention justifiable?)
- Third-party data processors used by Marion Mizzi (cloud storage, CRM, payment processors)
- Data breach history (any past breaches? Were they reported to the Information and Data Protection Commissioner?)
- Cross-border data transfers (any data stored outside Malta/EU?)

### B. Employee Data

Employee personal data also transfers with the business. This includes:
- Personal details (name, address, contact information, ID numbers)
- Employment records (contracts, performance reviews, disciplinary records)
- Payroll data (salary, bank details, tax information)
- Health data (sick leave records, medical certificates, occupational health assessments) — this is special category data requiring additional safeguards
- Social security contribution records

The buyer must issue a privacy notice to all transferring employees informing them of the new data controller and the purposes of processing.

### C. Practical Steps

1. **Pre-signing:** Conduct a preliminary data audit as part of due diligence to identify data volumes, categories, and compliance gaps
2. **Pre-closing:** Prepare and issue client notification letter/email (in English and Maltese) informing of the impending change of controller
3. **Closing:** Execute a data processing agreement between buyer and seller for the transition period; formally transfer data controller responsibilities
4. **Post-closing (Days 1-30):** Complete DPIA; issue updated privacy notices through all channels; set up opt-out mechanism and process any objections; appoint or confirm Data Protection Officer if required (Article 37 GDPR — mandatory for organizations processing special category data on a large scale)
5. **Post-closing (Days 30-90):** Complete data cleansing; implement Carisma's data security standards; migrate data to Carisma's systems; document all processing activities in the Records of Processing Activities (ROPA)

## 8. REGULATORY AND LICENSING

### A. Wellness and Healthcare Licensing

Malta's regulatory framework for wellness services spans several authorities. The licensing requirements vary by service type:

**Spa treatments (massage, facials, body wraps):**
- Regulated under the Health Care Professions Act (Chapter 464) and subsidiary legislation for certain professions
- Massage therapists are not currently subject to mandatory registration in Malta (unlike physiotherapists, who are registered health professionals), but the regulatory landscape is evolving
- Spa premises must comply with health and safety regulations, including workplace health and safety (Chapter 424 — Occupational Health and Safety Authority Act)
- No specific spa license is required at the national level, but local council permits and planning authority (PA) approvals may be needed for the commercial premises

**Body contouring treatments (cryolipolysis, radiofrequency, ultrasound):**
- Non-invasive body contouring treatments occupy a regulatory gray area in Malta
- These treatments are not classified as medical procedures if they do not involve injections, incisions, or pharmaceutical products
- However, the use of energy-based devices (radiofrequency, ultrasound) may require compliance with medical device regulations
- The operator should ensure that devices carry CE marking and that operators are properly trained as required by the manufacturer
- If treatments are marketed as having "medical" benefits (as opposed to aesthetic/cosmetic), they may trigger regulation under the Medicines Authority (Chapter 458)

**Nutrition counseling:**
- Dietitians and nutritionists are regulated under the Health Care Professions Act (Chapter 464) — specifically the Council for the Professions Complementary to Medicine
- Only registered dietitians may provide certain types of nutritional advice in Malta
- If Marion Mizzi employs or contracts with a registered dietitian, the registration is personal to the individual — it cannot be transferred as a business asset
- The buyer must ensure that any dietitians transferring under TUPE maintain their professional registrations

**Supplement sales:**
- Food supplements are regulated under the Food Safety Act (Chapter 449) and subsidiary legislation implementing EU Regulation (EC) No 178/2002
- The sale of dietary supplements requires compliance with labeling, safety, and marketing regulations
- If Marion Mizzi sells own-brand supplements, the product registrations and safety files must be transferred
- Any health claims on supplements must comply with the EU Health Claims Regulation (EC) No 1924/2006

**Gym operation:**
- Gyms in Malta require commercial premises permits and compliance with health and safety regulations
- No specific gym license at the national level, but local council requirements may apply
- Equipment safety and maintenance obligations under OHSA regulations

**Key question: Are licenses held by the company or by individuals?**

This is critical. If licenses and registrations are held by the company (Marion Mizzi Wellbeing Ltd), they may transfer with a share purchase but NOT with an asset purchase. If held by individuals (e.g., a registered dietitian's personal registration), they do not transfer in either structure — the individual must consent to continue practicing for the new employer.

In an asset deal, the buyer must:
- Identify all licenses and permits held by the seller
- Determine which can be transferred by assignment and which must be re-applied for
- Apply for new licenses in the buyer's name before closing (or as a condition precedent to closing)
- Budget for re-application fees and the timeline involved

### B. Future Licensing — GLP-1 Pivot

The acquisition memorandum identifies a strategic pivot to GLP-1 (Glucagon-Like Peptide-1) management as a key opportunity. The legal requirements for this pivot are significant:

**Medical prescription requirement:**
- GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide/Ozempic/Wegovy, tirzepatide/Mounjaro) are prescription-only medicines in Malta and the EU
- A wellness company cannot prescribe, dispense, or administer these medications without appropriate medical oversight
- A qualified medical practitioner (registered with the Medical Council of Malta under Chapter 31) must be involved in prescribing and monitoring

**Clinic licensing:**
- If the service model involves prescribing medications (even through a partnered doctor), the premises may need to be registered as a medical clinic under the Health Care Services (General) Regulations
- The Superintendent of Public Health has oversight of medical premises standards

**What Carisma CAN legally offer (without medical clinic registration):**
- GLP-1 management support: dietary counseling, exercise programs, lifestyle coaching, and monitoring for clients who are prescribed GLP-1 medications by their own doctors
- Body contouring services for GLP-1 users (addressing the "Ozempic face" and skin laxity concerns)
- Nutritional counseling to complement GLP-1 therapy
- Weight maintenance programs for clients transitioning off GLP-1 medications

**What requires medical licensing:**
- Prescribing GLP-1 medications
- Administering injections
- Conducting medical assessments or blood tests
- Monitoring for adverse effects (this is a medical function)

**Recommended approach:**
- Partner with a licensed medical practitioner or clinic for the prescribing and medical monitoring components
- Maintain the wellness facility as a non-medical support center providing dietary, exercise, and body contouring services
- This hybrid model avoids the complexity of medical clinic registration while capturing the GLP-1 management opportunity
- Ensure clear separation between medical and wellness services in marketing materials and client documentation to avoid regulatory challenge

## 9. TAX ANALYSIS

### A. Transaction Tax

**Asset deal tax implications:**

1. **VAT:** The transfer of a going concern (TOGC) should be outside the scope of VAT in Malta, provided the transfer constitutes the transfer of a totality of assets (or an independent part of an undertaking) and the buyer continues the same economic activity. This treatment is based on Article 19 of the EU VAT Directive (2006/112/EC) as implemented in Malta's VAT Act (Chapter 406). However, this treatment must be confirmed with the VAT Department before closing — incorrect treatment could result in a 18% VAT charge on the total consideration.

2. **Stamp duty:** Under Malta's Duty on Documents and Transfers Act (Chapter 364):
   - Transfer of immovable property (if any real estate is included in the deal): 5% stamp duty on the transfer value. However, if no real estate is being transferred (all locations are leased), this does not apply.
   - Transfer of marketable securities (shares): 2% stamp duty. Not applicable in an asset deal.
   - Assignment of leases: May attract stamp duty depending on the nature and consideration for the assignment. Typically, lease assignments with no separate consideration beyond the overall purchase price do not attract additional duty, but this should be verified.
   - Transfer of goodwill and intangible assets: The duty position on goodwill transfers is nuanced. Under recent practice, goodwill allocated in an APA may not attract separate stamp duty if the overall transaction is structured as a TOGC, but this must be confirmed.

3. **Capital allowances:** The buyer can claim wear and tear allowances (tax depreciation) on the cost allocated to depreciable tangible assets (equipment, furniture, IT systems) in the APA. The allocation of the purchase price between asset categories in the APA therefore has direct tax consequences for the buyer — maximize allocation to depreciable assets and minimize allocation to non-depreciable items (land, goodwill with infinite life).

4. **Goodwill amortization:** Under Malta's Income Tax Act (Chapter 123), goodwill arising from an acquisition is not automatically tax-deductible. However, if the goodwill is classified as a patent, trademark, or similar intellectual property right with a finite useful life, amortization may be deductible. Tax advice on the allocation of the purchase price to specific intangible assets (trademark, know-how, client relationships) is essential to maximize deductibility.

**Share deal tax implications (for reference, though not recommended):**

1. **Stamp duty on share transfer:** 2% of the higher of the consideration paid or the market value of the shares. On a EUR 2M deal, this would be approximately EUR 40,000.

2. **Tax losses:** Pre-acquisition tax losses of the target company may carry forward, but utilization by a new controlling shareholder may be challenged by the Commissioner for Revenue under anti-avoidance rules, particularly if the principal purpose of the acquisition was to obtain tax benefits.

### B. Ongoing Tax Optimization

**Holding company structure:**
- Carisma should consider whether to hold the acquired business through a Maltese holding company
- Malta's full imputation system allows shareholders to claim a tax refund of 6/7ths of the Malta tax paid by a company (effective tax rate of 5% on distributed profits for non-resident shareholders). This is one of Malta's key tax advantages and may benefit Carisma's ultimate shareholders.
- If Carisma Wellness Group has a multi-jurisdictional structure, the holding arrangement should be designed to optimize the flow of dividends, royalties (for brand licensing), and management fees

**Group relief:**
- If Carisma establishes a group structure with multiple Maltese entities, group relief provisions under the Income Tax Act may allow the offsetting of losses in one entity against profits in another (subject to conditions including 51% common ownership and election)

**Transfer pricing:**
- If the acquired business operates as a separate entity within the Carisma group, intercompany transactions (management fees, brand licensing, shared services) must comply with arm's length pricing principles
- Malta's transfer pricing rules (Article 51A of the Income Tax Act, supplemented by Transfer Pricing Rules published in 2022) require documentation supporting the arm's length nature of related-party transactions

### C. Seller's Tax Position

Understanding the seller's tax position is relevant because it affects their willingness to accept certain deal structures:

**Capital gains tax on sale:**
- If the seller is an individual (Petra as sole trader): Capital gains on the sale of business assets are taxable at the final withholding tax rate of 8% on the transfer value (not the gain) for transfers of immovable property, or at normal income tax rates (up to 35%) for other assets. The specific treatment depends on the nature of the assets and whether the sale constitutes a "transfer" for capital gains purposes.
- If the seller is a company: The company would be subject to Malta income tax at 35% on the gain, with potential for a shareholder refund of 6/7ths on distribution of the net proceeds.

**Asset deal vs share deal — seller preference:**
- Sellers often prefer share deals because the entire gain is taxed as a capital gain on the shares (potentially at reduced rates), rather than as a deemed disposal of individual assets (which may attract income tax on revenue-type assets and capital gains tax on capital-type assets)
- The buyer should consider this in negotiations — a seller who strongly prefers a share deal may need to be compensated through a higher price to accept an asset deal, or the buyer may need to provide enhanced tax indemnities

The APA must include comprehensive seller representations and warranties covering:

**Business warranties:**
- The seller has good title to all assets being transferred
- The assets are free from encumbrances, liens, and third-party claims
- The financial information provided is accurate and not misleading
- There has been no material adverse change in the business since the accounts date
- All material contracts are valid, binding, and in full force and effect

- All taxes have been paid in full and all tax returns filed correctly
- The business has complied with all applicable laws and regulations

- All intellectual property is owned by the seller free from encumbrances
- The client database has been maintained in compliance with GDPR
- All equipment is in good working condition and has been properly maintained
- All necessary licenses and permits are in force

**Indemnities:**
- Full indemnity for any pre-closing liabilities not disclosed in the disclosure letter
- Specific tax indemnity for any pre-closing tax liabilities
- Employment indemnity for pre-transfer employment claims
- Environmental indemnity (if applicable to premises)
- IP indemnity for any third-party IP infringement claims arising from pre-closing use

**Warranty and indemnity (W&I) insurance:**

W&I insurance is available in the European market but is unusual for transactions below EUR 10-20 million. For a transaction of this size (EUR 1.5-2.5 million), the cost of a W&I policy (typically 2-4% of the policy limit) may be disproportionate. However:
- A mini W&I policy or a "deal-specific" policy may be available from specialty insurers
- If the seller is unwilling or unable to provide meaningful indemnities (e.g., because Petra is exiting completely and does not wish to retain contingent liability), W&I insurance becomes more attractive as the buyer's only recourse
- Alternative: a retention/escrow mechanism (10-15% of the purchase price held for 12-24 months) is more practical and common in small Maltese transactions

**Caps, baskets, and survival periods:**
- Warranty cap: 100% of the purchase price for title and authority warranties; 50-75% for business warranties
- De minimis threshold (individual claim): EUR 5,000-10,000 (below which individual claims are excluded)
- Basket/deductible: EUR 25,000-50,000 (aggregate threshold before claims can be made)
- Survival period: 24 months for general warranties; 7 years for tax warranties (to cover the statute of limitations); indefinite for title and authority warranties
