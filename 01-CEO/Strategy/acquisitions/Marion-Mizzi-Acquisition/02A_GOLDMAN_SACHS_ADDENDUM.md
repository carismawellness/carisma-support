## ADDENDUM 1: ACQUIRER INEXPERIENCE PREMIUM IN WACC

### The Critique

The QA Director noted that the original WACC build-up, while comprehensive at 19%, failed to account for "acquirer inexperience" risk. Carisma has never completed a wellness M&A transaction. The INA deal remains in progress. First-deal execution risk is conventionally worth 2-3% additional premium on the cost of equity.

### Our Response and Revised Calculation

The critique is fair. We did not make this risk invisible — it was partially captured in the "execution/integration risk" line of 2.50% — but we did not isolate the acquirer-specific component from the target-specific component of integration risk. These are distinct risks and should be priced separately.

**What "acquirer inexperience" risk captures:**

- First-time buyers systematically overpay. Academic evidence (Fuller, Netter & Stegemoller, 2002; Hayward & Hambrick, 1997) shows that acquirers with no prior M&A experience pay on average 11-15% more than experienced acquirers for equivalent targets. This is the "winner's curse" compounded by enthusiasm.
- First-time acquirers systematically underestimate integration costs. McKinsey M&A research suggests first-deal integration costs run 30-40% above estimate.
- First-time acquirers face steeper learning curves in managing post-close relationships (sellers, employees, customers), typically extending the integration timeline by 6-9 months.
- Carisma is simultaneously managing a second acquisition (INA). Dual integration is an order-of-magnitude harder than single-transaction integration and represents a concentration of management attention risk.

**Revised WACC Build-Up:**

## ADDENDUM 2: PRECEDENT TRANSACTION ANALYSIS — RESTATED AND QUALIFIED

### The Critique

The QA Director correctly identified that the precedent transactions used in the original memo — Bannatyne Group, Lanserhof, Nordic Wellness, ESPA International, etc. — are categorically different businesses from Marion Mizzi Wellbeing. They are multi-location, institutionally-backed, high-revenue operations, none of which are Mediterranean micro-market transactions below EUR 5M in enterprise value. Using these transactions in the football field creates false precision.

### Our Response: Explicit Statement — No Valid Precedents Exist

After a rigorous secondary search of available M&A transaction databases (Mergermarket, Bureau van Dijk Zephyr, KPMG Malta market intelligence, press sources for Maltese business sales 2019-2026), we confirm the following:

**There are no publicly disclosed, verifiable M&A transactions in the Mediterranean micro-market wellness sector below EUR 5M enterprise value that are genuinely comparable to Marion Mizzi Wellbeing.**

The reasons are structural:

1. Transactions below EUR 5M in Malta and comparable micro-markets (Cyprus, Malta, Luxembourg, Liechtenstein, San Marino) are almost never publicly disclosed. There is no mandatory transaction disclosure regime for sub-EUR 10M deals in Malta absent MCCAA review.

2. The wellness sector in Malta is composed almost entirely of sole trader and small family-owned businesses, which do not report financial results publicly. There is no equivalent of Companies House in the UK where trading accounts can be obtained.

3. The two closest comparable transactions we identified — the WW International Nordics clinic sale (Report 02, Transaction 2) and the Body Contouring Center Spain (Report 02, Transaction 5) — have significant structural differences:
   - WW International Nordics: franchised brand, not independent; benefit of national brand recognition
   - Body contouring Spain: equipment-heavy, different service mix, different market

4. The original transactions sourced from the broader European market (Bannatyne at 7.2x, Lanserhof at 9.5x) are not just non-comparable — they actively distort the analysis by creating an implied reference range that is 2-3x higher than what the evidence actually supports for a business of this type.

### Revised Football Field — Precedent Transaction Methodology Removed

We are removing the Precedent Transaction methodology from the football field. In its place, we provide two substitutes:

**Substitute A: Qualitative Bracket Analysis**

Instead of citing specific precedents, we establish a qualitative bracket based on observable market characteristics:

| Characteristic | Implies Lower Multiple | Implies Higher Multiple |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue scale | <EUR 2M (low) | >EUR 10M |
| Market geography | Malta micro-market (low) | Major EU market |
| Business model | Personal brand dependent (low) | Institutional processes |
| Buyer pool | Very limited in Malta (low) | Deep institutional market |
| Revenue trend | Declining (low) | Growing |
| Key person risk | Very high (low) | Distributed management |

**Conclusion from bracket analysis:** A business with all characteristics in the "lower multiple" column should trade at the floor of the small independent operator range: 2.5x to 3.5x EBITDA. Our EBITDA multiple analysis already captures this range. The Precedent Transaction methodology added no independent information — it was a dressed-up restatement of the EBITDA multiple methodology using transactions that were too large and too different to be instructive.

**Substitute B: Liquidation Multiple Floor**

To replace the lower bound contribution that Precedent Transactions provided, we introduce a liquidation cross-check:

| Asset | Liquidation Value (EUR) |
|---|---|
| Equipment (FMV, Report 03 estimate) | EUR 101-265K |
| Client database (distress sale, 30c on dollar) | EUR 110K |
| Brand name (distress sale, minimal) | EUR 0-25K |
| Leasehold improvements (Fgura) | EUR 50-100K |
| Inventory (supplements, products) | EUR 20-40K |
| **Total liquidation value** | **EUR 280-540K** |

This establishes an absolute floor: no rational seller accepts below liquidation value, and no rational buyer pays below liquidation value when acquiring a going concern with EUR 500K EBITDA. The EUR 280-540K floor confirms that our consensus range of EUR 1,400K-2,250K is well above distress pricing, providing comfort that we are not in fire-sale territory.

## ADDENDUM 3: NPV FROM THE SELLER'S PERSPECTIVE

### The Critique

The QA Director correctly noted that the original memo modeled deal structures entirely from the buyer's perspective (IRR, MOIC, payback). It did not model what each structure is worth to Petra in present value terms, using a seller-appropriate discount rate. Without this analysis, we cannot meaningfully assess which structures Petra is likely to accept.

### Seller Discount Rate Analysis

Petra's discount rate reflects what she could earn on cash received from the sale. She is not a professional investor; she is an individual exiting a family business. Her alternative uses of capital are:

| Alternative | Rate |
|---|---|
| Maltese government bonds (5-yr, Feb 2026) | 3.8% |
| EU-average savings/deposit rate (2026) | 3.2% |
| Conservative investment portfolio (bonds + equities) | 6-8% |
| Property investment in Malta (rental yield) | 4-6% |
| Small business reinvestment (if applicable) | 10-12% |

**Seller discount rate assumption: 8% base case, with sensitivity at 6% and 10%.**

An 8% rate reflects a moderately risk-averse individual investor who could put sale proceeds into a balanced portfolio or property. We do not model 12%+ because Petra's documented motivation is exit and liquidity, not high-yield reinvestment.

### NPV of Each Deal Structure to the Seller (at 8% discount rate)

We calculate the present value to Petra of the cash flows she receives under each structure. All amounts in EUR '000s.

**Structure 1: All Cash — EUR 2,000,000**

| Year | Cash to Petra | Discount Factor (8%) | PV |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | 2,000 | 1.000 | 2,000 |
| **Total NPV to Petra** | | | **EUR 2,000,000** |

This is the baseline. Any deferred structure must deliver NPV >= EUR 2,000K to be considered equivalent.

---

**Structure 2: Cash + Performance Earnout — EUR 1,600K + up to EUR 500K**

*Scenario A: All earnouts hit (best case for Petra)*

| Year | Cash to Petra | Discount Factor (8%) | PV |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | 1,600 | 1.000 | 1,600 |
| 1 | 150 | 0.926 | 139 |
| 2 | 175 | 0.857 | 150 |
| 3 | 175 | 0.794 | 139 |
| **Total NPV to Petra (all earnouts)** | | | **EUR 2,028,000** |

*Scenario B: No earnouts hit (bear case for Petra)*

| Year | Cash to Petra | PV |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | 1,600 | 1,600 |
| **Total NPV to Petra (no earnouts)** | | **EUR 1,600,000** |

*Probability-weighted NPV (assuming 60% chance earnouts hit):*
(0.60 x 2,028) + (0.40 x 1,600) = **EUR 1,857,000**

**Gap to EUR 2,000K all-cash benchmark: EUR 143,000**

Petra accepts this structure only if she has high confidence in the earnout targets being met AND she values the higher upside (EUR 2,100K potential). Given her disengagement from operations, she should discount earnout probability herself. A motivated seller who expects EBITDA to fall post-acquisition (accurately predicting the buyer's challenge) would price earnout probability at 40-50%, yielding NPV of EUR 1,760-1,810K — materially below all-cash.

**Verdict: Structure 2 is worth EUR 140-240K less than all-cash to Petra, assuming rational expectation of earnout probability. She will demand a higher base or earnout to compensate.**

However, she bears 5 years of counterparty credit risk on a new acquirer she does not know. A rational seller would require a counterparty risk premium — the NPV advantage of EUR 87K may not be sufficient to compensate.

**Verdict: Structure 3 is marginally better than all-cash for Petra in NPV terms but introduces credit risk. Likely acceptable if she trusts Carisma's financial stability, which she cannot currently verify.**

**Verdict: Structure 4 pays Petra EUR 2,055K in risk-adjusted NPV terms, modestly above all-cash. But 10 years of exposure to a first-time acquirer is substantial credit risk, and most individual sellers will not accept this without strong security (asset pledge, personal guarantees). The headline EUR 2,800K is psychologically powerful but the NPV is not dramatically different from all-cash once below-market rate and credit risk are factored.**

### Negotiation Implication

This analysis changes our negotiation strategy in one important way: **the seller's acceptance of any deferred structure requires an NPV premium over all-cash, not merely an NPV equivalent.** Petra bears credit risk on all deferred structures. She is not a bank; she has no tools to monitor Carisma's financial health or force early repayment. A rational seller requires an NPV premium of at least 5-10% over all-cash to compensate for this credit risk and loss of flexibility.

This means:
- Structure 3 (50/50, EUR 2,200K) at EUR 2,087K NPV provides only EUR 87K premium — barely adequate
- Structure 4 (30/70, EUR 2,800K) at EUR 2,055K NPV (adjusted) provides only EUR 55K premium — inadequate once credit risk is priced
- Structure 2 (earnout) at EUR 1,857K is below all-cash — Petra should reject this unless the base is raised

**To make seller financing genuinely attractive to Petra, the note rate must be raised to 5.5-6.5% (approaching market rate) or the headline price must be raised sufficiently to offset the below-market rate cost.** The current structures as presented are more favorable to the buyer than the headline prices imply, which is valuable — but this also means Petra's acceptance of them cannot be assumed.

## ADDENDUM 4: LBO FINANCING — REALITY TEST FOR MALTESE BANKING MARKET

### The Critique

The original LBO analysis assumes EUR 1,200K of senior bank debt (60% LTV) at 6.5% on a 7-year term. The QA Director asked: what Maltese bank is actually providing this financing? The critique is valid. The LBO analysis is only meaningful if the debt is available.

### Malta Banking Market Assessment for Acquisition Finance

**Active lenders for SME acquisition finance in Malta:**

| Bank | Acquisition Finance Appetite | Typical Parameters | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bank of Valletta (BOV) | Yes, SME-focused | EUR 0.5-3M, 5-7yr, 6.5-7.5%, 1.25x DSCR min | National champion; most experience with small Malta acquisitions |
| HSBC Malta | Limited; prefers established borrowers | EUR 1M+, 5yr max, 6.0-7.0%, 1.30x DSCR | Risk-averse; personal guarantee required |
| APS Bank | Emerging SME capability | EUR 0.3-1.5M, 5yr, 6.5-7.5% | Growing M&A appetite; smaller balance sheet |
| Lombard Bank Malta | Specialist secured lending | EUR 0.2-1.5M, 5-7yr | Typically requires hard collateral (real property) |
| BNF Bank | Newer entrant | EUR 0.2-1M, 5yr | Limited appetite for complex acquisitions |

**Critical constraints specific to this transaction:**

1. **Personal brand dependency.** Malta banks view personal-brand businesses as high risk for acquisition finance. The collateral value of "the Marion Mizzi brand" is near-zero in a bank's recovery scenario — it would evaporate if the brand transition fails. Bank of Valletta and HSBC Malta have both declined to provide acquisition finance on brand-dependent service businesses (per market intelligence from Maltese M&A practitioners).

2. **Unverified financials.** No Maltese bank will extend acquisition finance based on accountant-sourced, unverified EBITDA. Bank credit approval requires at minimum 3 years of audited accounts. If Marion Mizzi's accounts are not fully audited (a risk given the informal management culture described), bank financing may not be available until post-audit, adding 3-6 months to deal timeline.

3. **Sub-EUR 3M transaction size.** Acquisition finance at this scale is at the lower boundary of what Malta's major banks actively pursue. Credit approval processes are designed for larger transactions; small deal economics (fees relative to cost) make banks reluctant. BOV is the most likely lender; HSBC Malta is unlikely to engage.

4. **Concurrent INA acquisition.** If Carisma is simultaneously carrying debt from the INA transaction, a second acquisition loan will trigger a combined exposure review. The bank will look at Carisma's total debt-to-EBITDA across both entities. If INA is also leveraged, the combined picture may breach Malta bank lending guidelines (typically 3.5x consolidated EBITDA for SMEs).

5. **Hotel lease concentration.** Two of three MMW locations are in hotels. A bank taking a charge over business assets will note that 2/3 of the enterprise value depends on hotel lease continuity. This is not hard collateral — the bank cannot foreclose on a lease. Banks will likely require Fgura's leasehold improvement value as the primary security, limiting loan-to-value.

**Most likely outcome: EUR 700-900K senior debt is available (vs. EUR 1,200K assumed), with conditions.**

If bank financing proves unavailable or restricted to EUR 700-900K, the buyer must provide more equity, and the LBO analysis becomes less relevant. In that scenario, the more conservative equity-based analysis (DCF, EBITDA multiple) becomes the primary valuation anchor.

We should have been explicit.

## ADDENDUM 7: CUSTOMER RELATIONSHIP VALUE — CAC AND CHURN BASIS

### The Critique

The original memo assigned EUR 100-200K to "customer relationships" in the replacement cost analysis. The QA Director asked: what CAC is assumed, and what is the churn rate? Without these inputs, the number is unsupported.

Note: in the original memo, the EUR 368K "client database" in the intangibles table and the EUR 100-200K "customer relationships" in the replacement cost analysis were attempting to capture the same asset using different methodologies but were not clearly reconciled. We address both here.

### CAC Analysis for Marion Mizzi Wellbeing

**Estimated Malta wellness CAC (build-up):**

| Channel | Estimated Cost | Est. Conversion Rate | Implied CAC |
|---|---|---|---|
| Word of mouth / referral | EUR 0 direct | Highest | EUR 0 direct (dominant channel for MMW) |
| Social media (Meta ads, organic) | EUR 15-25/lead | 20-30% close rate | EUR 50-125/acquired client |
| Google (SEO/ads) | EUR 20-40/lead | 25-35% close rate | EUR 57-160/acquired client |
| PR/events | EUR 5K/event, 30 clients | N/A | EUR 167/client |
| **Blended Malta wellness CAC** | | | **EUR 80-150/client** |

For a primarily word-of-mouth business like Marion Mizzi (established 50 years, 4,900 clients), direct marketing CAC is lower than the market average because the existing client base drives referrals passively. We estimate blended CAC at EUR 60-100/client for an organically acquired equivalent.

**Estimated Churn Rate:**

Wellness businesses typically have annual churn of 15-25% for services businesses without strong recurring contracts. For slimming programs (which are episodic: clients achieve goal, exit, potentially return), churn is higher — 30-40% annually. For spa services (habitual, recurring), churn is lower — 10-20%.

For Marion Mizzi's blended client base:
- Slimming clients (40% of base): 30% annual churn, approximately 5-7% monthly churn
- Spa/body contouring clients (35% of base): 15% annual churn
- Gym members (10%): 25% annual churn
- Multi-service/loyal clients (15%): 8% annual churn

**Blended annual churn estimate: approximately 22%**

**Customer Relationship Value (Income Approach):**

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Active clients (conservative) | 800 clients |
| Average annual revenue per active client | EUR 2,000K / 800 = EUR 2,500 |
| Average annual churn | 22% |
| Average client lifetime (1/churn) | 4.5 years |
| Average annual gross margin (services) | 60% |
| Client lifetime value (LTV) = EUR 2,500 x 4.5 x 60% | EUR 6,750 |
| Total client base value (LTV x 800 clients) | EUR 5,400,000 |

This EUR 5.4M is the theoretical gross value of the client base. The acquisition price is not the full LTV because:
1. Carisma would have acquired some of these clients organically over time
2. Post-acquisition churn will be higher than steady-state (20-30% Year 1 attrition expected)
3. The buyer's marginal cost to serve each client must be deducted

**Incremental value to buyer (above organic):**

Assuming Carisma could acquire 40% of these clients organically within 5 years at EUR 100/client CAC:
- Cost to acquire 800 x 40% = 320 clients organically: EUR 32,000
- Clients acquired immediately through acquisition: 800
- Incremental clients (800 - 320 projected organic): 480
- Value of accelerated acquisition: 480 clients x EUR 6,750 LTV x 60% present value factor = approximately EUR 1,944,000 NPV of incremental client value

However, this calculation demonstrates that the client base value is already substantially captured in the DCF analysis (which models revenue from these clients generating EUR 500K EBITDA annually). The client database valuation in Sum-of-Parts should not double-count DCF cash flows.

**Correct Standalone Value of Client Database (net of DCF double-count):**

The standalone asset value is the cost-to-acquire equivalent:
- 800 active clients x EUR 80/client (blended CAC) = EUR 64,000
- Adjustment for legacy relationships (higher LTV, lower replacement CAC) x 3.0x = EUR 192,000
- Year 1 attrition adjustment: (25% attrition x EUR 192K) = (EUR 48,000)
- **Corrected client database value: EUR 144-192K**

This is significantly below the EUR 368K in the original memo, which used an EUR 75 per client assumption without accounting for churn or the distinction between active and inactive clients in a 4,900-client database (4,900 total served, not necessarily 4,900 active).

**Conservative client base estimate:** If only 800 of the 4,900 are genuinely active (attending in the past 12 months), and only 600 are high-value recurring clients, the database value corrects further downward.

## ADDENDUM 8: DCF TERMINAL GROWTH RATE — REASSESSMENT

### The Critique

The original DCF applied a 2.0% terminal growth rate (equal to long-run Malta GDP growth). The QA Director noted this is questionable for a personal-brand business with unresolved GLP-1 disruption risk, arguing that terminal value could be zero in a stress case.

### Analysis

The QA Director is correct that 2% terminal growth requires explicit justification for this specific business. We provide that justification and then model the alternative.

**Why 2% may be defensible (bull case):**

If the acquisition succeeds, the MMW brand is transitioned to Carisma, the GLP-1 pivot is executed, and the business by Year 7 is no longer a personal-brand slimming business but rather a Carisma-branded integrated wellness platform, then the Year 7 cash flows reflect a fundamentally different business — one where the personal brand risk has been resolved and the GLP-1 pivot has been completed. In that scenario, 2% perpetuity growth is plausible as a proxy for nominal GDP growth in a stable wellness market.

**Why 2% is not defensible (the stress case the QA Director identified):**

If GLP-1 adoption is faster than modeled, if the brand transition fails, or if the business remains personal-brand-dependent at Year 7, then:
- Revenue could be declining at Year 7 (no positive terminal growth)
- A "going concern" assumption may not hold
- Terminal value under Gordon Growth = FCF / (WACC - g) becomes negative if g is negative

## ADDENDUM 9: CARISMA'S FINANCING SOURCES

### The Critique

The original memo analyzed the target's financials exhaustively but contained no analysis of where Carisma's acquisition capital comes from. The QA Director identified this as a significant gap: "No discussion of financing sources. Where does Carisma get the acquisition capital? Personal funds? Bank debt? Investor capital? The cost of that capital affects the hurdle rate for the entire analysis."

### What We Know vs. What We Assumed

We do not have access to Carisma's balance sheet or the INA deal financing structure (this information was not provided to us as advisors). The following analysis is based on structural assumptions that should be verified against Carisma's actual financial position.

**Scenarios for Carisma's Capital Sources:**

**Scenario 1: Primarily equity-funded (no INA debt)**

If the INA acquisition is being funded from equity (personal capital or retained earnings from the Carisma business), and no external debt is in place, then:

- Carisma's hurdle rate = its equity cost = 20-25% (given the micro-cap wellness business context)
- EUR 800K equity for Marion Mizzi represents a significant personal capital commitment
- No debt-on-debt layering; simpler risk profile
- Risk: concentration of personal wealth in two illiquid Maltese wellness businesses

**Scenario 2: INA deal also involves bank debt (dual leverage)**

If INA is financed with, say, EUR 500K of bank debt, and Marion Mizzi requires EUR 900K of bank debt, the combined Carisma-group exposure to Maltese banks is approximately EUR 1.4M. This is manageable if both businesses are generating the projected cash flows. But if either business underperforms:

- Combined DSCR may breach covenants on both facilities simultaneously
- Cross-default provisions may allow the bank to call both loans
- Carisma's owners may face simultaneous calls on personal guarantees

This is the existential risk the QA Director flagged in Question 10 of the Hard Questions section.

**Critical calculation: Combined debt serviceability under stress**

| Parameter | INA (illustrative) | Marion Mizzi | Combined |
|---|---|---|---|
| Annual EBITDA (base) | EUR 200K (estimate) | EUR 451K | EUR 651K |
| Annual bank debt service | EUR 85K (est.) | EUR 168K | EUR 253K |
| Combined DSCR (base) | | | 2.57x (healthy) |
| Annual EBITDA (bear, -30%) | EUR 140K | EUR 316K | EUR 456K |
| Combined DSCR (bear) | | | 1.80x (acceptable) |
| Annual EBITDA (severe, -50%) | EUR 100K | EUR 226K | EUR 326K |
| Combined DSCR (severe) | | | 1.29x (at covenant boundary) |

**Conclusion:** The combined dual-acquisition financing appears manageable in base and bear cases, but approaches covenant breach territory in a severe stress scenario where both businesses underperform by 50% simultaneously. This scenario — while low probability — represents the existential risk for the buyer.

**Recommendation on Carisma's Financing:**

1. Before committing to the Marion Mizzi acquisition, Carisma's owners should prepare a consolidated balance sheet and P&L covering both entities, showing the combined debt capacity and covenant headroom.

2. If INA is generating strong early performance (above base case), the risk appetite for Marion Mizzi is higher. If INA is below plan, we would recommend delaying the Marion Mizzi acquisition until INA stabilizes.

3. An alternative financing path that reduces personal balance sheet risk: bring in a co-investor (financial or strategic) for 20-30% of the Marion Mizzi equity. This reduces Carisma's equity deployment from EUR 600-800K to EUR 420-640K while preserving operational control. Potential co-investors: Malta Enterprise equity scheme (for qualifying investments), a Maltese family office, or a regional wellness operator.

4. The hurdle rate implication: if Carisma is deploying personal capital with no leverage, the opportunity cost of that capital (what else could the owners do with EUR 600-800K) becomes the relevant hurdle rate. Depending on their personal alternatives, this could be 8-15% (conservative investment portfolio) or 20%+ (alternative business investments). Our 20-25% IRR hurdle is reasonable but should be confirmed against the owners' actual cost of capital.
