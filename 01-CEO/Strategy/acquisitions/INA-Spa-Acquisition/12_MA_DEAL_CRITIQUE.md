### 2.2 What Is Right About BAU

**Revenue flat at EUR 430K.** Keeping revenue constant in BAU is the smartest thing in this model. It says: "I am not counting on growth. I am buying what exists." This is extremely defensible in negotiation. If the seller says "but the business is growing," you respond: "That is why I kept revenue flat -- I am not penalising you for growth slowing, but I am also not paying for growth that has not happened."

**Wages at 41%.** Going from 12 to 9 staff reduces wages from EUR 209K to EUR 177K -- a EUR 32K saving. At 41% of revenue, this is still above the 35-38% industry benchmark, which makes it conservative. You are not claiming unrealistic labour efficiency. Good.

**SG&A halved.** From EUR 36K to EUR 19K. This is aggressive but not unreasonable if the EUR 17K cut comes from eliminating duplicate management fees, unnecessary subscriptions, or one-time startup costs that were running through SG&A. You must be able to explain exactly what was cut.

### 2.3 What Is Wrong About BAU

**Rent at EUR 81,500 is higher than INA's EUR 71,689.** This makes no sense on the surface. If revenue is the same, why would rent go up by EUR 10K? The only explanation is the 5% turnover fee calculation. Your model appears to be using a different turnover base or correcting for something. This needs to be clarified because:

- If INA's EUR 71,689 is the actual rent paid (fixed + 5% turnover), then EUR 81,500 implies either the fixed component escalated (plausible -- 3% annual escalation from Year 5 to Year 6/7) or you are applying the 5% fee more aggressively.
- The seller will notice this discrepancy immediately. Have your calculation ready.

**Possible explanation and likely correct one:** The fixed rent has escalated from the figure embedded in INA's actual P&L (which may reflect Year 5 at approximately EUR 63-65K fixed) to the next lease year (approximately EUR 67K fixed). Add the 5% on EUR 430K above the EUR 350K minimum: 5% x EUR 430K = EUR 21.5K. So total rent = EUR 67K + EUR 21.5K = EUR 88.5K. Your EUR 81.5K may actually be conservative if the 5% only applies to the excess above EUR 350K. **Get clarity on whether the 5% is on ALL sales or on the excess above EUR 350K.** This is worth EUR 4-7K per year.

**Depreciation drops from EUR 55,516 to EUR 25,000.** This is correct directionally -- you are depreciating the purchase price (EUR 200-220K) plus your capex, not the seller's original EUR 738K fit-out. But EUR 25K depreciation on a EUR 200K purchase implies a 10-year straight-line schedule for the goodwill/asset allocation, plus minimal capex in BAU. This is reasonable for BAU but needs to be consistent with the asset purchase allocation in the APA.

### 4.4 Legal Risk in Malta

**Low, but not zero.** The key legal distinction under S.L. 452.85 (Malta's TUPE equivalent):

- **Dismissal "by reason of transfer"** = automatically unfair. Penalty: reinstatement or compensation.
- **Dismissal for "economic, technical or organisational reasons"** = potentially fair if proper process is followed.

Cutting 3 of 7 massage therapists because the spa only needs 4 to maintain current revenue IS an ETO reason. The business genuinely has overcapacity. The current utilisation data will prove it. But you must:

1. Wait at least 60 days post-transfer before initiating (to break any causal link to the transfer itself)
2. Document the business case exhaustively (room utilisation rates, therapist productivity data, revenue per therapist)
3. Apply a fair selection process (last in first out, performance metrics, skills assessment)
4. Offer voluntary redundancy first (some may want to leave -- the salaries are near minimum wage)
5. Follow full consultation procedure (individual meetings, appeal process, written notice)

**My practical advice from 200 deals:** In 60-70% of cases, when you buy a small business and announce your growth plans, 1-2 of the weakest staff members self-select out within the first 90 days. They sense the change in expectations and leave voluntarily. Plan for this. If 2 out of 3 leave naturally, you only need to formally restructure 1 role. The cost and risk become trivial.

## 6. DUE DILIGENCE GAP ANALYSIS: WHAT THE MODEL CANNOT TELL YOU

### 6.1 The P&L Annotations -- Your Own Questions Answered

You annotated the model with specific questions. Here is what each one means for the deal:

**Q1: "500k pre VAT?"**

Your model shows EUR 430,000 as Trading Income ex VAT. The actual reported revenue is EUR 426,018. The question is whether there is an additional EUR 70K somewhere -- either unreported income, cash payments not running through the books, or a gross-up for VAT that was incorrectly netted.

**What to do:** Request the VAT returns. Monthly VAT filings in Malta show total taxable supplies (Box 1). Compare VAT return Box 1 totals for 2025 to the reported EUR 426K. If Box 1 shows EUR 500K or higher, revenue is underreported in the P&L by EUR 74K+. This changes everything.

**Why it matters for the deal:** If actual revenue is EUR 500K, not EUR 426K, then:
- EBITDA is EUR 74K higher (EUR 102-110K, not EUR 28-36K)
- The business is worth EUR 400-550K on the same multiples
- Your EUR 200K offer becomes genuinely insulting
- But it also means the seller has been underreporting to avoid the 5% turnover fee, which is a lease violation you can use as leverage

**Q2: "Under reported?"**

This connects to Q1. Underreporting is common in cash-heavy wellness businesses in Malta. Signs to look for:
- Discrepancy between POS/booking system appointment count and bank deposits
- Cash-only treatment options not running through the booking system
- Product sales (retail) not appearing in the P&L
- Tip revenue being blended into treatment revenue
- Gift card redemptions creating timing differences

**Due diligence action:** Demand 24 months of bank statements and POS system transaction logs. Cross-reference monthly totals. If bank deposits consistently exceed reported revenue by 10%+, there is unreported income.

**Negotiation implication:** If you discover underreporting, you have two tactical options:
1. **Use it to reduce the price:** "The financial records are unreliable. We cannot verify the true P&L. This increases our risk and reduces what we can pay."
2. **Use it as leverage:** "We found discrepancies between bank deposits and reported revenue. This suggests the landlord has been underpaid on the 5% turnover fee. That is a lease compliance issue that we would inherit. We need a price reduction to cover the back-payment risk."

Option 2 is more powerful because it ties the underreporting to a concrete financial liability the seller has created.

**Q3: "Rent agreement on net revenue?"**

The lease (Clause 3.1) states: "five per cent (5%) fee calculated on the sales generated." "Sales generated" is ambiguous -- it could mean gross or net. In practice:

- **Gross (total sales before any deductions)** = higher turnover fee. 5% x EUR 430K = EUR 21,500.
- **Net (after VAT, refunds, discounts)** = lower fee. If net is EUR 380K, fee = EUR 19,000.

**What to do:** Ask the seller: "What figure do you declare to the landlord for the 5% calculation? Is it the same as your VAT-exclusive revenue?" Then cross-reference with what the landlord actually receives. If there is a gap, someone is either miscalculating or deliberately underreporting.

**The real question:** Is the EUR 350K minimum in the lease the floor for the 5% calculation (meaning 5% x EUR 350K = EUR 17,500 minimum fee), or is EUR 350K the floor for revenue above which the 5% kicks in? This is worth EUR 4,000/year and must be resolved by reading the lease literally.

**Q4: "Are utilities sub-metered?"**

EUR 14,741 for utilities seems low for a spa with a hammam, sauna, ice bath, HVAC, 7 treatment rooms, laundry, and a hair salon. Industry benchmark for a spa of this size in Malta: EUR 20-30K.

Possible explanations:
- The hotel absorbs some utilities (common in hotel spa concessions) -- this is a hidden benefit
- The spa is sub-metered and pays only for its own consumption -- verifiable
- The seller is allocating utilities incorrectly -- needs verification

**What to do:** Ask: "How are utilities billed? Do you receive a separate utility bill, or does the hotel bill you? Is there a sub-meter?" If the hotel absorbs EUR 10-15K in utilities, that is a hidden benefit that survives only if the hotel continues the arrangement post-acquisition.

**Q5: "What constitutes COGS?"**

COGS at EUR 44,437 (10% of revenue) needs disaggregation:
- Treatment products (oils, lotions, masks, wraps)
- Retail products for resale
- Laundry costs (towels, robes, linens)
- Consumables (disposables, wax strips, razors)
- Laser/T-Shape consumables (tips, cartridges, gel)

**Why it matters:** If COGS includes EUR 15K of retail product purchases but retail revenue is not broken out, the margin on treatments is better than 10%, and the margin on retail may be negative. You need to understand the margin contribution by service line, not in aggregate.

**What to do:** Request a COGS breakdown by category. If the seller cannot provide one, that tells you their cost tracking is inadequate -- another sign of management weakness that supports a lower price.

**Q6: "Marketing staff cost included here?"**

The EUR 25,000 marketing line -- does it include any personnel cost (a marketing coordinator, social media manager, freelance content creator)? Or is it purely media spend and agency fees?

**Why it matters:** If marketing includes a EUR 12K part-time marketing person, then:
- The actual media spend is only EUR 13K (3% of revenue -- very low)
- The marketing person should be in wages, not marketing expense
- This would reduce EBITDA if you reclassify

**What to do:** Ask for the marketing expense breakdown: media spend, agency fees, software/tools, personnel, events, printing, promotions. This also tells you how much of the EUR 25K is productive (generating leads) versus overhead.

**Q7: "2 cleaner's fees included here?"**

Two cleaners are not visible in the staffing model. At minimum wage in Malta (approximately EUR 850/month each), two cleaners cost EUR 20,400/year. If they are:

**This is a EUR 20K line item that could materially change EBITDA.** If cleaners are not in the model and should be, INA's actual EBITDA drops from EUR 28K to EUR 8K. If they are already captured, confirm where.

## 9. REVENUE UNDERREPORTING ANALYSIS

### 9.1 Why I Suspect Underreporting

Three data points create suspicion:

**Data Point 1: The "500k pre VAT?" annotation.**
You wrote this question on the model. That means you have seen or heard something that suggests revenue is closer to EUR 500K. If INA's total collections (before VAT extraction) are EUR 500K, then:
- VAT at 18%: EUR 500K / 1.18 = EUR 423.7K ex VAT. This is almost exactly EUR 426K -- so the EUR 500K figure may simply be the VAT-inclusive number. If so, there is no underreporting. The "500k" is just gross revenue.
- But if the EUR 426K is already net of VAT and there is additional EUR 74K unaccounted for, that is a different story entirely.

**Data Point 2: Cash-heavy business.**
Spas in Malta routinely receive 20-30% of payments in cash. Cash payments are the most commonly underreported income in the wellness sector. If 25% of EUR 426K (EUR 106K) is cash, and even 20% of that (EUR 21K) is not running through the books, there is EUR 21K of unreported revenue.

**Data Point 3: Revenue per appointment is below benchmark.**
At EUR 57 per appointment, INA is 32-42% below comparable Sliema spas. One explanation is underpricing. Another is that some appointments are not being recorded in the revenue figures -- particularly if higher-value treatments (laser at EUR 100-200 per session) are partially settled in cash.

### 9.2 How to Detect Underreporting

| Test | What to Compare | Red Flag |
|------|----------------|----------|
| **Bank deposit test** | Total bank deposits vs reported revenue | Deposits > revenue by 10%+ |
| **VAT return test** | VAT Box 1 (total supplies) x 4 quarters vs annual revenue | VAT returns show higher revenue |
| **POS reconciliation** | Total POS transactions vs reported revenue | POS > revenue (some sales not making it to P&L) |
| **Appointment count test** | Total appointments x average price vs revenue | Calculated revenue > reported revenue |
| **Landlord declaration test** | Revenue declared to landlord for 5% fee vs reported revenue | If different figures are used, deliberate |
| **Cash ratio test** | Cash payments as % of total | If >30%, high underreporting risk |

### 9.3 Negotiation Implications of Underreporting

**If revenue is genuinely EUR 500K+ (not just VAT confusion):**

This changes the entire deal dynamic:
- EBITDA could be EUR 100K+, not EUR 28-36K
- The business is worth EUR 400-500K on standard multiples
- Your EUR 200K offer is no longer defensible
- BUT the seller has been defrauding the landlord on the 5% turnover fee (underpaying by EUR 3,700/year)
- AND potentially underreporting to Malta's Commissioner for Revenue (tax evasion)

**Tactical use:** If you discover underreporting through bank statements, you have two options:
1. **Walk away** -- unreliable financials mean you cannot trust anything
2. **Negotiate harder** -- "The accounts are unreliable. We discovered material discrepancies. We will still proceed but at a lower price to account for the risk of inheriting undisclosed tax liabilities and the cost of implementing proper financial controls."

Option 2 is usually better. You get a lower price AND a more profitable business than the P&L shows.

## 10. THE TURNOVER FEE BOMB

### 10.1 The Problem

This is the single most underappreciated line item in the entire model.

The lease states: "five per cent (5%) fee calculated on the sales generated. The latter fee shall always be calculated on a minimum of three hundred fifty thousand Euro (EUR 350,000) sales or whichever amount is greatest."

Your BAU scenario keeps revenue at EUR 430K. Turnover fee: 5% x EUR 430K = EUR 21,500. Add to fixed rent of ~EUR 65K = total rent ~EUR 86.5K. Close to your EUR 81.5K line. Manageable.

Now look at Base and Bull:

**Your model shows rent at EUR 81,500 across all scenarios.** This is a critical error in Base and Bull.

In the Bull scenario, actual rent should be EUR 133K, not EUR 81.5K. That is EUR 51.5K more than modelled. This reduces Bull EBITDA from EUR 628,387 to approximately EUR 576,887.

### 10.2 The Deeper Problem

The 5% turnover fee means the landlord is a silent equity partner in every revenue growth initiative Carisma executes. For every EUR 100K of new revenue Carisma creates through clinic buildout, slimming services, and repricing, the landlord takes EUR 5,000 -- with zero investment, zero risk, and zero contribution.

- Cumulative turnover fees: EUR 480K+
- Cumulative fixed rent: EUR 750K+
- **Total rent paid to landlord over 10 years: EUR 1.2 million+**

The landlord makes more from this lease than Carisma pays for the business.

### 10.3 Implications for the Deal

**Implication 1: The model must be corrected.**
Base and Bull rent must reflect the actual 5% turnover fee on projected revenue. This is not optional -- it is a structural error that inflates the return profile of the deal.

**Corrected EBITDA:**

Any of these would be worth EUR 20-50K in NPV over the lease term. The landlord may accept because a flat rent provides income certainty, while a turnover fee creates audit burden and volatility.

Model the full 5% on all revenue. Accept it as a cost of occupancy. If the deal does not work with honest rent, the deal does not work.

## 12. FINAL ADVISOR ASSESSMENT

### 12.1 What This Model Gets Right

1. **Four-scenario framework is correct thinking.** Having INA/BAU/Base/Bull gives you internal visibility into the deal's risk-reward spectrum. Just never show Base and Bull externally.

2. **BAU as a flat-revenue restructuring play is the right anchor.** Keeping revenue constant and cutting costs is the most defensible negotiation position. Any competent buyer would do this.

3. **The wage restructuring is grounded in reality.** Going from 49% to 41% by cutting 3 of 7 massage therapists is achievable, legally defensible, and operationally sound.

4. **Base case revenue of EUR 961K is ambitious but not unreasonable.** Adding aesthetics (EUR 35K/month) and slimming (EUR 20K/month) on top of EUR 25K/month spa is plausible in a Sliema seafront location with street-level access and 78% local clientele.

### 12.2 What This Model Gets Wrong

1. **Rent is flat across scenarios.** The 5% turnover fee must scale with revenue. This is a EUR 33-51K error in Base and Bull that inflates returns and understates the landlord's participation in your upside.

2. **Missing cost items.** The P&L does not capture equipment maintenance, payment processing, professional fees, software, and potentially cleaning. These could total EUR 15-40K/year.

3. **Cleaning staff are unaccounted for.** If 2 cleaners at EUR 20K/year are missing, the model's EBITDA is materially overstated.

4. **The INA-to-BAU headcount bridge is unclear.** Going from 12 to 9 means cutting 3, but the model shows 7 massage therapists in INA dropping to 4 in BAU. Is the net 3 cut entirely from massage therapists? What about the potential redeployment to aesthetics or slimming in the Base case? The staffing transitions across scenarios need to be explicit.

5. **Depreciation in BAU is disconnected from reality.** EUR 25K depreciation on what asset base? If Carisma pays EUR 200K for the business and spends EUR 0 on capex in BAU, depreciation should reflect the purchase price allocation over the remaining lease term (~10 years = EUR 20K/year). The EUR 25K figure needs justification.

6. **No working capital or transaction cost modelling.** The P&L shows operating profitability but not the total investment required. Transaction costs (EUR 20-30K), working capital (EUR 30-40K), security deposit (EUR 20K), and risk contingency (EUR 30-50K) are not captured. The real total investment envelope is EUR 450-500K, not the EUR 200-220K purchase price.

### 12.3 The Bottom Line for Monday

This model tells you the business is worth EUR 200-220K if bought at BAU economics. It tells you the business COULD be worth EUR 400K+ if Carisma executes the clinic buildout. The gap between those two numbers is Carisma's opportunity -- and the seller has no claim to it.

On Monday, your job is simple:

1. **Get the data.** Bank statements, VAT returns, employment contracts. Without these, you are negotiating blind.
2. **Ask the P&L questions.** Cleaners, COGS, utilities, marketing. Frame them as "making sure I understand the business," not "finding problems."
3. **Do not discuss price.** Say: "I want to be fair, and fair means being thorough. Let me verify the data and I will put something formal in front of you within a week."
4. **Gauge the seller's emotional state.** Are they tired? Hopeful? Defensive? Angry? This determines whether EUR 200K will be received as a starting point or a final insult.
5. **Ask about the landlord.** This is the real gatekeeper. If the seller has not spoken to the landlord, the deal is further away than you think.

The model is your internal compass. It tells you where the value is, what the risks are, and what price range is defensible. But the model does not close deals. People close deals. On Monday, be the kind of buyer the seller wants to hand their life's work to. Then let the numbers do the rest.
