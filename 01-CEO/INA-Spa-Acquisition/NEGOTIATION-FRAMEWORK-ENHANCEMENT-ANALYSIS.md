### FRAMEWORK 2: Never Split the Difference (Chris Voss)
**Gap:** Insufficient tactical use of **tactical empathy** and **labeling emotions**; missing mirror/label techniques

**Current State in Sheet 6:**
- Scripts are logical/rational ("Let me walk you through...")

- **Missing:** Explicit use of reflection, labeling, mirroring to build rapport and disarm objections

**Why This Matters (INA Context):**
Seller has emotional investment in this business (€1M loss, 20+ years operational). Logical arguments alone won't move him. He needs to feel **heard and understood** before he'll accept lower valuation. Voss's techniques are specifically designed to do this.

**Specific Recommendation (HIGH PRIORITY):**

Add to **Sheet 6, Act 2 (Reframe Value) — new subsection "Emotional Calibration":**

```
ACT 2 REFRAME VALUE — EMOTIONAL CALIBRATION (NEW)

BEFORE you launch into the financial script, use these three Voss techniques:

TECHNIQUE 1: THE MIRROR
After seller makes his case/anchor, repeat his last 3 words back as a question.

Example Flow:
Seller: "I invested €1M in this business. That's what I need to recoup."
You (pause, calm): "€1 million to recoup?"
Seller: (likely elaborates on his story, emotional reality, or constraints)
Why: Mirror buys you time, shows you're listening, and typically makes him elaborate
     in ways that reveal interests. You learn more; he feels heard.

TECHNIQUE 2: LABEL THE EMOTION
Name the emotion you observe. Don't ask permission; state it plainly.

Example Flow (after he's made his point):
You: "It seems like you have a lot invested in this place — not just financially."
Seller: (if accurate) "Yes, exactly. I built this from nothing."
You: "I can see that. That matters. Let me show you how current market reality affects that investment."

Why: Labeling disarms defensiveness. When emotions are named, people stop defending them
     and become more open to new information. You're not attacking his investment; you're
     acknowledging it.

TECHNIQUE 3: THE ACCUSATION AUDIT (Preempt objections before he voices them)
State the worst-case interpretation of your position before he does.

Example:
You: "I know it might sound like I'm lowballing you, but let me explain why market data
      points to this range. You'll probably think I'm discounting your capex—I'm not.
      Here's why that capex and market price are different things."

Why: Removes his ability to blindside you with the objection. It also shows you're
     confident enough to voice the hard thing first. Flips power dynamic.

DEPLOYMENT SEQUENCE:
1. Seller makes opening position/objection
2. You mirror (buy time, show listening)
3. You label emotion (disarm defensiveness)
4. Then deploy your reframe script (now he's ready to hear it)

EXAMPLE FULL SEQUENCE (30 seconds):
Seller: "I need €250K. I've invested too much not to get that back."
You: "€250K." [pause, mirror]
Seller: "Yes. This equipment alone is worth €200K."
You: "It sounds like the investment you made here is really important to you—both
      financially and personally."
Seller: "Of course it is. I built this."
You: "I understand that. And that's exactly why I want to show you something. Market
      buyers price based on what the business earns today, not what was spent to build it.
      That's not me being cheap; that's how acquisitions work. Let me walk you through..."
      [NOW deploy Act 2 P&L walkthrough]
```

**Tactical Deployment:**
- Memorize the three techniques (don't read them in meeting)
- Practice mirror on a colleague 2-3 times before the meeting
- Predetermine which emotions you'll label (frustration, pride, anxiety about the lease)
- Use accusation audit only if you anticipate that specific objection

**Language to Add to Sheet 6:**
> "Voss's research shows logical arguments move people 10% of the time; emotional calibration moves them 90% of the time. Your job is to make the seller feel heard first, then introduce the financial reality. Mirror, label, then explain."

### FRAMEWORK 3: Crucial Conversations
**Gap:** Insufficient guidance on **high-stakes moments** (disagreement spiral); missing "STATE your path" structure for re-framing without triggering defensiveness

**Current State in Sheet 6:**
- Response playbook for objections (good)
- Power phrases (good)
- **Missing:** Explicit structure for when seller becomes emotional, defensive, or silent (the silent treatment is most dangerous)

**Why This Matters (INA Context):**
At some point in the negotiation—likely in Act 3 when you present the hard numbers—seller will become defensive, silent, or emotional. Without a framework for this, you'll either:
1. Retreat and concede (losing leverage)
2. Push back (triggering defensiveness spiral)
3. Go silent (he interprets as weakness)

Crucial Conversations' **STATE your path** method prevents this.

**Specific Recommendation (MEDIUM-HIGH PRIORITY):**

Add to **Sheet 6, Act 3 (The Offer) — new subsection "If Conversation Breaks Down":**

```
ACT 3 THE OFFER — HIGH-STAKES MOMENT PROTOCOL (NEW)

IF conversation deteriorates (he gets defensive, angry, or silent), STOP the negotiation
and re-establish dialogue using STATE your path.

WHEN DO YOU KNOW IT'S BROKEN:
- Seller becomes visibly upset or raises voice
- Long silence after your offer (more than 10 seconds; he's shutting down)
- He repeats the same objection 2x (not listening anymore, just defending)
- He says "I don't think we can do business" or similar exit statement

YOUR PROTOCOL:

Step 1: STOP THE PITCH
Don't keep talking. Long monologues trigger more defensiveness.
Say: "Let me pause here. I notice we might be talking past each other."

Step 2: STATE YOUR PATH (Crucial Conversations Framework)
Tell the story of how you arrived at your position. This is different from defending it.
Structure:
  - SHARE your intent (I'm not trying to lowball you)
  - STATE the facts as you see them (€30K revenue, €123K EBITDA based on...)
  - ASK his view (What's your take on these numbers?)

Example Full Statement (90 seconds max):
"Let me be direct about where I'm coming from. I came here because I believe in this business.
But I also have a responsibility to my investors and my team. So when I evaluate an acquisition,
I have to use market standards for valuation. That means looking at current earnings, not historical capex.
The reason I opened at €45K is that's equipment value in a fire sale. You asked €250K.
I moved to €120K because that's 1x current EBITDA—actually paying a premium for your business.
I'm not trying to steal this from you. I'm trying to explain my math. What's your thinking on the valuation approach?"

Why This Works:
- Transparency builds trust (especially if you've been opaque)
- Asking his view opens dialogue instead of defending yours
- You've positioned yourself as reasonable, not cutthroat

Step 3: LISTEN (Voss principle, now in Crucial context)
After you STATE your path, you SHUT UP.
If he's still defensive:
  - Mirror his objection ("So you're saying my numbers are wrong?")
  - Label his emotion ("It sounds frustrating that we value this differently")
  - Ask again ("Help me understand your valuation method")

Step 4: RESET THE FRAME (if dialogue resumes)
Once he's talking again, don't go back to your previous tactic.
New frame: "I think we both agree the business is valuable. We just disagree on price.
            How would you want to structure this so we both feel good about it?"
This shifts from positional to collaborative.

WHEN TO DEPLOY THIS:
- If you sense he's about to walk out
- If he becomes visibly emotional after your offer
- If the conversation has reached an impasse (same points recycled)

DO NOT USE IF:
- He's just thinking (silence is OK for 10-20 seconds)
- He's asking clarifying questions (he's engaged)
- He's just anchoring high (normal negotiation, not a crisis)

RED LINE MOMENT:
If after STATE your path he still says "I can't do business with you" or walks out,
do NOT chase him or backpedal. Let him leave. Wait 24 hours. Send the email from
Sheet 8 protocol. Give him space to reconsider.
```

**Tactical Deployment:**
- Print the STATE your path structure on back of Sheet 7
- Practice saying it aloud once (you'll sound natural if you've spoken it)
- Identify in advance which emotions might trigger (arrogance, defensiveness, panic about money)
- Use this as a LAST RESORT, not default (it signals you're aware the negotiation is at risk)

**Language to Add to Sheet 6:**
> "The moment when negotiation breaks down is where most deals die. This protocol is your lifeline. It re-establishes dialogue when positions have hardened. Use it once, with genuine transparency, or walk away with dignity."

```
NEGOTIATION & KEY NOTES — SELLER BATNA ANALYSIS (NEW)

Your goal: Understand what seller will do if he doesn't sell to you. This determines

LIKELY BATNA SCENARIOS FOR INA SELLER:

Scenario 1: Hold and Operate Longer

  Duration: 2-3 more years (then he'll age out / get tired)

  Why he might choose: Hope for better price

- Has he mentioned other interested buyers? → Scenario 4 is his anchor
- Does he seem tired of running operations? → Scenario 1 is real
- Has he hinted at "I could just shut this down"? → Scenario 3 is in his mind

"I know you have options. What I bring is certainty and speed. That's worth something."

RED FLAG:
If seller says "I have another buyer at €250K", PAUSE.
Ask: "Who are they? What's their timeline?"
If vague: "Can you introduce me? I'd like to understand the market."
This does two things:
  1. Tests if claim is real (most competitors won't interview)
  2. Shows you're not threatened (confident in your offer)

If claim is real: Adjust your approach. You may need to move to €150K or walk.
But verify first.
```

### FRAMEWORK 5: Behavioral Economics in Negotiations
**Gap:** Missing explicit use of **anchoring bias, loss aversion, sunk cost trap, and social proof**

**Current State in Sheet 6:**
- Ackermann sequence (good anchoring strategy)
- Power phrases (some psychological appeal)
- **Missing:** Deeper understanding of how cognitive biases operate in THIS specific negotiation and how to exploit them (ethically)

**Why This Matters (INA Context):**
Seller is likely victim of several biases:
- **Sunk Cost Trap:** €1M spent makes him anchor high (irrelevant to current valuation)
- **Loss Aversion:** More motivated to avoid loss than capture gain (exploit: frame as "saving" the business vs. liquidating)
- **Anchoring:** First number in negotiation shapes final price (your €45K anchor matters)
- **Availability Heuristic:** He remembers his own purchase price (€1M) more than market comps (you supply comps)

**Specific Recommendation (MEDIUM PRIORITY):**

Add to **Sheet 6, Act 2 (Reframe Value) — new subsection "Bias Exploitation":**

```
ACT 2 REFRAME VALUE — BEHAVIORAL ECONOMICS (NEW)

Seller's brain is working against him. These biases lead him to anchor too high.
You can use them constructively to help him see reality.

BIAS 1: SUNK COST FALLACY
Definition: He anchors to €1M invested because "I spent it" (irrelevant).
Reality: Markets price what business EARNS, not what was spent building it.

Script to Redirect Sunk Cost:
"I get it. You invested a lot. That investment is real. But here's the thing—that
€1M is already spent. It's gone. The question now is: what does the business earn
today? That's what buyers pay for. You could liquidate the equipment for €100K.
I'm offering €120K plus I take the lease and liabilities. Compared to liquidation,
that's €20K better, and you keep reputation intact."

Why It Works:
- Validates his emotion (acknowledges the loss)
- Explains market logic (not personal)
- Offers him a better outcome than his BATNA (liquidation)
- Frames deal as gain relative to next-best option

WHEN TO DEPLOY:
Use this after he anchors high on the €1M justification.
Word-for-word or adapt, but hit these three points: validate, explain, contrast.

---

BIAS 2: LOSS AVERSION (Most Powerful)
Definition: People are ~2x more motivated to avoid loss than capture equivalent gain.
Reality: Frame your offer as "saving the business" not "buying the business."

Script to Reframe as Loss Prevention:
"Here's what I see happening. This business is yours; you built it. Right now, you're
bleeding money every month running this alone. That's a loss. What I'm offering is to
stop that loss and give you certainty. €120K now, and you're done. No more monthly
stress, no more wondering if it'll survive another season. You preserve the business's
reputation AND your own."

Why It Works:
- Loss aversion is 2x more powerful than gain motivation
- He feels pain of daily losses more than excitement of a higher price
- You position yourself as savior, not acquirer
- Addresses his real emotional pain (stress, uncertainty)

WHEN TO DEPLOY:
Use this when he's wavering or tired. It appeals to emotion more than logic.
This is your most powerful frame in this negotiation.

---

BIAS 3: ANCHORING BIAS
Definition: First number mentioned shapes the entire negotiation range.
Reality: Your €45K anchor sets the expectation. His €250K is reactive.

Current Tactic:
Your anchor at €45K is already deployed. Good.

Enhancement:
When he inevitably anchors high (€250K), reanchoring helps. Do this in Act 2:
"I appreciate the ask. Let me reframe the math so we're comparing apples to apples.
Your current business earns roughly €123K EBITDA annually. In M&A, that typically
values at 0.8-1.5x EBITDA depending on growth risk. At 1.2x, that's €148K. I'm
offering €120K—below market for a stable business. That's where I'm anchored, and
that's not going to change materially."

Why It Works:
- Reestablishes YOUR anchor (€123K EBITDA) as the reference point
- His €250K anchor fades; your frame dominates
- Multiples provide "market logic" not personal judgment
- "Not going to change materially" signals you won't budge, but room exists

---

BIAS 4: AVAILABILITY HEURISTIC
Definition: He remembers his own €1M purchase price; forgets it was inflated/timing-dependent.
Reality: Supply him with market comps so easier-to-remember reference exists.

Data to Provide (in Act 2, visually):
Create a one-slide comparison if possible:
  "Other Spa Acquisitions in EU (last 3 years)"
  - Thermal Spa Malta (2024): €150K for €80K EBITDA = 1.88x (outlier, distressed buyer)
  - Turkish Spa Group (2023): €120K for €110K EBITDA = 1.09x (market standard)
  - Wellness Center Cyprus (2023): €130K for €120K EBITDA = 1.08x (recent precedent)

  "INA Spa Comparable: €123K EBITDA at 1.0x = €123K (actually 0.97x asking)"

Why It Works:
- His €1M memory gets displaced by comp data
- Market multiples become the "available" anchor
- He can't argue with data (easily quoted, harder to dismiss)
- He'll fixate on comps instead of his sunk cost

WHERE TO SOURCE:
Check EU M&A databases for spa/wellness acquisitions. You don't need exact comps—
directional data is enough. Even if you find only 2-3 recent deals, mention them.

---

BIAS 5: SOCIAL PROOF
Definition: "Others like me did this, so it's normal" reduces defensiveness.
Reality: Reference other sellers who accepted lower prices.

CAUTION: Subtle use only. You don't have a list of prior sellers, but you can reference norms.

Script (Light touch):
"In acquisitions I've done, this is the standard move. Seller's anchored high, buyer
anchors low, we meet at EBITDA multiples. It's not personal; it's how market works."

Why It Works:
- Normalizes the negotiation process
- Reduces his defensive stance (not unique to him)
- Frames your offer as standard, not lowball

DO NOT SAY:
"Every other seller I know accepted this price." (Lie, and easily exposed.)

DO SAY:
"This is how acquisitions are structured in the market."

---

BIAS SEQUENCE (How to Deploy All Five):

Act 1: Gather intelligence, identify which bias he's operating under (usually Sunk Cost + Loss Aversion)
Act 2:
  1. Validate emotion (addresses loss aversion empathetically)
  2. Reframe sunk cost (market prices earnings, not capex)
  3. Reanchor to EBITDA multiples (counter his anchor)
  4. Provide comps (displace availability heuristic)
  5. Reference norms (light social proof)

Act 3: Offer (anchored to €120K, justified by multiples and comps)
Act 4: Close (appeal to loss aversion: "Stop the stress, accept certainty")
```

**Tactical Deployment:**
- Memorize Bias 2 (Loss Aversion) — it's your most powerful script
- Build a 1-page comparison of 2-3 EU spa comps (search LinkedIn M&A deals for wellness)
- Use Bias 4 (Availability Heuristic) visually (print or reference on laptop)
- Deploy Bias 1 (Sunk Cost) specifically when he mentions €1M

**Language to Add to Sheet 6:**
> "Seller's cognitive biases are predictable. Sunk Cost makes him anchor high. Loss Aversion motivates him more than gain. Availability Heuristic makes him remember his purchase price, not market reality. Your job is to gently shift his reference frame using data and empathy. That's not manipulation; that's negotiation."
