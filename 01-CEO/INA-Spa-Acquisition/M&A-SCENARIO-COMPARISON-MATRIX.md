## WHY EACH SCENARIO FAILS OR SUCCEEDS

### ✅ SCENARIO 1: All-Cash EUR 350K

**✓ STRENGTHS:**
- Simplest structure (single payment, clean break)
- Zero seller leverage post-close
- Low execution complexity

**✗ WEAKNESSES:**
- Highest capital commit (EUR 350K tied up immediately)
- No seller incentive to support post-close clinic launch
- No contingency if clinic underperforms
- Lowest seller acceptance probability

**VERDICT:** Safe but capital-inefficient. Only pursue if seller demands simplicity above all.

---

### ✅ SCENARIO 2: EUR 300K + EUR 50K Earnout (12-month)

**✓ STRENGTHS:**
- Moderate upfront capital (EUR 300K)
- Time-based earnout is simple to administer
- Seller sees "upside" positioning

**✗ WEAKNESSES:**
- Earnout is time-based, NOT performance-based (seller gets EUR 50K regardless of clinic success)
- Misaligned incentives: Seller has zero motivation to support clinic post-close
- High likelihood of earnout disputes ("Why should I pay if clinic failed?")
- Seller may withhold support, expecting to get paid anyway

**VERDICT:** Avoids worst parts of Scenario 1, but earnout structure is flawed. Upgrade to performance-based (Recommended) instead.

---

### ✅ SCENARIO 3: EUR 300K + EUR 200K Seller Note (3-year @ 3%)

**✓ STRENGTHS:**
- Low upfront capital (only EUR 300K)
- Seller carries meaningful financing (shows confidence)
- Potential tax benefit to seller (sale recognized over 3 years)

**✗ WEAKNESSES:**
- CRITICAL execution risk: If clinic underperforms and EBITDA drops 15%, debt service becomes unmanageable
- Seller becomes CREDITOR (not ally): Has enforcement leverage, monthly audit rights, acceleration clauses
- Default probability: 30-40% if clinic underperforms by 20%
- Litigation risk: Seller can foreclose and take back business if you struggle in Year 2
- Refinancing risk: EUR 200K note due in Year 3; must refinance or pay off

**VERDICT:** Worst structure. Converts seller from ally to creditor with power to shut you down. AVOID.

---

### ✅ SCENARIO 4: Phased Acquisition (EUR 250K Year 1 + EUR 250K Year 2)

**✓ STRENGTHS:**
- Lowest Year 1 capital commit (EUR 250K)
- Proves out clinic concept before full commitment
- Year 2 price adjusts based on Year 1 performance
- Excellent capital efficiency if it works

**✗ WEAKNESSES:**
- Legally complex: Partnership structure required for Year 1 (not typical acquisition)
- Operational friction: Seller retains residual ownership during Year 1, creating conflicts
- Year 1-to-Year 2 renegotiation battles: If EBITDA lower than expected, fight over Year 2 price
- Seller likely rejects: "I want out completely in Year 1, not 2-year entanglement"
- Accounting/tax complexity: Two separate transactions, dual documentation

**VERDICT:** Sound concept, but legal/operational complexity exceeds benefit. Seller will reject due diligence demands for partnership structure.

---

### ✅ SCENARIO 5: EUR 250K + EUR 250K Seller Note (5-year @ 3%)

**✓ STRENGTHS:**
- Lowest upfront capital (only EUR 250K)
- Maximizes capital optionality
- Highest seller acceptance (EUR 250K/250K feels like "fair split")
- 5-year note spreads risk

**✗ WEAKNESSES:**
- CRITICAL over-leverage: 2.03x leverage ratio means EUR 16.25K annual debt service
- If clinic underperforms by 20%, debt service becomes 18% of cash flow (stressful)
- Seller as 5-year creditor: You're operating under seller's ongoing approval for 60 months
- Refinancing risk: EUR 250K due in Year 5; must refinance or face forced sale
- Execution constraint: Can't pursue aggressive clinic scaling without exceeding leverage limits

**VERDICT:** Maximizes seller acceptance, but over-commits your balance sheet. Only viable if clinic success is 95%+ certain. Otherwise, EXCESSIVE RISK.

---

### ✅ SCENARIO 6: EUR 350K + EUR 150K Over 5 Years @ 3%

**✓ STRENGTHS:**
- Lowest leverage ratio (1.22x) = lowest financial risk
- Strong debt coverage even if EBITDA drops
- Simplest debt structure (small deferred payment)

**✗ WEAKNESSES:**
- Highest upfront capital (EUR 350K) = worst capital efficiency
- EUR 100K capital tied up that could fund clinic equipment + training
- No seller incentive alignment (deferred payment is passive, not performance-linked)
- Seller may resent "low" total deal value (EUR 500K is fixed, not upside-based)

**VERDICT:** Safest financial structure, but capital-inefficient. Only pursue if you're risk-averse and clinic scaling is secondary priority.

## HOW TO USE THIS COMPARISON

### For Negotiation Positioning

**When seller anchors high (EUR 250K+):**
- Use this matrix to show why performance earnout is FAIREST structure
- "You want upside participation in success—this structure gives you that."
- "Time-based earnout doesn't make sense; you'd get paid even if clinic fails."
- "Performance-based means we're aligned; we both win if clinic succeeds."

**When seller demands all-cash:**
- Use matrix to show opportunity cost of EUR 350K upfront
- "EUR 300K upfront is 75% of total deal value guaranteed at close. That's certainty."
- "EUR 100K earnout is conditional on clinic success—which we're confident about."
- "If you're confident too, performance earnout aligns us both."

**When seller resists earnout:**
- Use matrix to show earnout dispute risk in time-based structures
- "Time-based earnout creates dispute risk. Performance-based is cleaner."
- "We both care about clinic success. Earnout incentivizes you to help."
- "No earnout means I take all execution risk solo. Fair compromise is shared upside."

### For Internal Decision-Making

**If your clinic success confidence is 95%+:**
- Consider Scenario 5 (EUR 250K + EUR 250K note) despite risk
- Performance-based earnout (Recommended) is still safer

**If your clinic success confidence is 75-85%:**
- RECOMMENDED structure is optimal
- Balances seller acceptance, capital efficiency, and execution risk

**If your clinic success confidence is <75%:**
- Consider Scenario 1 (all-cash EUR 350K) or Scenario 6 (EUR 350K + deferred)
- Don't over-leverage with seller note (Scenarios 3, 5)
