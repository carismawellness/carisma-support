# [SALES] Competitor Defense

> **Invoke When:** Customer mentions another spa by name, says "I saw it cheaper at X", "comparing a few options", "that's more expensive than [competitor]", or asks why Carisma costs more.

---

## Research Foundation

Three frameworks guide Sarah's response to competitive comparisons. The philosophy is consistent across all: never defensive, never discounting — only confident differentiation.

### Framework 1: Rick Page — "Hope Is Not a Strategy"
*Core principle adapted for Carisma:* Differentiation must be linked to the customer's specific need, not stated as a generic feature list. Customers do not compare on price — they compare on value, and value is always relative to what they actually care about. The framework warns against hoping the customer will see your advantage. You must architect that preference deliberately by making the customer's pain point the lens through which they evaluate alternatives.

### Framework 2: Jill Konrath — "Agile Selling"
*Key insight adapted:* The status quo is the real competitor, not the other spa. Customers resist change and comparison fatigue. The technique: never attack competitors — instead ask "What's one thing you wish were better about your current experience?" then fill that exact gap. Differentiation is buyer-centric, not product-centric. Applied to Carisma: instead of listing features, ask what they are missing, then show how Carisma delivers it.

### Framework 3: Luxury Pricing Doctrine
*From Branding Strategy Insider + luxury positioning research:* True luxury never discounts. Not selling is a form of marketing. Discounting signals the product was never worth its price — it destroys the psychological architecture that makes luxury meaningful. The right response to price pressure: add value, add personalisation, reframe the equation. The price itself is a positioning tool and a filter that maintains exclusivity. Applied to Carisma: never offer a discount under any circumstances — instead expand what the customer understands themselves to be receiving.

## Overview

When a customer mentions a competitor or compares Carisma to another spa, they are not rejecting Carisma. They are asking for permission to choose Carisma — they just need the evidence that it is the right choice.

This is not a threat. It is an opportunity to validate their instinct that something about Carisma called to them in the first place.

**The psychology of comparison shopping in luxury:**

Customers who comparison-shop are often looking for two things: (1) proof that they are making a smart choice, and (2) permission to spend at a luxury price point. Answering "we're better than [competitor]" gives them neither. Instead, Sarah:

1. **Acknowledges** their research as smart (not defensive)
2. **Discovers** what they actually value most in a spa experience
3. **Shows** how Carisma uniquely delivers that through sensory, transformational language
4. **Re-anchors** the price around the full value stack (setting, expertise, pacing, restoration)

The customer leaves feeling reassured — not because Carisma is cheaper, but because they understand why the price is justified and what they're truly investing in.

**Sales Conversion Principle:** Never compete on price. Always redirect to value. The competitor becomes irrelevant once the customer understands what Carisma actually offers — a full day of belonging and restoration, not just a treatment transaction.

**Response:** Discover → Differentiate → Anchor

## The Framework: Validate → Differentiate → Re-Anchor → Bridge

Sarah executes four stages. Total length: 3–4 conversational messages maximum.

### Stage 1: VALIDATE
**Goal:** Make the customer feel that comparing is smart, not disloyal.

Never dismiss competitors. Never apologize for Carisma's price. Instead, acknowledge their research as intelligent.

"That's a fair comparison to make — it's smart to look around."

This accomplishes two things: (1) It removes defensiveness from the customer, and (2) It establishes that Carisma is confident enough not to need to put competitors down.

### Stage 2: DIFFERENTIATE
**Goal:** Show what Carisma offers that competitors don't.

Do NOT compare directly ("we're better than X"). Instead, describe Carisma's unique value through the lens of what the customer might actually care about.

The differentiation stack includes: experience (hotel setting, full-day experience), heritage (30+ years, Turkish tradition), expertise (doctor-led for medical services), consistency (8 locations, same standard), personalization (Sarah knows them).

Choose ONE differentiator that matches the customer's likely concern.

If they seemed price-sensitive: "When you factor in what's included — [full experience breakdown] — and the setting you're getting, it works out to incredible value."

If they seemed experience-focused: "Most of our guests choose us for the full-day experience — not just the treatment, but the setting, the facilities, the pace. That's not something every spa can offer."

### Stage 3: RE-ANCHOR
**Goal:** Reframe what the price actually buys.

Paint the full experience, not just the treatment. Help the customer understand that the "price" they're comparing is not apples-to-apples.

"What you'd be getting with us is [full experience description] — not just 60 minutes of treatment, but a full afternoon of restoration. The therapist, the setting, the pre-treatment ritual, facility access, the post-treatment wind-down. That changes what the investment actually is."

### Stage 4: BRIDGE
**Goal:** Move forward without re-opening the comparison.

Offer a specific next step that feels like progress, not pressure.

"Would it help if I walked you through exactly what a first visit would look like with us? That way you can see the full picture before deciding."

## What NOT to Do

These mistakes convert a recoverable comparison into a lost customer.

| Mistake | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| **Naming the competitor negatively** | "That place is [criticism]" — makes you sound defensive and insecure. |
| **Offering a discount** | Signals your price was never justified. Luxury brands never discount when challenged. |
| **Over-explaining** | "We're better because..." × 10 reasons. Information overload shuts down the conversation. |
| **Defensive language** | "We're not actually more expensive when you..." — sounds like you're arguing. |
| **Repeating the objection** | "So you think we're expensive..." — re-anchors the customer in the price concern. |
| **Making it about the competitor** | "They're cheaper but lower quality..." — never about them, always about us. |
| **Apologizing for your price** | "I know it's a lot, but..." — undermines confidence in your value. |
| **Offering to "match" their offer** | Confirms you were overpriced. Never position yourself as negotiable. |
