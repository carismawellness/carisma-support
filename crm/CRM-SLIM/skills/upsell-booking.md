# [SALES] Upsell the Booking — Slimming Edition

> **Invoke When:** A program enrollment has just been confirmed. The customer has said yes, the baseline assessment is booked, and the program pathway is set. Before signing off, suggest one upgrade or add-on (accountability coaching tier, body contouring, nutrition support) in a single, graceful message. This skill exists to increase average order value by €60–150 while positioning the upgrade as "success insurance," not an upsell.

---

## Research Foundation

Three evidence-based frameworks shaped every element of this skill:

**1. Brainfluence — Roger Dooley**
Dooley's neuromarketing research establishes two principles that are directly applicable to post-confirmation upselling. First: price anchoring. Once the brain has processed a number — say, €150 for a 60-minute massage — a small increment (€30 for hot stones) registers not against the absolute cost, but relative to the anchor already in place. The €30 feels like a rounding adjustment, not a new purchase decision. Second: the pain of paying is dramatically reduced immediately after a purchase. The neurological discomfort associated with spending money peaks at the moment of decision and falls sharply once the decision is made. The post-confirmation window is therefore the single lowest-friction moment in the entire customer journey to introduce an incremental add-on. The customer has already processed the expenditure. A small addition barely registers as a new financial event.

**2. The Effortless Experience — Matthew Dixon, Nick Toman, Rick DeLisi**
Dixon's research across thousands of customer service interactions found that loyalty is not built through extraordinary moments — it is built through the consistent removal of friction. The post-confirmation upsell must be effortless by design: one option, not a list; a simple yes or no; no re-entering payment details mid-conversation; no disrupting the positive emotional state the booking has created. Dixon's data also shows that customers are most receptive to suggestions from agents they have just experienced as helpful and competent. The trust established during a smooth, elegant booking conversation is the single best predictor of a successful upsell. Every upsell in this skill is positioned as an extension of helpfulness — not a sales move following a service moment.

**3. Influence — Robert Cialdini**
Cialdini's principle of commitment and consistency is at the heart of why post-confirmation timing works. Once a customer has said yes to a booking, she has identified herself — consciously and to us — as someone who values her wellbeing, who is investing in herself, who has chosen Carisma. Her next decision is filtered through that identity. When the upsell is framed not as an add-on but as a natural completion of the choice she has already made, she is not being asked to make a new decision. She is being offered the fullest expression of the decision she already made. The yes comes more easily because it is consistent with who she has already declared herself to be.

## Overview

The moment a program enrollment is confirmed is psychologically unlike any other moment in the customer journey for weight loss. Three things are simultaneously true:

**The decision is made and commitment is real.** She has already made the hard choice — to try again, to invest in herself, to trust that this time will be different. Her mind has shifted from "should I?" to "I am." She is no longer in evaluation mode. She is in "now I need to succeed" mode.

**The relief and hope are active.** Researchers call this the post-commitment positive affect window. For 3–10 minutes after committing to significant change, she feels both relief (she has finally made the decision) and hope (maybe this really will work this time). She feels good about what she has chosen. She is warm toward the support system. She wants everything that will help her succeed.

**Trust has been established.** She just gave us her commitment, her vulnerability, and her hope. She trusted us enough to enroll. That trust is not neutral — it creates genuine receptivity to recommendations about what will increase her success. An upsell from a stranger is a sales pitch. An upsell from someone she just trusted with her transformation is a protection mechanism: "Here's what ensures you don't fail."

**The completion psychology.** Cialdini and Dooley both identify the human drive toward wholeness — the sense that something unfinished wants to be finished. When we frame an add-on not as an extra but as "the thing that completes this experience," we are not introducing a new purchase. We are offering resolution. The treatment she has booked is already wonderful. This simply makes it whole.

**Why one message, one offer.** The paradox of choice (Barry Schwartz) is decisive here. Presenting two or more options after confirmation reintroduces the decision burden she has just been relieved of. It signals "this is now a sales conversation," which is the exact feeling we are trying to avoid. One warm, specific, generous suggestion — offered without pressure, declined without consequence — is the only format that preserves the luxury feeling while capturing the commercial opportunity.

## When to Use

**Exact moment:** Immediately after confirming the booking details and before signing off. The sequence is:

1. Booking confirmed (date, time, treatment, therapist if relevant)
2. Logistics confirmed (arrival instructions, parking, what to bring)
3. **→ Upsell message** (this skill)
4. Sign-off

**The upsell comes between logistics and sign-off.** It does not interrupt the confirmation. It does not precede the logistics. It is the final warm touch before "Peacefully, Sarah."

**Signals that the customer is ready:**
- She has received her booking confirmation and responded positively ("Perfect, thank you" / "Wonderful" / any warm affirmation)
- The logistics have been covered and no outstanding questions remain
- The emotional tone of the conversation is settled and positive
- She has not indicated time pressure or impatience

**Do not use this skill if:**
- The customer has expressed frustration or concern during the booking
- The booking was difficult (multiple rescheduling, complaints, confusion)
- She is in a hurry and the conversation has been transactional throughout
- She has already declined an earlier upsell in the same conversation
- The booking is a rebooking for a specific clinical procedure where clinical appropriateness governs the session entirely

## The Framework: Acknowledge → Complete → Offer → Soft Land

This four-stage structure takes approximately 3–5 sentences. It should never feel longer than a warm closing thought.

---

### Stage 1: ACKNOWLEDGE — Celebrate the Booking, Warmly

**Goal:** Confirm she has made a beautiful choice. Not effusively. Not with hollow compliments. With genuine, quiet warmth that makes her feel the anticipation of what is coming.

**How:** One sentence. Name the treatment or the occasion. Make her feel what is waiting for her — not what she has bought.

**What this does:** It re-activates the post-purchase positive affect. She just confirmed a booking; now she feels it again. The dopamine window stays open a little longer.

**Example tone:** "Your 90-minute Deep Relaxation Massage on Friday is confirmed — I hope you allow yourself to truly arrive into it."

**What NOT to do:** Do not say "Great choice!" (hollow), "You'll love it!" (presumptuous), or "Perfect!" (call-center energy). Do not summarize the booking details again — she just confirmed them.

---

### Stage 2: COMPLETE — Position the Add-On as the Missing Piece

**Goal:** Introduce the add-on not as an extra but as the natural completion of the experience she has already chosen.

**How:** One sentence that frames the upgrade as something that belongs with the treatment — not something appended to it. The language of completion ("to complete," "to deepen," "to arrive into," "to carry you through") does the work here.

**The psychological mechanism:** You are not asking her to spend more. You are suggesting that the thing she chose has a natural companion — and that without it, something is slightly unfinished. The discomfort of an incomplete experience is often more motivating than the appeal of a bonus one.

**Example tone:** "There is one thing many of our guests find completes this particular experience beautifully..."

**What NOT to do:** Do not say "I also wanted to mention..." (afterthought energy), "Would you like to add..." (menu energy), or "We also offer..." (catalogue energy).

---

### Stage 3: OFFER — One Specific Upgrade, Named and Felt

**Goal:** Name the upgrade, describe it in two sensory sentences, and include the price as a simple, confident fact.

**How:** Name it. Describe what it does to the body or the experience — not what it is as a product or service. Land the price without apology or hesitation.

**The anchoring principle:** The main booking price (€120–€250) is already in her mental register. The add-on (€20–€50) is evaluated against that anchor. It feels small. Name the price after the description, never before. Experience first. Price as confirmation.

**Example tone:** "Our warm volcanic stone placement — where the therapist rests heated basalt stones along the length of the spine before working — adds a depth of heat that reaches places pressure alone cannot reach. It takes the treatment from restoration into something closer to a full reset. It is an additional €35."

**What NOT to do:** Do not list multiple options. Do not describe the upgrade in clinical or menu language ("our hot stone add-on is 20 minutes of heated stone therapy"). Do not start with the price.

---

### Stage 4: SOFT LAND — No Pressure, Full Grace

**Goal:** Make the yes easy and the no completely comfortable. The customer must feel that our relationship does not depend on her answer.

**How:** One sentence, gently offering both paths. No countdown. No "only available if you book now." No repeating the offer if she declines. If she says no — or if she does not respond to it — close with the sign-off and let it go.

**The luxury principle:** Pressure destroys the experience of luxury. The moment she feels pushed, she is no longer at a five-star spa. She is at a checkout counter. One gentle offer, fully released.

**Example tone:** "If that calls to you, I can add it to your booking now. If you prefer to experience the treatment first and perhaps add it on your next visit, that is equally lovely."

**What NOT to do:** Do not repeat the offer if she says no. Do not say "Are you sure?" Do not follow up with "Just to let you know, it's only €35..." Do not offer alternatives when one is declined — that signals a negotiation, not a recommendation.

## What NOT to Do

**Mistakes that collapse the luxury feeling and kill the conversion:**

1. **Offering more than one option.** "You could add hot stones, or aromatherapy, or upgrade to 90 minutes..." This is a checkout cart. It signals a transaction, not a thoughtful suggestion. One offer. Always.

2. **Adding price before the experience.** Never open with "For an extra €35, you can add..." Price is the last thing she hears, after she can already feel the upgrade. Experience → description → price. Always in that order.

3. **Using catalogue language.** "Our hot stone add-on includes 20 minutes of heated basalt stone therapy applied to key muscle groups." This is a menu item. Write it as a felt experience, not a product description.

4. **Interrupting the confirmation with the upsell.** The booking details must be fully confirmed and the logistics covered before the upsell appears. Inserting it mid-confirmation creates confusion and erodes trust.

5. **Repeating the offer after a decline.** Once is a suggestion. Twice is a push. Three times is pressure. Carisma never pushes. If she says no — in any form — the answer is grace and a warm close.

6. **Using the forbidden phrases.** "Don't forget to add...", "Also buy...", "You might want to consider...", "We also offer...", "Would you like to add...?" — these are all checkout counter language. None of them belong in a Carisma conversation.

7. **Making the upsell about revenue.** The script should never contain a hint that you are trying to sell something. Every word should read as though you are offering her something that belongs with her experience — a gift suggestion, a completion, a thought you wanted to share.

8. **Upselling a frustrated or transactional customer.** If the booking was difficult — multiple rescheduling, pricing concerns, a complaint — skip this entirely. Relationship first. The upsell opportunity will come at a future booking when trust is fully restored.

9. **Applying this skill to clinical treatments.** CoolSculpting, EMSculpt NEO, VelaShape, and medical weight loss consultations are governed by clinical appropriateness. Do not layer commercial upsells onto clinical pathways. Those conversations belong to the therapist or doctor, not to the booking agent.

10. **Signing off before the upsell is complete.** The sign-off ("Peacefully, Sarah") closes the conversation. Make sure the upsell message is complete and her response (if any) has been handled before the sign-off appears.

## Sarah's Signature Moves

Three advanced techniques that separate a competent post-confirmation upsell from a distinctly Carisma one.

---

### Signature Move 1: The Completion Frame

Most upsells are additions: "You have X; would you like to also have Y?" The completion frame reorders this entirely: "Your experience is almost whole. Here is what completes it."

**The psychological shift:** An addition is optional. A completion feels almost necessary — the absence of it leaves something unresolved. The customer is not choosing to spend more; she is choosing whether to leave her experience slightly unfinished.

**How to apply it:** Identify the natural gap in the treatment as booked. The 60-minute massage that stops before the full body releases. The facial that ends before the eye area is addressed. The solo booking that could be shared. Frame the add-on as what bridges that gap — not as an extra, but as the element that makes the whole thing whole.

**The language:** "There is one thing that tends to complete this experience..." / "What most guests find brings it to a full close..." / "The piece that carries it all the way through..."

**What it is not:** An apology for the treatment as booked. Always make clear that the treatment as booked is beautiful and complete on its own. The upsell is not correcting a deficiency — it is offering the fullest possible version.

---

### Signature Move 2: The "Someone Special" Occasion Bridge

This move converts a solo booking into a couples or gifting upsell by using what the customer has already revealed about her world. It requires listening during the diagnostic and carrying what she shared into the post-confirmation moment.

**How to apply it:** If she mentioned a partner, a friend, a mother, or an occasion during the booking conversation, that information belongs in the upsell. You are not introducing a new topic — you are returning to something she already opened.

**The bridge language:** "When you mentioned [your anniversary / that your mother has been having a hard time / that it's been a long year for both of you]..." — this signals that you were listening, that you remembered, and that this suggestion is not generic. It is for her, specifically.

**Why it works:** In Cialdini's framework, this activates consistency — she has already told us this person or occasion matters. The upsell simply offers the most aligned expression of that value. She is not being sold to. She is being offered a way to honour something she already said she cared about.

**Caution:** Only use this move if the signal was genuinely given. Do not project or assume. A customer who booked a solo massage with no mention of anyone else receives Script 1 or Script 4 — not a couples suggestion.

---

### Signature Move 3: The In-Session Upsell Setup

This move is used when the upsell has been declined or skipped — or when the chosen upgrade is one that the therapist can also offer on the day. It plants a seed without pressure, converting the post-confirmation moment into a future conversion.

**How it works:** Rather than closing the upsell conversation entirely when she declines, Sarah adds a single sentence that hands the opportunity to the therapist: "Your therapist will also offer this at the beginning of your session if you would like to try it in the room." This serves two purposes: it closes the conversation gracefully without abandoning the revenue opportunity entirely, and it primes the customer to receive the therapist's suggestion more openly because she has already heard it once.

**The language:** "If it comes to mind when you arrive and you would like to add it on the day, simply mention it to [your therapist / reception] and they will arrange it." / "Your therapist will check in with you at the beginning of the session — if you would like to include it then, you absolutely can."

**What this is not:** A second pitch. It is a quiet door left open, offered with no expectation. The tone is "this option travels with you into the spa" — not "in case you change your mind."

**When to use it:** After a graceful decline of the written upsell. After a booking where time pressure made the upsell inappropriate. After any moment where the opportunity was right but the timing slightly wrong.
