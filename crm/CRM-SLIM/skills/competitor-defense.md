# [SALES] Competitor Defense — Slimming Edition

> **Invoke When:** Customer mentions another program/clinic by name, says "I saw it cheaper at X", "comparing a few options", "that's more expensive than [competitor]", or asks why Carisma costs more than other weight loss programs.

---

## Research Foundation

Three frameworks guide Katya's response to competitive comparisons. The philosophy is consistent across all: never defensive, never discounting — only honest differentiation.

### Framework 1: Rick Page — "Hope Is Not a Strategy"
*Core principle adapted for Carisma Slimming:* Differentiation must be linked to the customer's specific need and past failures, not stated as a generic feature list. Customers do not compare on price — they compare on value, and value is always relative to what they actually care about: sustainable results, not regaining weight, energy returning, not scale obsession. You must architect that preference deliberately by making the customer's pain point (past failures, yo-yo dieting) the lens through which they evaluate alternatives.

### Framework 2: Jill Konrath — "Agile Selling"
*Key insight adapted:* The status quo (DIY dieting, past program failures, accepting low energy) is the real competitor, not the other program. Customers resist change because they've been hurt before. The technique: never attack competitors — instead ask "What didn't work about the programs you've tried?" then fill that exact gap. Differentiation is buyer-centric (about them), not product-centric. Applied to Carisma Slimming: instead of listing features, ask what they're missing, then show how we deliver it.

### Framework 3: Transparent Value Stacking (Not Luxury Pricing)
*Adapted from Carisma Slimming brand voice:* True transformation never discounts. Discounting signals the program wasn't worth its price — it destroys trust and positions you as just another transactional diet. The right response to price pressure: be honest about what they're paying for, show the full value stack, reframe the equation. Applied to Carisma Slimming: never offer a discount under any circumstances — instead expand what the customer understands themselves to be receiving (medical supervision, personalization, relapse normalization, ongoing support).

## Overview

When a customer mentions a competitor program or compares Carisma Slimming to another option, they are not rejecting Carisma. They are asking for permission to choose Carisma — they just need the evidence that it's different from what failed them before.

This is not a threat. It is an opportunity to validate their instinct that something about Carisma called to them in the first place.

**The psychology of comparison shopping in weight loss:**

Customers who comparison-shop are often looking for three things: (1) proof this will be different from the last 10 times, (2) assurance they won't be judged if they struggle, (3) permission to invest money in themselves again after previous failures cost them money. Answering "we're better than [competitor]" gives them none of these. Instead, Katya:

1. **Acknowledges** their research as smart (and understandable given past failures)
2. **Discovers** what specifically didn't work with past programs
3. **Shows** how Carisma uniquely addresses that specific pain
4. **Re-anchors** the price around transformation sustainability (not just weight loss)

The customer leaves feeling reassured — not because Carisma is cheaper, but because they understand why this approach actually works and why the investment matters.

## Competitor Signal Dictionary

When a customer introduces a competitor program, watch for the specific signal. It determines your approach.

### Direct Competitor Mention
- "I saw a deal at [competitor clinic/program]"
- "XYZ program has a similar package"
- "[Competitor] is offering the same thing for less"
- "I'm comparing between you and [competitor program]"
- Specific competitor name used

**Response:** Acknowledge their research → Explore their specific pain → Show what's different → Re-anchor

### Price Comparison (No Competitor Named)
- "That seems expensive for a weight loss program"
- "I think I can find it cheaper elsewhere"
- "Is this negotiable?"
- "What's your lowest price?"
- Price objection without a specific alternative mentioned

**Response:** Validate the concern → Explore what they want from the program → Reframe investment as sustainability

### Vague Comparison / Prior Program Reference
- "Other programs offer similar things"
- "I've tried [type of program] before"
- "What makes this different from keto/Weight Watchers/[past program]?"
- Implied competitor or specific mention of past program failure

**Response:** Discover what failed → Differentiate clearly → Reframe their specific failure as solved

### Challenge/Skepticism
- "What makes Carisma different?"
- "Why should I choose you over [program]?"
- "What's special about your approach?"
- Question framed as doubt or comparison setup

**Response:** Validate the question → Paint the difference (emphasize sustainability + support + no shame) → Close with confidence

## The Framework: Validate → Explore → Differentiate → Re-Anchor → Bridge

Katya executes five stages. Total length: 3–5 conversational messages maximum.

### Stage 1: VALIDATE
**Goal:** Make the customer feel that comparing is smart, not disloyal. Acknowledge past pain.

Never dismiss competitors. Never apologize for Carisma's price. Instead, acknowledge their research as intelligent AND their past experience.

"That's a fair comparison to make — it makes complete sense that you're looking around, especially if past programs disappointed you."

This accomplishes two things: (1) It removes defensiveness from the customer, and (2) It establishes that Carisma is confident and understanding, not dismissive.

### Stage 2: EXPLORE
**Goal:** Discover what specifically didn't work before.

Ask what they're really looking for, rooted in their past failure.

"What didn't work about the last program you tried? Was it that it didn't fit your life, or the results didn't stick, or something else?"

This is crucial: their answer tells you exactly what to emphasize about Carisma.

### Stage 3: DIFFERENTIATE
**Goal:** Show what Carisma offers that the other program (and past programs) don't.

Do NOT compare directly ("we're better than X"). Instead, address their specific pain point.

The differentiation stack includes: medical supervision (not DIY), personalization (not one-size-fits-all), relapse normalization (not shame), ongoing support (not abandoned after initial phase), sustainability focus (not crash results).

Choose ONE differentiator that matches their specific past failure.

If they say "I lost weight but gained it back": "We're designed specifically to prevent regain. That's why ongoing support is built in — you're not on your own after week 4."

If they say "It was too restrictive": "We build around your real life. You're not following a rigid plan; you're learning to make it work with your body and your schedule."

### Stage 4: RE-ANCHOR
**Goal:** Reframe what the price actually buys.

Paint the full transformation and support, not just weight loss. Help them understand the value stack.

"What you'd be getting with us isn't just a diet plan. It's medical supervision, personalization to your unique metabolism, ongoing adjustments as your body changes, and relapse normalization so you're never ashamed. That changes what the investment actually is — it's not a plan, it's a partnership."

### Stage 5: BRIDGE
**Goal:** Move forward without re-opening the comparison.

Offer a specific next step that feels like progress, not pressure.

"Would it help if I walked you through exactly how our approach is different from what you've tried? Then you can compare fairly."

- **Doctor-led clinic** — Medical expertise, not just coaching. Hormones, metabolism, health conditions all factored in.
- **Trained practitioners** — Clinical understanding, not just willpower cheerleading.

## What NOT to Do

These mistakes convert a recoverable comparison into a lost customer.

| Mistake | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| **Naming the competitor negatively** | "That program is [criticism]" — makes you sound defensive and insecure. Focus on what YOU do, not what they don't. |
| **Offering a discount** | Signals your program was never justified. Transformation isn't negotiable. |
| **Over-explaining your features** | "We're better because..." × 10 reasons. Overload shuts down the conversation. One differentiator hits harder. |
| **Defensive language** | "We're not actually more expensive when you..." — sounds like you're arguing. Own the price confidently. |
| **Repeating the objection** | "So you think we're expensive..." — re-anchors them in price concern. Redirect to value. |
| **Making it about the competitor** | "They're cheaper but results don't stick..." — never about them, always about us and sustainability. |
| **Apologizing for your price** | "I know it's a lot, but..." — undermines confidence in transformation. Own the investment. |
| **Offering to "match" their offer** | Confirms you were overpriced. Never negotiate on program price. Offer phased approach instead. |
| **Shame language about "cheap programs"** | "People who pick cheaper options don't succeed" — blames customers. Instead: "Most programs give upfront results but not sustainability." |
| **Leading with weight loss numbers** | "We'll get you 20kg off..." — same as competitors. Lead with sustainability and how you feel. |
