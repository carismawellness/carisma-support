# [SALES] Cross-Sell from Slimming to Complementary Services

> **Invoke When:** A Slimming member shows signals that suggest she would benefit from complementary services (fitness coaching, nutrition specialist consultation, skin clinic follow-up for confidence boost) — at any natural conversation close point: after a milestone is hit, after a visible result, or when she volunteers something that opens the door. NOTE: Slimming cross-sells are about expansion within the weight loss journey, not about moving them to another clinic.

---

## Research Foundation

Three evidence-based frameworks shaped every element of this skill:

**1. The Challenger Sale — Matthew Dixon & Brent Adamson**
Dixon and Adamson's research across thousands of sales professionals found that the highest-performing reps did not simply mirror customer wants — they taught customers what they should care about, and they did it through insight, not pressure. The principle that applies here: a cross-sell is not an add-on. It is an insight. "You've been caring for your body through the spa — there is something our clinic offers that takes that a step further, and not many spa guests know it exists." Teaching her something she did not know creates momentum that a straight product pitch never achieves. The cross-sell succeeds when she feels informed, not sold to.

**2. Consultative Selling — Mack Hanan**
Hanan's foundational principle: stop selling products, start improving lives. The value of any recommendation is measured in the difference it makes to the customer's experience of her own body, skin, and sense of self — not in the treatment itself. Applied to cross-selling across Carisma's three divisions: the goal is never to "mention the clinic." It is to extend her wellbeing journey in a direction she has already pointed toward herself. The signal always comes from her. Sarah simply reflects it back and opens the door.

**3. Cialdini's Principles of Influence — Robert Cialdini**
Two principles are directly applicable to warm cross-selling in the luxury wellness context: **Reciprocity** (when a customer has been well cared for, she is psychologically open to receiving further guidance from the person who cared for her) and **Authority** (the doctor-led positioning of Carisma Slimming and the clinical credibility of Carisma Aesthetics both function as trust accelerators — the cross-sell carries the weight of expertise, not salesmanship). A third principle, **Unity** (the sense of "we are on the same journey"), is the frame that makes the bridge feel natural rather than commercial.

## Overview

Carisma is not three separate businesses. It is one wellbeing journey with three entry points.

- **Carisma Spa** — the body, rest, and restoration. The starting point for most guests.
- **Carisma Aesthetics** — the skin, the face, the visible self. The natural complement to spa care for guests who have started thinking about how they look, not just how they feel.
- **Carisma Slimming** — the shape, the composition, the relationship with the body. The clinical pathway for guests who are ready to go further than relaxation.

The cross-sell exists because many Spa customers do not know the other two divisions exist, or do not know that their concern — a mention of their skin, a comment about their confidence, a question about body contouring — is exactly what those divisions are designed for.

**The philosophy:** The cross-sell is never a referral. It is never "you should try our other clinic." It is an invitation to go deeper into a journey she has already begun. The language is always: "Since you are already thinking about yourself in this way..." or "Something that complements this beautifully..." — never a product mention, never a listing of services.

**Why this converts better than feature-led pitches:** A feature-led pitch ("We also offer Botox at our Aesthetics clinic") asks her to evaluate a product she did not come here for. An invitation-led bridge ("There is something that a lot of our spa guests find goes perfectly alongside the kind of care you have been giving yourself...") invites her to continue something she has already chosen. She is not being sold something new. She is being offered more of what she has already decided she wants: to feel well in her own body.

The cross-sell is most powerful at close — after trust has been built, after the spa booking is secured, after she has been genuinely cared for. It is one sentence. It is unhurried. It plants a seed. It does not demand an immediate answer.

## Customer Profiling: Who Goes Where

### Spa → Aesthetics Signals

These signals suggest the customer is thinking about her skin, her face, or her visible self — and would benefit from an introduction to Carisma Aesthetics.

1. **She mentions her skin directly.** "My skin has been so dull lately." "I have been breaking out more than usual." "My skin just doesn't look the way it used to." These are not passing comments. They are open doors.

2. **She asks about facials or skin treatments at the spa.** Even if the spa does not offer what she is looking for, the question tells you where her mind is.

3. **She comments on aging or visible changes.** "I've noticed lines appearing around my eyes." "I feel like my face looks tired even when I'm not." "I've started to notice my skin sagging a little." She is not necessarily asking for anything — she is noticing. That noticing is the signal.

4. **She mentions a special event or photos coming up.** A wedding, a milestone birthday, a reunion, a holiday. Upcoming visibility creates skin-awareness. She wants to look her best, not just feel her best.

5. **She asks about "glow" or skin radiance.** "What would give me a glow for my daughter's wedding?" "I want to look fresh for the summer." Glow language is aesthetics language — she is describing a skin outcome, not a relaxation outcome.

6. **She has had multiple spa visits and is looking for what's next.** A returning guest who has explored the spa's treatments and is asking "is there anything else you'd recommend?" is signalling openness to going further.

7. **She mentions a specific skin concern that falls outside what spa facials can address.** Fine lines, deeper wrinkles, pigmentation, loss of facial volume, persistent acne — these are clinical concerns, and she knows it. She may be asking anyway, hoping you can help.

8. **She expresses frustration with skincare products not working.** "I've tried everything and nothing works." "I've spent so much on creams." This is a woman ready to be told there is a professional answer.

9. **She mentions she has been to a dermatologist or aesthetics clinic elsewhere.** She is not a stranger to aesthetics — she already knows the territory. The question is whether she knows Carisma Aesthetics exists.

10. **She asks what Carisma offers beyond the spa.** Any question about the wider brand is an invitation. She wants to know if there is more. There is.

### Spa → Slimming Signals

These signals suggest the customer is thinking about her body composition, her confidence, or her shape — and would benefit from an introduction to Carisma Slimming.

1. **She mentions weight, shape, or body composition directly.** She does not need to say "I want to lose weight." "I've been struggling with my tummy since the baby." "My body just isn't bouncing back the way it used to." Any reference to how her body is shaped or how she feels inside it is a signal.

2. **She asks about body contouring, fat reduction, or skin tightening.** CoolSculpting, body wraps, slimming treatments — if she is asking, she already knows these things exist. She is looking for someone to show her the right door.

3. **She mentions confidence or how she feels in her clothes.** "I just don't feel confident in a swimsuit anymore." "I have this dress I want to wear and I don't feel good in it yet." Confidence language tied to appearance is a body-composition signal, not a relaxation signal.

4. **She mentions a goal event — a wedding, a holiday, a reunion.** She has a target. She wants results. A spa treatment maintains wellbeing; a Slimming consultation moves toward a goal.

5. **She has had multiple spa visits, suggesting a wellness commitment.** A customer who keeps returning to invest in her wellbeing is the ideal Slimming candidate — she has already proven she values professional support for her body.

6. **She mentions she has tried diets, gym programs, or other solutions without lasting success.** "I've tried everything." "I lost weight but it came back." "The gym isn't touching my stomach." This is a woman ready to hear about a doctor-led clinical approach.

7. **She asks about post-pregnancy body changes.** Loose skin, changed shape, muscle tone — these are exact target concerns for CoolSculpting, EMSculpt NEO, and VelaShape. She may not know the treatments exist by name.

8. **She mentions energy, metabolism, or hormonal changes.** "Since my forties, everything has slowed down." "My doctor mentioned my metabolism has changed." "I think it might be hormonal." This is a woman who would respond to the doctor-led, science-backed positioning of Carisma Slimming.

9. **She expresses frustration or hopelessness about her body.** "I've given up on trying." "I don't know what else to do." This needs to be handled with the utmost care and warmth — not as a sales opportunity, but as a moment of genuine care. The cross-sell here is an offering of hope, not a pitch.

10. **She asks about treatments for specific areas.** Belly, thighs, arms, under the chin — area-specific concerns that diet and exercise have not resolved are the precise indication for CoolSculpting and EMSculpt NEO.

### Neither — Wait

Do not cross-sell in these situations. The relationship comes first. The seed can be planted another time.

- **She is in the middle of a complaint or a problem.** Resolve the issue first. A customer who feels heard will return. A customer who feels sold to during a complaint will not.
- **She is grieving or going through a personal difficulty.** A loss, a health scare, an emotional crisis. This is a time to be a calm presence, not a business.
- **She has signalled she is "just here to relax."** Some guests want quiet restoration with no further engagement. Read this and honour it. Not every conversation needs to go further.
- **She has already declined once.** If you have introduced the other clinic and she showed no interest, do not revisit it in the same conversation. Plant the seed once. Leave it.
- **She is a first-time contact and trust is not yet established.** For brand-new customers, complete the primary booking first. Let her experience Carisma Spa. The cross-sell conversation belongs in the second or third interaction, when the relationship has proven itself.

## The Framework: Acknowledge → Bridge → Invite → Release

This four-stage process ensures the cross-sell never feels like a sales pitch. It takes less than three sentences when done well.

### Stage 1: ACKNOWLEDGE
Name what she has shared. Reflect it back in a way that shows you heard it — not as a segue, but as genuine recognition.

"You mentioned your skin has been feeling really dull lately — that is something a lot of women notice especially after a busy season."

The acknowledge stage has one rule: it must be about her, not about what you are about to offer. If she feels acknowledged, she will lean forward for what comes next.

### Stage 2: BRIDGE
Connect what she shared to the natural extension — Carisma Aesthetics or Carisma Slimming — using the language of continuity, not redirection.

"Since you're already in a place of investing in yourself, there's something that a lot of our spa guests find goes hand in hand with this..."

Bridge language to use: "Since you're already...", "Something that complements this beautifully...", "A lot of women who come to us for spa treatments also find that...", "This is actually something we hear often from guests like you..."

Bridge language to avoid: "We also offer...", "You should check out...", "Have you heard of our other clinic?", "By the way, did you know..."

### Stage 3: INVITE
One sentence. Name the division, describe what it does in the warmest possible terms, and let it land.

"Carisma Aesthetics is our skin clinic — it's where our spa guests go when they're ready to take their skin further than what a facial can do on its own."

"Carisma Slimming is our doctor-led clinic, just for women — it's where the clinical support lives for everything that sits beyond what a spa treatment can reach."

One sentence. No features. No prices. No urgency. Just an introduction.

### Stage 4: RELEASE
Remove all pressure. Plant the seed and walk away from it gently. The release is what makes the whole thing feel safe rather than salesy.

"I just wanted you to know it's there, in case it ever feels right."

"No need to do anything now — just something to keep in mind if you'd ever like to explore it."

"Whenever you're ready for that conversation, we're here."

The release is not a soft close disguised as generosity. It is genuine. Many customers who receive a cross-sell without pressure return to it within days or weeks — on their own terms, which is the only way a luxury purchase truly converts.

## What NOT to Do

**Mistakes that turn a warm introduction into a lost relationship:**

1. **Body-shaming language, even accidentally.** Never use: "fix your body," "get rid of fat," "problem areas," "excess weight," "let yourself go." The Slimming cross-sell must use language of empowerment and care. "Your body's journey," "how you want to feel," "what you're working toward" — these are the frames. Avoid any language that positions the body as a problem to be corrected.

2. **Clinical jargon without warmth.** "Cryolipolysis," "radiofrequency," "body composition index" — these belong in clinical conversations, not in an opening cross-sell. The introduction uses warm language. The clinical detail comes after she asks for more.

3. **Mentioning weight loss as the primary goal.** "Lose weight" is not the frame. "How you feel in your body," "going further in your wellbeing," "what the clinic can offer you" — these are the frames. Even when the goal is clearly weight-related, lead with how she will feel, not what the scale will say.

4. **Cross-selling to a customer in an active complaint.** This is non-negotiable. If she is unhappy, confused, or resolving an issue, the cross-sell does not exist in that conversation. Resolve the issue with full attention. Everything else waits.

5. **Cross-selling more than once per conversation.** Plant the seed once. If she shows no interest, release it. Revisiting it reads as pressure, and pressure is the fastest way to end a relationship in the luxury space.

6. **Leading with price.** The cross-sell introduction never opens with "our Slimming Starter Pack is only €199." Price comes after interest has been established, always. Interest first. Information second. Price third.

7. **Using retail-style bridging language.** "Also check out our...", "We also offer...", "Don't forget about our..." — these phrases position the cross-sell as an upsell, not as an invitation. The language must always feel like care, not commerce.

8. **Cross-selling within the first contact.** A brand-new customer who has not yet experienced Carisma Spa is not a cross-sell candidate. She needs to experience and trust the brand first. The cross-sell belongs in the second interaction or later, when the relationship has earned it.

9. **Describing the clinic as "separate" or "different."** Carisma is one journey. Never frame the Slimming or Aesthetics division as "a completely different thing" or "a separate business." The language is always: "alongside the spa," "part of what Carisma offers," "the clinical side of the same journey."

10. **Forgetting to release.** Every cross-sell must end with explicit pressure removal. "No need to do anything now." "Just something to keep in mind." "Whenever you're ready." Without the release, the invitation becomes a push — and pushes in luxury settings almost always close doors rather than open them.

## Sarah's Signature Moves

Three advanced techniques that elevate the cross-sell from competent to distinctly Carisma:

---

### Signature Move 1: The Deeper Journey Frame

Most cross-sells position the new service as a different thing. Sarah's approach positions it as more of the same thing — just taken further.

**How it works:** Before introducing the other division, use language that frames the customer's current behaviour as the beginning of a journey she is already on. Then the cross-sell is simply the next chapter of that journey — not a detour.

"You've clearly been thinking about yourself in a really intentional way — the spa visits, the attention to how you feel. What we have in Carisma Aesthetics [or Slimming] is simply where that kind of intention can go deeper."

The psychological effect: she does not need to decide to do something new. She only needs to continue doing what she is already doing — investing in herself. The cross-sell feels like acceleration, not direction change.

**When to use:** With returning spa guests, with customers who speak about their wellness journey with care and self-awareness, with customers who frame their spa visits as a lifestyle rather than a one-off.

### Signature Move 2: The "Our Doctor Would Love to Meet You" Warm Introduction

For the Slimming cross-sell in particular, the warm introduction of the doctor as a person — not a credential — transforms the clinical into the personal.

**How it works:** Instead of describing the clinic, describe the doctor. A person is more compelling than a service. A person who "would love to meet you" creates a different feeling than "we offer consultations."

"I just want you to know — our doctor at Carisma Slimming has been doing this for over thirty years, and she has a very particular way of approaching each woman she works with. I think she would genuinely love to meet you and hear your story. The conversation alone is worth having."

The effect: the customer is not being invited to a clinic. She is being introduced to someone. That is an entirely different emotional proposition. Trust is easier to extend to a person than to a service.

**When to use:** When the customer seems hesitant, when she has had bad experiences with clinical programs before, when she needs to feel safe before she can consider.

### Signature Move 3: The "Something We Offer That Not Many People Know About" Intrigue Opener

This technique works because it removes the commercial frame entirely. It positions the cross-sell as insider knowledge — something Sarah is sharing because she genuinely thinks it would help, not because she has been asked to.

**How it works:** The phrasing "something that not many people know about" triggers curiosity (she leans in) and reciprocity (she feels privileged to receive insider knowledge, not targeted for a sale). It also signals honesty — if many people knew, it would feel like marketing. The fact that "not many" know makes it feel like a genuine recommendation.

"Something I don't always mention, but I feel like I should tell you — we have a clinical skin clinic alongside the spa. Most guests don't know it exists, and I think for what you're describing, it's actually the more interesting conversation."

The effect: she feels like she is receiving something special, not being pitched something commercial. The cross-sell becomes a favour rather than a feature.

**When to use:** When the customer has not asked about any other service but has dropped a strong signal. When the mood of the conversation is warm and trusting. When you want to introduce the cross-sell very gently, without it dominating the conversation.
