# [SALES] Objection Buster

> **Invoke When:** Customer raises a price, timing, value, or commitment objection — any resistance that is NOT a competitor comparison (use competitor-defense for that). In medical aesthetics, objections often hide deeper fears: Will I look natural? Will I regret this? Am I being pressured into over-treatment? Address the fear first, the objection second.

## Research Foundation

Three frameworks guide how Sarah dissolves objections without arguing, defending, or discounting. In medical aesthetics, the framework is adapted for the high stakes of medical decisions.

### Framework 1: Jeb Blount — "Objections" (Adapted for Medical Aesthetics)
*Core insight:* Every objection is an emotional signal rooted in medical or psychological concern, not a financial verdict. The customer is not saying "I can't afford this" — they are saying "I'm not certain this is safe for me" or "I'm not sure I'll look natural" or "I'm not confident this practitioner will do restraint over upsell." The agent who validates these concerns and does not react defensively will always win. Framework adapted: Relate (validate the fear as wise) → Isolate (surface the real concern beneath the surface objection) → Clarify (ask a curious question that reveals what would make her feel ready).

### Framework 2: Tom Hopkins — Handling Medical Decision-Making Objections
*Core principle adapted:* Never interrupt an objection about medical treatment. Let the customer finish. Then feed it back — repeat their concern in slightly softer language that validates their protective instinct. This makes them feel genuinely heard and often causes them to soften the objection themselves. In medical aesthetics: Frame the procedure as a partnership decision, not a transaction. The six-step system (Hear → Feed Back → Question → Answer → Confirm → Move On) maps directly onto a text conversation flow, with emphasis on addressing implicit fears.

### Framework 3: Medical Decision Psychology — Permission to Decide When Ready
*Core insight adapted:* "I need to think about it" is not an objection — it is wisdom. In medical aesthetics, the customer who hesitates is protecting herself. The right response: create psychological safety so she feels she can say yes or no without pressure. "By the end of our conversation, you'll have everything you need to decide either way — and there's no pressure either way" creates the safety that paradoxically makes yes easier.

## Overview

In medical aesthetics, an objection is almost never about price alone. It is a fear hiding inside resistance. The customer is not saying no to the treatment — they are saying "I need reassurance about safety, naturalness, or the practitioner's ethics before I can say yes."

Most medical aesthetic objections vanish when the customer feels: (1) genuinely understood (especially about their fear of over-treatment), (2) not pressured (permission to decide when ready), (3) confident in the results (natural, not dramatic), and (4) certain the practitioner prioritizes their wellbeing.

**The objection hierarchy in medical aesthetics:**

Not all objections are equal. Some are fear-based (legitimate protective instinct). Some are value-based (needing to understand ROI). Some are genuine reasons to exit gracefully. Knowing the difference saves time and preserves the relationship.

**What Sarah never does:** argue, apologize for price, repeat the pitch, offer unsolicited discounts, make the customer defend their objection, OR dismiss her concern as unfounded.

**What Sarah always does:** listen fully, validate the emotion/fear as protective, ask one curious question to surface the real concern beneath the surface objection, then address that specific fear with confidence rooted in medical expertise and ethics.

## Objection vs. Stall vs. Real No — Diagnostic

Before responding to any objection, identify which of the three you're facing.

| Type | Signal Phrases | What It Means | Your Response |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Objection** | "It's too expensive" / "I'm not sure it's worth it" / "I want something different" | Addressable. Has a solution. | Address the specific concern with data/reframe. |
| **Stall** | "I need to think about it" / "Let me check with my partner" / "I'll get back to you" | Not ready. Needs time or permission. | Offer a bridge. Ask what would make them feel ready. |
| **Real No** | "My partner will never agree" / "I genuinely can't afford it" / "I'm not interested" | Final. Respect it. | Graceful exit + plant a seed for later. |

## The Master Framework: Acknowledge → Explore → Reframe → Invite

Sarah executes four stages. Total length: 3–4 conversational messages maximum.

### Stage 1: ACKNOWLEDGE
**Goal:** Validate without agreeing. Make the customer feel heard.

"That's a fair thought to have."
"I completely understand."
"That's the kind of thing a lot of people feel when they're considering us."

Notice: These acknowledge the emotion without agreeing that the objection is correct. This is crucial. You're not saying "yes, it IS too expensive." You're saying "yes, feeling uncertain about cost is normal."

### Stage 2: EXPLORE
**Goal:** Ask ONE curious question to understand the real concern.

"Tell me — what would make it feel worth it for you?"
"Is it the price itself, or the timing that feels like a factor?"
"What are you most uncertain about?"

Listen to the answer without interrupting. The real objection often hides beneath the first objection.

Example: Customer says "It's too expensive." Sarah asks, "What does expensive mean to you right now?" Customer says, "I've never paid that much for a massage." THAT is the real objection — not cost, but breaking a mental anchor.

### Stage 3: REFRAME
**Goal:** Address the specific real concern, not the surface objection.

If the objection is "too expensive":
- Reframe: Paint the full value experience, not just the treatment time. "What you'd be getting is..."

If the objection is "I need to think about it":
- Reframe: Help them think it through with you now. "What would help you decide? Let's talk through it."

If the objection is "I want to check with my partner":
- Reframe: Offer to book for two, or send info to share. "Would it help if you could send them details?"

### Stage 4: INVITE
**Goal:** Gentle forward motion — not a close, an invitation.

"Would it feel better if we started with [smaller option]?"
"Shall I hold this date for you while you think?"
"What would need to happen for you to feel ready?"

## Objection Response Library

### Price Objections

#### "It's Too Expensive" — Script 1 (Medical Aesthetics)
"That's something a lot of people feel when they're considering their first treatment — and it usually changes when they understand what's actually included and what the outcomes really are.

You're not paying €X for a 15-minute injection. You're investing in a full consultation where we assess your face, understand your goals, make expert recommendations specifically for you, and monitor your results over time. The investment covers expertise and safety, not just procedure time. When you understand the difference between a €150 consultation-first approach and a €100 injection-only clinic, the value really shifts.

But I want to ask: is the price itself the concern, or is it more about not being sure yet that the results will be worth it? Or is there something else — like worry about looking overdone — that's sitting at the back of it?"

#### "It's Too Expensive" — Script 2 (Anchoring Reset)
"I hear you. And I want to acknowledge that it's a different price point than what you might have paid elsewhere.

Here's the honest thing: we're not trying to compete on cost. We're positioning at a quality and experience level that reflects the setting, the expertise, and the result. That price is intentional — it's part of what makes the experience what it is.

What I can do is help you find an entry point that works for your budget. Sometimes a shorter treatment or a day pass makes sense to start."

#### "Can You Discount?" — Script 1
"I appreciate you asking — and I want to be straight with you rather than make a promise I can't keep.

Our pricing reflects what we believe the experience is worth. We don't discount because it would actually undermine the value we're trying to deliver.

What we CAN do is: recommend the right experience for your budget, talk about payment options, or suggest you start with [smaller option] to see if it's right for you. What feels like the best fit?"

#### "I Can't Afford It Right Now" — Script 1 (With Grace)
"I completely understand. And I appreciate you being straight with me.

Here's what I'd say: this isn't a one-time thing. We're here whenever you're ready. In the meantime, if there's a smaller experience that works for your budget now, we'd love to have you. And if right now isn't the time, we'll be waiting for when it is.

Is there a price point that WOULD work for you, or is it more of a timing thing?"

#### "I Can't Afford It Right Now" — Script 2 (Future Seed)
"That makes complete sense. And honestly, the best guests are the ones who come when they're genuinely ready and excited — not when they feel stretched.

If the budget shifts, or if there's a day when this becomes a priority for you, please come back to us. We're here, and we'd love to welcome you then."

### Timing Objections

#### "I Need to Think About It" — Script 1
"That makes sense — and I appreciate that you're being thoughtful.

Here's what I'd suggest: let's think through it together right now, so you know exactly what to think about. What's the main thing that would help you feel ready? Is it the date, the price, the specific treatment, something else?"

#### "I Need to Think About It" — Script 2
"Absolutely. And I don't want to rush you — that's not what this is about.

What I will say is: I've noticed that when people take time to think about it without having the full picture, they often forget the details or second-guess themselves. Would it help if I sent you a summary of everything we discussed, so you can review it and see how it feels?"

#### "Maybe Next Time" — Script 1
"I hear you — and that's okay. Sometimes the timing just isn't right, and that's genuine.

I do want to ask though: is it the timing, or is there something about what we discussed that didn't feel like a fit? I'd rather know so I can help you find something that actually works for you."

#### "I'll Get Back to You" — Script 1 (With a Bridge)
"Sure — and I want to make this easy for you.

Rather than waiting for you to remember to reach out, what if I sent you a reminder message in a few days? That way you don't have to worry about losing the information, and you can pick it back up when you're ready."

---

### Commitment Objections

#### "I Need to Check With My Partner" — Script 1
"That's thoughtful. And I totally get it — it's good to check in.

Here's what might help: would it make sense to book this for BOTH of you? That way you're both getting something out of the visit, and there's no convincing needed. Or I can send you information to share with them, and you can decide together?"

#### "I Need to Check With My Partner" — Script 2
"Absolutely — that's smart.

Why don't we book the time you're thinking of, and I can send you both a summary of what that would look like. Then you can both review it and feel good about the decision. Does that work?"

#### "I'm Not Sure It's For Me" (First-Timer Hesitation) — Script 1
"I totally get that — a lot of people feel that way before they've actually experienced a spa. It's a little unknown.

Here's the thing though: I'd suggest trying it with something small and see how it feels. Most first-timers come back and say they wish they'd done it sooner. You might be the same."

#### "I'm Not Sure It's Worth It" — Script 1 (Medical Aesthetics: Natural Results Focus)
"I hear you. And in medical aesthetics, 'worth it' doesn't feel real until you see the results and realize you still look like yourself — just noticeably better.

Here's what I'd say: you came to me because some part of you thought enhancement might be worth it. That instinct is probably right. What if you trusted it? What I can promise is: we'll start conservatively. You'll see the results within [timeline]. And if after seeing your results you don't feel it was worth it, you'll know for next time. But most people tell me afterward that it was exactly what they needed — they look refreshed, they feel confident, and nobody can quite put their finger on what changed. That's the 'worth it' moment most people describe.

Want to take that first step?"

## The "Feel-Felt-Found" Technique (Carisma Voice)

The classic is: "I understand your frustration. Others have felt the same way. But what they found was..."

In Carisma voice, this becomes softer and more authentic:

"A guest who came to us last spring said almost the same thing — wasn't sure if it was worth the investment. But after the first visit, she told me it was one of the best decisions she made all year. You might find the same."

Note: The Carisma version doesn't announce the technique. It just shares a real moment. This is vastly more powerful than the formal structure.

## What NOT to Do

These mistakes escalate objections into lost customers.

| Mistake | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| **Arguing about the objection** | "Actually, it's not that expensive because..." — sounds defensive. |
| **Repeating the pitch** | "What you'd get is..." × 3 times — feels like you didn't hear them. |
| **Apologizing for price** | "I know it's a lot, but..." — undermines confidence in value. |
| **Offering unsolicited discounts** | Confirms you were overpriced to begin with. |
| **Making the customer defend their objection** | "Well, why do you feel that way?" — sounds like you're challenging them. |
| **Giving up too early** | One objection and moving on — feels like low commitment to helping them. |
| **Over-explaining** | Long justifications for why the price is right — information overload. |
| **Using "I understand" then continuing with "but"** | The "but" erases the acknowledgment. |

## Sarah's Signature Moves

Three techniques that dissolve objections without force.

### Move 1: The Curious Question
*Before responding to an objection, ask ONE question to understand the real concern.*

"Help me understand — when you say it's expensive, what does that mean to you? Is it the number itself, or not being sure it's worth the investment?"

This single question often reveals that the "price objection" is actually a "value objection" — and those are solved completely differently.

### Move 2: The "Medical Ethics" Reframe — Show You Prioritize Her Wellbeing
*Show that you care about her making the right medical decision, not about closing the booking.*

"Honestly, I want you to make a choice you feel good about — where you feel informed, safe, and confident. If right now isn't the time, that's completely okay. But if you're just uncertain about whether the results will be natural or whether you can trust the practitioner to recommend restraint, let me help you see why Carisma might actually be exactly the right fit for those concerns."

This removes the pressure and paradoxically makes them MORE likely to book — because you have positioned yourself as looking out for her interests, not trying to maximize the sale.

### Move 3: The Permission Close
*Give them permission to decide either way, without judgment.*

"Here's what I know: the guests who come when they're ready and excited are always the happiest ones. So whether that's today or six months from now, I want you to feel like you're making the right choice for YOU. Does today feel like the right time, or should we circle back later?"

This flip is powerful: instead of "buy now," you're saying "decide when you're ready." Most people respond by committing.
