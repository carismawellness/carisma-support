**Day 4 — February 27 (Thursday)**
- If NDA still pending: Call the advisor (not email). See script in Section 7.
- If NDA signed: Send data room request email within the same day. See Section 3 for exact email.

**Day 5 — February 28 (Friday)**

**Days 6–7 — March 1–2 (Weekend)**

### WEEK 2: Data Room Opens (Days 8–14)

**Estimated data room opening: Monday–Tuesday, March 2–3**

**Day 8 — The Day Data Arrives**
- Do NOT immediately respond with "Great, thank you!" That reads as junior.
- Wait 90 minutes after receiving the access link, then send a brief acknowledgment. See Section 4 for exact email.
- Immediately download everything. Do not rely on the portal staying up.
- Begin triage: Categorize documents in order of priority:
  1. Lease agreement and hotel management contract (deal-killer risk)
  2. Last 3 years P&L (verify the EUR 400K revenue figure)
  3. Staff contracts and payroll (therapist retention risk)
  4. Client database records (revenue concentration risk)
  5. Equipment list and condition reports
  6. Any pending litigation or regulatory notices

**Days 9–10 — Deep Analysis**
- P&L forensics: Reconstruct EBITDA from scratch. Verify every revenue and cost line. The seller's "breakeven EBITDA" claim needs stress-testing. Look for owner's personal expenses running through the business (common in small owner-operated spas). Look for one-off items that inflate the "normal" year. Look for understated depreciation.
- Lease deep-dive: Is the lease assignable? What are the hotel's rights to terminate or change terms? What happens to the lease if ownership changes? This is the single biggest deal risk. Flag immediately if lease requires hotel consent for assignment.
- Staff file review: Who are the top 2–3 therapists? Are they on contracts? Do those contracts bind to the spa entity or to the personal service of the owner? What are notice periods?
- Revenue concentration: Does any single client represent more than 10% of revenue? Any corporate accounts with the hotel directly?

**Days 11–12 — First Questions**
- Send your first formal due diligence question list on Day 11 (3 days after data room opens). See Section 4 for exact email.
- Keep questions to 3 categories: Financial, Legal/Lease, Operational. 8–12 questions total. Do not overwhelm — this is round one.
- Tone: Professional curiosity, not interrogation. You are doing normal process.

**Day 13 — Informal Check-In Call with Advisor**
- Call (not email) the M&A advisor. This is your first real information extraction call.
- See Section 7 for the exact script, including the "what does your client really need?" conversation.
- Objective: Extract the seller's true constraints. Is there a hard timeline? Personal debt? Health issue? Divorce? Other buyers? The advisor will signal something if you ask correctly.
- Do NOT discuss price on this call. That is premature.

**Day 14 — Internal Pause**
- Synthesize all findings. Map risks against your EUR 260–280K target.
- Key question to answer by Day 14: Does the lease assignment issue give you a 20–30% discount justification? If yes, that becomes your most powerful negotiating chip.
- Update your Due Diligence Risk Register (see Section 5 framework).

### WEEK 3: Pre-Offer Positioning (Days 15–21)

**Day 15 — Preliminary Findings Call**
- Request a call with the advisor to "share some preliminary observations from the data room."
- This is not an offer call. This is a reality-calibration call.
- You will share 3–4 concerns (truthfully based on data) that explain why price is a challenge.
- See Section 8 for exactly how to frame this.
- Keep tone collegial: "Look, I want this to work. I just need to walk you through what we found."

**Day 16–18 — Soften Price Expectations Indirectly**
- If the advisor is a friend of the deal, he will begin managing the seller's expectations downward after your Day 15 call. Give him 48–72 hours to do that work.
- Do NOT contact the owner during this window.
- If advisor calls you back with "seller says EUR 600K is the floor" — that means the advisor hasn't started the work yet. Push back gently: "I hear you, but the data is telling a different story. Let me put it in writing so you can share it with your client."

**Day 19 — Written Preliminary Findings (Optional but Powerful)**
- Consider sending a 1-page "Preliminary Observations" document to the advisor. Not an offer — a data-driven reality check. See Section 8 for format.
- This document does the following: anchors a lower number in the seller's mind before the formal offer arrives; gives the advisor ammunition to manage his client; shows you are serious and analytical.
- Subject line: "INA Spa — Preliminary Observations from Data Review"

**Day 20–21 — Pre-Offer Call with Advisor**
- Call the advisor. Ask directly: "Based on what I've shared, where does your client's head need to be for this to move forward?"
- This is the intelligence gathering call. He will either give you a real number ("he needs at least EUR 350K") or a fake number. Either is useful.
- If he gives you a real number below EUR 320K: you are close. Formalize the offer.
- If he gives you a number above EUR 320K: begin expectation-resetting. Do not make your offer yet.

## SECTION 7: THE ADVISOR RELATIONSHIP

### Who He Is and What He Wants

The advisor gave you his business card at the seller's first meeting. That is a signal. He is telling you: "I want to facilitate this deal." His incentive is his commission — typically 2–5% of deal value on a small transaction in Malta. At EUR 260K, that is EUR 5,200–13,000. Not life-changing, but real money for a Malta advisor on a small deal.

He is also reputation-conscious. A clean, professional deal that closes is worth more to him than a messy one where he has to fight for every euro. Use that.

**What he wants:**
1. His commission (primary)
2. A deal that closes (secondary — no commission if it falls apart)
3. The buyer and seller both feeling treated fairly (reputation protection)
4. Minimum conflict and drama
5. A buyer who is professional and makes him look good to his client

**What he fears:**
1. The deal falling apart at the last minute
2. The buyer going directly around him
3. The seller becoming irrational and blocking the deal
4. Getting caught between two intractable parties

**How you use this:**
- Make him feel like he is the key person keeping this deal alive. He is.
- Give him information he can use to manage his client.
- Make it easy for him to do his job.
- Never embarrass him in front of his client.

---

### First Informal Call Script (Call him Day 4–5, before data room opens)

**Context:** The NDA is either just signed or about to be. This is a check-in call, not a substantive call. It builds rapport before the hard work begins.

**Dial at:** 10:30 AM on a weekday. Not first thing Monday. Not Friday afternoon.

---

> "Hey [name], it's [your name] from Carisma. How are you?
>
> [Brief small talk — 2 minutes maximum. Malta weather. Mutual acquaintance if any.]
>
> Listen, I just wanted to check in before we get into the data room. I appreciate you facilitating the introduction and I want to make sure this process works well for everyone.
>
> I'll be honest with you — I like the business. I think there's something real here, especially given what Carisma could do with the clinical side. But I also need the numbers to work. I've seen too many deals fall apart because buyer and seller are too far apart and no one manages expectations early. I don't want that here.
>

>

**What you are listening for:**
- Any time pressure on the seller ("he needs to move by [date]")
- Any emotional attachment clues ("he built this from nothing, it's his baby")
- Any competing offers ("there are one or two other parties looking")
- The advisor's own assessment of the deal ("honestly, between us...")

**The phrase "I won't go around you"** is the most important line. Say it once. Mean it. It buys you loyalty from the advisor.

### The "What Does Your Client Really Need?" Conversation

**Timing:** Day 13–14 of data room (after you have completed your review, before your offer). Run this as a standalone phone call.

---

> "[Name], before we submit our formal position, I want to make sure I understand what matters most to your client — because frankly, price is often not the only thing.
>
> What I mean is: is this primarily about maximizing the total number? Or is there something else that matters — the staff being looked after, the brand continuing, a clean and quick close, a payment structure that works for his situation? Some sellers care more about certainty than price. Some care about speed.
>
> I'm not asking you to breach any confidence. I'm asking because if there's a way to structure this that works better for your client without costing me more money, I'd rather do that than have us fight over EUR 20K for two months.
>
> What can you tell me?"

**What this accomplishes:**
- Signals you are sophisticated (not just a cash-and-go buyer)
- Invites the advisor to reveal the seller's real constraints
- Opens door to non-price deal sweeteners (faster close, staff retention commitments, continuity of brand name)
- Makes the advisor feel like a trusted partner, not just a postbox

**Common answers you might get and what they mean:**

| Advisor says | What it means | Your response |
|---|---|---|
| "He just wants a fair price" | No hard constraints; normal negotiation | Proceed with standard offer |
| "He needs to close before end of Q2" | Time pressure — modest leverage | EUR 5K price reduction possible for speed |
| "He's worried about the staff" | Emotional attachment to his team | Offer staff retention commitments in writing |
| "He's still not over the EUR 800K he put in" | Sunk cost anchor — hard negotiation ahead | Empathize, but never pay for sunk costs |
| "Between us, he'd take EUR 300K" | Advisor just gave you the floor | Thank him, go to EUR 240K offer, settle at EUR 270–280K |
| "There are other parties looking" | May be true or a bluff | "Good to know — I'll make sure my offer is clean and executable" |

---

### How to Give the Advisor Incentive to Push Your Way

Never offer the advisor additional payment. That is illegal (it would breach his fiduciary duty to his client) and in a small market like Malta, word gets around.

Instead, use softer incentives:

1. **Professionalism:** Be the most professional buyer he has ever worked with. Send materials on time. Answer questions quickly. Don't create drama. He will want to work with you again.

2. **Speed:** Offer a clean, quick close. "We can close in 45 days from agreed terms with no financing contingency." The faster the deal closes, the faster he gets paid.

3. **Certainty:** Signal that you are the most certain buyer. "I am not a tire-kicker. This is my only active acquisition target." Advisors hate deals that fall apart after months of work.

4. **Future pipeline:** In your first informal call, subtly mention that Carisma is growing and may acquire more assets. "This won't be our last acquisition in Malta." He hears: future commissions.

5. **Referral:** After the deal closes (if it goes well), tell him you will refer him when Carisma does its next transaction. In a small market, that is meaningful currency.

## SECTION 8: DEAL STRUCTURE PRESENTATION

### When Presenting Findings: The Framework

Never lead with the total number. Lead with the math. Then arrive at the number. Here is why: if you say "EUR 200K" first, the seller hears "insult" and stops listening. If you walk them through the math and arrive at EUR 200K as the logical conclusion, you have a chance of being heard.

**The Framework: Four Steps**

**Step 1: Acknowledge the investment**
"I want to start by acknowledging that you've built something real here. The team is good, the client feedback is strong, and the infrastructure is in place. I respect what went into this."

Never skip this step. The seller spent EUR 800K. He needs to feel heard before he can hear anything else.

**Step 2: Separate investment from value**
"Our job in a transaction like this is to figure out what the business is worth based on what it earns today — not what it cost to build. I know that's a hard reframe. But that's how buyers and lenders look at it, and it's the only way to arrive at a number that's actually executable."

This is the most important conceptual shift. You are educating, not attacking.

**Step 3: Walk through the math**
"Here's what the data shows us: EUR 400K revenue, breakeven EBITDA, 13-year remaining lease. In spa M&A, the market pays 2–4x EBITDA for a going concern with an assignable lease. At breakeven, the honest anchor is replacement cost of equipment and goodwill — which gets us to the EUR 200–250K range."

Keep this factual. Use the data they gave you. Never make up numbers.

**Step 4: Explain what would change the number**
"The things that would move the number meaningfully: confirmed lease assignment from Numi Hotel; a 2-year P&L showing EUR 50K+ EBITDA trend; and committed staff contracts for the top two therapists. If those things are in place, the number moves."

This step is powerful because it: (a) gives the seller a path to a higher number; (b) puts conditions on that path; (c) signals you know what you are doing.

**Why this document works:**
- It is factual, not emotional
- It arrives before the formal offer, so the seller has time to process
- It demonstrates thoroughness (builds credibility)
- It anchors the range as EUR 200–260K before the formal offer reinforces EUR 200K

### "I'm Doing You a Favor" vs. "This Is Fair Market Price"

**Never use the "doing you a favor" frame.** It is condescending and immediately puts the seller on the defensive. It says: "You should be grateful I'm even here." Sellers hear: "He thinks my business is worthless."

**Always use the "fair market price" frame.** "This is what the market pays for a business at this earnings level. It is not a reflection of the value you created — it is a reflection of the cash flows a buyer is acquiring."

One additional frame that works well with emotionally invested sellers:

**The "opportunity cost" frame:**
"The question is not what you paid — the question is what you could do with EUR 260K today versus continuing to hold an asset that is generating zero return. If you take EUR 260K and put it into [something with yield], you have something working for you. Right now this business is working against you at breakeven."

This reframes the sale from "losing money on my investment" to "making a smart capital allocation decision." Sellers who have been anchored on their EUR 800K sunk cost often respond to this.

## SECTION 9: COUNTER-OFFER MECHANICS

### Expected Counter Ranges and Responses

**Scenario A: Seller counters at EUR 500K–650K**

Response timing: Wait exactly 48 hours. Not 24 — too eager. Not 72 — appears disengaged.

Response approach:
- Do not write an emotional response. Write a factual one.
- Move your offer to EUR 220K–230K. Small movement, justified by data.
- Accompany with a short paragraph referencing one or two data room observations that support the number.
- Email, not phone. You want this in writing.

> "[Advisor first name], thank you for the response.
>
> We've considered the counter carefully. Our analysis of the data room — particularly [lease assignment uncertainty / EBITDA trending near breakeven / equipment condition] — constrains our ability to move significantly from our opening position. However, in the spirit of progress, we are prepared to move to EUR 225,000.
>
> We believe this reflects a fair position given the current earnings profile and the risk items we have identified. We remain ready to move quickly to close.
>
> Best, [name]"

---

**Scenario B: Seller counters at EUR 350K–450K**

This is closer to the zone. Response timing: 24 hours.

Response approach:
- Move to EUR 250K–260K.
- Explicitly acknowledge the movement in their direction.
- Signal you are getting closer to your ceiling.

> "Thank you — we appreciate the movement. We have considerable respect for what your client has built. We are prepared to move our position to EUR 255,000. I want to be transparent: we are approaching the upper boundary of what our analysis supports. I'd like to find a way to close the remaining gap if we can."

---

**Scenario C: Seller counters at EUR 280K–320K**

You are in the zone. Response timing: 48 hours (do not appear too eager even when you are close).

Response approach:
- If counter is EUR 280K: split the difference at EUR 260–270K and signal this is final.
- If counter is EUR 300K–320K: move to EUR 270–280K. This is your target range. Accept if they hold at EUR 300K max — that is only EUR 20K above your EUR 280K target. Consider a meet-in-the-middle close.

### When to Introduce the "Parents" Constraint

"The parents" is a classic negotiation technique: you introduce a higher authority whose approval you need, giving you cover to hold a position without appearing stubborn.

For a first-time acquirer, this is naturally the investment committee, board, or co-investors. Use it when:
- You have reached your target price and need to hold firm without closing the door
- The seller is pushing for EUR 300K+ and you need to buy time
- You want to signal that your number is not arbitrary — it has been approved by someone above you

**Script:**
> "I hear you. I need to be honest — I've brought this to my partners, and they have signed off on a ceiling. I am not in a position to go above EUR [X] without going back to them for additional approval, and frankly I don't think I can get that. Is there any flexibility on your side? Because I want to make this work within the parameters I have."

Use this once. Using it twice loses credibility.

---

### When to Make a "Final Offer"

Use the phrase "final offer" only when you mean it. Reserve it for the moment when you are at your ceiling and genuinely prepared to walk away if rejected.

**Timing:** No earlier than Round 3 of negotiation. Typically Day 28–31.

**Script:**
> "[Advisor first name], I want to be straight with you. We have worked hard on this and I believe we've been fair and constructive throughout. I am prepared to move to EUR [280K] — that is our best and final position. I am not in a position to go above this number. I wanted to give you and your client a clear decision point rather than have us continue going back and forth. Please let me know by [specific date — 5 business days] so we can move to documentation or part ways cleanly."

The phrase "part ways cleanly" is important. It signals respect and no hard feelings. It leaves the door open for a return if the seller cannot find another buyer.

---

### Walk-Away Script (If Seller Holds Above EUR 320K)

If the seller is holding above EUR 320K after three rounds of negotiation, you are beyond your walk-away. Exit professionally.

**Do NOT:**
- Burn the relationship
- Make threats
- Send a long email explaining your analysis again
- Make the advisor feel bad

**Script (via phone call, followed by confirmation email):**

> "Look, I want to be respectful of your time and your client's time. We've made our best effort here and the gap is simply too wide for us to bridge at this point. Our analysis leads us to a different place than your client's expectations, and I don't see that changing in the near term.
>
> I genuinely respect what he's built. If his situation changes — if he wants to revisit this conversation in 6 months — we're open to it. No hard feelings on our end. I hope you feel we've been professional throughout."

**Confirmation email (same day):**

> "[Advisor first name], following our call — I wanted to confirm that we are stepping back from the INA Spa process. We've truly enjoyed getting to know the business, and I have the utmost respect for what has been built there. If circumstances change and your client wants to revisit a conversation, we are genuinely open to it. Thank you for your professionalism throughout.
>
> Best, [name]"

**Why write this email:** In Malta's small business community, word travels. This email protects your reputation and leaves the door genuinely open. Deals that "die" at first attempt often revive 3–12 months later when sellers realize their asking price does not match the market. You want to be the buyer they call when that moment arrives.

## SECTION 10: CLOSING MECHANICS

### Phase 1: Price Agreement to LOI (Days 1–5 post-agreement)

**Day of agreement:** Confirm verbally on a phone call. Send email same day confirming the agreed number and key terms. Keep email short — this is a heads-up, not a legal document.

> "[Advisor first name], great speaking. To confirm: we are in agreement at EUR [X], subject to satisfactory completion of legal documentation. I will have our lawyer reach out to yours to begin the SPA. Good deal."

**Days 2–5:** Brief LOI (Letter of Intent) — 2–3 pages maximum. This is not the SPA. It is a non-binding expression of agreed terms that protects both parties while the SPA is drafted.

LOI should include:
- Agreed purchase price
- Structure (asset purchase vs. share purchase — see below)
- Payment terms (100% cash at closing, or structure if any)
- Conditions precedent
- Exclusivity period (30 days — prevents seller from shopping while you complete legal DD)
- Expected closing date
- Governing law (Maltese law)

---

### Asset Deal vs. Share Deal

For a sub-EUR 500K deal in Malta at this size:

**Recommend an asset purchase, not a share purchase.**

Reasons:
- You acquire only the assets you want (equipment, lease, IP, client database), not the legal entity with all its historical liabilities
- Cleaner from a tax and legal standpoint for the buyer
- Standard for small spa/operator transactions
- Simpler documentation

Assets to acquire:
- Lease rights (or new lease from hotel — prefer new lease to avoid inherited liabilities)
- Spa equipment (valued individually)
- Client database (GDPR-compliant transfer)
- Brand name and any IP (if included — negotiate)
- Staff transfer under Maltese employment law (TUPE-equivalent)
- Existing supplier contracts (novation of key ones)

Assets you do NOT want to acquire:
- Any historical tax liabilities
- Any pending litigation
- Personal goodwill of the owner (this walks out the door regardless)

---

### SPA Structure for a Sub-EUR 500K Asset Deal in Malta

The Sale and Purchase Agreement for an asset deal at this size should be drafted by a Maltese lawyer. Budget EUR 3,000–6,000 in legal fees.

Key clauses:

**1. Purchase Price and Payment**
- Full cash at closing
- No deferred consideration unless you specifically negotiate an earnout (not recommended at this size — creates post-closing disputes)
- Payment via bank transfer on completion day

**2. Conditions Precedent**
- Lease assignment or new lease from Numi Hotel (critical)
- Staff transition agreements executed
- Clear title to all equipment confirmed
- No material adverse change between LOI and closing

**3. Representations and Warranties**
- Seller warrants: No undisclosed liabilities; P&L accurate; all disclosed contracts valid and transferable; no pending litigation
- Keep reps and warranties simple — this is not a EUR 10M deal

**4. Indemnification**
- Seller indemnifies buyer for any pre-closing liabilities that were not disclosed
- Cap indemnification at purchase price (EUR [X])
- 12-month survival period — standard for a transaction this size

**5. Non-Compete**
- Seller agrees not to operate a competing spa business in Malta for 2 years post-closing
- This is standard and enforceable under Maltese law in the context of a business sale

**6. Transition Services**
- Seller agrees to provide 30 days of handover support post-closing (introductions to key clients, staff training, operational handover)
- Compensated at a nominal daily rate (EUR 200–300/day) or included in purchase price depending on negotiation

---

### Escrow or No Escrow

**At EUR 260–280K with a funded cash buyer: No escrow needed.**

Escrow is appropriate when:
- Deal is above EUR 1M
- Payment is partially deferred
- There are complex earnout arrangements
- Seller's creditworthiness for indemnification is uncertain

At this deal size, the SPA indemnification provisions provide adequate protection. Paying a third-party escrow agent adds EUR 3,000–5,000 in fees and 2–3 weeks of delay for no meaningful benefit.

### The Advisor's Role at Closing

The M&A advisor's active role ends once price is agreed and LOI is signed. From that point, his role is:
- Available for minor interventions if seller gets cold feet
- Available if any last-minute issues arise during SPA negotiation
- Present at closing if desired (his call)

His commission is typically paid at closing out of the seller's proceeds. Confirm this structure early — in some cases small Malta advisors want partial payment at LOI stage. If he asks for that: agree to 50% at LOI, 50% at closing. This aligns his incentive to push the deal through to completion.

## FINAL NOTES FROM THE DESK

**Three things that kill small deals at this stage:**

1. **Impatience.** Small sellers are not investment bankers. They move slowly. Chasing them too hard signals desperation and invites them to negotiate harder. Build silences into your timeline deliberately.

2. **Going around the advisor.** Malta is a small market. The advisor has one client in this deal and possibly knows two dozen others. If you undercut him, the story travels. Route everything through the advisor unless he specifically invites direct contact.

3. **Forgetting the emotional dimension.** The seller spent EUR 800K and eight-plus years on this business. He does not need you to validate the bad investment decision — he needs to feel that the sale is a dignified conclusion to that chapter, not a defeat. Every communication should respect the human being behind the transaction. That is not sentimentality. It is deal mechanics. Sellers who feel respected close. Sellers who feel cornered walk.

**The number that matters most is not the price. It is the lease. Confirm the lease assignment early. If Numi Hotel will not approve the assignment, you do not have a deal at any price. Make this the first resolved condition before you spend another hour on this process.**
