## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The EUR 140K gap between your target (EUR 260-280K) and the seller's implied anchor (EUR 400K) is not primarily a financial gap. It is a psychological one. The seller is not wrong about his revenue. He is confused about what revenue means in the context of a sale. Your job is not to argue against his number — it is to make him feel that his number and your number are measuring different things entirely. This memo gives you the frameworks, language, and scripts to do that.

---

## 1. WHY SELLERS ANCHOR ON REVENUE, NOT EBITDA

Revenue is a number that sellers understand viscerally. It is the money that has passed through the business. It feels real because they watched it arrive — payments processed, invoices settled, bank account populated. EBITDA, by contrast, is an abstraction. It requires subtraction, categorization, and accounting judgment. For a small business owner who has never sold a company before, EBITDA is what accountants talk about. Revenue is what the business actually did.

The cognitive mechanism at work is **availability heuristic** — people assign importance to information that is most mentally accessible. A spa owner knows his revenue intimately. He probably quotes it proudly. "We do EUR 400K a year." It has become his identity metric, the number he uses to describe the business to friends, family, and potential partners. EBITDA requires him to remember operating costs, depreciation schedules, and lease obligations. Revenue requires him to remember nothing — it is already in his head.

There is also a deeper mechanism: **narrative compression**. When sellers think about what their business is worth, they are not running a discounted cash flow model. They are telling themselves a story. "I built something that does EUR 400K a year. That is worth at least as much as one year of what it produces." This story is intuitive, satisfying, and wrong — but it is also deeply human. You will not dislodge it with spreadsheets. You will only dislodge it by offering a better story.

The "1x revenue" rule is the most common valuation error in small business sales precisely because it sounds logical. Revenue is real. One times anything sounds conservative. It does not sound greedy. It sounds reasonable, which is the most dangerous kind of wrong.

---

## 2. THE SELLER'S INTERNAL MATH: RECONSTRUCTING HIS EUR 400K

To negotiate effectively, you need to understand exactly how he arrived at his number, because his number is not arbitrary. It is the output of several compounding biases:

**Sunk cost fallacy.** He invested EUR 800K in capex. He is looking at a number on his personal balance sheet that says he is already EUR 400K underwater at your offer. He is not asking "what is this business worth?" He is asking "how do I minimize my loss?" These are completely different questions that produce completely different anchor points. The sunk cost is not an asset. It is a sunken boat. But to him, it feels like a down payment on your offer.

**Endowment effect.** Research by Kahneman and Thaler consistently shows that people value things they own at approximately 2x the price they would pay for the same item as a buyer. The seller has been inside this business every day. He knows every treatment room, every staff member, every regular client. His ownership has inflated his valuation by a factor that has nothing to do with financial performance.

**Loss aversion.** Selling at EUR 260K does not feel like gaining EUR 260K. It feels like losing EUR 140K from EUR 400K. Losses are psychologically weighted at roughly 2-2.5x the equivalent gain. He is not comparing your offer to zero. He is comparing it to his mental anchor, and the gap feels like a wound.

**Round number bias.** EUR 400K is a round, clean, satisfying number. EUR 260K is not. Round numbers feel more legitimate in negotiation because they imply deliberate calculation rather than opportunistic positioning. Your offer at EUR 260-280K should be framed carefully — either as a round number (EUR 275K) or as a specific number with a visible rationale (EUR 267K because of X, Y, Z), never as a vague range that looks like you are fishing.

## 3. THE "REVENUE EQUALS VALUE" ILLUSION

Here is the analogy you should use in conversation, and it works because it is true:

> "Imagine two taxis. Both drive exactly 100,000 km per year. One has a full tank, maintained engine, and earns the driver EUR 40,000 a year after costs. The other has a cracked engine block, bald tyres, needs a new transmission, and after fuel and repairs, the driver breaks even. If you were buying one of those taxis, would you pay the same price for both because they both drove 100,000 km?"

Revenue is kilometres driven. It tells you how busy the business is. It tells you nothing about what arrives at the end of the journey. A EUR 400K revenue business at breakeven is not "worth EUR 400K with the profit to be unlocked." It is a business that is consuming EUR 400K in resources to generate EUR 400K in output. The value of the asset is in what it earns, not what it turns over.

The specific dynamic here is worse still: the spa has 14 staff for 7 rooms. Industry benchmarks suggest 6-8 staff for that room count. The seller has a structural cost problem embedded in his operations. Whoever buys this business is buying a turnaround, not a cash machine.

---

## 4. REFRAMING FROM REVENUE TO EARNINGS: THE DIALOGUE

Your goal is to get the seller to answer the question "what does this business earn?" before you ever discuss price again. Here is the sequence:

**Step 1: Acknowledge the revenue without disputing it.**
"EUR 400K in revenue for a 7-room spa in Malta — that's genuinely strong top-line performance. You've built real client volume. That matters."

**Step 2: Introduce the earnings frame as a shared interest.**
"The question I always ask when I'm looking at a business — and I think you'd ask the same if you were in my position — is: what does this business earn? Because what I'm buying is not revenue. I'm buying future income."

**Step 3: Walk through the numbers together, not at him.**
"Can we look at this together? EUR 400K revenue. Staff costs for 14 people — conservatively EUR 280K. Hotel lease at 5% revenue — EUR 20K. Consumables, product, utilities — call it EUR 60K. What's left?"

**Step 4: Let him say the number.**
Do not say "zero." Let silence do the work. Ask: "So what does that leave us with?" If he deflects, say: "I'm not making an argument. I'm trying to understand what I'm actually buying."

---

## 5. THE EMOTIONAL GAP: WHAT IS HE REALLY PROTECTING?

The EUR 140K gap is not about EUR 140K. You need to diagnose what narrative the seller needs to preserve. There are three common patterns:

**The Identity Narrative.** He built something. He poured EUR 800K into it. Selling for EUR 260K means admitting the project failed. The price becomes a verdict on his judgment as an entrepreneur. If this is the dynamic, your approach is to reframe the sale as intelligent capital recycling, not defeat. "You built a client base and a brand in a hotel format that doesn't match your ambitions. That's not failure — that's a pivot. The question is whether you want to spend the next 13 years of your lease proving a point or deploying your equity into something better structured."

**The Social Narrative.** He needs to be able to tell people he sold for a good price. EUR 260K is hard to say out loud. EUR 400K is satisfying to say. This is where deal structure becomes your tool (see Section 6).

**The Justice Narrative.** He genuinely believes he deserves more because he worked hard, invested heavily, and built something real. This is the most emotionally charged version and requires explicit validation before any reframing. "I understand what you put in. EUR 800K in capex, years of operations, the team you've built. That's real. My job is not to dismiss any of that. My job is to be honest with you about what a buyer can pay and still make the business work."

## 6. THE HEADLINE NUMBER SOLUTION

If the seller needs to tell people he sold for EUR 400K, you can structure a deal that creates that headline while delivering EUR 260-280K in economic value.

**Structure option:**

- **EUR 260K cash at completion** — clean, immediate, bankable
- **EUR 80K earnout** — paid over 24 months if revenue stays above EUR 380K threshold (he controls for 24 months in transition, threshold is achievable but not guaranteed, you are comfortable paying it if the business performs)
- **EUR 60K deferred payment** — tied to staff retention and client base transfer milestones at 6 and 12 months

**Total headline: EUR 400K.**
**Expected economic cost to buyer: EUR 260-300K** depending on business performance.

This structure does several things simultaneously. It gives him his EUR 400K headline number for social purposes. It aligns incentives during transition — he has reason to cooperate on client handover and staff retention. It shifts risk appropriately — he only earns the full number if the business he is selling actually delivers what he claims it delivers. And it gives you downside protection on the EUR 260K base if the transition goes poorly.

Present this as: "What if we structured something where you could honestly say you sold for EUR 400K? Let me show you how that works."

## 7. THREE SCRIPTS FOR THE REVENUE OBJECTION

**When he says: "But my business does EUR 400K in revenue. You're offering me less than that."**

**Script A — The Taxi Analogy**
> "You're right that it does EUR 400K in revenue, and that number is real. But let me ask you something — if two taxis both drive 100,000 km a year, but one earns its driver EUR 40,000 and the other just breaks even, do they have the same value? Revenue tells me how busy the business is. What I'm buying is what the business earns. And right now, what it earns is zero. I'm not saying you haven't built something real. I'm saying what I can pay is based on what I can earn back."

**Script B — The Honest Partnership Frame**
> "I want to be straight with you, because I think you deserve that. If I pay EUR 400K for a business that earns nothing, I have to earn back EUR 400K from operations before I make a single euro in profit. At current margins, that takes longer than the lease. I can't make that work, and I don't think you'd want me to start this partnership by making a promise we both know I can't keep. What I can pay is what the earnings justify, and I want to find a structure where you feel good about what you walk away with."

**Script C — The Reframe to the Future**
> "Let me ask you something differently. Forget for a moment what the business has done. What do you think it will earn over the next five years? If we can agree on that number, we can work backwards to a price we both feel is fair. Because what you're selling me is not last year's revenue — it's next year's income, and the year after that, and the year after that. That's what we're both pricing."

---

## CLOSING NOTE

The seller's EUR 400K is not a negotiating position. It is a psychological fortress built from sunk costs, endowment effects, and a narrative he has been telling himself for years. You cannot storm that fortress with financial logic. You enter it by acknowledging what he built, validating what he invested, and then — gently, precisely — helping him see that what he has built and what that building is worth are two completely different questions.

The goal of every conversation is not to win an argument. It is to give him permission to accept less without feeling that he has lost.

That permission, once granted, is worth far more than any counterargument.
