# PART 1: DEAL STRUCTURE OPTIONS

## 1.1 The Fundamental Question: Share Purchase vs. Asset Purchase

There are three possible structures for this deal. Each has materially different implications for the lease, for tax, for liability, and for Mert's two deal breakers (sublease rights and hair salon conversion). I analyze each exhaustively below.

### OPTION A: Asset Purchase (Current Term Sheet Structure)

**How it works:** Carisma buys the business assets -- equipment, goodwill, client database, inventory, IP -- from Well Being Services Limited. The lease is assigned from Well Being Services Limited to Carisma (or a Carisma nominee company). Well Being Services Limited continues to exist as a shell.

**Lease implications:**
- Requires formal lease assignment under Clause 5.26
- Landlord consent is required and can be withheld "in their absolute discretion"
- This is a direct trigger -- there is no ambiguity
- Assignment creates a tripartite agreement (seller, buyer, landlord)
- The assignment negotiation is the natural moment to renegotiate ALL lease terms

**Tax implications (Malta-specific):**
- Transfer of a Going Concern (TOGC) -- if structured correctly under Article 13(2) of the VAT Act (Cap. 406), no VAT applies on the transfer. If TOGC fails, 18% VAT on EUR 250K = EUR 45K immediate cash flow hit (recoverable but painful)
- Stamp duty applies: 2% on goodwill, 5% on any component treated as immovable property interest. Optimal purchase price allocation between equipment (depreciable, attracts capital allowances) and goodwill (non-depreciable) must be modeled jointly with your accountant and notary
- Capital allowances on equipment: 20% initial allowance + 20% annual wear-and-tear under the Sixth Schedule to the Income Tax Act. On EUR 120-150K allocated to equipment, this generates EUR 24-30K in Year 1 deductions
- Malta Enterprise Investment Aid Tax Credits (IATC) may apply to clinic capex: 25-50% tax credit on qualifying investment = EUR 20-37.5K on EUR 80K clinic investment

**Liability implications:**
- Clean break from Well Being Services Limited's historical liabilities
- No inherited tax disputes, regulatory issues, employment tribunal claims, or contractual obligations
- Cherry-pick assets: take the Candela laser, leave any problematic contracts
- Hidden liabilities remain with the seller's company

**Practical implications:**
- TUPE transfer of all employees under S.L. 452.85 -- all 13 employees transfer automatically on existing terms
- New insurance policies required in Carisma's name
- New security deposit required (3 months' rent as banker's guarantee: approximately EUR 20-22K)
- Regulatory licenses (Malta Medicines Authority for Candela laser) may need new registration
- All supplier contracts need consent for assignment

**VERDICT ON OPTION A:** This is the cleanest structure for Carisma. The liability ring-fence is worth the hassle of the assignment process. The assignment negotiation creates a natural opportunity to renegotiate lease terms, which is critical for Mert's deal breakers.

### OPTION B: Share Purchase (Buying Well Being Services Limited, C 49894)

**How it works:** Carisma (or Mert personally) purchases 100% of the shares of Well Being Services Limited from John Muscat. The company continues to exist with the same lease, same employees, same contracts. Only the shareholder changes.

**CRITICAL ANALYSIS -- Does Clause 5.26's "Change of Control" Still Trigger?**

**YES. Unambiguously yes.**

The lease definition of "Change in Control" is: *"a change in the direct or indirect ownership or right to exercise (directly or indirectly) the majority of the owners' or members' or shareholders' voting rights in a company, whether the change is single or cumulative; and for the purposes of this definition, the provisions of the Ninth Schedule to the Companies Act (Chapter 386 of the Laws of Malta) shall mutatis mutandis apply."*

If Mert buys 100% of the shares of Well Being Services Limited from John Muscat, this is a textbook Change of Control. The "direct ownership" of the "majority of the shareholders' voting rights" changes from John Muscat to Mert/Carisma. This is "deemed to be an assignment without the prior written consent of the Lessor" under Clause 5.26.

**There is no structural workaround that avoids triggering this clause when 100% of shares transfer.**

The reference to the Ninth Schedule of the Companies Act (which deals with the interpretation of beneficial ownership and control) means that the clause catches both direct AND indirect changes. Using a holding company structure, a nominee arrangement, or any intermediary does not avoid the trigger -- the Ninth Schedule traces through corporate layers to the ultimate beneficial owner.

**The bottom line: share purchase requires exactly the same landlord consent as asset purchase.** The perceived advantage of share purchase -- avoiding the assignment trigger -- does not exist under this lease.

**Tax implications:**
- No stamp duty on share transfer in Malta (shares in Maltese companies are exempt from duty on documents)
- No TOGC issue (no asset transfer, so no VAT)
- BUT: no capital allowances step-up. Carisma inherits the company's existing depreciation schedule. Equipment that has been depreciated down to EUR 50K book value cannot be stepped up to EUR 150K fair market value. This eliminates approximately EUR 20-30K of tax deductions over 5 years
- The 5% effective tax rate (via 6/7ths imputation refund) applies regardless of structure
- Notional Interest Deduction (NID) may not apply as favorably -- the equity injection is for shares, not risk capital deployed in the business

**Liability implications -- THIS IS THE CRITICAL RISK:**
- Carisma inherits ALL liabilities of Well Being Services Limited -- known AND unknown
- Any outstanding VAT, income tax, social security obligations transfer with the company
- Any pending or threatened litigation becomes Carisma's problem
- Any employment tribunal claims in progress transfer
- Any environmental or regulatory compliance failures transfer
- The excluded costs (director's wage, marketing) may indicate broader accounting irregularities. In a share purchase, those irregularities are now inside Carisma's corporate perimeter
- Given that 109 of 136 DD items remain outstanding, buying a company with unverified liabilities is reckless

**Practical implications:**
- Marginally simpler operationally -- no need to transfer licenses, contracts, or insurance (they stay with the company)
- Employees remain employed by the same legal entity (no TUPE notification required)
- Banking relationships, POS systems, supplier accounts continue unchanged
- BUT: the company's credit history, any defaults, and any guarantees also transfer

**VERDICT ON OPTION B:** Share purchase provides no advantage for lease purposes (Change of Control still triggers) and creates massive liability risk. The only advantage is avoiding stamp duty (saving approximately EUR 5-10K) and operational simplicity. The liability exposure far outweighs this saving. **Not recommended.**

### OPTION C: Minority Stake Structure -- Can Mert Avoid Triggering Change of Control?

**The question:** Could Mert structure the deal so John Muscat retains a minority stake, keeping John as the "majority" owner on paper while Mert has effective operational control?

**Theoretical structure:**
- Mert/Carisma acquires 49% of shares in Well Being Services Limited
- John Muscat retains 51% of shares
- A shareholders' agreement gives Carisma full operational control, management authority, board appointment rights, and a call option to acquire the remaining 51% after a defined period (e.g., 2-3 years)
- The call option would be at a pre-agreed price (or formula)

**Does this avoid the Change of Control trigger?**

**Technically, possibly. Practically, it is dangerous and likely to fail.**

The clause requires "a change in the direct or indirect ownership or right to exercise (directly or indirectly) the majority of the... voting rights." If John holds 51% of the voting rights, his majority has not changed. On a purely literal reading, no Change of Control has occurred.

**However, there are five serious problems:**

**Problem 1: Substance over form.** The lease references the Ninth Schedule to the Companies Act *mutatis mutandis*. The Ninth Schedule deals with beneficial ownership and control. If a shareholders' agreement gives Carisma effective management control, board appointment rights, profit-sharing rights, and a call option on the remaining shares, a competent landlord's lawyer would argue that the "right to exercise... indirectly... the majority of the... voting rights" has effectively transferred to Carisma, even if legal title to 51% of shares remains with John. The call option itself may be treated as an indirect right to exercise the majority of voting rights in the future. Malta's courts would likely look at the substance of the arrangement, not just the form.

**Problem 2: The landlord is not stupid.** The Zammit Tabona family has been operating commercial property in Sliema for decades. Their lawyers drafted a Change of Control clause that specifically references indirect ownership and the Ninth Schedule. They know what they are doing. If Carisma approaches the spa as the new operator, rebrands it, installs Carisma management, and John Muscat is nowhere to be seen, the landlord will know a Change of Control has occurred regardless of what the share register says. At that point, the landlord can claim a breach of Clause 5.26 (deemed assignment without consent) and trigger termination under Clause 9.1.2 ("assigns, sub-lets, disposes of, or otherwise ceases to occupy or to control the Premises or any other part in breach of this Lease").

**Problem 3: The call option creates a ticking bomb.** When Carisma eventually exercises the call option (acquiring the remaining 51%), that IS a Change of Control. The landlord consent that was avoided at Step 1 must now be obtained at Step 2. If the landlord refuses at that point, Carisma is stuck in a perpetual minority position with John Muscat. If Mert has already invested EUR 250K+ and built out the clinic, the leverage has shifted entirely to the landlord and to John.

**Problem 4: John Muscat becomes a permanent partner.** In the minority stake structure, John retains 51% of the company. He has fiduciary duties as a majority shareholder. He can veto extraordinary decisions. If the relationship deteriorates, Carisma is trapped in a joint venture with a majority shareholder who has veto power. There is no clean exit without John's cooperation or a court order. In Malta's small business community, this creates a dependency that is commercially intolerable.

**Problem 5: It poisons the landlord relationship from day one.** If the landlord later discovers that the minority stake structure was designed to circumvent the Change of Control clause, the relationship is permanently damaged. The landlord will view Carisma as a party that tried to deceive them. Every future negotiation -- sublease consent, alteration consent, lease extension -- will be poisoned by this perception. Given that Mert needs the landlord's cooperation on sublease rights and the hair salon conversion, starting the relationship with deception is catastrophic.

**VERDICT ON OPTION C:** The minority stake structure is a legal time bomb disguised as a clever workaround. It avoids the literal trigger of Change of Control but creates five compounding risks that are far worse than simply obtaining landlord consent. **Absolutely not recommended.**

## 1.2 Recommended Structure: Asset Purchase with Negotiated Lease Assignment

The asset purchase is the correct structure. Here is why, summarized:

| Factor | Asset Purchase | Share Purchase | Minority Stake |
|--------|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| Requires landlord consent | Yes | Yes (Change of Control) | Technically no, but practically yes |
| Creates lease renegotiation opportunity | **Yes** | No | No |
| Clean liability ring-fence | **Yes** | No | No |
| Capital allowances step-up | **Yes** | No | No |
| Stamp duty cost | EUR 5-10K | EUR 0 | EUR 0 |
| Operational simplicity | Medium | High | Low |
| Risk of landlord relationship damage | Low | Low | **Very High** |
| Tax optimization flexibility | **High** | Medium | Low |
| Recommended | **YES** | No | Absolutely Not |

**The EUR 5-10K stamp duty cost of asset purchase is the cheapest insurance policy in this deal.** It buys a clean liability ring-fence, a capital allowances step-up worth EUR 20-30K over 5 years, and -- most importantly -- a natural negotiation moment with the landlord to secure sublease rights and the hair salon conversion.

# PART 3: STRUCTURING THE SUBLEASE RIGHT

## 3.1 Why This Matters

Mert has identified sublease rights as a deal breaker. The ability to sublease portions of the spa premises -- for example, creating a nail salon concession, a retail beauty space, or a specialist treatment room -- is integral to Carisma's operating model and revenue optimization strategy.

Clause 5.27 of the lease currently prohibits subletting without "the prior written consent of the Lessor." Unlike the non-structural alterations clause (5.9), there is NO "not to be unreasonably withheld" standard. The landlord can refuse subletting consent for any reason.

## 3.2 Common Structures in Hotel-Spa Agreements

In my experience across 200+ hotel-spa lease negotiations in Europe, the following sublease structures have successfully balanced tenant operational flexibility with landlord control:

**Structure 1: Pre-Approved Sublease Categories**

The lease amendment lists specific categories of sub-tenant that are pre-approved: nail salon/manicure-pedicure operators, beauty/skincare retail concessions, medical aesthetics practitioners, nutrition/wellness consultants, physiotherapy/rehabilitation services.

For any sub-tenant within these pre-approved categories, the lessee does not need additional consent, provided: (a) the sub-tenant meets minimum quality standards defined in the amendment, (b) the sublease term does not exceed the remaining term of the head lease, (c) the lessee remains fully liable for all head lease obligations, and (d) the landlord receives copies of all sublease agreements within 14 days of execution.

**Why this works for the landlord:** They have pre-vetted the categories and retain quality control. They are not giving blanket consent -- they are consenting to specific, defined uses that enhance the property.

**Likelihood of success here: 40-50%.** The Fortina lease is conservatively drafted. Pre-approved categories represent a meaningful concession. The landlord may agree if the categories are narrowly defined and the quality standards are high.

**Structure 2: Landlord Veto with Deemed Consent**

The lessee may sublease up to 20% of the lettable area (approximately 100 sqm of 510 sqm) subject to: (a) giving the landlord 30 days' written notice with full details of the proposed sub-tenant and sublease terms, and (b) the landlord having the right to object within 30 days on reasonable grounds. If the landlord does not object within 30 days, consent is deemed granted.

**Why this works for the landlord:** They retain a veto, but the veto must be exercised -- passive silence equals consent. This prevents indefinite delay. The "reasonable grounds" standard is enforceable in Malta and prevents arbitrary refusal.

**Likelihood of success here: 30-40%.** This is a more aggressive ask. The current lease gives absolute discretion. Moving to "reasonable grounds" plus deemed consent is a significant shift in the landlord's position.

**Structure 3: Revenue Transparency Model**

The lessee may sublease portions of the premises provided: (a) the landlord receives monthly statements of sublease income, (b) the 5% turnover fee under Clause 3.1 applies to ALL revenue generated on the premises, including sublease income (or a share thereof), (c) the sub-tenant operates within the Permitted Use definition, and (d) the landlord approves each sub-tenant (consent not to be unreasonably withheld).

**Why this works for the landlord:** The landlord captures additional revenue from subleasing through the enhanced turnover fee. Every euro of sublease income generates 5 cents for the landlord. The economic incentive is aligned.

**Likelihood of success here: 50-60%.** This is the most likely structure to succeed because it gives the landlord a financial upside from subleasing. The landlord makes MORE money when Carisma subleases, not less. Present it as: "We are expanding the revenue base from which your turnover fee is calculated."

## 3.3 What Commercial Concessions Might Unlock Sublease Rights

The landlord will not grant sublease rights for free. What can Mert offer in return?

| Concession from Carisma | Value to Landlord | Cost to Carisma |
|---|---|---|
| Increase the minimum sales threshold for turnover fee from EUR 350K to EUR 400K | EUR 2,500/year additional guaranteed turnover fee | EUR 2,500/year |
| Commit to EUR 100K minimum renovation investment within 12 months | Property improvement at Carisma's cost | Already planned (clinic buildout) |
| Extend the lease commitment: waive the "di rispetto" exit right for the first 3 years of Carisma's tenure | Landlord gets 3 years of guaranteed tenancy | Limits Carisma's exit flexibility |
| Offer the landlord a 10% share of net sublease income (on top of the 5% turnover fee) | Additional income stream | Modest cost on relatively small sublease revenue |
| Agree to a personal guarantee by Mert for 24 months | Additional security for rent obligations | Significant personal exposure |

**Recommended package:** Offer the revenue transparency model (Structure 3) combined with: increased minimum sales threshold to EUR 400K, EUR 100K renovation commitment, and 10% landlord share of net sublease income. This gives the landlord three financial improvements in exchange for sublease flexibility.

# PART 4: THE HAIR SALON CONVERSION

## 4.1 The Legal Argument

Converting the existing hair salon space into another spa/wellness service is a **non-structural internal alteration** under Clause 5.9 of the lease.

**Clause 5.9 states:** "Not to divide the Premises nor to make or permit to be made any alterations or additions or improvements to the Premises or change or permit to be changed the design, layout and appearance of the Premises except with the prior written consent of the Lessor and in accordance with plans and specifications (to be submitted in duplicate) approved in writing by the Lessor in advance of commencement of the works, **which consent and approval shall not be unreasonably withheld in the case of non-structural internal alterations only** but which consent may otherwise be withheld or granted subject to such conditions as the Lessor may in their sole discretion consider appropriate."

**Key legal analysis:**

Converting a hair salon to a treatment room, consultation room, or retail space involves:
- Potentially removing or relocating a wash basin (non-structural plumbing modification)
- Repainting and redecorating walls (cosmetic, non-structural)
- Installing new furniture and equipment (fixtures and fittings, non-structural)
- Potentially adding partition walls (non-structural internal)
- Updating electrical outlets and lighting (non-structural)
- Changing signage (governed separately by Clause 5.10)

None of these works are structural. They do not affect load-bearing walls, foundations, the building envelope, roofing, or the structural integrity of the premises. They are internal modifications to the layout and fit-out of the space.

**Therefore, under Clause 5.9, the landlord's consent cannot be unreasonably withheld.**

Under Maltese law (and following the general principles of European commercial lease law), a landlord who unreasonably withholds consent to a non-structural internal alteration is in breach of the lease obligation. The tenant can apply to the Maltese courts for a declaration that the landlord's refusal is unreasonable and for an order compelling consent.

**What constitutes "unreasonable" withholding in Malta:**
- Refusing consent without providing any reason
- Refusing consent on grounds unrelated to the property or the lease (e.g., personal dislike of the tenant)
- Refusing consent when the proposed works will improve the property
- Imposing conditions that are disproportionate to the nature of the works

**What constitutes "reasonable" withholding:**
- The proposed works would reduce the property's value
- The proposed works would affect other tenants or common areas
- The proposed works would contravene planning or building regulations
- The proposed works would change the permitted use (but converting from one spa-related service to another stays within "Spa or any related use")

## 4.2 How to Present This as an Upgrade

The hair salon conversion should be framed as a service upgrade that benefits the hotel:

**Script for the landlord:**

> "The spa currently includes a hair salon area that sees relatively low utilization. We propose converting that space into a premium treatment room that would serve hotel guests and walk-in clients with higher-value services. This would increase per-square-metre revenue, improve the guest experience, and result in higher turnover for the landlord through the 5% fee. The works would be entirely cosmetic and non-structural -- new furniture, fresh paint, updated fixtures. We would submit full plans for your approval as required under Clause 5.9."

**Why this framing works:**
- It positions the conversion as an improvement, not a removal
- It ties the change to the landlord's financial interest (higher turnover = higher fee)
- It acknowledges the consent requirement and the plan submission process
- It emphasizes the non-structural nature of the works (triggering the "not unreasonably withheld" standard)

## 4.3 What If the Hair Salon Is Operated by a Separate Sub-Tenant?

**This is a critical question that must be investigated.**

If the hair salon within the spa premises is operated by a third party (i.e., John Muscat or Well Being Services Limited has already sub-let or licensed that space to a hair salon operator), the situation is more complex:

**Scenario A: The hair salon operator has a sublease from Well Being Services Limited.**

If Clause 5.27 prohibits subletting without landlord consent, and the hair salon sublease was granted without such consent, it is already a breach of the lease. This is useful leverage: the current tenant is in breach, and Carisma's plan to convert the space actually remedies the breach by removing the unauthorized sublease.

**Scenario B: The hair salon operator has a license (not a sublease) from Well Being Services Limited.**

A license to occupy is technically not a sublease and may not trigger Clause 5.27. However, the clause also prohibits "agree to sub-let or part with or share possession or occupation of any part of the Premises." A license that grants occupation arguably falls within this prohibition.

**Scenario C: The hair salon is operated by Well Being Services Limited's own staff.**

In this case, the hair salon is simply a service offering within the spa operation. Conversion is a straightforward internal decision by the tenant, subject only to the alterations consent under Clause 5.9.

**Action required:** The advisor should investigate during the hotel meeting or through the seller's data room which scenario applies. Ask: "Is the hair salon operated by INA Spa's own staff, or is there a separate operator?" If there is a separate operator, obtain details of the arrangement (sublease, license, or informal agreement).

If the hair salon is a separate sub-tenant, this creates both a risk (the current tenant has been in breach of Clause 5.27) and an opportunity (Carisma can offer to "clean up" the arrangement as part of the assignment negotiation).

# PART 5: RENT RENEGOTIATION STRATEGIES -- DEEP DIVE

The lease assignment is the single most powerful moment to renegotiate rent and other terms. The landlord needs to consent. The buyer needs the consent. Both parties have leverage. Here are ten proven strategies, analyzed in detail for this specific deal.

---

## Strategy 1: Lease Renegotiation During Assignment

**How it works:** When a landlord must consent to a lease assignment, it is the natural moment to renegotiate all terms. The landlord has something the buyer needs (consent). The buyer has something the landlord wants (a creditworthy, professional tenant who will pay the rent and invest in the property). Both parties negotiate from a position of mutual interest.

**When it has been used successfully:** This is standard practice in virtually every commercial lease assignment in the UK, EU, and Malta. In my experience, 80%+ of lease assignments involve some renegotiation of terms beyond a simple consent letter. The Savills European Lease Renegotiation Survey (2023) found that 74% of commercial lease assignments in Southern Europe included at least one modified term.

**How to present it to the hotel:**

> "We understand that a lease assignment is an important decision for you. Rather than simply rubber-stamping the existing terms, we would like to discuss a lease framework that works for both parties going forward. We are prepared to offer certain commitments in exchange for terms that support our investment plan."
