# SECTION 1: REVENUE VERIFICATION DUE DILIGENCE

Before offering a single euro more, Mert must independently verify that the revenue the seller reported is real. ## 1.1 VAT Returns -- The Gold Standard

**What they are:** Every Maltese VAT-registered business must file periodic VAT returns with the Commissioner for Revenue (CFR). These returns declare total taxable supplies (revenue) for each period. Filing false VAT returns is a criminal offence under Article 76 of the Value Added Tax Act (Cap. 406), carrying penalties of up to EUR 11,646 per offence plus imprisonment.

**Why they matter:** Revenue reported to the tax authorities is the most reliable figure because the penalties for fraud are severe and criminal, not just civil. If INA reported EUR 430K in revenue to Mert but EUR 350K to the Commissioner, that is tax fraud. If they reported EUR 430K to the Commissioner but EUR 500K to Mert, they were inflating revenue to justify a higher price. Either discrepancy is a red flag.

**How to request them:**

1. Ask the seller directly for copies of all VAT returns filed for the period January 2024 through December 2025 (i.e., all returns filed since the business opened). In Malta, VAT returns are typically filed quarterly for businesses of this size.
2. Request the actual submissions, not summaries. You need the Forms VP (quarterly return) showing: total value of supplies at 18% rate, total value of exempt supplies, total output tax, total input tax, and net VAT payable/refundable.
3. Cross-reference total annual supplies against reported annual revenue: the sum of quarterly Box 1 (taxable supplies at standard rate) should approximately equal the gross revenue figure (including VAT) the seller provided. At 18% VAT, EUR 430K ex-VAT equates to EUR 507K inclusive. The quarterly submissions should sum to approximately EUR 507K in gross supplies for 2025.
4. If the seller claims VAT returns are "with the accountant" or "not available," that is a red flag. These are statutory documents. Every business has them. Refusal to provide is grounds to halt the process.

**What to look for:**
- Quarterly figures that do not sum to the reported annual revenue
- Quarters with suspiciously low or high figures that do not match the monthly revenue breakdown already provided
- Any penalty notices or assessments from the Commissioner (indicating prior compliance issues)
- Input VAT claims that seem high relative to reported COGS (could indicate purchases not reflected in the P&L)

**Malta-specific note:** Since January 2024, Malta has been rolling out digital VAT compliance. Ask whether Well Being Services Limited files electronically through the CFR portal. If so, a PDF export of all filings is trivial to produce.

## 1.2 Bank Statements -- 24-Month Business Account History

**What they are:** Complete transaction records for all bank accounts held by Well Being Services Limited.

**Why they matter:** Bank statements are the single most important verification document because cash does not lie. Every revenue euro must enter a bank account (card settlements, bank transfers, or cash deposits). Every expense euro must leave a bank account (payroll, rent, supplier payments). The bank statements provide an independent, third-party record of what the business actually earned and spent.

**How to request them:**

1. Request complete statements for ALL accounts held by Well Being Services Limited from January 2024 through February 2026 (24+ months). Emphasize "all accounts" -- businesses sometimes operate multiple accounts (operating, savings, payroll, merchant settlement).
2. Request PDF exports directly from the banking portal (not hand-prepared summaries). In Malta, most businesses bank with BOV, APS, HSBC Malta, or MeDirect. All provide downloadable PDF statements.
3. Also request the merchant acquirer settlement statements if card processing settlements come from a separate provider (e.g., Global Payments, Worldpay, Trust Payments Malta).

**What to look for:**

| Check | What It Reveals | Red Flag |
|-------|----------------|----------|
| Total deposits vs. reported revenue | Whether all revenue is captured | If deposits are materially lower than reported revenue, revenue is overstated. If materially higher, there is unreported income. |
| Cash deposits frequency and amounts | Whether significant cash revenue exists | Regular large cash deposits (>EUR 1K/week) are unusual for a modern spa where 70-85% of transactions are card-based. Could indicate underreported cash revenue or other income sources. |
| Payroll outflows | Actual wage cost | Total payroll debits should approximately match reported wages. If payroll debits are EUR 209K but do NOT include a EUR 2,750/month director's payment, the director is paid from a different source or not paid at all (which raises questions about the directorship arrangement). |
| Rent payments | Actual rent paid | Monthly rent debits should match the lease schedule. If the business is paying EUR 5,000/month fixed + quarterly turnover fee adjustments, the pattern will be visible. |
| Transfers between related accounts | Fund flow manipulation | Transfers between Well Being Services Ltd and the director's personal account, or to other entities controlled by the same person, could indicate costs being absorbed elsewhere (which is exactly what the excluded director's wage and marketing costs suggest). |
| Seasonal patterns | Revenue consistency | Monthly deposit patterns should match the seasonality data already provided (low in July, high in October-December). If bank deposits show a different seasonal pattern, the monthly revenue breakdown is unreliable. |
| Loan repayments or financing | Undisclosed debt | Regular fixed-amount outflows could indicate equipment financing, business loans, or credit facilities that have not been disclosed. These could transfer to the buyer if structured as a share purchase. |

**The director's wage test:** Specifically look for any regular monthly payment of approximately EUR 2,750 (gross) or EUR 2,200-2,400 (net after NI) leaving the business account and going to the director or a related entity. If this payment exists in the bank statements but NOT in the P&L wages line, the seller deliberately excluded it. If this payment does NOT exist in the bank statements, the director may be unpaid (in which case, who is doing the director's job for free, and what happens when Mert has to pay someone to do it?).

## 1.3 POS / Booking System Data Export

**What it is:** INA Spa almost certainly uses a spa management software system (common platforms in Malta include Zenoti, Mindbody, Booker, Phorest, Fresha, or a custom solution). This system logs every appointment, every transaction, every no-show, every refund, and every staff member's productivity.

**Why it matters:** The POS system is an independent record of actual business activity. It captures data that neither bank statements nor VAT returns show: number of appointments, average ticket value, treatment mix, therapist utilization, no-show rates, and client demographics.

**How to request:**

1. Ask the seller which booking/POS system they use. If they claim not to use one, that is itself a significant operational red flag for a business claiming 7,474 appointments per year.
2. Request a full data export for 2024 and 2025 covering: (a) all appointments (booked, completed, cancelled, no-show) by date, time, therapist, treatment type, and revenue; (b) all financial transactions including payments, refunds, and gift voucher redemptions; (c) client database summary (total unique clients, new vs. returning, visit frequency distribution).
3. Cross-reference total POS revenue against both bank statements and VAT returns. All three should agree within a small tolerance (1-3% for timing differences).

**What to look for:**

- **Appointment count verification:** The seller claims 7,474 appointments in 2025. The POS system will show the exact number. If the POS shows 6,000, the revenue per appointment is higher (suggesting some transactions are not appointments) or the appointment count was inflated. If the POS shows 9,000, either 1,500+ were no-shows/cancellations or the revenue figure is understated.
- **Average transaction value:** Total POS revenue / total completed appointments should approximately equal the EUR 57 ATR already identified. If the POS shows EUR 65 ATR, either the total revenue is understated or the appointment count is overstated.
- **Refund rate:** A refund rate above 2% warrants investigation. High refunds could indicate service quality issues, pricing disputes, or gift voucher problems.
- **Gift voucher liability:** The POS should show total gift vouchers sold, total redeemed, and total outstanding. Outstanding vouchers are a liability that transfers to the buyer. If EUR 20K of vouchers are outstanding, that is effectively a EUR 20K addition to the purchase price (services owed for cash already collected by the seller).
- **Therapist utilization:** Which therapists generate the most revenue? If 2-3 therapists produce 60%+ of revenue and they leave post-acquisition, the client base may not transfer.

## 1.4 Landlord's Turnover Fee Records

**What it is:** The lease requires INA to pay a 5% turnover fee on ALL sales. The landlord (Fortina Developments / Zammit Tabona family) calculates this based on the revenue that INA reports to them.

**Why it matters:** This is a powerful triangulation point. INA must report its revenue to the landlord to calculate the turnover fee. If INA reported EUR 430K to Mert but EUR 350K to the landlord, it is defrauding the landlord. If INA reported EUR 430K to the landlord, the landlord's records will confirm it independently.

**How to request:**

1. Ask the seller what revenue figures they reported to the landlord for the purpose of the 5% turnover fee calculation in 2024 and 2025.
2. Request copies of the turnover fee invoices or statements from the landlord (these would show the revenue base used for calculation and the resulting fee amount).
3. Alternatively -- and this is more powerful -- when Mert has the landlord conversation (which is required for lease assignment anyway), ask the landlord directly: "Can you confirm the turnover fee payments received from INA for the last two years?" The landlord has no incentive to lie.

**What to look for:**

- **Revenue reported to landlord vs. revenue reported to buyer:** These MUST match. Any discrepancy is fraud in at least one direction.
- **Turnover fee amount:** At EUR 430K revenue, the 5% fee should be EUR 21,500. If the landlord received EUR 17,500 (implying EUR 350K revenue reported), the seller has been underreporting to the landlord to save on the fee -- which means the "real" revenue is somewhere between EUR 350K and EUR 430K, and the seller has been committing lease fraud.
- **Payment timing:** Are turnover fees paid quarterly, annually, or monthly? Late payments could indicate cash flow stress.

**What they are:** Monthly statements from the card payment processor showing total card transactions processed, chargebacks, and net settlement amounts.

## 1.6 Employment Records (ETC / Jobsplus Verification)

**What they are:** In Malta, every employer must register employees with Jobsplus (formerly the Employment and Training Corporation / ETC). Jobsplus maintains records of all registered employees, their engagement dates, termination dates, and job classifications.

**Why it matters:** The seller claims 13 employees. Jobsplus records will independently confirm how many people are actually registered as employees of Well Being Services Limited, their roles, and their engagement dates. This is particularly important given the discovery that the Spa Director's wage is excluded from the P&L -- is the director even registered as an employee, or are they a consultant/shareholder drawing dividends?

**How to verify:**

1. Request the seller's Jobsplus employer registration number.
2. Ask the seller to provide a current Jobsplus employee listing (employers can download this from the Jobsplus portal).
3. Cross-reference against the staff list already partially received: 1 manager, 1 senior therapist, 7 therapists, 2 receptionists, 2 cleaners. The total should be 13 (or possibly 14/15 if the director and/or a marketing person are registered separately).
4. Check whether the Spa Director appears on the Jobsplus listing. If not, they may be a director of the company (registered at the Malta Business Registry as a director, not an employee) drawing fees or dividends rather than a salary. This changes the tax treatment and the cost structure.

**What to look for:**

- **Headcount discrepancy:** If Jobsplus shows 15 registered employees but the seller reports 13 staff in the data room, who are the extra 2? Could be the director and a marketing person -- which would confirm their costs are real but excluded from the P&L.
- **Recent terminations:** If employees were recently terminated (in the months before the sale), the seller may be downsizing to improve the optics of the P&L. This matters because TUPE protections require transferring all employees employed at the date of transfer.
- **Part-time designations:** If some "full-time" staff are registered as part-time at Jobsplus but paid as full-time, there could be NI compliance issues that transfer to the buyer.

## 1.9 Hotel Occupancy Cross-Reference

**What it is:** 22% of INA's clients are hotel guests. If Barcelo Fortina's occupancy data is available, it can be used to cross-check this proportion.

**How to approach:**

1. This is a secondary verification method. Do not request directly from the hotel (it could alert the hotel to the deal and create complications with lease assignment).

If the seller claimed hotel guests are 40% of clients (~2,990 appointments), that would imply 3.5% spa penetration -- high but not impossible for a spa located within the hotel.

**Limitation:** This is a sanity check, not a precise verification. It confirms the order of magnitude rather than exact numbers.

## 1.10 Additional Malta-Specific Methods

**a. Commissioner for Revenue -- Income Tax Cross-Reference**

If considering a share purchase (buying the company rather than assets), request the company's income tax returns and assessments for 2024 and 2025. The taxable income declared should be consistent with the P&L. If the company declared EUR 36.5K EBITDA in its tax return, the revenue figure used in the tax computation should match. Any discrepancy between tax return revenue and sale memorandum revenue is a material misrepresentation.

**b. FSS (Financial Services Supervision) -- Customs VAT Cross-Check**

If INA imports products from outside Malta (many spa products come from Italy or France), the customs import records will show the value of goods imported. Cross-reference against COGS: if customs records show EUR 30K in imports and reported COGS is EUR 44K, the remaining EUR 14K should be from local suppliers or retail product purchases.

**c. Insurance Broker Records**

Request details of the business insurance policy. The insured revenue figure (used to calculate premium for business interruption insurance) should match reported revenue. Insurers set premiums based on declared turnover. If INA declared EUR 300K in turnover to the insurer but EUR 430K to the buyer, one of those figures is wrong.

**d. Utility Records**

Request ARMS (Malta electricity/water) bills for the spa premises. Energy consumption patterns correlate with business activity. High electricity bills in months with reported low revenue would be inconsistent. Conversely, if utilities are flat year-round despite seasonal revenue variation, either the hotel absorbs some utilities (hidden benefit) or the seasonal revenue pattern is artificial.

**e. Professional Body Registration**

Therapists performing certain treatments (laser, IPL) should be registered with the relevant Maltese professional body or have certifications from recognized training providers. Verify the number of registered/certified therapists matches the claimed headcount. If INA claims 7 therapists but only 4 are registered for laser operations, either 3 therapists are unqualified (regulatory risk) or the headcount is overstated.

## 2.2 What Does This Say About the Seller?

There are three possible interpretations, and the correct response differs for each:

### Interpretation A: Incompetence (40% probability)

The seller genuinely does not understand that the director's wage and marketing costs should be in the operating P&L. This is possible if:
- The director draws fees through a separate company or personal arrangement and the accountant treated it as a distribution rather than an operating cost
- Marketing is handled by a related company (perhaps the director's other venture) and the costs are absorbed there
- The P&L was prepared by a junior bookkeeper who excluded items they were told to keep "separate"

**If incompetent:** The seller does not know their own numbers. This means other costs may also be misallocated, the reported EBITDA has never been the real EBITDA, and the seller may genuinely believe the business generates EUR 36.5K when it actually loses money. The response should be educational, not confrontational: "We have identified some costs that appear to be missing from the P&L. Can we walk through this together?"

### Interpretation B: Deliberate Manipulation (35% probability)

The seller -- or more likely, the facilitator/adviser -- deliberately excluded the director's wage and marketing costs to inflate EBITDA and justify a higher asking price. This is the interpretation if:
- The director is clearly a full-time role with a salary that would normally be in the wage line
- Marketing costs are clearly business expenses, not personal
- The P&L was specifically prepared for the sale process with these items stripped out

**If deliberate:** The seller's credibility is fundamentally compromised. Every number they have provided must now be independently verified (hence Section 1 of this document). The response should be firm but professional: "We have identified material operating costs that were not included in the P&L. This has changed our assessment of the business significantly."

### Interpretation C: Desperation (25% probability)

The seller knows the business loses money at its true cost basis. They need to sell because they cannot continue absorbing losses. They (or their adviser) stripped out costs to make the P&L look presentable enough to attract a buyer.

**If desperate:** This is actually the best scenario for Mert, because a desperate seller will ultimately accept a lower price. The response should avoid humiliating them while making the price implications clear: "We understand the financial picture has some complexity. Our offer reflects the economics as we see them."

### Most Likely Reality

It is probably a combination: the seller is losing money (Interpretation C), the facilitator cleaned up the P&L to make it more saleable (Interpretation B), and the seller may not fully grasp the magnitude of the issue (Interpretation A). The facilitator -- who is incentivized by commission -- is the one most likely to have made the decision to exclude these costs.

## 3.3 The "We'll Get Back to You" Response -- What It Means

In Maltese deal dynamics, "we'll get back to you" after a buyer's presentation typically means one of three things:

4. **Regulatory issues with medical aesthetics.** If the lease's "permitted use" clause does not allow medical services, the Base case revenue is at risk. Assess whether a lease modification is obtainable before walking.

## 5.6 How to Use the Excluded Costs as Leverage

### The Excluded Costs Are Your Single Strongest Weapon

Before the discovery, Mert's argument was: "At MY cost structure, the business loses money." The seller could counter: "But at OUR cost structure, the business makes EUR 36.5K."

After the discovery, Mert's argument is: "At YOUR cost structure -- your actual, real costs -- the business loses money. You excluded EUR 55-65K from your own P&L."

This is unanswerable. The seller cannot claim "but our EBITDA is EUR 36.5K" when their own director's wage and marketing costs bring it to negative. The anchor has shifted permanently.

### Deployment Strategy

**DO NOT use the excluded costs as a weapon in the first sentence.** Weaponizing it immediately ("you lied about your costs") creates defensiveness and closes doors.

**Instead, deploy it as a "discovery" that you are "helping" the seller understand:**

Phase 1 -- Introduction:
> "In reviewing the data, we noticed the director's compensation doesn't appear in the wage line. Is that correct?"

Phase 2 -- Confirmation:
> Wait for them to confirm. If they do, ask: "And where do the marketing costs sit? We understand there is a marketing function but we cannot see it in operating expenses."

Phase 3 -- Impact:
> "When we include these -- conservatively, EUR 55-65K -- the EBITDA picture changes materially. I want to be fair: this is not an accusation. But it does mean the financial basis for our discussions needs to be updated."

Phase 4 -- Implication (delivered calmly, not aggressively):
> "The EBITDA we should be working from is not EUR 36.5K. It is somewhere around zero or negative. And that changes the valuation conversation."

Phase 5 -- Bridge to offer:
> "Given this, our range of EUR 200-220K is actually generous. It values the business not on what it earns -- because what it earns is negative -- but on the value of the assets, the lease, and the platform that Carisma can build on."

### Timing

Do NOT deploy the excluded costs until the follow-up meeting. Using them in a WhatsApp message or email loses the impact of face-to-face delivery and gives the seller time to prepare a counter. In person, the immediate reaction -- surprise, discomfort, concession -- is valuable. In writing, they have time to construct a narrative.
