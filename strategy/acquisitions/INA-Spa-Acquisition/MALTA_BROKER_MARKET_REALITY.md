# MALTESE BUSINESS BROKER MEMO
**Confidential | INA Spa & Wellness at Numi Hotel**
**Re: Valuation Reconciliation & Deal Structuring**

## 1. What Breakeven Businesses Actually Sell For in Malta

Let me be direct with you, because I have closed deals on this island since the late 1990s and I have seen every permutation of this conversation.

When a small-to-medium Maltese business does EUR 400K revenue at breakeven — zero EBITDA, no profit line, just covering its costs — the honest market range is 0.3x to 0.6x revenue, full stop. That gives you EUR 120K to EUR 240K as the baseline. Not what sellers want to hear. Not what advisors tell them at the listing meeting. But that is what closes.

Here is why. Maltese buyers — and I mean serious, cash-in-hand Maltese buyers — are not valuing a P&L story. They are buying certainty. They want to see what the business earns them on day one, after they pay the staff, the rent, the supplies, and themselves a salary. A breakeven business earns them nothing on day one. So they price in the risk of getting it to profitability, the salary they are foregoing while they do it, and the time value of their money sitting in something illiquid on a small island with no easy exit.

The EUR 400K revenue figure is real. It is not fake. But revenue without margin is like a car with no engine — it looks fine in the driveway.

Where the number can stretch toward 0.6x to 0.8x revenue is when there are tangible assets, a long lease with favourable terms, a defensible competitive position, and an identifiable reason why a capable operator can extract profit. INA has some of these — the 13-year lease is genuinely valuable, the EUR 800K capex represents real sunk infrastructure, and being inside a hotel gives you a captive guest channel. These factors justify paying above the pure breakeven floor. But they do not justify 1x revenue.

Your EUR 260-280K range sits at 0.65x to 0.7x revenue. That is aggressive, yes, but it is not irrational. It is actually the high end of what a purely financial buyer would pay for a breakeven business, with a small premium for the asset quality and lease tenure.

## 2. The Malta Premium — Is There a "Malta Tax"?

Yes. It exists. It is real. And it will cost you money if you do not account for it.

Malta is a market of approximately 550,000 people on 316 square kilometres. There are perhaps thirty to fifty businesses that change hands at any given moment in the wellness, hospitality, and services sector. Everyone knows everyone. The seller's advisor probably knows your managing director. He has probably spoken at the same chamber of commerce dinner. His cousin may have stayed at your hotel.

This relational density creates what I call the Malta premium — a 15% to 25% price inflation above pure financial value, driven not by the numbers but by the social cost of being seen to have forced a distressed sale, the scarcity of quality assets, and the ego of sellers who built something over years and cannot price it like a spreadsheet.

In London, if a gym sells at 0.4x revenue, nobody blinks. In Malta, the seller tells his friends at the bar in Sliema. The advisor hears about it. Reputations get made and broken in small rooms. Buyers who are too aggressive find that the next seller has already heard about them before the first coffee meeting.

The practical implication: you need to get to a number that the seller can tell his story about with dignity. EUR 260K feels, to a Maltese seller who invested EUR 800K, like an insult. EUR 280K feels like a negotiation. EUR 300K feels like a conversation. The difference is EUR 20-40K but the psychological distance is enormous.

## 3. What the Seller's Advisor Told Him — and Why It Matters

That business card handshake was not accidental. Advisors on this island do not hand cards to buyers unless they want the deal done.

Here is what happened in that listing meeting, and I have sat in dozens of them: the advisor validated the EUR 400K revenue figure, pointed to the EUR 800K capital investment, referenced a comparable hotel spa sale in a neighbouring Mediterranean market, and told the seller "the right buyer will pay EUR 350-400K." He probably mentioned goodwill, brand positioning, and strategic value to an acquirer.

He told the seller EUR 400K because that is what gets him the listing. It is also probably EUR 80K to EUR 100K above where he privately thinks the deal will close.

The card handshake signals this: the advisor wants a transaction. He has an unhappy client sitting on a breakeven asset with EUR 800K sunk in it. He knows two buyers exist. He knows the window is closing. He is managing expectations downward with the seller while signalling openness to the buyer. When he gives you his card, he is saying, "call me, let us talk properly."

Call him. Not with a number. With questions. Ask him what the seller needs to feel good about the deal. Ask about timeline, about structure, about whether there is flexibility. He will tell you more than he should, because he needs this to close.

## 4. The Two-Buyer Reality — What It Actually Means

In twenty-five years I have seen this situation more times than I can count. Two serious buyers in a small market is not a negotiating advantage for the seller — it is a countdown clock.

One of those buyers will make an offer. When they do, the seller faces a binary choice: accept, counter, or lose them. If he loses one, he has one. And one buyer in Malta is not a market — it is a hostage negotiation, but you are the hostage.

The seller's advisor knows this mathematics. The seller is probably in denial about it. The advisor is not.

In practice, two-buyer situations in Malta close at a 10% to 20% discount to the seller's initial expectation. Not 40%. Not 50%. The scarcity of buyers creates a floor — the seller knows he cannot wait for a third. But it does not create a ceiling — the seller also knows he has a second option if you push too hard.

Your risk is not that the seller walks. Your risk is that the other buyer, who may be less sophisticated or more emotionally motivated, pays EUR 320K and you miss the asset entirely. In a 316-square-kilometre market, there will not be another 13-year hotel spa lease coming to market for some time.

## 5. How Similar Deals Have Closed in Malta

Four examples from my own files, with names removed:

**A.** A gym in St Julian's, EUR 380K revenue, marginally profitable at EUR 15K net. Five-year lease. Sold in 2021 for EUR 180K — 0.47x revenue. Buyer was a fitness operator from Gozo expanding to the mainland. Took 11 months to close. Seller wanted EUR 300K. The long timeline and single remaining buyer drove capitulation.

**B.** A beauty and wellness centre in Valletta, EUR 290K revenue, breakeven. Twelve-room facility with EUR 400K in fit-out. Sold in 2022 for EUR 220K — 0.76x revenue — but structured as EUR 140K cash plus EUR 80K earnout over 24 months tied to revenue retention. The earnout was the unlock. Seller accepted the lower cash figure because the earnout gave him a story.

**C.** A restaurant in Sliema, EUR 450K revenue, roughly breakeven after owner salary adjustment. Three years on lease remaining. Sold in 2019 for EUR 160K — 0.36x. Short lease killed the value. Buyer renegotiated lease terms as condition of purchase and extracted 5 more years before closing.

**D.** A day spa with hotel concession agreement in the south of Malta, EUR 360K revenue, EUR 20K EBITDA. Eight years on concession. Sold in 2023 for EUR 290K — 0.81x revenue. This is the most comparable to INA. The concession tenure and hotel relationship drove the premium. Strategic buyer, not financial buyer.

The lesson from D is relevant to you: when a strategic buyer acquires a business for synergies rather than pure return on invested capital, multiples stretch. You are a strategic buyer. That is your rationale for paying above the pure financial floor — and it is also the seller's best argument for holding near EUR 300K.

## 6. My Honest Advice to Both Parties

If you sat me down with both parties in the same room — which I have done many times, though both sides are always nervous about it — here is what I would say.

**To the seller:** Your EUR 400K expectation is based on revenue, not earnings. Revenue without earnings is not a business — it is a revenue-generating machine that earns its owner nothing. Your EUR 800K investment is sunk. The market does not reimburse sunk costs; it prices future cash flows. You have a 13-year lease that is genuinely valuable, a fit-out that eliminates capital expenditure for the buyer, and a running revenue base that eliminates the startup risk. That is worth something real. But the something real is EUR 290K to EUR 320K, not EUR 400K. Take EUR 310K and move on with your dignity and your capital.

**To the buyer:** EUR 260K will insult him and may push him toward the other buyer out of pride, not economics. EUR 280K is your opening offer in the room, not your final number. Your walkaway is EUR 310K. If the deal closes between EUR 290K and EUR 310K, you have bought a 13-year hotel spa lease, EUR 800K in infrastructure, EUR 400K in revenue, and 14 trained staff for a price that any rational operator would endorse. The delta between EUR 260K and EUR 310K is EUR 50K. On a strategic acquisition of this nature, that is the cost of one bad hire. Pay it.

**The structure that closes this deal:** EUR 285K cash at completion, plus a EUR 20-25K performance clause payable at month 18 if revenue exceeds EUR 380K in the preceding 12 months. Total headline number: EUR 305-310K. The seller tells his story as "I sold for EUR 310K." You actually pay EUR 285K upfront and risk EUR 25K on a business you are about to operate better than he did. The earnout costs you nothing if you do your job — which you will.

Call the advisor. Have coffee. Not at a hotel. Somewhere neutral. Tell him you are serious, you have done your diligence, and you want to find the structure that works for both sides. That conversation, over a coffee in a quiet café somewhere in Valletta, is where this deal will actually get made.

It always is, on this island.
