## Framing Note

Spreadsheets do not close deals. People do. The financial model in your existing playbook is excellent — it gives you the intellectual ammunition. This document gives you the psychological ammunition. The deal is won or lost in the three inches between the seller's ear and the decision-making center of their brain.

## 1. SELLER PSYCHOLOGICAL PROFILE

### Who You Are Dealing With

Based on the meeting behavior — the admission about the M&A advisor, the 5% revenue share, the maintenance gap, the overstaffing, and the "only 2 buyers" confession — you are dealing with a seller in a very specific psychological state. Understanding this state is more valuable than any financial model.

### Sunk Cost Bias: Critical Severity

The EUR 800K spent (or EUR 1M as discussed in prior documents) is not just a number to them. It is the organizing narrative of their identity in this business. Every decision they made — the equipment upgrades, the staffing choices, the hotel contract terms they accepted — felt right at the time. The EUR 800K represents thousands of individual decisions that each felt justified.

When you say "the market doesn't care what you spent," you are not just challenging a valuation. You are telling them that all those individual decisions were wrong. That is an identity attack, not a financial correction. They will defend against it viscerally, not rationally.

**Your approach:** Never attack the investment directly. Instead, separate the investment from the current question: "What you built is impressive — that setup would cost any operator serious capital to replicate from scratch. The question we're both trying to solve is: given where the business is today, what's the right structure that makes this worth it for both of us?" You acknowledge the build without valuing it at EUR 800K. You move the conversation from historical spend to forward value.

**Sunk cost anchor strength: 9/10.** This will not loosen quickly. Expect it to resurface even after you think you've neutralized it. Budget three separate conversations where this comes back before it dissolves.

### Cognitive Dissonance: Moderate-to-High

They built something that does not work as a standalone business. The hotel contract gives them no maintenance support. The 5% revenue share erodes profitability. The staffing model bleeds cash. And yet — they were in the wellness business for presumably years before this. They have some professional pride invested in the operation.

The dissonance: "I am a capable businessperson who makes good decisions" versus "I built a business with breakeven EBITDA on EUR 800K invested."

**How they are rationalizing this:** They are reframing the business as a "platform" or a "foundation" — something that requires the right operator to unlock its potential. This is not entirely dishonest, but it is convenient. It explains away the underperformance ("it needs the right system, not a different model") and elevates their role ("I built the infrastructure that enables someone else to win").

**Your approach:** Do not take this reframe away from them entirely. You need them to feel like they built something of value — otherwise they cannot sell it without shame. But redirect the reframe: "You've built an operational foundation — the systems, the relationships with the hotel, the trained therapist team. That's real. The missing piece isn't effort, it's the integrated clinic model and the marketing infrastructure. You've done your part of the build. I'm the right partner to complete the picture." This preserves their dignity while anchoring the clinic upside as yours to capture.

### Ego Involvement: High

Selling at EUR 250K when you spent EUR 800K is not just a financial loss. In a small market like Malta — where everyone knows everyone — it is a public admission of failure. The seller's peers, other hotel operators, other wellness entrepreneurs will hear the number. In their mental model, a "good deal" is one they can talk about at dinner without embarrassment. EUR 250K is not that deal.

**Critical insight:** The headline number matters more than the structure. A deal at EUR 280K total (EUR 260K at close, EUR 20K earnout) will feel worse to them than a deal at EUR 300K total (EUR 200K at close, EUR 100K earnout) — even though the expected value might be similar. They will tell people the gross headline number, not the probability-weighted present value.

**Your approach:** Use this. When structuring the final deal, consider making the headline number feel larger even if you reduce the cash-at-close component. "EUR 300K — EUR 240K at close and EUR 60K deferred" creates a EUR 300K narrative. That is easier for them to explain than EUR 260K flat.

### Time Pressure: Moderate, Rising

The business is at breakeven EBITDA. That means every month with a bad revenue performance — one slow December, one staff illness, one hotel issue — bleeds cash. They are not in acute distress, but they are in a slow bleed situation.

The psychological toll of running a breakeven business when you had hoped to build something significant is also significant. Every month that passes without a deal is another month of doubt. Another month of looking at numbers that do not inspire. Another month of explaining to their family or spouse why the exit is not happening.

**Their real time horizon:** Based on the "only 2 buyers" admission and willingness to share data, they want this done in 90-120 days. If the process drags past that, expect them to either drop to your number out of fatigue or get emotionally defensive and blow up the deal out of frustration.

**Your approach:** Do not rush them. But do not let the process drift either. Each stage should have a clear next step with a timeframe attached. This keeps momentum without creating pressure they can feel. Momentum is your friend because every day the process moves forward is another day of sunk cost investment in the deal itself — making it harder for them to walk away.

### Emotional Triggers

**What makes them say yes:**
- Feeling understood and respected as someone who built something real
- Believing the buyer will not immediately dismantle everything they created
- A number they can defend publicly with some dignity
- A process that feels professional and fair (no lowballing that feels like an insult)
- Confidence that the deal will actually close (not fall apart in due diligence)

**What makes them walk:**
- Any moment where they feel humiliated or disrespected
- A sense that the buyer is "fishing" for problems to renegotiate
- Feeling like the M&A advisor is not in their corner
- A dramatically lower offer than what they anchored on (below EUR 200K would likely kill the relationship permanently)
- Discovery that the buyer was not acting in good faith

### Their Real Walk-Away: EUR 220-240K

This is the number they will accept and still tell themselves a coherent story. At EUR 220K, the narrative is: "I recovered a significant portion of my investment, and the right buyer is going to build what I envisioned." Below EUR 200K, the narrative breaks — there is no frame that makes EUR 175K feel like a good outcome on EUR 800K invested.

Their stated walk-away is higher. Their emotional walk-away is EUR 220-240K. This is above your target (EUR 260-280K), which means there is a deal here.

## 2. THE HIGHER AUTHORITY TACTIC — DEEP EXECUTION GUIDE

### Theoretical Foundation

Herb Cohen's Higher Authority tactic operates on a simple principle: it takes the decision out of your hands and puts it in the hands of someone the other party cannot access or argue with directly. This gives you the ability to express personal enthusiasm for the deal while protecting yourself from being pressured into concessions you do not want to make.

Chris Voss's complementary framework is "tactical empathy" — making the other party feel heard so thoroughly that they lower their defenses. The combination is powerful: "I personally love this deal and want to make it happen. But my parents have the final say on capital deployment, and they are cautious. Help me give them a reason to say yes."

You become the seller's ally. The parents become the immovable constraint. The seller's energy shifts from negotiating against you to helping you convince an invisible third party.

### Why "Parents" Is Credible in This Context

In Malta specifically — and in family business culture broadly — major investments requiring parental approval are entirely normal, not a weakness. A buyer in their 30s or early 40s who is working with family capital is common and credible. The wellness business context makes it more so: parents who own or invest in wellness businesses is a plausible and sympathetic story.

The credibility factors:
- You are deploying significant capital (EUR 260-280K plus EUR 500K capex = significant family commitment)
- Family partnerships in hospitality/wellness are the norm in Malta
- Showing that you operate with family oversight signals that you are a serious, relationship-oriented buyer — not a financial flipper
- It explains why you cannot make a final decision on the spot without being "indecisive"

### Timing: When to Introduce

Do NOT introduce this in the NDA phase or the early data room phase. At that stage, you are in analysis mode. Introducing Higher Authority too early makes it feel like a prepared tactic rather than a natural reality.

The right moment is just after preliminary findings — when you have finished your initial data review and are about to move toward a formal offer. Specifically, introduce it in the conversation where the seller is starting to press for numbers.

**Trigger phrase from seller:** "So what are you thinking in terms of price?" or "When are we going to hear a number?"

**Your introduction:** "Before we get to numbers — and I do want to get there — I should be upfront about my process. My parents are co-investors on this. I manage the operations, but capital decisions above a certain level require their sign-off. I've briefed them on INA and they're genuinely interested, but they're conservative. They're going to ask hard questions about the financials before they approve any offer. I want to make sure I give them the right ammunition."

This does three things at once:
1. Explains why the process cannot move at the speed the seller might want
2. Creates a sympathetic figure (cautious parents you need to convince) rather than a cold financial analysis
3. Shifts the dynamic — the seller now needs to help you convince your parents, not just convince you

### Exact Dialogue at Each Stage

**Stage 1: Introduction (After Preliminary Review)**
"My parents raised concerns about the hotel contract situation and the staffing ratios when I showed them the numbers. I understand the business well enough to see the upside — but they're focused on the current state. Is there context I can share with them that would address the contract terms specifically?"

Effect: Seller now has an incentive to provide you with favorable information. They are selling to your parents, not just to you.

**Stage 2: Softening the Seller's Expectations**
"I had a long conversation with my parents last night. They're supportive of the concept but they came in at a lower number than I expected. I told them what I know about what you've built here and that I think there's real value. But they're anchored on the current EBITDA figures. I'm going to need something to show them that explains the platform value."

Effect: You are delivering bad news (lower number incoming) but positioning yourself as their advocate, not their adversary.

**Stage 3: First Offer Delivery**
"I want to be transparent with you before we put anything formal on paper. My parents have authorized me to move forward at EUR 260K. I pushed them to get there — their initial instinct was EUR 220K. I think EUR 260K is fair given what you've built. I know that's not where you started, but I genuinely believe it's where we can get this done."

Effect: EUR 260K is now framed as a hard-won concession from your parents, not an aggressive opening offer from you. The delta between EUR 220K (their "instinct") and EUR 260K (what you "pushed them to") creates a perception of movement in your favor.

**Stage 4: Handling Counter-Offer**
Seller comes back at EUR 380K (or any number above EUR 320K).

"I appreciate you working with me on this. I need to go back to my parents with that number. I'll be honest — I don't think they'll move above EUR 280K, but let me have the conversation and see where they land. Give me a couple of days."

Effect: You never reject the number personally. The parents reject it. You remain the ally.

**Stage 5: Escalation to EUR 280K**
"I spent two hours on the phone with my parents this weekend. I made the case for everything — the client base, the operational foundation, the hotel relationship. They finally agreed to come up to EUR 280K. That's their absolute ceiling. I know it's not EUR 380K, but I want to make this deal happen and EUR 280K is where we can actually close."

Effect: Seller hears that significant effort was expended on their behalf. EUR 280K now represents the maximum possible outcome of a real advocacy effort. The framing of "I want to make this deal happen" maintains your ally position.

**Stage 6: The Close**
"My parents gave me final authorization at EUR 280K. They want this structured as EUR 260K at close and EUR 20K on a 12-month earnout tied to spa revenue staying above EUR 25K. That's their comfort level — they want some protection given the transition. I think it's a fair ask. Can we shake on this and move to final documentation?"

### When This Tactic Backfires

The Higher Authority tactic fails in two specific circumstances:

**Failure Mode 1: Excessive Delays**
If you use "I need to talk to my parents" too many times, the seller (or their advisor) will eventually say "can we just get your parents on a call?" or "why don't we all meet together?" You need to have an answer ready: "They're in [Turkey / not available for direct calls on this / prefer to communicate through me on deals of this type]." Practice this answer so it is smooth, not defensive. Alternatively, a trusted family friend or business partner can occasionally play this role in person if the deal requires it.

**Failure Mode 2: Discovery**
In a market the size of Malta, people talk. If the seller discovers that the "parents" constraint was a tactic — particularly through mutual connections — the relationship damage is severe. You need to decide whether to use this tactic based on your honest assessment of the discovery risk.

**Mitigation:** Keep the parents narrative consistent, simple, and not overly elaborate. The more detail you add, the more that can be contradicted. "My parents are co-investors in this" is enough. Do not create a detailed backstory that can be checked.

**Failure Mode 3: Advisor Perception**
The M&A advisor may recognize this tactic immediately. A sophisticated advisor has seen it many times. If the advisor suspects it is a tactic, they will tell the seller to call your bluff by asking for a meeting with your parents. Handle this proactively: build rapport with the advisor and frame your parents role as genuine early, before the offer stage.

## 3. THE ADVISOR PSYCHOLOGY — COMMISSION LEVER

### Understanding the Advisor's Incentive Structure

The M&A advisor is commission-motivated. Assuming a fee of 5-8% on a EUR 260-280K deal, they are looking at EUR 13-22K in fees. That is real money. More importantly, their risk profile is asymmetric: if the deal closes at EUR 260K, they get their commission. If the deal dies, they get nothing — not even for the months of work already invested.

This creates a powerful alignment of interest with you that the advisor may not fully acknowledge to themselves or to their client. They want this deal to close. That makes them a potential ally.

### Building Advisor Rapport

The first priority is establishing personal rapport with the advisor before the formal offer stage. The best setting is a brief one-on-one — a coffee, a quick call after a joint meeting — where you speak candidly and off the record.

**Opening framing:** "I want to be straight with you about where I am on this deal. I think there's a real transaction here, but I need your help understanding what the seller actually needs to feel good about this. I'm not asking for confidences — I just want to make sure I'm structuring an offer that works, not one that insults them."

**What this accomplishes:**
- It positions you as someone who respects their role
- It invites them to share seller psychology without asking for breach of fiduciary duty
- It signals that you are serious about closing (good for their commission)
- It opens a channel for them to quietly reset the seller's expectations

**The key question to ask:** "In your experience — and I know you've done a lot of these — what does someone in Petra's position typically need to see, beyond the headline number, to feel like the deal was worth doing? Is it the structure, the earnout design, the transition terms?"

This question is brilliant because it gives the advisor permission to tell you what the seller actually needs while framing it as general expertise. They will almost certainly tell you something real.

### Using the Advisor to Deliver Bad News

When due diligence reveals unfavorable findings — and it will — you have two options: deliver them yourself (which positions you as adversarial) or allow the advisor to deliver them (which diffuses the adversarial dynamic).

**The technique:** After significant due diligence findings, share them with the advisor first under the framing of "giving them advance notice so they can prepare the seller." In practice, you are inviting them to do the expectation management for you.

"I wanted to flag before we send the formal findings — the staffing cost analysis came in higher than I modeled initially, and the revenue verification shows some volatility in Q4. I'm not walking away, but this is going to affect where I land on price. I wanted you to have context so you're not blindsided when the offer comes in. Is there anything the seller can provide that would address those specific concerns?"

The advisor now has advance warning, a specific ask they can bring back to the seller, and the opportunity to manage expectations before the formal offer arrives. They will do this because it protects their commission and their relationship with the seller.

### Arming the Advisor to Convince the Seller

Give the advisor the intellectual tools to do expectation management. Specifically:

**The "Real Alternatives" argument:**
"I want to share something with you off the record. I've actually modeled the build-from-scratch option in parallel. The math works — it takes 12 months and EUR 350K but I get a cleaner asset and better contract terms. The reason I'm here is because I genuinely think acquiring INA is faster and better. But I have a real alternative. If the seller is expecting EUR 400K+, they should know I have a walk-away that I've already stress-tested."

The advisor now understands that you are not desperate. They will communicate this to the seller — carefully, in a way that does not feel like a threat but does reset expectations.

**The "Deal That Can Actually Close" argument:**
"The worst outcome for everyone here is a deal that falls apart in due diligence because expectations were misaligned from the start. I'd rather be transparent about where I can get to — EUR 260-280K — and build a clean process, than chase a number that doesn't work and waste six months."

This framing gives the advisor exactly what they need to reset the seller's expectations: a professional, reasonable explanation for a lower number, delivered not as an attack but as a process management observation.

### Red Flags: Detecting Advisor Manipulation

Watch for these behaviors:

**Artificial urgency:** "Another buyer is coming in this week and they're looking at EUR 350K." Response: Request to see written evidence. If none is provided, acknowledge it diplomatically but do not adjust your offer: "I understand — if there's a better offer, they should take it. My number is EUR 260K and I can execute clean and fast."

**Bidding war creation:** If the advisor starts giving you new information that conveniently supports a higher offer ("Petra told me the hotel just offered to renegotiate the contract"), verify everything directly or through due diligence. Do not bid against a number you have not seen on paper.

**Scope inflation mid-process:** If the advisor starts adding items to what is included in the transaction after your offer is on the table ("don't forget you're also getting the vendor contracts and the CRM database"), resist the framing. You priced what was presented to you. New information requires new analysis.

## 4. ANCHORING, FRAMING, AND COGNITIVE BIASES

### Anchors Already Established

Your first meeting established three anchors that are now operating in the seller's subconscious:

1. **EUR 200K (build from scratch):** This is your BATNA floor. The seller knows you can do this. It anchors all future offers above EUR 200K as "better than the buyer's alternative."

2. **EUR 20-30K monthly revenue (low-end framing):** You referenced this range. This keeps their revenue story in the conservative zone even if actual performance has been higher.

3. **EUR 800K as "money wasted on bad decisions":** This is your most powerful existing anchor. You reframed their investment narrative from "embedded value" to "poor capital allocation." This seed needs continued watering.

### New Anchors to Plant

**The EUR 0 EBITDA Anchor**

In your preliminary findings conversation, lead with this: "When I look at the business as it currently operates — without any restructuring — I'm seeing EBITDA that's essentially at zero or very close to it. The current model is not generating the kind of return that justifies a significant acquisition premium."

Do not say this in an attacking tone. Say it as an observation, a puzzle you are trying to solve together: "I want to understand what's driving the margin compression, because the revenue numbers are reasonable but the flow-through isn't there."

This anchor does several things. It makes any positive EBITDA the seller can demonstrate feel like a win. It resets the conversation from "you have a EUR 72K EBITDA business" to "you have a breakeven business." And it positions any offer you make as generous relative to zero.

**The EUR 100K Restructuring Cost Anchor**

"Before I can form a final view on offer price, I need to budget for what I'd have to spend in Year 1 just to get the business to where it should operate. The staffing restructuring alone — if I need to right-size the team to profitable ratios — is probably EUR 30-40K in severance and retraining. The contract renegotiation with the hotel is going to need a legal process. There's deferred maintenance. I'm modeling at least EUR 80-100K in Year 1 costs just to fix the current structure before I can invest in growth."

This anchor is critical. It shifts the conversation from "what are you paying for the business" to "what are you paying for the business minus EUR 100K in cleanup costs." In the seller's mental math, your EUR 260K offer now implies a gross "effective" offer of EUR 360K — closer to their aspiration.

**The Monthly Bleed Anchor**

"One thing I keep coming back to is the opportunity cost for you. If the deal takes another 90 days to close — which is realistic given due diligence and legals — that's EUR 3-5K per month in net losses if the business has a bad stretch. That's EUR 10-15K off the effective deal value just from waiting. I want to move quickly, but I also want to be thoughtful."

This anchor creates urgency without you issuing an ultimatum. You are doing the math for them. Waiting has a cost. The cost is real. You are being transparent about it while implicitly inviting them to expedite.

### Framing Techniques

**Frame price as turnaround investment, not business value:**
"I'm not paying for current performance — I'm paying for the platform you've built and the right to invest in turning it around. That's why the number looks like what it looks like. I'm absorbing the restructuring risk in my price."

**Frame EUR 800K as lessons, not value:**
"Those learnings are in this business — you know what doesn't work, and that's genuinely useful. But sunk costs don't transfer as value in a deal. What transfers is current cash flow and the physical assets. I respect what you went through, but I can only price what the business generates today."

**Frame the offer as rescue, not extraction:**
"A deal at EUR 260K gets you liquid. It ends the monthly uncertainty. It lets you deploy that capital somewhere with better returns. I'm not trying to take advantage of you — I'm trying to create a path forward for both of us."

**Frame the deal as generous:**
"Honestly, EUR 260K for a business with EBITDA in the range you're showing is at or above market. I'm paying a relationship premium because I think the foundation you've built is real and I don't have to spend six months finding good therapists or training staff. If I were purely financial about this, the number would be lower."

## 5. SILENCE AND DELAY AS WEAPONS

### The Psychology of Not Responding

Every unanswered message is a signal. The question is what signal you want to send and for how long.

**What silence communicates:**
- "I have other things going on" (scarcity of your attention)
- "This decision requires real thought" (the deal is being scrutinized, not rubber-stamped)
- "I am not chasing this" (no desperation)
- "I may have alternative paths I am evaluating" (your BATNA is real)

The seller — who has admitted to only two buyers — does not have silence as a weapon. Every day that passes without buyer engagement is a day they wonder if you are drifting away. This asymmetry is your structural advantage. Use it deliberately.

### Silence Timing by Stage

**After the first meeting (completed):** You should have waited 24-48 hours before sending any follow-up. The right cadence now is deliberate but not urgent.

**NDA stage:** Send the NDA request email the afternoon of the day after you planned to. Not the morning you planned to — the afternoon. This tiny delay signals that you are not refreshing your inbox waiting to send it. It costs you nothing and signals calm.

**After receiving the data room:** Wait 5-7 business days before acknowledging receipt and providing preliminary observations. A serious acquirer does not skim data rooms. They read them. Taking a full week signals rigor.

**After preliminary findings conversation:** 48 hours minimum before sending any written summary. If they press you for a timeline, your answer is: "I want to get this right rather than fast. Give me the rest of the week."

**After delivering first offer:** This is the most important silence window. After you deliver an offer, say nothing additional. Do not follow up. Do not explain. Do not soften. Let the offer sit for at least 72 hours before any contact. Every minute of silence after your offer is pressure on them to respond.

**After their counter-offer:** "I need to think about this" followed by 48 hours of silence. Then: "I've thought about it and I've gone back to my parents. Here's where we are." The pause signals serious consideration. The "parents" framing explains the delay without making you look weak.

### When to Break Silence

The only good reasons to break a self-imposed silence early:

1. New positive information you want to use (e.g., you did something to strengthen your relationship with the hotel and want to mention it casually)
2. The silence has gone so long that it risks being read as disinterest rather than deliberation (more than 10 business days on any single stage)
3. The advisor calls you with a genuine signal of urgency from the seller

**Breaking silence correctly:** Never break silence with an apology or an explanation of the silence. Break it by immediately advancing to the next thing: "I've finished my review of the financials. Are you free Thursday for a call?" No "sorry for the delay." No "I've been busy." Just forward motion.

### Strategic Silence vs. Ghosting

The difference is important. Ghosting — going dark without any signal of re-engagement — destroys deal momentum in a small market and damages your reputation. Strategic silence is punctuated: you respond, then go quiet, then respond, maintaining a cadence that signals deliberation without disengagement.

The tell for ghosting: more than 10 business days without any contact from either side is when deals start to emotionally die, even if they are not formally dead. Keep the cadence even if the communications are brief.

## 6. THE EMOTIONAL JOURNEY OF THE SELLER

### Stage 1: Post-Meeting (Current State)

**What the seller is feeling:** Defensive but engaged. The fact that they revealed "only 2 buyers" and shared the bad decision data was not accidental. They are testing whether you are a serious buyer or just fishing for information. They are also recalibrating — your EUR 200K anchor has landed and they are trying to figure out how far from EUR 800K this deal is actually going to be.

**What they need to hear:** Nothing yet. This is the silence window. Your professionalism and punctuality on the NDA are your communication at this stage. Operate professionally and on-schedule. That speaks louder than words.

**What will make them walk:** Insulting follow-up. If you send a 2-line casual email that treats the meeting as a small thing, they will read it as disrespect. Match the seriousness of the meeting in every communication.

**Ideal buyer behavior:** Send a professional, brief, warm NDA email within the agreed timeframe. No exuberance, no coldness. Business-appropriate.

---

### Stage 2: NDA Signed

**What the seller is feeling:** Committed to the process but still skeptical about where this lands. Signing the NDA is a psychological commitment device — they have now formally opted into the deal process. This is the moment when momentum starts working for you.

**What they need to hear:** Acknowledgment of the process milestone. Something brief that signals: "I take this seriously and I'm ready to do the work." No numbers, no pressure.

**What will make them walk:** Anything that suggests you are going to waste their time. If you ask for an unreasonably large data room immediately, or impose complex conditions on the NDA itself, they will feel that this is going to be adversarial and difficult.

**Ideal buyer behavior:** Accept the NDA promptly. Thank them for their willingness to proceed. Outline a clean, professional data request list that is thorough but not exhausting.

---

### Stage 3: Data Room Open

**What the seller is feeling:** Vulnerable. Exposed. They have handed you the financial truth of their business — the real numbers, the real costs, the real contracts. This is the stage where sellers are most psychologically fragile. They are waiting to be judged. They are waiting for the buyer to use the data against them.

**What they need to hear:** Acknowledgment that you have received everything and are reviewing carefully. An occasional question that signals genuine curiosity rather than problem-hunting. Treat this like a professional, respectful review process.

**What will make them walk:** Any early indication that you are building a case for a dramatic price reduction. If your data room questions feel like a cross-examination rather than diligence, they will get defensive and protective. Their advisor will tell them you are "fishing for reasons to cut the price," which is true but must not feel true.

**Ideal buyer behavior:** Receive the data professionally. Ask clarifying questions that demonstrate you are reading carefully. If you find something concerning, note it internally but do not raise it until the preliminary findings conversation — where you can frame it constructively.

---

### Stage 4: First Formal Offer Received

**What the seller is feeling:** Shock and disappointment. Even if they have been told to expect a lower number, hearing it formally is always a jarring moment. They will read the number, feel their stomach drop, and their first reaction will be emotional, not rational.

The psychological sequence in the 24 hours after receiving your offer:
- Hour 1: Shock ("that's insultingly low")
- Hour 4: Anger ("who does he think he is")
- Hour 12: Consultation (they call the advisor, their spouse, someone they trust)
- Hour 24: Rationalization ("okay, maybe there's something to work with here")
- Hour 48: Engagement (they formulate a counter)

**What they need to hear:** Nothing for the first 48 hours. Do not follow up your offer with anything. Let them complete the emotional cycle without interference.

**What will make them walk:** An offer that feels contemptuous. EUR 150K would likely blow this deal up. EUR 260K — framed carefully — lands in "painful but negotiable" territory, which is exactly where you want it.

**Ideal buyer behavior:** Deliver the offer thoughtfully (see scripts below), then go silent for 72 hours.

---

### Stage 5: Negotiation — Anger, Resignation, Acceptance

**What the seller is feeling:** This stage has three phases and you will see all three.

Anger phase: They come back at EUR 400K+ or with a list of grievances about your offer. They feel you have undervalued everything they built. The advisor is managing them.

Resignation phase: After one or two rounds of offers and counter-offers, the emotional energy drains. They start to see the deal rather than the affront. Their conversations with their advisor become more pragmatic. They start asking "what would we actually need to see to get to EUR X."

Acceptance phase: They realize the deal is the deal. The energy shifts from "how do I get a better number" to "how do I make this feel okay." This is when ego protection (headline number, transition terms, being thanked for what they built) becomes as important as the number itself.

**What they need to hear in each phase:**
- Anger: Validation without concession. "I hear your concerns. Help me understand what's driving the difference in our views."
- Resignation: Incremental movement with explicit credit for their patience. "I went back to my parents. I've moved them."
- Acceptance: Dignity. Acknowledgment of what they built. A close that feels like a handshake between equals, not a surrender.

**Ideal buyer behavior:** Patient, steady, professionally empathetic. Never match their emotional temperature. If they are angry, be calm. If they are resigned, be warm. If they are ready to close, be decisive.

### Stage 6: Agreement

**What the seller is feeling:** Relief. The decision is made. The uncertainty is over. The monthly anxiety about the numbers is about to end. There is grief too — they built this and are letting it go — but mostly it is relief.

**What they need to hear:** Genuine gratitude for what they built. Explicit acknowledgment that the transition period matters to them and you will handle it with respect. Confidence that the business will be well-managed. Something that lets them tell their story positively.

**Ideal buyer behavior:** Be warm at close. The deal is done — there is nothing to gain from coldness now. A brief note acknowledging what they created, your intention to build on it, and the professional relationship you hope to maintain in Malta's small market is both genuine and strategically correct.

### Script 3: The Preliminary Findings Conversation

**Setting:** A call or in-person meeting. This is not an email conversation.

**Your opening:**
"I've gone through the data and I want to give you an honest picture of where I'm landing. Some of it is consistent with what you shared in our first meeting. Some of it is a bit more complicated than I initially expected. Can I walk you through my thinking?"

**When presenting concerns:**
"The staffing cost structure — when I model it against the revenue — creates a margin profile that's tighter than the headline numbers suggest. I'm not surprised, but I need to be honest that this is going to affect where I come in on price. Not dramatically, but it's in the picture."

**The setup for Higher Authority:**
"I also need to be transparent about something. My parents are co-investors in this. I manage the operations side, but above a certain capital threshold, I need their sign-off. I've briefed them on INA and they are interested — but they're conservative investors. They're going to press me on the current margin structure before they authorize an offer. I want to get ahead of that."

**The ask that sets up collaboration:**
"Is there context — anything not fully captured in the data — that would help me make the case to them for the platform value here? I already know the argument. I just want to make sure I have the right ammunition when I sit down with them."

### Script 4: First Formal Offer Delivery

**Setting:** In person if possible. Not email. The emotional weight of this moment requires a human setting.

**Preamble:**
"I've spent a lot of time on this over the past two weeks. I've gone through every line item. I've run the projections forward in several scenarios. And I've had a hard conversation with my parents about where they're willing to come in."

**The setup:**
"I want to say something before I give you a number. I genuinely believe in what you've built here as a foundation. The trained therapist team, the systems, the hotel relationship — those are real and they matter to my thesis. I'm not trying to low-ball you. I'm trying to give you a number that I can actually execute on and get closed."

**The offer:**
"My parents have authorized me to move forward at EUR 260,000 — EUR 245,000 at close and EUR 15,000 deferred over 12 months tied to spa revenue staying above EUR 22,000 per month. That protects both of us on the transition. I know that's different from where you started. I want to hear your reaction."

**After delivering the number: silence.** Do not immediately explain, justify, or soften. Let it land. Hold eye contact. Wait for them to speak.

### Script 5: Handling the Counter-Offer

They will come back somewhere in the EUR 380K-450K range.

**Your response (on the phone, not email):**

"I hear you. And I expected you'd need more than what I put on the table. Let me be honest with you — I can't just say yes or no right now. This needs to go back to my parents. But let me understand your number: what's the core of it? Is it the headline cash, is it the structure, or is it something specific about the way the deferred piece is designed?"

**Why this works:** You are not rejecting the counter. You are buying time with a legitimate process reason (parents need to authorize). And you are asking a diagnostic question that will tell you exactly what they care most about — is it the cash-at-close? Is it the total headline? Is it the structure of the earnout?

Whatever they tell you is more valuable than the counter-offer itself.

### Script 6: The "Parents Aren't Positive" Pause

Used after a counter-offer, to reset expectations before your final offer.

**Setting:** A call, not email.

"I spent this weekend working on this. I sat down with my parents Saturday and walked them through your counter at EUR 380K. They didn't move as much as I hoped. They kept coming back to the current EBITDA number and the restructuring costs I've flagged. I made the case for the platform value — I really did — but they're constrained by what the numbers show today."

"They've agreed to come up to EUR 280K. I want to be clear that I pushed hard for that. Their first instinct was to hold at EUR 260K. I'm at EUR 280K total — EUR 260K at close, EUR 20K deferred on the same earnout structure. That's their ceiling and I'm telling you that honestly because I don't want to waste either of our time."

**The pivot:**
"I think there's a deal here at EUR 280K. I think it works for you — it's liquid, it closes the chapter cleanly, and it sets the foundation for something you can be proud of. But I understand if you need time to think about it."

### Script 7: The Final Offer Delivery

**Setting:** In person. This is a closing meeting, not a negotiating meeting.

"I want to have an honest conversation. We've both been patient with this process. I think we both want to close. I've gone as far as I can go — EUR 280,000 structured as EUR 260,000 at close and EUR 20,000 in a 12-month deferred payment. That's my final number and I'm not able to go higher."

"What I can do is make the rest of the process as clean as possible. Fast legals. A transition period that works for your team. And a genuine commitment to building on what you've created here — not tearing it down."

"I'd like to shake your hand today and move to documentation. Can we do that?"

---

### Script 8: The Close

When they nod or say yes:

"Thank you. I mean that. This has been a thorough process and I respect how you've handled it. I'll have the heads of terms to your advisor by Thursday. Let's keep the momentum going."

Brief. Warm. Forward-moving. No celebration in front of them — it would make them feel they sold too cheap.

## 8. MALTA-SPECIFIC DYNAMICS

### The 520,000-Person Problem

Malta is not a city. It is a village that thinks it is a country. The welness industry in Malta has perhaps 200-300 people who matter: spa operators, hotel GMs, aesthetics practitioners, regulators, investors, and advisors. Every significant deal in this space gets discussed. Every deal that goes badly gets discussed more.

This creates both a constraint and an opportunity.

**The constraint:** Hardball tactics that humiliate the seller will be talked about. If word gets out that you drove a seller to distress and extracted maximum value without regard for their dignity, you will carry that reputation into every future deal, hiring conversation, and hotel partnership negotiation. Malta has a long memory.

**The opportunity:** If you close this deal professionally and with genuine respect for what the seller built, you gain a reference in the market. The seller — even if they initially feel they sold at the low end — will tell people "the buyer was professional, the process was fair, they've done right by the business." That is worth more than EUR 20K in deal savings.

**The rule:** Never sacrifice your long-term market reputation for short-term deal economics. EUR 260K vs EUR 280K does not matter in 10 years. Being known as a trustworthy operator does.

### Reputation as a Negotiating Asset

Use your reputation proactively. When you are in the final stages of negotiation, you can say:

"One thing that matters to me — and this is as much about my long-term position in Malta as it is about this deal — is that we do this in a way where you feel good about the outcome. I'm going to be operating here for the next 20 years. I want you to be someone I can have a coffee with."

This is not soft. This is strategic. It signals that you are a long-term player, not an opportunist. It also subtly invokes the Malta-small-market norm: professional people here do deals that they can both live with.

### Informal Settings: Coffee Culture

Malta runs on coffee. The informal meeting — a coffee at a hotel lobby, a light lunch at a neighbourhood restaurant — is not just social. It is where actual decisions get made and actual commitments get formed.

**When to use informal settings:**

After the NDA is signed, suggest a brief informal catch-up before the data room — not to discuss numbers, but to maintain the human relationship through what becomes a formal process. "Let's grab a coffee — not to talk numbers, just to stay connected while the advisors do their thing."

Between rounds of formal offer and counter-offer, an informal coffee with the advisor (not the seller) is valuable. This is where you get intelligence about where the seller actually is psychologically.

At the close, suggest signing the heads of terms over a proper lunch rather than in a lawyer's office. The setting matters — it signals celebration, not transaction.

### The Maltese Handshake

In Malta, a verbal commitment between people who know each other carries genuine weight — not legal weight, but social weight. When someone says "we have a deal" in a personal setting, they mean it. And walking back on it carries a social cost that Maltese businesspeople feel acutely.

**Use this carefully.** If you push too hard in the closing meeting and extract a verbal "yes" from the seller under pressure, they may ratify it legally but feel resentful. A resentful seller in a small market is a liability.

**The right approach:** In Malta, the close is not extracted — it is arrived at together. Your job is to create conditions where they feel they reached the decision themselves. Then a genuine Maltese handshake — followed promptly by documentation — is the right close.

**What a genuine close sounds like:** "Okay. Let's do it." Three words. Said calmly. Not enthusiastically. Not with relief. Just resolution. If you hear those three words in that tone, you have a deal.

## 9. DECISION TREE FOR CRITICAL SCENARIOS

### If seller goes silent after your first offer:

Wait 5 business days. Then: "Just following up to see if you've had a chance to think about what we discussed. No pressure — I just want to make sure you have everything you need to consider the offer."

If still silent after another 5 days: contact the advisor directly. "I haven't heard back and I want to make sure the process stays moving. Can you give me a read on where things stand?"

### If seller says they want to pull out of the process:

Do not panic and do not concede immediately. Instead: "I respect that. Before you make that final call, I'd like one more conversation — not to change your mind necessarily, but to understand if there's something I've missed that could make this work. Can we give this one more hour?"

A seller who says they want to pull out has usually not yet walked away — they are testing your response. Your calm response ("I respect that, but let's talk one more time") demonstrates the very qualities (stability, respect) that they need to see to close.

### If a second buyer emerges:

Ask for written confirmation of the offer. Request a deadline by which you need to make your final decision. Respond with your best offer — do not enter a bidding war. "I can go to EUR 280K. That's my best number. If the other party can do better, I understand. I can't bid against a number I haven't seen on paper."

### If due diligence reveals a significant liability:

Do not bring it up as an accusation. Bring it up as a puzzle to solve together: "In the contracts review, I found something about the revenue share terms that I want to make sure I understand correctly before we finalize anything. Can we walk through this together?"

If the liability is material enough to warrant a price adjustment, deliver the adjustment in the same way you delivered the original offer — in person, with context, using the parents framing: "This has changed the picture for my parents. I need to come down to EUR 240K to reflect the risk I'm absorbing."

## 10. FINAL STRATEGIC PRINCIPLES

**1. Never be the most emotionally invested person in the room.**
Your BATNA (build from scratch) is real and tested. You know the math works. Every time you feel impatient or anxious, remember: you are fine either way. That internal certainty communicates itself.

**2. Respect creates deals in small markets; aggression destroys them.**
The EUR 20K difference between EUR 260K and EUR 280K is not meaningful to your financial model. Your reputation in Malta over the next decade is worth considerably more. Optimize for both the deal and the relationship.

**3. The best negotiators let the other party feel like they won.**
At EUR 280K — which is EUR 80K more than your EUR 200K anchor and EUR 520K less than their EUR 800K aspiration — they will feel they "won" something. Help them frame the story. The frame is: "I protected myself on this. The buyer tried to go lower but I held firm and got to EUR 280K." Let them own that narrative.

**4. Process discipline is as important as tactics.**
Every stage should have a clear timeline and deliverable. Professional process management signals serious buyers. Drift and vagueness signal opportunists who will waste their time.

**5. Information is asymmetric — protect yours.**
You know your walk-away (EUR 320K). They do not. They know theirs (approximately EUR 220-240K, estimated). You do not confirm. Neither should you reveal. Every concession should be incremental and clearly labeled as coming from a specific party (your parents). Every number you share should be framed as authorization from that party, not as your personal analysis.
