# 2. CRITICAL FLAWS (Top 10 Problems That Could Sink the Deal)

## CRITICAL FLAW #1: The Spa Block Replacement Plan Is Treated as a Footnote When It Should Be the Entire Conversation

**The facts from the lease contract (Schedule Part 2, pages 25-26):**

The Complex description explicitly states: *"It will see the construction of an additional 5 floors and replacement of the existing 'Spa Block' with a 13-storey extension."*

And: *"The below-street-level facilities will be reworked to accommodate a larger gym, indoor spa and pool."*

This is not buried subtext. This is the hotel's stated development plan, written into the lease contract that both parties signed. The spa premises at -1 level are directly in the path of a planned demolition and reconstruction.

**How the three documents handle this:**
- Document 1 (Negotiation Strategy): Does not mention it at all.
- Document 2 (M&A Memo): Mentions it briefly in Section 2.2 ("Is the Fortina Complex being marketed for sale?") but does not connect the dots to the Spa Block replacement plan.
- Document 3 (Lease Playbook): Mentions it in Section 3.2 ("The Schedule describes plans to replace the existing Spa Block with a 13-storey extension") and in Section 5.7, but treats it as one risk among many rather than THE risk.

**Why this is critical:** If the hotel is actively planning to replace the Spa Block, then:
- Clause 9.3 becomes not a theoretical risk but a near-certainty
- The entire EUR 250K investment plus EUR 150K in renovations will be destroyed
- The compensation under Clause 9.3 (EUR 150K + depreciated investment) will not cover the loss
- No amount of negotiating sublease rights or hair salon conversions matters if the building is being demolished

**What should have happened:** The FIRST question in today's meeting -- before any discussion of sublease rights, hair salon conversions, or rent renegotiation -- must be: "What is the current status of the Spa Block replacement described in Schedule Part 2? Is this still planned? What is the timeline?" If the answer is "within 5 years," Mert should seriously reconsider the entire deal. All three documents bury this question when it should be the opening salvo.

## CRITICAL FLAW #2: The 5% Turnover Fee Math Is Fundamentally Miscalculated in Document 1

Document 1 (Negotiation Strategy) presents this as the STRONGEST argument:

> "Your 5% turnover fee applies to ALL sales on the premises. More operators means more revenue, which means a larger turnover fee for you. At our projected revenue levels, your annual turnover fee increases from roughly EUR 4,000 today to EUR 30,000-48,000 per year."

**The problem:** The lease states the 5% fee is calculated on *"a minimum of three hundred fifty thousand Euro (EUR 350,000) sales or whichever amount is greatest."* This means the 5% applies to ALL sales from euro one, not just the excess above EUR 350K. The EUR 350K is a floor, not a deduction.

At EUR 430K current revenue: 5% x EUR 430,000 = EUR 21,500. NOT EUR 4,000.

Document 1 appears to calculate EUR 4,000 as 5% of the amount ABOVE EUR 350K (i.e., 5% x EUR 80K = EUR 4,000). This is wrong. The lease says the fee is calculated ON the greater of EUR 350K or actual sales. At EUR 430K actual, the fee is 5% of EUR 430K = EUR 21,500.

**Document 3 (Lease Playbook) gets this correct** -- it shows EUR 18,000 as 5% of EUR 360K, and EUR 17,500 as 5% of EUR 350K minimum.

**Document 2 (M&A Memo) also gets it right** -- it references "approximately EUR 21K/year" at EUR 430K revenue.

**Impact:** Document 1's entire financial argument to the hotel is built on this error. If the advisor presents "your turnover fee is about EUR 4,000" when the hotel knows it is EUR 21,500, Mert's team instantly loses credibility. The hotel will think either the buyer does not understand the lease or is being deliberately misleading. Either is fatal to trust.

**The real uplift story is still compelling** -- going from EUR 21.5K to EUR 30-48K is a 40-120% increase, not a 650-1,100% increase. But presenting the wrong baseline number is an unforced error that could destroy the meeting.

## CRITICAL FLAW #5: Clause 5.27 (Sub-Letting) Is Even More Restrictive Than the Documents Acknowledge

The actual text of Clause 5.27 from the lease:

*"The Lessee may not sub-let, agree to sub-let or part with or share possession or occupation of any part of the Premises and to charge, grant rights over the same in favour of any third parties or otherwise to dispose of or deal with the Lessee's interest in the Premises, except with the prior written consent of the Lessor."*

**What the documents miss:** This clause does not just prohibit subletting. It prohibits:
1. Sub-letting
2. AGREEING to sub-let
3. Parting with possession
4. SHARING possession or occupation
5. Charging or granting rights over the premises
6. ANY disposal or dealing with the Lessee's interest

The "management agreement workaround" proposed in Document 1 (Section 4.1, Option A) and referenced in Document 2 would need to navigate the "share possession or occupation" prohibition, not just the "sub-let" prohibition. If a specialist practitioner is physically present in the spa treating clients in a dedicated room, a hostile landlord's lawyer will argue that possession or occupation is being shared, regardless of what the contract between Carisma and the practitioner says.

The documents present the management agreement structure as a clever workaround with "legal merit." The reality is that this clause is drafted specifically to catch exactly these structures. The phrase "or otherwise to dispose of or deal with the Lessee's interest" is a sweeper clause designed to catch anything the specific prohibitions miss.

**Impact on strategy:** The management agreement is not a reliable fallback if the hotel refuses formal sublease consent. Mert should plan for the possibility that he needs to operate entirely with employees, not contractors or sub-tenants.

## CRITICAL FLAW #6: The Clause 9.3 Compensation Calculation Is Wrong in Document 3

Document 3 (Lease Playbook) Section Strategy 7 calculates:

> "If the hotel sells in Year 9 (3 years into Mert's operation), Mert receives: EUR 150K lump sum + Investment reimbursement: EUR 150K minus 10% depreciation for 1 year (after 2-year grace period) = EUR 135K. Total compensation: EUR 285K."

**The problem:** Clause 9.3 says the Lessor shall reimburse the Lessee for "any investment made within the Premises upon receiving all fiscal receipts." The investment being reimbursed is the investment IN THE PREMISES -- meaning the renovation/fit-out, not the purchase price of the business.

Mert's EUR 250K purchase price is for the business (goodwill, equipment, client list). It is NOT an "investment made within the Premises." Only the EUR 150K renovation capex qualifies for reimbursement.

**This means:**
- Mert invests: EUR 250K (acquisition) + EUR 150K (renovation) + EUR 50K (working capital) = EUR 450K
- If hotel sells in Year 9: Mert receives EUR 150K lump + EUR 135K depreciated renovation = EUR 285K
- **Mert's actual loss: EUR 165K + ALL future profits**
- The EUR 250K acquisition cost is entirely unrecoverable under Clause 9.3

Furthermore, there is a serious question about whether the Clause 9.3 reimbursement applies to an ASSIGNEE's investment or only the ORIGINAL LESSEE's investment. The clause says "the Lessee" -- after assignment, Carisma becomes the Lessee, so this should work. But this has not been tested and should be confirmed with a Maltese lawyer before relying on it.

# 5. MISSING RISKS AND BLIND SPOTS

## Risk #1: The Hotel Has Already Decided to Redevelop -- and the Meeting Is a Courtesy

Schedule Part 2 describes a massive 50,000 sqm redevelopment with a completion date of January 2021 (per the original lease signed in 2020). It is now 2026 -- more than 5 years past the stated completion date. This means one of two things:

**Scenario A:** The redevelopment was delayed (COVID, financing, permits) but is still planned. In this case, the hotel is playing out the clock on the lease, collecting rent until they are ready to demolish. Mert investing EUR 400K into a building scheduled for demolition is catastrophic.

**Scenario B:** The redevelopment has been abandoned or indefinitely postponed. In this case, the spa lease continues and the hotel benefits from a stronger operator. This is the scenario all three documents assume.

**No document addresses how to distinguish between these scenarios.** The advisor must ask directly: "What is the status of the redevelopment described in the lease schedule? We noticed the original completion target was January 2021." The hotel's answer -- or evasiveness -- tells Mert everything.

## Risk #2: The Hotel May Want to Operate the Spa Themselves

The lease schedule describes "below-street-level facilities will be reworked to accommodate a larger gym, indoor spa and pool." If the hotel is moving toward a 5-star positioning, they may want to bring the spa in-house under their own brand (or a white-label international brand like ESPA, Six Senses, or similar). In this scenario, the hotel has no interest in ANY third-party operator -- they want the space back.

None of the three documents adequately address this possibility. Document 2 mentions it briefly in the BATNA analysis ("Hotel operates the spa itself -- This requires capital investment...") but dismisses it as unlikely. In a 5-star redevelopment context, it is not unlikely at all.

## Risk #3: Side Agreements Between John Muscat and the Hotel

Has John Muscat made any informal or formal agreements with the hotel that are not reflected in the lease? For example:
- Has John agreed to vacate by a certain date?
- Has John agreed to a reduced rent during COVID that created a future obligation?
- Has the hotel already given John informal notice that they plan to exercise Clause 9.3?
- Is John selling BECAUSE he has inside knowledge that the hotel plans to redevelop?

If John is selling because he knows the hotel will terminate the lease within 2-3 years, then the EUR 250K purchase price includes a massive hidden liability that Mert is not being told about. This is the single most dangerous information asymmetry in the deal.

**None of the three documents recommend asking John directly:** "Has the hotel communicated any plans to you about the spa space, the redevelopment, or the future of the lease?"

## Risk #4: Medical Aesthetics Licensing in a Hotel Spa Environment

The documents discuss converting the hair salon into a medical aesthetics clinic but do not address:
- Malta Medicines Authority requirements for operating medical devices (lasers, IPL) in a hotel environment
- Whether the hotel's insurance covers medical procedures performed on the premises
- Whether the hotel's planning permission allows medical use at -1 level
- Whether the Health and Safety Directorate requires separate certification for medical aesthetics versus spa services
- Potential liability issues if a medical procedure goes wrong in a hotel spa (the hotel's vicarious liability exposure could make them refuse)

These regulatory and insurance considerations could be the REAL reason the hotel refuses the clinic conversion -- not because of the physical alterations, but because of the liability and regulatory implications of medical procedures being performed in their hotel.

## Risk #5: The "Numi" Rebranding Suggests New Management With New Priorities

Document 2 mentions the hotel has been through multiple brands (Fortina, Barcelo, now "Numi"). Frequent rebranding suggests management instability or a strategic pivot. New management means:
- Old relationships (including John's) may count for nothing
- New management may have their own preferred spa operator
- New management may be more aggressive on rent and terms
- The person John is meeting today may not be the person who makes the final decision

## Risk #6: Construction Disruption Risk

If the redevelopment is proceeding (even in phases), the spa will be affected by:
- Noise and vibration from construction
- Restricted access for clients
- Dust, debris, and safety concerns
- Potential loss of Thalassa Room and Hammam Pool access (which are in "Common Parts" subject to the hotel's Reserved Rights)
- Reduced hotel occupancy during construction = fewer referral clients

Schedule Part 4, Reserved Rights, Clause 7 allows the hotel to "vary, alter, restrict, including in the Premises, or extend the Common Parts provided that such variation, alteration or extension does not materially adversely affect the Lessee's use and occupation of the Premises." The word "materially" gives the hotel enormous latitude. Construction that reduces client access by 30% might not be "material" in a legal sense but could be devastating commercially.

## Risk #7: The EUR 858K "Investment" Claim by the Seller

Documents reference that the current tenant invested EUR 858K in the premises. But the lease says the premises were given in "SHELL FORM STATE." The initial fit-out investment by John Muscat is being used to calculate Clause 9.3 compensation. But has the EUR 858K been verified? Are there fiscal receipts? Has depreciation been correctly calculated? If John inflated the investment figure, the Clause 9.3 compensation calculation is unreliable.
