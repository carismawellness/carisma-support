## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

You built a EUR 400,000 revenue business inside a five-star hotel in Malta with 13 years of lease runway, EUR 800,000 in installed infrastructure, medical-grade equipment, and 14 trained staff. The most serious buyer at the table -- Carisma Wellness Group -- operates 50+ hotel spas and has obvious strategic reasons to acquire your asset. Their internal documents (which we must assume exist even without seeing them) will attempt to price your business at EUR 220-310K using EBITDA multiples applied to your current breakeven operations. That framing is designed to transfer hundreds of thousands of euros in value from you to them.

**Our recommended defended price: EUR 400,000 total consideration.**
**Minimum acceptable cash at close: EUR 320,000.**
**Walk-away floor: EUR 300,000 all-in.**
**Absolute red line: Below EUR 280,000 at any structure.**

This memo builds the case, provides the tactical playbook, and gives you an honest private assessment of where this deal lands.

## 1. THE PRICE YOU SHOULD DEFEND: EUR 400,000

### 1.1 Why EUR 400K Is Defensible -- Not Emotional, Not Wishful

The buyer will frame this as "a breakeven business worth 3-4x zero EBITDA." That framing is intellectually dishonest because it ignores three things the buyer is actually purchasing.

**Asset 1: The 13-Year Lease (Value: EUR 100-150K standalone)**

Your lease inside Numi Hotel has 13 years remaining at 5% of revenue -- approximately EUR 20,000 per year. Commercial spa real estate in Malta, if you could find it at all, runs EUR 60-80K annually. You are paying one-quarter of market rate. Over 13 years, the rent savings alone have a net present value of EUR 100-150K at any reasonable discount rate.

More importantly, this lease is irreplaceable. Hotel spa partnerships in Malta are relationship-driven, often exclusive, and deeply resistant to cold approaches. The buyer cannot go negotiate a comparable lease elsewhere -- there may not be one available on the island. Scarcity is not an abstraction here. It is a hard constraint on the buyer's alternatives.

If a judge asked "what would a willing buyer pay for the right to operate a spa inside a five-star hotel for 13 years at below-market rent," the answer is not zero. It is six figures.

**Asset 2: The Physical Infrastructure (Replacement Value: EUR 500-600K)**

You invested EUR 800,000 in capital expenditure. The buyer's experts will depreciate this to EUR 150-250K using standard schedules. But depreciation is an accounting convention, not a market reality. Your Candela laser, T-Shape body sculpting device, hammam, sauna, and ice plunge are functioning medical and wellness assets. Their replacement cost in 2026 -- including procurement, shipping to Malta, installation, and commissioning -- is EUR 500-600K minimum.

The buyer knows this. That is precisely why they are acquiring rather than building from scratch. If they could replicate your infrastructure for less than your asking price, they would. The fact that they are at the negotiating table is proof that your installed base has economic value they cannot easily replicate.

**Asset 3: The Revenue Platform (EUR 400K proven, captive channel)**

EUR 400,000 in annual revenue is not an abstraction. It is verified income flowing through a booking system, served by trained therapists, supported by hotel guest traffic, and proven across multiple years. The buyer does not need to spend a single euro on customer acquisition to generate that revenue on Day 1.

The buyer's own thesis -- and this is critical -- is that they can take your EUR 400K revenue platform and generate EUR 150-200K in EBITDA from it. If they believe that (and they do, or they would not be at the table), then your platform is worth substantially more than zero-EBITDA pricing suggests. You built the engine. They plan to tune it. The question is how much of that tuning premium belongs to you.

### 1.2 The Turnaround Premium: You Built What They Plan to Profit From

This is the argument that should dominate the negotiation.

Carisma believes they can generate EUR 150,000+ in annual EBITDA from this asset. At even a modest 4x EBITDA multiple, that makes the post-turnaround business worth EUR 600-800K. The buyer is attempting to purchase a EUR 600-800K asset for EUR 260-280K.

Every euro of that future profit is made possible by infrastructure you built, a lease you negotiated, staff you trained, and a customer base you cultivated. The buyer's operational improvements -- staff rationalization, pricing optimization, brand integration -- are incremental changes to a platform that already exists. Without your platform, they have nothing to optimize.

The standard M&A principle that "the buyer keeps turnaround value" applies when the buyer is purchasing a distressed asset from a motivated seller at liquidation pricing. You are not distressed. You are breakeven -- your business covers all its costs, pays 14 employees, and has 13 years of contractual certainty. You are not facing foreclosure, creditor pressure, or lease expiry. You are choosing to sell. That changes the power dynamic entirely.

**The defensible argument:** A reasonable share of the turnaround value -- 25-35% of the buyer's projected Year 3 enterprise value uplift -- belongs to the seller who created the platform. That share, applied to a EUR 300-400K value creation scenario, supports EUR 75-140K in turnaround premium on top of the asset base value. Combined with the lease, infrastructure, and revenue platform, EUR 400K is the right number.

### 1.3 Seller-Favorable Valuation Methodologies

| Method | Basis | Value |
|---|---|---|
| Replacement cost (depreciated) | EUR 500-600K infrastructure at 70% of new | EUR 350-420K |
| Revenue multiple (service business) | 0.8-1.0x for operational business with lease | EUR 320-400K |
| Sum of parts (lease + equipment + revenue) | EUR 120K + EUR 200K + goodwill | EUR 380-450K |
| Turnaround value share (25% of buyer's upside) | 25% x EUR 600-800K post-turnaround EV | EUR 150-200K premium |
| DCF at 10% (seller-favorable rate) | 13-year lease, improving EBITDA trajectory | EUR 380-500K |

**Defensible range: EUR 350-450K. Midpoint: EUR 400K.**

## 2. COUNTERING THE "ZERO EBITDA" NARRATIVE

The buyer will hammer one point relentlessly: "EBITDA is zero, therefore the business is worth zero plus a small premium for assets." Here is how you destroy that argument.

### 2.1 EBITDA Is Temporarily Suppressed, Not Structurally Zero

Your revenue is EUR 400,000. Your breakeven is a staffing decision, not a market failure. You have 14 employees for 7 rooms -- a 2:1 ratio where industry standard is 1.2-1.5:1. You are overstaffed by 4-5 positions, costing approximately EUR 90-110K in excess labor annually.

If you terminated 4-5 employees tomorrow, your EBITDA would be EUR 90-110K. That is a 22-27% EBITDA margin on a EUR 400K revenue business. At 4x EBITDA, that values the business at EUR 360-440K.

**The script for the room:**

> "You say EBITDA is zero. I say EBITDA is temporarily suppressed by a staffing decision I made to preserve service quality during a growth phase. If I fired four people today, I'd have EUR 100K in EBITDA and we wouldn't be having this conversation about price. The fact that I chose to invest in capacity rather than optimize for short-term profit is a feature, not a defect. You're buying a business with built-in margin expansion -- that should increase the price, not decrease it."

### 2.2 Revenue Proves Market Demand

EUR 400,000 in revenue from a 7-room spa in Malta is not trivial. It proves that the market exists, the location works, the services are demanded, and the customer acquisition problem is solved. The buyer is not being asked to validate a concept or test a hypothesis. They are being handed a proven revenue stream inside a captive hotel environment.

**The reframe:**

> "You're not buying a business that earns zero. You're buying a business that generates EUR 400K in revenue with an obvious, well-documented path to EUR 100K+ EBITDA. The margin improvement is baked in -- all you have to do is optimize staffing ratios that I've already identified. You're buying a solved problem, not an unsolved one."

### 2.3 The Taxi Analogy -- Turned Against the Buyer

The buyer may use a taxi analogy: two taxis drive 100,000 km but one earns money and one doesn't. Your counter:

> "I'll use your own analogy. My taxi drives 100,000 km a year -- proven. The engine needs a tune-up that costs EUR 10K. After that tune-up, it earns EUR 100K a year for the next 13 years. Are you really telling me that taxi is worth the same as a broken-down vehicle? Or are you telling me it's worth the present value of EUR 100K per year for 13 years, minus the EUR 10K tune-up cost? Because that's a very different number than what you're offering."

## 3. DEAL STRUCTURES THAT PROTECT THE SELLER

### 3.1 The Seller's Preferred Structure

**Option A: Clean Cash Deal (Strongest Position)**

- EUR 380-400K cash at close
- No earnouts, no deferrals, no seller financing
- 30-day transition, then full separation
- Standard representations and warranties (12-month survival, EUR 20K cap)

**Why this is optimal:** You transfer all risk to the buyer on Day 1. No ongoing entanglement, no disputes over earnout calculations, no dependence on the buyer's post-close performance.

### 3.2 If the Buyer Demands Structure

They will propose earnouts and deferred payments to reduce their cash exposure. Your response framework:

**Earnouts -- Accept Only Under These Conditions:**

1. **Revenue-based, not EBITDA-based.** EBITDA is controlled by the buyer post-close. They set the staffing, the pricing, the marketing spend. If the earnout is tied to EBITDA, they can manipulate it by overspending. Revenue is harder to manipulate downward -- the hotel sends guests regardless.

2. **Threshold must be achievable without buyer cooperation.** If the earnout requires the buyer to invest in marketing, hire staff, or maintain specific service levels, the buyer controls whether you get paid. Threshold should be at or below current revenue: EUR 350K annual revenue, not EUR 400K.

3. **Maximum earnout period: 12 months.** Anything longer extends your risk exposure and delays your capital recovery.

4. **Escrow the earnout amount.** The earnout payment should sit in a third-party escrow account funded at close. This converts the earnout from "the buyer's promise to pay" to "the buyer's money, held by a neutral party, released on conditions."

**Seller Financing -- Resist Aggressively:**

Seller financing is a gift to the buyer. You are lending them money at below-market rates, subordinated to operating expenses, with your only collateral being a business you no longer control. If they mismanage the business, your note is worthless. Refuse seller financing unless:

- The interest rate is 8%+ (above commercial lending rates)
- The note is secured by specific equipment with first-lien priority
- The term is 18 months maximum
- Default triggers acceleration of the full remaining balance

### 3.3 Minimum Acceptable Cash at Close

**EUR 320,000.** This represents your walk-away floor minus the maximum you are willing to leave at risk in any structured component. The remaining EUR 60-80K (to reach your EUR 400K target) can be structured as earnout or deferred, but only under the protective conditions above.

**Under no circumstances accept less than EUR 300,000 in Day 1 cash.** Below that threshold, you are effectively financing the buyer's acquisition with your own capital.

## 4. TACTICAL PLAYBOOK

### 4.1 Creating Urgency Without Bluffing

You have two serious buyers. That is your leverage. Use it.

**Tactic 1: Controlled Information Release**

Do not tell Carisma who the second buyer is. Do not tell them the second buyer's offer. Simply state:

> "We are in discussions with another qualified party. I want to give you the opportunity to put your best offer forward because I respect what you've built in Malta. But I do need to move forward with whichever arrangement best serves my interests."

This is not a bluff. It is a statement of fact. You have two buyers. The buyer's fear of losing the asset to a competitor is more powerful than any financial argument you can make.

**Tactic 2: Deadline Pressure**

Set a deadline for offers. Not an aggressive one -- two weeks is appropriate. Frame it as procedural, not confrontational:

> "I've asked both interested parties to submit their best terms by [date]. After that, I'll make a decision. I want to be fair to everyone involved, including myself."

**Tactic 3: The Hotel Relationship Card**

You have an established relationship with Numi Hotel. The lease is in your name. Assignment requires the hotel's cooperation. Subtly remind the buyer:

> "The hotel has been very happy with our operation. They've told me they'd prefer continuity with an operator I endorse. I want to make sure the transition is smooth for everyone."

This signals that the lease assignment -- which the buyer needs -- is partially within your gift. It is not a threat. It is a reminder of a structural reality that gives you leverage.

### 4.2 Information Management

**Reveal early:**
- Revenue figures (verified -- this builds credibility)
- Lease terms and remaining duration (your strongest asset)
- Equipment inventory and condition (demonstrates value)
- Client volume and repeat rates (proves demand)

**Hold back:**
- Your walk-away price (never reveal this)
- The second buyer's identity or offer level
- Any urgency to sell (even if you have it)
- Your awareness of the buyer's planned clinic conversion
- Your willingness to accept structure over cash

**Never reveal:**
- That you would accept below EUR 350K
- That you understand the EBITDA argument has merit
- That you are concerned about the staffing ratio criticism

### 4.3 Power Moves

**Move 1: The Pre-Emptive Valuation**

Commission your own independent business valuation from a Maltese chartered accountant. Brief them on the lease value, the replacement cost of the infrastructure, and the medical equipment. A well-briefed independent valuation will produce a number in the EUR 300-400K range. Bring this to the negotiation as "third-party validation."

**Move 2: The Walk-Away Demonstration**

If Carisma opens at EUR 260-280K, do not counter immediately. Let 48 hours pass. Then respond:

> "I appreciate the offer. It's below where I can transact. I'm going to continue discussions with the other interested party. If circumstances change on your end, I'm open to revisiting."

This is the most powerful thing you can do. The buyer expects a counter-offer. Silence and a pivot to the alternative buyer creates genuine fear of loss.

**Move 3: The Lease Renewal Hint**

If the buyer pressures you on the 13-year finite term, respond:

> "The hotel has indicated informally that they would consider a renewal or extension of favorable terms with a quality operator. That option is available to me as the current leaseholder. Whether it transfers to a new operator would depend on the hotel's comfort with the transition."

This implies the lease could be extended -- creating additional value -- but only if you facilitate the relationship. It costs you nothing and adds pressure.

### 4.4 Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Do not negotiate against yourself.** If you ask for EUR 400K and they offer EUR 260K, do not come back with EUR 350K. Come back with EUR 395K. Small moves signal strength. Large moves signal desperation.

2. **Do not engage with their EBITDA framework.** The moment you start debating multiples of zero, you have lost. Redirect every conversation to replacement cost, lease value, and revenue platform.

3. **Do not reveal your staffing inefficiency awareness.** If you acknowledge that 14 employees is too many, you are validating their argument that EBITDA is artificially suppressed. Let them discover this in due diligence. In the meantime, your narrative is "I invested in service quality and capacity."

4. **Do not accept a meeting where they present their valuation models.** If they want to walk you through a DCF showing your business is worth EUR 250K, decline politely. "I have my own valuation work. I'd rather discuss terms than debate methodologies."

5. **Do not show urgency to close.** Even if you want the capital now, behave as though you are comfortable holding the business for another 5-10 years at EUR 72K/year (your EBITDA after minor rationalization). "I'm in no rush. The business runs itself. I'm selling because the right opportunity should benefit both parties, not because I need to."

## 5. THE SELLER'S RED LINES

### 5.1 Terms You Must Refuse

- **Total consideration below EUR 300K.** Below this, you are giving away the lease, the infrastructure, and the revenue platform for less than their combined standalone value. Walk away.

- **EBITDA-based earnouts where the buyer controls the EBITDA calculation.** This is a blank check for the buyer to manipulate your payout. Insist on revenue-based triggers or escrow.

- **Seller financing exceeding EUR 50K without hard collateral.** You are not a bank. Do not act like one.

- **Transition periods longer than 90 days.** The longer you stay, the more you help the buyer succeed -- for free. Your time has value.

- **Non-compete clauses exceeding 12 months or extending beyond Malta.** You cooperated in good faith. A 5-year non-compete across Europe is punitive.

- **Indemnification caps exceeding EUR 30K or survival periods exceeding 18 months.** The buyer is doing their own due diligence. Do not guarantee their findings.

### 5.2 Your Walk-Away Number

**EUR 300,000 total consideration (all sources combined).** Below this, the economics of holding the business -- EUR 72K/year EBITDA after modest staff optimization -- produce better returns over the remaining 13-year lease than selling.

The math: EUR 72K/year x 13 years = EUR 936K in cumulative EBITDA (undiscounted). Discounted at 10%, that is approximately EUR 530K in present value. Selling for EUR 280K sacrifices EUR 250K in present value. The only reason to sell below EUR 300K is if you have a time-critical need for capital that outweighs the ongoing income stream.

### 5.3 When Does It Make More Sense NOT to Sell?

- If the best offer is below EUR 300K and you have no pressing capital needs
- If you can optimize staffing from 14 to 10 employees, generating EUR 90K+ EBITDA, and then sell in 12-18 months with demonstrated profitability (which supports a EUR 360-450K valuation with ease)
- If the hotel indicates willingness to extend the lease, increasing your runway and bargaining position
- If a third buyer emerges (medical tourism operator, international wellness brand entering Malta)

**Recommendation:** If offers stall below EUR 300K, pause the sale process. Spend 6-12 months demonstrating EUR 80-100K EBITDA through modest staff rationalization. Return to market with a profitable business and a defensible 4-5x EBITDA valuation of EUR 320-500K.

## 6. HONEST PRIVATE ASSESSMENT

### 6.1 The Realistic Closing Range

After building the strongest possible seller case, here is the truth:

**Expected closing range: EUR 310-360K total consideration.**
**Most likely landing zone: EUR 320-340K.**
**Structure: EUR 280-310K cash at close + EUR 30-50K in earnout/deferred.**

This range reflects the reality that the buyer has genuine leverage from the zero-EBITDA fact pattern, while you have genuine leverage from the lease, the infrastructure, and the scarcity of comparable assets in Malta.

### 6.2 Where Your Arguments Are Weakest

**The EBITDA problem is real.** A business generating zero profit is, by any standard methodology, worth less than one generating EUR 100K. Your "temporarily suppressed earnings" argument is legitimate but requires the buyer to trust that the staffing problem is easily fixable. They may reasonably ask: "If it's so easy, why haven't you done it?" Be prepared to answer this -- the most honest answer is that you chose service quality and employee loyalty over profit optimization, which is a legitimate operational philosophy but does produce a lower valuation.

**The EUR 800K capex is a sunk cost.** You know this. The buyer knows this. No serious valuation professional will give you credit for historical spending. What matters is the current fair market value of the assets, which is EUR 150-250K at most on a depreciated basis. Arguing from EUR 800K invested signals emotional anchoring, not analytical rigor.

**The second buyer may not materialize at the level you need.** If the second buyer's best offer is EUR 250K, your leverage against Carisma evaporates. Do not overplay this hand without knowing what the alternative actually is. Pursue the second buyer actively and get a written indication of interest before using them as leverage.

**Revenue multiples for breakeven businesses do not support 1x.** The honest market data for breakeven service businesses in Southern Europe is 0.3-0.6x revenue. At best, your lease premium and infrastructure push you to 0.7-0.8x. Claiming 1x revenue requires you to ignore the margin structure, and sophisticated buyers will not let that pass unchallenged.

### 6.3 Concessions You Will Likely Need to Make

1. **You will not get EUR 400K in cash at close.** Accept that the total consideration may be EUR 400K but the cash component will be EUR 300-320K with the balance structured. This is still a strong outcome.

2. **You will need to accept some form of earnout or transition support.** A 60-90 day transition with a small revenue-based earnout (EUR 30-50K) is a reasonable concession that demonstrates good faith without material risk.

3. **You may need to accept a total number starting with 3, not 4.** If the negotiation stalls and the second buyer does not materialize, EUR 330-350K total with EUR 300K cash is a realistic and dignified outcome. It represents 0.8-0.9x revenue for a breakeven business with premium lease terms and medical-grade infrastructure -- well above market for this asset class.

4. **You should accept representations and warranties up to EUR 25K with 12-month survival.** This is standard. Fighting it signals that you have something to hide.

### 6.4 The Bottom Line

You built something real. EUR 400K in revenue, a 13-year lease inside a five-star hotel, EUR 800K in infrastructure, and a trained team of 14. The buyer wants to pay for a broken business. You are selling a platform. The truth is somewhere in between -- and that somewhere is EUR 320-350K.

Defend EUR 400K in the room. Signal flexibility at EUR 370K. Accept EUR 340K with good structure. Walk away below EUR 300K. And if no deal materializes at acceptable terms, optimize the business for 12 months and return to market in a position of strength.

This is a negotiation, not an auction. Patience is your most valuable asset. You have 13 years of lease remaining. The buyer is the one with a strategic timeline. Let them feel the urgency. You feel nothing.
