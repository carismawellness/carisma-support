## Opening Statement

You walked into this negotiation anchored at EUR 200K, briefed four experts who knew your walk-away, and every single one of them told you exactly what you wanted to hear. That should bother you more than it does. Before you walk away from a 13-year lease inside a hotel in Malta because a seller wants EUR 400K, you owe it to yourself to sit with the uncomfortable possibility that your entire analytical framework is wrong.

This memo argues that case. Aggressively.

## 1. The Case for EUR 400K: What the Buyer Is Missing

The buyer's team has built its entire valuation framework on EBITDA multiples. EBITDA is near zero, therefore the business is worth near zero — plus a small premium for the lease, the staff, the equipment. Call it EUR 220-280K.

That logic would make sense if you were buying a passive income stream. You are not. You are buying a platform.

INA Spa has EUR 400K in verified annual revenue from a 7-room spa operation inside a functioning hotel in Malta. That revenue did not come from thin air. It came from a booking system, a customer base, operational systems, trained staff, hotel relationships, and a brand that has already established itself in the market. The seller built something that works at the revenue level. The profitability problem is an operational problem, not a market problem.

The buyer's own team has acknowledged they plan to take this to EUR 150-200K EBITDA. They believe the revenue is real and the cost structure is fixable. If you believe that, you are not buying a broken business. You are buying a fixable business with EUR 400K in revenue, a captive hotel location, and 13 years of runway. That is a fundamentally different asset than your EBITDA multiple implies.

---

## 2. Revenue Multiple Reality Check

In service businesses where EBITDA is temporarily suppressed due to overstaffing, operational inefficiency, or founder-led overhead — not structural market failure — 1x revenue is a legitimate transaction multiple. This is standard in marketing agencies, professional services, beauty and wellness studios, and hospitality operations being acquired for turnaround.

Why does 1x revenue apply here rather than an EBITDA multiple? Because EBITDA multiples assume a stable, normalized earnings base. When EBITDA is near zero due to a correctable operational problem — 14 employees for 7 rooms is a correctable operational problem — the EBITDA multiple becomes a fiction. It punishes the seller for inefficiencies the buyer is about to fix with the very capital they are negotiating over.

You cannot simultaneously argue (a) the business will generate EUR 150K+ EBITDA once we fix it, and (b) the business is worth EUR 260K because EBITDA is currently zero. Those two positions are in direct tension. One of them is wrong.

---

## 3. The Lease Is Worth Something Significant

A 13-year lease inside Numi Hotel in Malta is not a line item. It is the deal.

Consider what it would cost the buyer to independently negotiate a 13-year spa operating lease inside a comparable Malta hotel from scratch. The answer is: it may be impossible. Hotel spa partnerships are relationship-driven, often exclusive, and deeply resistant to cold approaches. The seller did not just build a spa — the seller secured a long-term position inside a captive revenue environment that the buyer cannot simply go replicate elsewhere.

The lease terms — 5% revenue share with no maintenance obligation on the buyer — are attractive. At EUR 400K revenue, that is EUR 20K per year in rent. The hotel handles maintenance. For 13 years, you have a known, capped, fixed occupancy cost in a premium hotel. Try finding commercial spa real estate in Malta for EUR 20K per year.

If you value the lease conservatively as a standalone asset — access to captive hotel foot traffic, no maintenance liability, 13-year term, predictable cost structure — you are looking at EUR 100-150K in standalone lease value before you count a single piece of equipment, a single trained employee, or a single existing customer relationship.

Your experts appear to have valued the lease as a secondary consideration. It should be the primary consideration.

## 4. The Turnaround Premium: The Buyer Is About to Capture Enormous Value

This is the argument that should keep the buyer's team up at night.

The buyer believes they can generate EUR 150-200K EBITDA from this asset annually. At a modest 4x EBITDA multiple, that is a business worth EUR 600-800K once stabilized. The buyer is trying to purchase a EUR 600-800K asset for EUR 260-280K.

The seller knows this. Of course the seller knows this. The seller is not stupid — the seller built a EUR 400K revenue business. The seller's EUR 400K ask is not irrational greed. It is the seller asking for a share of the value the buyer is about to create using the seller's infrastructure, the seller's lease, and the seller's customer base.

If the buyer captures EUR 150K per year for 13 years on a EUR 260K investment, the return is extraordinary — and every euro of that return was made possible by the seller's EUR 800K in original capital investment and the 13-year lease the seller negotiated. Paying EUR 400K for that position is not overpaying. It may actually be underpaying.

## 5. Confirmation Bias Warning: The Expert Problem

Four experts. All engaged by the buyer. All briefed on the deal structure by the buyer. All told — explicitly or implicitly — that the buyer's walk-away is EUR 320K.

Under those conditions, what is the probability that even one of those experts would come back and say, "Actually, you should pay EUR 400K"? Close to zero. Not because EUR 400K is wrong, but because no paid advisor tells a client to pay more than the client wants to pay. That is not how engagement relationships work.

Independent valuation requires independence. It requires that the appraiser does not know the client's position before forming their own view. None of your four experts had that independence. They were anchored by your anchor before they ran a single model.

This is not a conspiracy. It is a well-documented psychological phenomenon called anchoring bias, and it operates powerfully even on trained professionals. The EUR 220-280K consensus is not four independent data points. It is one data point — yours — repeated back to you four times by people you are paying.

The intellectually honest move is to acknowledge that your valuation range may be correct and it may also be a self-fulfilling prophecy. You do not currently have the evidence to know which one it is.

## 6. The "Walk Away at EUR 320K" Test

The buyer says the walk-away is EUR 320K. The seller holds at EUR 350-400K. The buyer walks.

What actually happens?

The buyer goes back to looking for a comparable asset in Malta. A 7-room spa inside a hotel with a 13-year lease, EUR 400K in verified revenue, established staff, and a working booking system. How long does that search take? What does it cost in time, legal fees, due diligence, and opportunity cost? How many comparable assets exist in Malta right now? There are only 2 serious buyers in this process — which implies the seller also has limited options — but the buyer should be honest that their outside options are also limited.

If this deal does not happen, the buyer does not deploy their capital into a comparable asset next month. They sit on capital while the Malta wellness market continues to develop. The EUR 40K gap between EUR 360K and EUR 320K is not just EUR 40K. It is EUR 40K plus the cost of the next 12-18 months of searching, plus the cost of not capturing EUR 150K EBITDA for another year, plus the very real possibility that no comparable deal materializes.

The rational walk-away is not EUR 320K. It is whatever number, when subtracted from the NPV of the projected cash flows, still produces an acceptable return. If your projections are real, that number is closer to EUR 380K than EUR 320K.

## 7. Honest Conclusion: Did This Exercise Change Anything?

Yes. Partially.

The seller's EUR 400K expectation is not unreasonable. It is the emotional anchor of someone who invested EUR 800K, built something real, and refuses to accept that their creation is worth less than the capital they put in. That emotion is understandable and it contains a kernel of legitimate argument — specifically around the lease value and the turnaround premium.

However, EUR 400K is still likely above fair market value for a breakeven operation in its current state. The seller's EUR 800K in capex is a sunk cost and should not drive the negotiation. The business as-is today generates zero profit, carries 14 employees against 7 rooms, and requires immediate operational restructuring. Those are real problems.

My honest view after this exercise: the business is worth EUR 320-360K. Not EUR 260-280K, and not EUR 400K. The buyer's experts landed too low because they were anchored. The seller is too high because they are anchored to their own capital investment.

A deal in the EUR 330-350K range — potentially with a small earnout component tied to Year 1 EBITDA performance — reflects fair value for both parties and captures the legitimate arguments on the seller's side without overpaying for a turnaround that has not yet happened.

The buyer should be wary of treating EUR 320K as a principled walk-away. It is an arbitrary anchor dressed up as analysis.
