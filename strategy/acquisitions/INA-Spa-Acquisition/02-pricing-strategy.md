## Section 1: The Three Prices Every Buyer Must Know

**The Core Principle of Your Opening Offer:**
You are buying a currently underperforming business, not a business at its peak, not a business at its potential, and not the memory of what was invested in it. Your offer reflects one thing: what this business demonstrably earns today.

The key move here is to present the arithmetic transparently. Buyers who show their work are harder to dismiss. You are not being insulting — you are being precise. Precision is your ally.

**Target price reasoning shifts from 1.0x to 2.0x–2.5x EBITDA** because:
- You credit the upside case (Carisma can manage this space properly)
- You credit the lease if rent is below market (lease value is real)
- You still apply a goodwill discount for transition risk, brand repair, and the cost of re-staffing

## Section 2: Deal Structure Options and Their Price Implications

The structure of the deal is often as important as the price. A seller who cannot psychologically accept €150,000 in cash may accept €250,000 structured properly. Your goal is to find the structure that minimizes your real cost while maximizing the seller's perceived value.

---

### Option A: Full Asset Purchase (Lump Sum)

**Structure:** Carisma pays a single agreed price on closing for: lease assignment + all moveable equipment + the operating business as a going concern. Clean, final, complete.

**When to use:** When the seller needs a clean exit — either because he has personal debts to service, wants to move on with complete certainty, or cannot afford the ongoing uncertainty of a phased arrangement.

**For the seller:** Certainty, finality, maximum emotional relief. He can close the chapter.

**For the buyer:** Simplest to execute. All risk is known upfront. No future obligations to the seller.

**Risks:** If undisclosed liabilities emerge post-closing, buyer bears full exposure. Conduct thorough due diligence before committing.

**Price Range under Option A:**

| Rent Scenario | Lump Sum Price Range (€) |
|---|---|
| Rent €5,000/mo | 195,000 – 265,000 |
| Rent €7,000/mo | 150,000 – 215,000 |
| Rent €9,000/mo | 110,000 – 175,000 |
| Rent €11,000/mo | 65,000 – 120,000 |

**Effective cost to buyer:** Face value of the lump sum.
**Effective value received by seller:** Full agreed price, but often feels low relative to sunk cost.

**Negotiation note:** This is the seller's preferred structure emotionally (certainty), but it gives you the least leverage. Do not volunteer this structure first. Offer it as a reward for concession on price.

---

### Option B: Phased Payment (Installments / Earnout)

**Structure:** Buyer pays a reduced upfront sum on closing, with a series of subsequent payments either on a fixed schedule or tied to performance milestones.

**Example Structure:**
- Upfront on closing: €50,000–€75,000
- Payment 6 months post-closing: €40,000–€60,000
- Payment 12 months post-closing: €30,000–€50,000
- Performance earnout (if revenue reaches €40,000/month): €20,000–€40,000
- **Total effective price: €140,000–€225,000**

**When to use:** When the seller does not fully trust the buyer, wants assurance the business will succeed, or is using the deferred payments as a bridge income. Also useful when buyer wants to de-risk the acquisition — the earnout means if the business underperforms, the final payment is lower.

**For the seller:** Higher headline number than a lump sum; ongoing sense of security; feels more "fair" relative to his sunk cost anchor. The earnout component lets him tell himself he might recover more.

**For the buyer:** Lower initial capital outlay. Execution risk partially transferred back to seller (if performance doesn't materialize, earnout doesn't trigger). Cash flow advantage in months 1–12 when the business is being re-set.

**Risks to buyer:**
- Seller may interfere in operations post-sale if he has skin in the game via earnout
- Define earnout triggers very carefully — revenue, not profit (profit is manipulable)
- Ensure lease assignment is unconditional on closing — do NOT let payment schedule create lease uncertainty

**Price Range under Option B:**

| Component | Low Scenario (€) | High Scenario (€) |
|---|---|---|
| Upfront on Closing | 50,000 | 90,000 |
| Deferred Installments (12 months) | 60,000 | 90,000 |
| Earnout (performance-linked) | 20,000 | 50,000 |
| **Total Effective Price** | **130,000** | **230,000** |

**Effective cost to buyer:** €130,000–€230,000 total, with capital deployment spread over 12 months.
**Effective value received by seller:** Higher headline number; partial performance upside; psychological comfort.

**Key tactic:** When offering installments, you can justify a higher headline number precisely because you are sharing the risk. Say: "We can work toward a higher number, but given the uncertainty we are inheriting, we would need to structure a portion of that as performance-linked payments. That way, we are both invested in making this work."

---

### Option C: Revenue Share / Lease Assumption Only

**Structure:** Carisma assumes the lease, takes over operations, and pays a minimal or zero premium. The seller is released from an ongoing liability. No ongoing earnout, no future payments — just a clean transfer of obligations.

**When to use:** If the business is operating at a loss or near break-even under the current owner, and the primary value to the buyer is the lease (particularly if rent is well below market). The framing here is not "we are buying your business" — it is "we are rescuing you from a liability that is getting more expensive every month."

**For the seller:** Not appealing psychologically (feels like he is giving the business away). But it is better than the alternative: continued cash burn, finding no buyer, and eventually breaking the lease at enormous cost. The seller needs to understand this is not zero — it is the difference between a structured exit and a catastrophic one.

**For the buyer:** Minimal capital outlay. Maximum protection — you pay only for what you actually want (the lease and specific equipment). You are not paying for goodwill that has evaporated.

**Framing for the seller:**
> "We are not asking you to take nothing. We are asking you to consider what your real options are. If the business is covering costs but not generating meaningful income, then every month you hold on, you are paying the overhead without getting a return. We are willing to take this off your hands entirely — assume the lease, take over the operations, relieve you of every obligation going forward. We would pay a fair value for the equipment we need. And you walk away clean. That is worth something — and I think when you look at what the next 12 months look like without a buyer, you will agree."

**Price Range under Option C:**

| Component | Amount (€) |
|---|---|
| Lease Assumption Premium | 0 |
| Equipment (only items Carisma needs) | 20,000 – 50,000 |
| **Total Effective Price** | **€20,000 – €50,000** |

**Effective cost to buyer:** €20,000–€50,000 plus assuming the lease (which is the desired asset).
**Effective value received by seller:** Release from lease liability, closure of an ongoing operating loss, small cash payment for equipment.

**Use Option C as a threat, not a first offer.** Raise it only if negotiations stall or the seller names an unrealistic number. The implicit message is: "If you won't negotiate reasonably, the market will eventually price this closer to zero — and we are offering something now."

## Section 3: The Sunk Cost Problem — How to Price Against It

This is the single most important negotiation challenge in this deal. The owner has €1,000,000 of emotional capital tied to this space. That number will be in the room, even if he does not say it.

### 3.1 — Why Sunk Cost Is Analytically Irrelevant

From a corporate finance and valuation standpoint, a sunk cost is a cost that has already been incurred and cannot be recovered regardless of future decisions. It has zero bearing on the fair market value of an asset.

**The correct valuation question is never: "What did you pay for it?"**
**The correct valuation question is always: "What will this asset generate going forward?"**

This principle is not an opinion — it is the foundation of every DCF model, every comparable transaction analysis, and every professional appraisal standard (RICS, IVS, and IFRS all explicitly require that sunk costs be excluded from fair value assessments).

A restaurant that cost €500,000 to fit out but generates €50,000 per year in EBITDA is worth €100,000–€150,000. Full stop. The fit-out cost is a past event. It does not appear in future cash flows.

### 3.2 — Your Counter-Narrative

Memorize this. Say it calmly, with empathy, not as a lecture:

> "I understand completely why you look at what was put into this space and feel that the number should reflect that. What went into it was extraordinary — the quality of the build, the equipment, the vision behind it. None of that is lost on us. But the market — any buyer, whether it's us or someone else — will ask a very specific question: what does this business earn today? That's the only question that determines value. Not what it cost to build, not what it could earn in the best case, but what it earns right now, in the hands of its current operator. And we have to price accordingly. That is not a judgment of you — it is just how acquisitions are priced."

### 3.3 — The Pain Gap Analysis

| Metric | Amount (€) |
|---|---|
| Capital Invested (sunk cost) | 1,000,000 |
| Fair Market Value (target price, mid scenario) | 175,000 |
| **The "Loss" Already Crystallized** | **€825,000** |

This loss is not contingent on whether he sells. It has already occurred. The €825,000 is gone whether he sells today, in six months, or never.

**The critical insight to convey:**

> "I want to help you think about this clearly, because it matters. The €1M that was invested — that's in the past. That value has already been diminished by two years of declining revenue, a failed partnership, and a business that is running below its potential. Whether you sell to us today or walk away from this conversation, that value does not come back. The question is not: 'How do I recover my investment?' — because that is not available to any buyer. The question is: 'How do I stop the bleeding?' Because every month this business runs below its potential costs you money. And we are the buyer who can actually stop that."

This reframe is powerful because it is true. And because it shifts the conversation from the past (sunk cost) to the future (ongoing cost of inaction).

### 3.4 — The Lease Liability as Ongoing Pain

If the business is generating €22,500 in revenue and rent alone is €8,000–€11,000/month, the owner is running a business that — after all costs — may be generating very little or even losing money. Let us model this explicitly.

**Cost of Holding On (Monthly Cash Burn Analysis):**

| Scenario | Monthly Revenue | Monthly OpEx (ex-rent) | Monthly Rent | Net Monthly Result |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Current Best Case | 25,000 | (12,000) | (7,000) | +6,000 |
| Current Mid Case | 22,500 | (12,200) | (8,000) | +2,300 |
| Current Downside | 20,000 | (12,500) | (9,000) | (1,500) |
| Continued Decline | 18,000 | (12,500) | (9,000) | (3,500) |

**Even in the best case, the owner is generating €6,000/month from a €1M asset — a 7.2% annual return on a business that is getting worse, not better.** A term deposit would outperform this.

### 3.5 — Cost of Inaction: The Time Value of Delay

Present this to the seller directly. These numbers show what refusing to sell actually costs him.

**Assuming Mid-Downside Case (Net Monthly Result: +€2,300 or negative):**

| Time Period | Cumulative Net Cash if Business Stays Flat | Cumulative Net Cash if Business Declines | Cost vs. Selling Now at €150k |
|---|---|---|---|
| 6 months | +€13,800 | (€10,500) | €136,200 or €160,500 still "lost" from his €1M |
| 12 months | +€27,600 | (€21,000) | €122,400 or €171,000 still lost |
| 24 months | +€55,200 | (€42,000) | €94,800 or €192,000 still lost |
| 36 months | +€82,800 | (€63,000) | €67,200 or €213,000 still lost |

**The devastating insight:** Even if he holds on for 36 months in the flat case and generates €82,800 in cumulative net profit, he has not increased the value of his business — he has almost certainly decreased it further (further revenue decline, equipment aging, lease term shortening). Meanwhile, taking €150,000 today gives him €150,000 of certain cash versus 36 months of operational uncertainty and continued management distraction.

The deal he is being offered today is not the floor — it is likely the best offer he will receive. Every month that passes without a buyer, the next offer will be lower.

## Section 4: Equipment Pricing Strategy

### 4.1 — The Core Principle

Equipment in an asset acquisition is priced at depreciated fair market value — what it would fetch on the secondary market today, from an informed buyer, in its current condition, with reasonable time to sell.

This is not what it cost. It is not what it was insured for. It is not what it would cost to replace. It is what a buyer on the open market would pay for it right now.

**Equipment depreciation in medical aesthetics and wellness is aggressive:**
- Medical aesthetics devices (lasers, RF machines, HIFU, etc.): Depreciate 20–40% per year
- Spa equipment (treatment beds, steamers, massage tables): Depreciate 15–25% per year
- Salon equipment (chairs, dryers, stations): Depreciate 20–30% per year
- Built-in infrastructure (sauna, steam room, plumbing, tiling, custom woodwork): Zero transferable value — these are embedded assets that belong to the premises and cannot be sold separately

**€1M invested 24 months ago at average depreciation of 25%/year:**

| Year | Remaining Depreciated Value |
|---|---|
| At purchase | €1,000,000 |
| After Year 1 (25% depreciation) | €750,000 |
| After Year 2 (25% depreciation) | €562,500 |
| **Current Fair Market Value (estimated)** | **€400,000–€562,500** |

But this is for ALL assets. The critical distinction is between moveable assets (which the buyer can acquire and redeploy) and embedded assets (which stay with the building regardless).

### 4.2 — Moveable vs. Embedded Assets

**Moveable Assets (Carisma can value and acquire):**

| Asset Category | Notes | Estimated FMV Range (€) |
|---|---|---|
| Medical aesthetics devices (laser, RF, etc.) | High depreciation; model-dependent | 30,000 – 80,000 |
| Hydrotherapy / spa equipment | Whirlpool baths, flotation, etc. | 10,000 – 25,000 |
| Treatment beds and plinths | Per unit; condition-dependent | 5,000 – 15,000 |
| Salon chairs and stations | If resaleable | 3,000 – 8,000 |
| Nail technician equipment | Low value, specialized | 1,000 – 4,000 |
| Retail product inventory | If any remains | 2,000 – 8,000 |
| **Subtotal Moveable** | | **€51,000 – €140,000** |

**Embedded Assets (Stay with the building — zero buyer value):**

| Asset Category | Notes |
|---|---|
| Sauna and steam rooms | Built-in; landlord asset effectively |
| Custom tiling and stone work | Cannot be removed |
| Plumbing and drainage for hydrotherapy | Structural |
| Custom woodwork and cabinetry | Specific to space |
| HVAC and ventilation | Built-in |
| Reception desk and built-in furniture | Specific to build |
| Lighting installations | Structural |

**On embedded assets, your position is firm:**
> "We are not paying for what is in the walls or the floors. That belongs to the building. What we can value are the items that come with us — the equipment we can use, redeploy, or sell. Everything else stays. That is standard in any asset acquisition."

### 4.3 — The Independent Appraisal Recommendation

Before any number is agreed for equipment, Carisma should commission an independent equipment appraisal from a certified valuations firm. This is not optional — it is standard practice and provides you two advantages:

1. **Credibility:** The appraisal report is independent. The seller cannot argue with a third-party valuation without ordering his own. If he orders his own and it comes in higher, you negotiate between the two — and independent appraisers rarely diverge by more than 15–20%.

2. **Protection:** If the seller claims a device is worth €80,000 and the appraisal says €25,000, you have documentation. You are not guessing — you are following the evidence.

**Your language when requesting (or suggesting) an appraisal:**
> "We want to pay you fairly for the equipment — not what we think it's worth, not what you think it's worth, but what the market says it's worth. The cleanest way to do that is an independent appraisal. We are happy to split the cost. That way, neither of us is negotiating against a number we invented."

### 4.4 — The Selective Equipment Purchase Lever

Carisma does not need every piece of equipment in the space. You need the items relevant to spa, slimming, and medical aesthetics operations. The hair salon equipment, for example, may be irrelevant.

**Use this strategically:**
- Offer to buy specific items at fair value
- Explicitly decline items you do not want ("We do not operate a hair salon, so we would not be acquiring those assets")
- This gives the seller a sense of control (he can try to sell the unwanted items separately)
- It reduces your total equipment spend while appearing to be a transparent, fair negotiator

This tactic also protects you from paying for equipment that becomes a headache: old, non-compliant medical devices that require re-certification, for example, or equipment the seller has modified without documentation.

## Section 5: Owner Psychology Pricing Adjustments

Understanding how this seller thinks is as important as knowing what the business is worth. You are not just negotiating a price — you are helping someone make one of the hardest decisions of his professional life.

### 5.1 — Loss Aversion (Kahneman & Tversky)

Kahneman's research shows that the psychological pain of losing €1 is approximately twice as powerful as the pleasure of gaining €1. The seller has already "lost" €825,000 relative to his cost basis. He knows this, even if he has not said it. That pain is active, ongoing, and compounding.

**How to frame the deal so saying NO feels like continued loss:**

The seller's subconscious default is to anchor on €1,000,000 and feel that selling for €150,000–€200,000 means "losing" €800,000+. You need to reframe his reference point.

**Shift his reference point from "what I invested" to "what I have left."**

> "Think about where you are today — not where you were two years ago. Right now, you have a business generating €22,500 a month, with costs that are eating most of that, a lease obligation stretching 13 more years, no management team, and a partnership behind you that did not work out. That is the asset you are holding today. We are offering you €X for that asset — not for the business you wished it had become, but for the one it actually is. Taking that offer does not make you €800,000 poorer. You are already poorer. This makes you €X richer than you are today, with zero risk going forward."

**How to make saying YES feel like ending the bleeding:**

The seller must come to believe that the day he signs is the day the loss stops. Before that day, every sunrise costs him money. After that day, he is free.

> "The moment we close, that lease is off your balance sheet. The payroll risk is gone. The monthly uncertainty is over. You will have certainty — and certainty, at this point in this situation, has real value."

## Section 7: Price Concession Strategy

Concessions are not weaknesses — they are tools. But concessions without structure destroy value. Every concession you make must be: smaller than the last, conditional on something from the seller, and preceded by visible deliberation (do not give them away instantly — that signals more room).

**Pre-Planned Concession Map:**

Assume your anchor is €70,000 and your target is €175,000 (mid-rent scenario, mid-equipment scenario). Here is how you move from one to the other over four concession stages.

---

### Concession 1 — The "We Hear You" Move

**From:** €70,000
**To:** €105,000
**Size:** +€35,000 (the largest concession, at the start — signals good faith)
**Condition:** Seller provides you with the actual P&L (even informally), rent confirmation, and staff list

**Why this works:** The first concession is always the largest — it signals you are a serious buyer who can move. But you tie it to information disclosure. This gives you legitimacy AND gets you the data you need to refine your model.

**Language:**
> "I want to find a path here. We came in at €70,000 because that is where the numbers take us with the information we have. But I recognize that if you can show us the actual P&L and confirm the rent, we may find there is more here than our conservative model assumed. If you share that with us in good faith, we are prepared to move to €105,000 as a starting point for a more informed conversation. That is a significant move on our part, and I want you to see that we are serious."

---

### Concession 2 — The "We Recognize the Lease Value" Move

**From:** €105,000
**To:** €140,000
**Size:** +€35,000 (same size as Concession 1, but you are now at your midpoint — this will be your last large move)
**Condition:** Seller confirms lease terms (monthly rent, renewal options, subletting/assignment clause), AND provides equipment inventory list

**Why this works:** You are now explicitly crediting the lease value — which you deliberately excluded from the anchor. This feels to the seller like a meaningful discovery: "Oh, they are starting to see the real value here." In reality, you always knew this — but you are revealing it as a concession.

**Language:**
> "We have had a chance to look at the lease terms more carefully. A 13-year remaining term at the rent level you have confirmed is a meaningful asset — we would not be able to recreate that easily, and we know it. We are prepared to reflect that in our offer. We can move to €140,000, conditional on seeing the full equipment inventory so we know exactly what we are acquiring. That is a fair number for what you have here."

---

### Concession 3 — The "Goodwill Credit" Move (Small and Deliberate)

**From:** €140,000
**To:** €160,000
**Size:** +€20,000 (noticeably smaller than previous — signals you are approaching your limit)
**Condition:** Seller agrees to a 30-day exclusivity period (no other buyer conversations during due diligence), AND agrees to remain available for a 60-day post-closing handover

**Why this works:** You are reducing the size of your move, which signals to the seller that you are near your ceiling. The conditions you attach (exclusivity, handover support) are both genuinely valuable to you — exclusivity protects your due diligence investment, and handover reduces transition risk.

**Language:**
> "We are getting to a place where I think we can make this work. We are prepared to go to €160,000 — and I want to be honest with you, that is moving us toward the upper end of our range. To get there, I need two things from you: a 30-day period where you are not speaking with other buyers while we complete due diligence, and your agreement to be available to us for 60 days post-closing to ensure a smooth handover. Your knowledge of this space, the clients, the operations — that has real value to us, and we are paying for it."

---

### Concession 4 — The "Final and Conditional" Move

**From:** €160,000
**To:** €175,000 (target)
**Size:** +€15,000 (smallest concession — signals this is absolutely your final position)
**Condition:** Deal closes within 45 days, seller covers his own legal fees (buyer covers theirs), and any undisclosed liabilities above €10,000 are indemnified by seller

**Why this works:** The diminishing size of the concession (€35k → €35k → €20k → €15k) creates a clear pattern: the well is running dry. Experienced sellers recognize this pattern and understand that the next ask will get nothing. The conditions attached to this final move are also your most important protections — closing timeline and liability indemnification.

**Language:**
> "I have gone back to our board on this. We can stretch to €175,000, and that is our final number — I want to be completely clear about that. To get to €175,000, I need us to agree on three things: we close within 45 days, each side covers their own professional fees, and you indemnify us for any liabilities that emerge from the business prior to closing that exceed €10,000. Those conditions are non-negotiable for us at this price. If those terms work, we have a deal."
