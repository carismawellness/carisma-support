## 1. OPENING FRAME -- THE FIRST 90 SECONDS

### The Principle

Whoever speaks first defines what "fair" means for the rest of the meeting. You are not walking in to argue. You are walking in to establish a shared analytical framework. Once the adviser accepts your framework, every number you present lands inside that frame.

### Your Posture Before You Speak

- Arrive 5 minutes early. Not 15 (too eager), not on time (no buffer for disruptions).
- Choose the seat that faces the door if possible. This is a subconscious dominance signal. The person facing the exit is the person who can leave.
- Place your printed P&L face-down on the table. Do not open it immediately. This creates curiosity and gives you control over when the numbers enter the conversation.
- Have a pen and a blank notepad visible. No laptop. No phone. Visible note-taking signals you value what they say.

### Your Opening Script

Deliver this standing, during the handshake and the first 30 seconds of settling in. Keep it warm. Keep it genuine. Do NOT read it -- internalize the structure and say it in your own words.

> "Thank you for making time for this. I appreciate the process you have been running -- it has been thorough and I respect that.
>
> I want to set the tone for today upfront. I have spent a lot of time on this -- gone through the data room, spoken with my investment team, modeled several scenarios. What I have found is a business with real strengths and real structural challenges.
>
> What I would like to do today is walk you through how I see the economics. Not to argue about whose numbers are right, but so you can see my reasoning and we can have a productive conversation about where value lives in this business.
>
> I have put together a P&L that I would like to share with you."

### Why This Opening Works

1. **"My investment team"** -- establishes the Higher Authority early. You are not the sole decision-maker. This creates a shield for when the price conversation gets hard.
2. **"Real strengths and real structural challenges"** -- you are not adversarial. You see good things. But you have concerns. This is the language of a fair evaluator, not a lowballer.
3. **"Walk you through how I see the economics"** -- you are framing this as sharing your perspective, not delivering a verdict. This is collaborative language that disarms defensiveness.
4. **"Not to argue about whose numbers are right"** -- explicitly defuses confrontation. You are inviting dialogue, not a fight.
5. **"I have put together a P&L"** -- signals seriousness. You did not come with a vague gut feeling. You have a document. You have done the work.

### Then: The Pause

After the opening, pause for 3-4 seconds. Look at the adviser. Let them respond. They will likely say something like "of course, let's hear it" or "go ahead." This micro-consent is important -- they have now INVITED you to present. You are no longer pushing numbers on them. They asked.

### Now Turn Over the P&L

Slide a printed copy across the table. Say:

> "Let me walk you through this. I have built three scenarios. BAU is the business exactly as it stands today -- no changes, same operations. Base is a 10% revenue lift. Bull is 20%. I want to start with BAU, because I think that is the honest starting point for any conversation about value."

**Critical:** Point to the BAU column first. Your finger should literally be on the BAU numbers. Do not let their eyes wander to Bull first. Control their gaze.

## 2. THE BAU STORY -- MAKING "NEGATIVE EBITDA" LAND

### The Strategic Objective

BAU is the centerpiece of your entire argument. If the adviser accepts that the business loses money under current operations, your price is justified. Every minute you spend establishing BAU as the truth is worth EUR 10,000 in the final deal.

### How to Present BAU -- Step by Step

Do NOT rush through this. Slow down. This is where the deal is won.

#### Step 1: Revenue

> "I am keeping revenue exactly where it is -- EUR 430,000 ex VAT. I am not discounting the revenue. I am not saying it will decline. I am taking the business at face value."

**Why this matters:** You are pre-empting the objection that you are being unfair. You accepted their revenue number. This gives you moral authority when you present the cost side.

#### Step 2: Wages (THE CRITICAL LINE)

Lower your voice slightly. Look at the P&L, not at the adviser. This signals you are reading data, not making an accusation.

> "Wages. This is the number that drove most of my analysis. At EUR 238,200, wages represent 55% of revenue. The industry benchmark for a spa of this size is 35-42%. This business is carrying a wage burden that is 13-20 percentage points above what the industry considers sustainable."

**Pause here. Let the silence sit for 3-4 seconds.** Then:

> "To be clear -- I am not saying the staff are overpaid individually. What I am saying is that for EUR 430,000 in revenue, the headcount is more than the business needs. That is a structural issue, not a people issue."

**The question to ask:**

> "How do you see the relationship between the staffing level and the revenue? Do you think 13 staff is the right number for EUR 430K revenue?"

This question is devastating because the answer is always "well, with growth you would need them" -- which hands you the opening to say "but I am not paying for growth that has not happened."

#### Step 3: Rent

> "Rent at EUR 81,500 -- and this is a number I want to flag because it only gets worse. The fixed component escalates 3% every year. On top of that, there is a 5% turnover fee on all sales. So the landlord participates in every euro of revenue this business generates, without investing a cent."

**Pause. Then deliver the cumulative rent number. This is your bomb.**

> "Over the remaining lease term, the total rent obligation is approximately EUR 867,000. That is not a trivial commitment. That is nearly a million euros that goes to the landlord before I earn anything."

**Say the number slowly. Let "eight hundred and sixty-seven thousand" land.** Watch their face. Do not fill the silence.

#### Step 4: EBITDA Reveal

Now point to the EBITDA line. Your voice should be calm, factual, almost regretful -- like a doctor delivering a diagnosis.

> "When I put it all together, BAU EBITDA is negative EUR 10,034. The business, as it operates today, at Carisma's wage rates, does not generate a return. It loses money."

**Pause. Count to five in your head. Do NOT soften this. Do NOT add "but of course with improvements..." That comes later. Let the negative number breathe.**

Then:

> "And after depreciation -- EUR 55,000 of annual depreciation on the fit-out -- profit before tax is negative EUR 65,550. The business is losing EUR 65,000 a year on a pre-tax basis."

#### Step 5: The Frame Sentence

After the BAU walkthrough, deliver the frame sentence. This is the single most important sentence in the entire meeting.

> "This is the business I am being asked to buy. Not the business it could become with my investment and my execution -- but the business as it is today. And today, it loses money."

**Do not elaborate. Do not qualify. Let the frame sit.**

### Handling the Adviser's Pushback on BAU

The adviser WILL push back. Here are the three most likely objections and your responses:

**Objection: "You are using your wage rates, not the seller's. The seller's EBITDA is EUR 36,538."**

> "That is fair. But I have to model what the business costs ME to run, not what it cost them. If I acquire this business, I am paying Carisma rates. Those are the rates I have to work with. The seller's wage structure might produce a different number -- but that is not the business I am buying into."

**Why this works:** You are not disputing the seller's numbers. You are saying "those are their economics, but these are mine." The adviser cannot argue that you should value the business at someone else's cost structure.

**Objection: "BAU assumes no changes -- any buyer would make improvements."**

> "Exactly. And that is why I have the Base and Bull columns. But the value of a business is what it produces today, not what a buyer might create tomorrow. The turnaround premium belongs to the buyer who does the work and takes the risk."

**Objection: "Depreciation is a non-cash charge. EBITDA is the right metric."**

> "I agree that EBITDA is the standard metric for valuation. But depreciation represents the real cost of the assets wearing out. If I do not account for it, I am pretending the EUR 738,000 fit-out lasts forever. It does not. The assets depreciate. They will need replacement. Depreciation is not imaginary -- it is deferred capital expenditure."

## 3. THE REVENUE GROWTH STORY -- MAKING 10-20% SEEM GENEROUS

### The Strategic Objective

The adviser will argue that 10-20% growth is conservative. Your job is to anchor 10-20% as the CEILING, not the floor. You do this by emphasizing three things: the growth has already flattened, the market is small, and no operator achieves growth without investment.

### How to Present Base and Bull

After establishing BAU as the baseline, transition to Base:

> "Now, I have also modeled upside scenarios because I want to be fair. Base assumes a 10% revenue lift to EUR 473,000. Bull assumes 20% to EUR 516,000. I want to be clear -- these are optimistic assumptions. Let me explain why."

### The Three Arguments That Make 10-20% Feel Generous

**Argument 1: Growth Has Already Flattened**

> "The business grew 33% from Year 1 to Year 2. But that was ramp-up growth from a standing start. When I look at H2 2025, the picture changes. July was down 17% year-over-year. September was down 3%. December was down 3%. The H2 2025 annualized run-rate is EUR 382,000 -- not EUR 430,000.
>
> If I take H2 as the real indicator of steady-state performance, then a 10% lift to EUR 473,000 is actually a 24% improvement on the second-half run-rate. That is not conservative. That is ambitious."

**Argument 2: Revenue Growth Requires Investment**

> "Revenue does not grow by itself. A 10% lift requires marketing investment -- which I have budgeted at EUR 27,500. It requires operational improvements. It requires my management time and my team's time. Those are real costs that I bear. The revenue lift is not free."

**Argument 3: The Maltese Market Is Small**

> "Malta has 500,000 people. The addressable market for spa services in Sliema is measurable. At EUR 430,000, the business has already captured a meaningful share of local demand. Getting to EUR 473,000 means finding 10% more clients in a market that does not grow 10% per year. It is achievable -- but it is not easy."

### The Question That Anchors the Ceiling

After presenting these arguments, ask:

> "Can you walk me through what growth rate you think is realistic? And what investment you think it would require to get there?"

This is a trap question. If the adviser says "20-30% growth is easy," you respond: "Great -- then in your view, the Bull case is achievable. Even at Bull, EBITDA is only EUR 76,000 and the business barely breaks even after depreciation." If the adviser says "10-15% is realistic," they have just validated your Base case.

## 4. THE RENT TRAP -- MAKING EUR 867K+ TERRIFYING

### The Strategic Objective

The cumulative rent obligation is one of your most powerful weapons because it reframes the deal from "buying a business" to "buying the right to pay EUR 867,000 to a landlord."

### The Visual Presentation

When you reach the rent line, slow down dramatically. Look at the adviser and say:

> "I want to spend a moment on rent because this is where the real economics of this deal become clear."

Then, on a blank piece of paper in front of both of you, write this with your pen (not printed -- the act of writing creates emphasis and forces them to watch):

**Year 6:** EUR 81,500
**Year 7:** EUR 83,945
**Year 8:** EUR 86,463
**Year 9:** EUR 89,057
**Year 10:** EUR 91,729
**...**
**Year 15:** EUR 106,380+

Then write underneath:

**Cumulative remaining obligation: EUR 867,000+**

As you write each number, say it out loud. Let the escalation pattern become visible and audible.

> "The fixed component escalates 3% every year. The 5% turnover fee scales with every euro of revenue growth. By Year 10, I am paying over EUR 91,000 a year in rent. By Year 15, over EUR 106,000."

Then deliver the key insight:

> "And here is the structural problem. The 5% turnover fee means the landlord is a silent equity partner in every improvement I make. If I grow revenue from EUR 430,000 to EUR 550,000, the landlord's take goes up by EUR 6,000 a year. He invests nothing. He risks nothing. But he captures 5% of every euro of growth."

**The silence after this is critical.** Let the adviser absorb the full weight of EUR 867,000 in rent obligations.

### The Killer Question

> "Has the seller explored renegotiating the turnover fee with the landlord? Because the 5% on ALL sales -- not just above a threshold -- is unusually punitive."

This question has two possible outcomes, both good for you:
1. **"No, they have not."** -- This proves the seller accepted bad lease terms and never tried to fix them. It supports your narrative of operational mismanagement.
2. **"Yes, and the landlord refused."** -- This proves the lease terms are locked and the risk is real. It supports a lower valuation.

### The Unfavorable Contract Terms -- Pile On

After the rent discussion, add the operational deficiencies of the lease:

> "Beyond the rent itself, the lease contains several terms that create additional cost for any operator. There is no maintenance provision from the landlord. No laundry service. No staff lunch arrangement. No free parking for employees. These are standard inclusions in most hotel spa concessions. Their absence means I bear costs that would typically be shared or absorbed by the hotel."

**Ask casually:**

> "Is that your understanding of the lease terms as well? No maintenance, no laundry?"

You are not asking for information -- you know the answer. You are getting the adviser to CONFIRM the deficiencies out loud. Once they say "yes, that is correct," they cannot later argue the lease is premium.

## 5. THE 3x MULTIPLE -- DEFENDING THE LOW MULTIPLE

### The Strategic Objective

The adviser will argue for 5-6x EBITDA. You need to justify 3x as the correct multiple for this business. You do this by stacking risk factors until 3x feels generous, not punitive.

### The Argument Structure

Present this as a logical cascade. Each risk factor compresses the multiple.

> "I know the adviser may have a view on multiples, so let me explain my reasoning.
>
> Industry standard for a single-location spa in good health is 4-5x EBITDA. But that assumes several things that are not present here:
>
> **One** -- the business is profitable. This one loses money on a BAU basis. That alone justifies compressing the multiple to 3-4x.
>
> **Two** -- the business owns its location or has a secure long-term lease. This lease has assignment at the landlord's ABSOLUTE discretion. Until we have written consent, there is a binary risk that the entire deal is worth zero.
>
> **Three** -- the business has brand equity. INA Spa has 4.2 stars on review platforms and an out-of-service hammam. There is reputation repair work needed, not brand premium to price.
>
> **Four** -- the business is independent. This one is entirely dependent on a hotel. If the hotel changes management, changes direction, or sells the complex, the lease contains a termination clause. My investment is at the mercy of decisions I do not control.
>
> **Five** -- the fit-out is depreciating. EUR 55,000 a year of depreciation means the asset base is eroding. Every year I hold this business, the physical infrastructure is worth less.
>
> When I stack those five risk factors, 3x is not aggressive. It is a fair reflection of the risk I am absorbing."

### If They Counter with "5-6x Is Standard"

> "5-6x is standard for businesses with 15-20% EBITDA margins, secure property positions, and proven multi-year earnings. Can you point me to the comparable that has negative EBITDA, a discretionary lease assignment clause, and two years of trading history that trades at 5-6x? I am genuinely asking -- because if that comparable exists, I want to understand it."

**This is a question designed to have no answer.** There is no comparable. The adviser will have to concede or change the subject. Either outcome works for you.

### The Valuation Math on Paper

Write this on the same piece of paper you used for the rent cascade:

**Base EBITDA:** EUR 41,953
**x 3.0 =** EUR 125,859

**Bull EBITDA:** EUR 76,003
**x 3.0 =** EUR 228,009

> "At 3x Base, we are at EUR 126,000. At 3x Bull, we are at EUR 228,000. And remember -- Bull assumes 20% revenue growth, which I have explained is an optimistic assumption."

Then -- and this is critical -- draw a line between the two numbers and point to the space:

> "The deal is somewhere in this range. I believe the right number is closer to Base than to Bull, because Base represents achievable growth while Bull requires everything to go right."

## 6. THE DEPRECIATION SWORD

### The Strategic Objective

Depreciation is your secondary weapon after BAU EBITDA. EUR 55,516/year in depreciation means that even at Base (EBITDA EUR 41,953), the business has a pre-tax loss of EUR 13,563. This means:

- The business does not generate enough cash to replace the assets it consumes.
- The buyer inherits a depleting asset base.
- The seller's fit-out investment is literally disappearing at EUR 55K/year.

### How to Weaponize Depreciation

After presenting the EBITDA lines, move to PBT. Lower your voice. This should feel like a sobering moment, not an attack.

> "I want to draw your attention to profit before tax. Even at Base -- which assumes a 10% revenue lift -- PBT is negative EUR 13,563.
>
> What that means in practical terms is this: the business does not generate enough earnings to cover the cost of the assets wearing out. The fit-out, the equipment, the infrastructure -- it all depreciates. EUR 55,000 a year. And the business cannot fund its own renewal.
>
> At Bull, PBT is finally positive -- EUR 20,487. But that requires a 20% revenue lift. And even then, the return is EUR 20,000 on an investment of... how much?"

**Let the adviser fill in the number.** If they say EUR 857K, you have your moment:

> "So even at the most optimistic scenario, the business generates a EUR 20,000 annual return on a EUR 857,000 investment. That is a 2.4% return. A savings account pays more."

### The Depreciation Timeline

If the adviser pushes back with "depreciation is non-cash," you have a ready response:

> "I understand the accounting treatment. But let me ask a practical question: in 10 years, when the fit-out is fully depreciated and the treatment rooms need refurbishment, the hammam needs repair, the equipment needs replacement -- where does that capital come from? It comes from earnings. And right now, the business does not generate enough earnings to fund it. The depreciation is not imaginary. It is a deferred bill."

## 7. LINE-BY-LINE TALKING POINTS AND QUESTIONS

For every single line in the P&L, here is your argument and your question.

### Trading Income (EUR 430,000 / 473,000 / 516,000)

**Argument:** "I have taken the current trading income at face value and built modest growth scenarios. EUR 430,000 is a fair starting point. The 10% and 20% lifts require real investment from my side and are not guaranteed."

**Question to ask:** "What is your view on the sustainability of EUR 430,000? Is this the run-rate you see continuing, or do you think H2 2025 performance suggests some softening?"

*This question forces the adviser to either defend EUR 430K (in which case BAU is the truth) or admit softening (in which case even your BAU revenue is generous).*

---

### Wages and Salaries (EUR 238,200 / 221,700 / 221,700)

**Argument:** "At 55% of revenue in BAU, wages are the primary reason this business is unprofitable. Industry benchmark is 35-42%. I have modeled a modest optimisation in Base and Bull -- reducing the manager role from 1 FTE to 0.5 FTE, because Carisma's existing management infrastructure provides that oversight. Even with that change, wages are still 47% of revenue at Base. Still above benchmark."

**Question to ask:** "If the seller were to continue operating for another two years, how would they address the wage-to-revenue ratio? Do they see a path to getting wages below 50%?"

*This question is devastating because the honest answer is "they cannot" -- which proves the business needs a different operator, which proves the turnaround premium belongs to the buyer.*

### Rent (EUR 81,500 / 83,650 / 85,800)

**Argument:** "Rent is the second-largest cost after wages. It escalates contractually. And the lease assignment clause gives the landlord absolute discretion to block the deal entirely."

*This question probes the single biggest binary risk in the deal. If the answer is "no, they have not spoken to the landlord," the deal's foundational assumption is untested.*

### Utilities (EUR 14,741 across all scenarios)

**Question to ask:** "How are utilities billed? Is the spa sub-metered, or does the hotel absorb some of the cost?"

**Argument:** "Cost of goods at 10% of revenue is within range for a spa operation. I have scaled it proportionally with revenue in Base and Bull."

**Question to ask:** "Can you break down what is in the COGS line? I want to understand the split between treatment consumables, retail inventory, and laser/device consumables."

*This question serves your due diligence. If COGS includes EUR 15K of retail product purchases, the treatment margin is better than 10%, but it also means retail is a separate economics discussion.*

### Advertising & Marketing (EUR 25,000 / 27,500 / 30,000)

**Argument:** "I have budgeted EUR 25,000 at BAU and scaled modestly with revenue. Any revenue growth requires marketing investment -- this is real money I need to spend."

**Question to ask:** "What is the current marketing approach? Is the EUR 25,000 purely media spend, or does it include any staff time?"

*If marketing includes a part-time marketing person, the actual media spend is only EUR 13,000 -- indicating the seller has barely invested in demand generation. This supports your narrative of a business that has not been optimised.*

### SG&A (EUR 36,156 across all scenarios)

**Argument:** "SG&A includes insurance, IT, cleaning, repairs, professional fees, and general operating expenses. I have kept it flat across scenarios, which actually works against me -- in reality, SG&A would scale with a larger operation. But I wanted to present conservative numbers."

**Question to ask:** "The repairs and maintenance figure I have seen is EUR 2,117 for the year. That is 0.5% of revenue. For a spa with a hammam, 7 treatment rooms, laser equipment, and wet areas, that seems extremely low. What is the actual state of the maintenance backlog?"

*This question exposes deferred maintenance. EUR 2,117 in repairs means things are breaking and not being fixed. The 4.2-star reviews and the out-of-service hammam are the visible symptoms. You are making the adviser confront the fact that the physical asset is deteriorating.*

---

### Depreciation (EUR 55,516 across all scenarios)

**Argument:** "Depreciation is calculated on the EUR 738,000 fit-out over the lease term. It represents the real cost of the assets being consumed. After depreciation, even Base is loss-making."

**Question to ask:** "What is the remaining useful life of the major equipment -- specifically the Candela laser, the T-Shape, and the hammam infrastructure? When would these need significant reinvestment?"

*This question makes capital expenditure feel immediate and real. If the Candela needs servicing or the hammam needs repair, those are costs the buyer inherits but that are not in the EBITDA.*

---

### EBITDA (Negative EUR 10,034 / EUR 41,953 / EUR 76,003)

**Argument:** "EBITDA at BAU is negative. Even at Base, the margin is only 9%. Healthy spas operate at 16-28% EBITDA margins. This business is structurally below the profitability threshold at current operations."

**Question to ask:** "What EBITDA margin does the seller believe is achievable with the current operating model, without any changes?"

*If the adviser says "8-10%," they have just validated your Base case. If they say "15-20%," you ask: "Then why has it not been achieved in two years of operation?" Either answer works for you.*

---

### Valuation at 3x (EUR 125,859 / EUR 228,009)

**Argument:** "At 3x Base EBITDA, the business is worth EUR 126,000. At 3x Bull, EUR 228,000. This is the range I am working within. I believe 3x is appropriate given the risk profile I have outlined -- negative BAU, discretionary lease assignment, hotel dependency, depleting asset base, and thin margins."

**Question to ask:** "What multiple do you think is justified, and what comparable transactions support it?"

*Force them to name a number and defend it with data. If they say "5x," you immediately calculate: 5x EUR 41,953 = EUR 209,765 -- still below EUR 220K. If they say "5x on the seller's EBITDA of EUR 36,538," that is EUR 182,690. Their own math works against them.*

## 8. BODY LANGUAGE AND TIMING

### Before the Meeting

- Read this document once more, then put it away. You need the arguments in your head, not on the page.
- Eat a full meal beforehand. Low blood sugar makes you concede faster.
- Stand for 2 minutes in private before walking in. Power posture. Shoulders back. This is neurochemical -- it raises testosterone and lowers cortisol.

### During the Meeting: The Three Speeds

**Speed 1: Normal conversational pace** -- For opening pleasantries, context-setting, and questions. This is most of the meeting.

**Speed 2: Deliberately slow** -- For delivering the three hardest numbers: negative EBITDA, the EUR 867K rent obligation, and negative PBT. When you say these numbers, slow your speech by 30%. Lower your volume by 15%. Look at the P&L, not at the adviser. This signals: "I am reading a fact. It is not my opinion. It is on the paper."

**Speed 3: Complete silence** -- After delivering each hard number, STOP TALKING. Count to five in your head. The first person to speak after a hard number usually concedes something. Let the silence do the work.

### Key Physical Cues

| Moment | What to Do | What NOT to Do |
|--------|-----------|----------------|
| Presenting BAU EBITDA | Look at the paper. Lower voice. | Do not smile. Do not shrug. |
| They push back | Nod slowly. "I hear you." | Do not lean forward. Do not argue immediately. |
| Writing rent escalation | Write slowly. Say each number. | Do not rush. Do not apologise for the math. |
| They mention EUR 857K invested | Pause. Soften expression. | Do not dismiss. Do not roll eyes. Do not immediately counter. |
| They name a high price | Look down at your notes. Write their number. | Do not react visibly. Do not say "no." |
| Silence after your key point | Lean back slightly. Rest your hands. | Do not fill the silence. Do not add qualifiers. |

### The Mirror Technique

When the adviser gets heated or raises their voice, do the opposite. Lower your voice. Slow your pace. The calmer you become under pressure, the more authority you project. This is the single most effective body language technique in negotiation.

### Note-Taking as a Signal

Take visible notes when the adviser speaks. This is both respectful AND strategic. It signals you are paying attention. But more importantly, when you write something down in response to a statement they make, they subconsciously feel committed to that statement. They cannot easily walk it back because they watched you record it.

## 9. THE EUR 857K ANCHOR DEFENCE

### Why This Is Their Most Powerful Weapon

The seller invested EUR 857K. This number has enormous emotional weight. It makes any offer below EUR 400K feel like an insult. The adviser will deploy this number early and often. You MUST neutralize it without dismissing the emotional reality behind it.

### Exact Language to Neutralize

When the EUR 857K comes up (and it will), pause. Soften your expression. Lean forward slightly -- this is one of the few moments where leaning forward shows empathy, not eagerness.

> "I understand. And I want you to know that I do not take that number lightly. EUR 857,000 is a significant commitment. Building a spa from zero inside a hotel, fitting out 7 treatment rooms, installing the hammam, bringing in the Candela -- that is real work and real capital. I respect it genuinely.
>
> But I have to separate what was invested from what the business produces. These are two fundamentally different questions. The market does not price businesses on what was spent building them. If it did, every business would be worth its construction cost, and there would be no risk in entrepreneurship.
>
> Here is the harder reality: EUR 738,000 of the investment is in the fit-out. That is embedded in the landlord's building. The walls, the plumbing, the hammam infrastructure -- it stays with the hotel if the lease ends. The portable assets -- the laser, the T-Shape, the beds -- those have real market value. At current depreciated rates, EUR 80-120,000.
>
> What I am buying is the income stream, the client relationships, and the right to operate in this location. Those are what determine the price."

### The Reframe

After delivering the above, reframe the EUR 857K as something that SUPPORTS your price:

> "If I think about it from the seller's perspective, they invested EUR 857,000 and the business generates EUR 36,000 in EBITDA. That is a 4.2% return on capital -- below the risk-free rate. Every month they continue operating, they are earning below what a savings account would produce, while the fit-out depreciates and the rent escalates.
>
> A transaction, even at a price below their investment, stops the clock on that. It converts a depreciating asset into liquid capital that can be deployed more productively."

### If They Press: "But Surely You Have to Respect the Investment"

> "I do respect it. And I want to be clear -- I am not trying to take advantage of anyone. But I have to be honest about what the business is worth based on its earnings, not based on what was spent. If someone invested EUR 2 million building a restaurant that earns zero profit, the restaurant is not worth EUR 2 million. The investment history is information, but it is not a valuation input.
>
> Every professional valuation methodology -- IVS, ASA, NACVA -- determines fair market value based on future cash flows, comparable transactions, and asset replacement cost. None of them use historical investment as a determinant. I am applying the same standard that any accountant, any banker, or any independent valuer would apply."

## 10. THE WALK-AWAY SIGNAL

### When to Use It

Use this ONLY if the adviser holds a position above EUR 260K total consideration (all structures included) after you have made your case and explored creative structures. This is your final card.

### How to Signal Without Being Aggressive

The walk-away must be CREDIBLE. It must feel genuine, not theatrical. The way to make it credible is to be quiet, calm, and slightly sad -- not angry or threatening.

**Pre-signal -- slow everything down:**

Take a breath. Put your pen down. Sit back in your chair. Let 3-4 seconds of silence pass. Look at the adviser with a steady, calm expression.

**The Script:**

> "I want to be straightforward with you, because I think we both deserve that at this point.
>
> I have gone as far as my investment group will authorise. The numbers I have shown you today are not a negotiating position -- they are the genuine economics as I see them. Above a certain level, the returns do not justify the risk for us.
>
> I believe in what was built here. I think the location is exceptional. I think the client base is real. And I think with the right operator, this becomes something special. I want to be that operator.
>
> But I am not able to pay for results I have not achieved. The business loses money at BAU. It barely breaks even at Base after depreciation. The lease carries structural risks. The rent escalates every year. I have to price all of that.
>
> If the current expectation is above what works for us, I understand completely. I genuinely wish you well. And if something changes in the future -- if you want to revisit this conversation -- I am always open to it."

### After Delivering the Walk-Away

**Stop talking. Do not add anything.** Do not soften. Do not say "but maybe we can find a way." The power of a walk-away comes from its finality. Any qualifier destroys it.

**Wait for them to speak.** The first person to talk after a walk-away signal has lost.

If they respond with a counter -- ANY counter, even an unreasonable one -- the deal is alive. They are not walking. They are negotiating.

If they say "let us think about it," that is a positive signal. Respond: "Of course. Take whatever time you need." Then leave.

If they say "thank you for your time" and begin to close the meeting, shake their hand warmly. Look them in the eye. Say: "Thank you. I mean that." Walk out. Do not look back. Do not say "maybe we can work something out" on the way to the door.

### The 72-Hour Rule After Walking

- No contact for 72 hours minimum.
- Do not call, text, WhatsApp, or email the adviser or the seller.
- Do not ask anyone connected to the deal "how did it go."
- Do not increase your offer without a formal counter from them.
- The silence IS your negotiation. Every hour that passes without contact from you, the adviser replays the meeting in his head and asks "did I push too hard?"
- In 80% of small Mediterranean deals, the seller's adviser calls back within 7-14 days. His commission depends on a deal closing. He will work on the seller. Let him.

## 11. QUESTIONS THAT ARE ACTUALLY ARGUMENTS

These questions sound innocent but plant specific doubts about business value. Use them strategically throughout the meeting, woven into natural conversation.

### On Revenue Sustainability

> "I noticed revenue was down 17% year-over-year in July 2025. Was that seasonal, or was something else happening?"

*Plants the seed that growth is not guaranteed and has already reversed in some months.*

### On Operational Efficiency

> "Has the seller ever benchmarked the wage ratio against other spas in Malta? I am curious whether they have looked at it."

*Implies the seller never optimized wages -- a polite way of saying the business has been mismanaged.*

### On the Lease

> "If the hotel is sold -- which the Fortina complex sale clause contemplates -- what happens to the business? What does the EUR 150,000 termination payment actually represent?"

*Makes the adviser do the math in real time: the seller's own lease caps their downside at EUR 150K plus depreciated investment. Why should the buyer pay more than the seller's own contractual liquidation value?*

### On Client Retention

> "What percentage of clients have been with the spa since Year 1? Is there data on repeat visit rates?"

*If the answer is "we do not know," it proves the client base is unmeasured and therefore unproven. If the answer is "high," it leads to the follow-up:* "And what happens to those relationships when ownership changes? In my experience, clients follow therapists, not brands."

### On Equipment Condition

> "When was the last Candela laser service? What does the annual maintenance contract cost?"

*If there is no maintenance contract, the EUR 65,000 laser is being run without professional servicing -- which means it may need repair or replacement sooner than assumed. This supports your depreciation argument.*

### On the Hammam

> "I understand the hammam has been out of service. How long has it been down, and what would it cost to repair?"

*The hammam is one of 8 treatment spaces. If it is non-functional, 12.5% of the spa's capacity is offline. This supports the narrative of deferred maintenance and operational neglect.*

### On Future Capex

> "Looking forward 3-5 years, what significant capital expenditures do you see? Treatment room refurbishment? Equipment replacement? Lease-required improvements?"

*This question makes future costs feel immediate. Whatever number the adviser says, you can add it to the purchase price in your head and say "so the total investment to acquire and maintain this business is EUR [price] plus EUR [capex] -- and the return on that combined investment is..."*

### On the Landlord

> "How would you describe the seller's relationship with the Zammit Tabona family? Is it collaborative, transactional, or difficult?"

*This is reconnaissance. If the relationship is difficult, lease assignment becomes a genuine obstacle. If it is collaborative, you have information you can use in the landlord conversation.*

### On the "Only 2 Buyers" Disclosure

> "You mentioned previously that there are two interested parties. Has the other party progressed to a similar stage of analysis?"

*This forces the adviser to reveal the competitive landscape. If the other buyer has NOT progressed this far, you are the serious buyer. If they HAVE, you need to know. Either way, the question signals you are not intimidated by competition.*

## 12. WHAT NOT TO SAY -- THINGS THAT UNDERMINE YOUR POSITION

These are phrases, topics, and behaviors that would damage your negotiating leverage if they appear in the meeting. Memorize this list.

### NEVER Mention These:

1. **Average treatment rate is 30-40% below market (EUR 57 vs EUR 85+).** If you mention this, the adviser immediately says "so there is massive pricing upside -- price it in." Repricing is YOUR value creation. Keep it secret.

2. **Your clinic buildout plans.** Do not mention aesthetics expansion, slimming integration, or nurse practitioner hiring. If the adviser learns that you plan to triple revenue, they will demand participation in that upside. If asked "what are your plans for the business," say: "We would continue the spa operations and look for modest ways to improve efficiency."

3. **Your internal EBITDA projections (EUR 273K+ at Base in your internal model).** The adviser should only ever see the P&L you are presenting, which shows max EBITDA of EUR 76K. If any internal model leaks, the negotiation is over.

4. **"We really want this deal."** Never express strong desire. Instead: "We are evaluating this as one of several options." BATNA power requires them to believe you can walk away.

5. **"My parents are excited about this."** The parents must always be cautious, skeptical, conservative. They are the Bad Cop. You are the Good Cop. If the parents are "excited," they can no longer serve as the constraint.

6. **"To be honest" or "frankly."** These phrases imply everything you said before was dishonest. Cut them from your vocabulary.

7. **"With all due respect."** This always precedes disrespect. Just make your point.

8. **Any specific walk-away number.** Never tell the adviser (or anyone) your ceiling. If they know EUR 260K is your max, they will start at EUR 260K. Your ceiling is private.

9. **Criticism of the seller personally.** "The seller mismanaged this" or "the seller made bad decisions" -- these create defensiveness and close doors. Instead, frame issues as structural: "The business has structural challenges that need addressing."

10. **The word "lowball."** If the adviser accuses you of lowballing, do not use the word yourself. Say: "I understand this is below expectations. Let me explain the reasoning." Never adopt their frame.

11. **Revenue per appointment data (EUR 57).** This is too close to revealing that you know prices are below market. Keep all treatment-level data internal.

12. **The Marion Mizzi acquisition.** Do not mention specific alternative deals by name. Say "we have other opportunities we are evaluating" -- leave it vague. Naming a specific alternative lets the adviser research it and potentially discover information about your position.

### NEVER Do These:

- Do not check your phone during the meeting.
- Do not smile when delivering a low number -- it reads as condescending.
- Do not cross your arms -- it reads as defensive.
- Do not stand up or pace -- it reads as aggression.
- Do not bring more than one person. This is a one-on-one conversation. An entourage signals insecurity.
- Do not leave the printed P&L behind. Take your copy with you. Do not let it be photocopied. "I will send you a clean copy by email" -- this lets you control what they retain.

> "These are not criticisms of what was built. They are inherited issues that any buyer takes on. The 4.2 stars, the hammam, the deferred maintenance -- these represent costs I need to budget for on top of the purchase price. They compress the value of the business as-is."

> "I have walked you through how I see the numbers. I would genuinely like to hear your perspective. Where do you see it differently?"

## 15. THE STEADY-STATE ARGUMENT

This is one of the strongest arguments in your arsenal and it deserves its own section.

### The Core Insight

The seller will argue "the business is growing." Your counter:

> "The business opened in January 2024. Year 1 revenue was EUR 321,000. Year 2 was EUR 426,000. That is 33% growth -- but from a standing start.
>
> Every new business follows the same pattern: rapid early growth as you fill initial capacity, followed by a plateau. It is textbook. The question is not whether the business grew from Year 1 to Year 2 -- of course it did. The question is whether it will grow from Year 2 to Year 3 at anything close to that rate.
>
> The data says no. H2 2025 shows flat-to-declining performance. The growth has already decelerated. The business has reached steady state -- or very close to it -- under the current operating model.
>
> That is why BAU is the right starting point for valuation. The business is at EUR 430,000. The ramp-up is complete. The growth story that justifies premium multiples is over. What remains is a business that generates negative EBITDA at current wage rates."

## 17. POST-MEETING PROTOCOL

### Immediately After the Meeting

- Thank the adviser. Shake hands. Do not linger.
- Walk to your car. Do NOT make any phone calls in the parking lot where you might be seen.
- Drive away. Park somewhere quiet. Write down everything that was said while it is fresh. Specifically:
  - What number (if any) the adviser mentioned
  - What emotional state the adviser was in
  - What questions they asked (this reveals what they care about)
  - What they conceded (even small acknowledgments like "I see your point on the wages")
  - What they pushed back hardest on (this is their anchor)

### The 72-Hour Silence

Do not contact the adviser or the seller for 72 hours. This is non-negotiable. The silence works in your favor:
- The adviser replays the meeting and processes your numbers
- The EUR 867K rent obligation sits in their mind
- The negative EBITDA becomes a reference point
- Your calmness and preparation signal strength

### What to Do If They Contact You

If the adviser calls or messages within 72 hours, respond briefly and warmly:

> "Thank you for following up. I am still processing our conversation with my team. I will come back to you early next week with my thoughts."

Do not discuss numbers. Do not express eagerness. Keep it short.
