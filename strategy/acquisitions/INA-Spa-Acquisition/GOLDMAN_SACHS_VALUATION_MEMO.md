## SECTION 1: REVENUE MULTIPLES IN WELLNESS M&A — WHEN IS 1x APPROPRIATE?

Revenue multiples are a shorthand heuristic. They are only valid when the business generating that revenue retains a meaningful portion of it as profit. The market-clearing question is not "what is revenue?" but "what margin does that revenue carry?"

**Comparable Wellness Transaction Data (2021–2025):**

| Transaction Profile | EV/Revenue | EBITDA Margin | Implied EV/EBITDA |
|---|---|---|---|
| Branded day spa chain, 15%+ EBITDA margins | 0.8x–1.2x | 15–20% | 5–8x |
| Single-location spa, 10% EBITDA margin | 0.4x–0.7x | 10% | 4–7x |
| Single-location spa, 5% EBITDA margin | 0.2x–0.4x | 5% | 4–8x |
| Single-location spa, 0% EBITDA margin | 0.05x–0.15x | 0% | Not applicable |

The rule is straightforward: **1x revenue only makes sense when the asset carries 15–20%+ EBITDA margins**, where the buyer is paying for a scalable profit engine, a brand with proprietary positioning, or locked-in recurring revenue streams. A EUR 400K/year spa generating EUR 0 EBITDA does not meet any of these conditions.

Landmark transactions that inform 1x+ revenue valuations — Marriott's acquisition of spa management contracts, Elements Massage's franchise rollups — all involve either significant margin, proprietary systems, or national brand equity commanding price premiums. INA Spa is a single leasehold inside a third-party hotel with 14 staff generating zero profit. There is no comparable transaction in the dataset that would support 1x revenue at 0% EBITDA.

**Conclusion:** 1x revenue for INA Spa is not a valuation methodology. It is a seller's emotional anchor to capital invested.

**IRR at EUR 260K purchase price:**

Same operational assumptions, same cash flows:

**The EUR 140K price differential (EUR 400K vs. EUR 260K) is the difference between a value-destroying deal at 7.2% IRR and a value-creating deal at 16.8% IRR.** No M&A committee should approve an acquisition below its cost of capital. EUR 400K does not clear the hurdle.

## SECTION 6: THE BRIDGE — CREATIVE DEAL STRUCTURE TO EUR 400K HEADLINE

There is a structure that can deliver a EUR 400K headline price to the seller while protecting the buyer economically. We call this the **Deferred Performance Bridge**.

**Structure:**

- **Closing payment:** EUR 260,000 (cash at close, unconditional)
- **Earnout Tranche 1:** EUR 40,000 payable if cumulative EBITDA in Years 1–2 exceeds EUR 60,000 (i.e., EUR 30K/year average — achievable only if operational improvements materialise)
- **Earnout Tranche 2:** EUR 60,000 payable if cumulative EBITDA in Years 3–4 exceeds EUR 140,000 (i.e., EUR 70K/year — stabilised turnaround scenario)
- **Seller Financing Note:** EUR 40,000 at 4% interest, repayable over 5 years from operating cash flow, subordinated to all operating obligations

**Total headline: EUR 400,000**

**Buyer's effective exposure analysis:**

If the business achieves the turnaround (the only scenario where it makes economic sense to pay EUR 400K), the buyer is generating the cash to fund the earnout payments from the very performance that triggers them. The earnouts are self-financing. The seller participates in the upside they claimed existed. If the business does not achieve the turnaround, the earnout payments are not triggered and the buyer's total outlay remains EUR 260K + the seller note (EUR 40K). Effective floor exposure: **EUR 300K** — still within the EUR 320K walk-away threshold.

**Why this is strategically superior:**

1. The seller gets to say the business sold for EUR 400K (important for their narrative)
2. The buyer's IRR in the downside scenario: 13.4% (still above hurdle)
3. The buyer's IRR in the upside scenario: 18.1% (outperforms standalone purchase)
4. The earnout aligns seller incentives during a transition period if seller stays in a consulting capacity

**One condition:** The earnout must be calculated on EBITDA, not revenue. A revenue-based earnout would incentivise the seller (if retained) to grow top line at the expense of margins, compounding the structural cost problem that produced zero profit in the first place.

The seller's EUR 400K ask reflects a psychological attachment to sunk cost (EUR 800K invested) and a misapplication of revenue multiples divorced from earnings reality. The buyer's current anchor of EUR 260K is not an aggressive lowball — it is the upper bound of DCF and squarely within the football field's central tendency.

Hold at EUR 260K. If negotiation requires movement, offer the Deferred Performance Bridge to a EUR 300K–320K effective value, with EUR 400K as the headline. That structure is economically rational for the buyer, emotionally satisfying for the seller, and — critically — only pays EUR 400K in a world where the business is actually worth EUR 400K.

**The seller asking EUR 400K for a breakeven business is not a negotiation. It is a request for the buyer to finance the seller's sunk cost at a 7.2% IRR. Decline politely. Present the bridge. Close at EUR 260K–295K.**
