### 2.2 Rent as % of EBITDA -- The Critical Ratio

At status quo operations (EUR 36.5K EBITDA), rent consumes nearly 200% of EBITDA. The business cannot service its rent from current profits. This is only viable because the P&L shows EUR 36.5K EBITDA *after* rent -- meaning the business generates EUR 108K operating profit ex-rent, from which EUR 71.7K goes to rent.

The concern is the trajectory: rent grows at approximately 3.5% per year (compounding fixed + variable), while revenue growth is uncertain. If revenue stalls or grows below 3.5%, EBITDA shrinks in real terms.

**Break-even analysis: what revenue growth rate keeps EBITDA stable?**

At current cost structure (wages at 49.2%, COGS at 10.4%, other opex at 15.7%), the business needs revenue growth of at least 3-4% annually just to maintain current EBITDA as rent escalates. Below that growth rate, EBITDA declines toward zero.

### 2.4 Lease Risk Assessment

**Risk 1: Assignment at Absolute Discretion (Clause 5.26)**

This is the single highest risk in the transaction. The Fortina lease states that assignment of the lease requires the lessor's consent, exercisable at "absolute discretion." This is not a reasonableness standard -- the landlord can refuse for any reason or no reason.

Change of control of the lessee entity (Well Being Services Ltd) is deemed an assignment, also requiring consent. This means even a share purchase (buying the company rather than the assets) triggers the consent requirement.

**Mitigation options:**
- Negotiate landlord consent as a condition precedent before signing any offer
- If landlord demands a new lease, negotiate terms directly (risk: higher rent)
- Structure as share purchase to argue existing lease remains with entity (risk: landlord still has Clause 5.26)

**Probability of successful assignment:** We estimate 70-75%. Fortina has commercial incentive to maintain a quality operator, but absolute discretion means they could demand concessions (rent increase, shorter term, personal guarantee).

**Risk 2: Complex Sale Termination**

If the Fortina Hotel complex is sold to a third party, the new owner can terminate the lease with:
- EUR 150,000 one-time payment to the lessee
- Reimbursement of investment at depreciated value (0% for first 2 years, then 10% per year)

At current depreciation schedule (investments made 2022-2024, now 2-4 years old), lessee recovery would be:
- EUR 150K termination payment
- Investment recovery: ~70-80% of EUR 857K = EUR 600-686K depreciated
- Total recovery: EUR 750K-836K

This actually provides meaningful downside protection in a hotel-sale scenario. However, the buyer loses the operating asset and clinic investment.

**Risk 3: "Di Fermo" Period Expiring**

Years 1-5 are irrevocable ("di fermo"). We are currently in Year 2. The irrevocable period ends in approximately Year 5 (2028). After that, the lessee can terminate with 6 months' notice -- which is optionality for the buyer (good), but the lessor may also have enhanced rights depending on contract interpretation.

## SECTION 5: WHAT THE SELLER WILL ARGUE (AND HOW TO COUNTER)

### Argument 1: "Revenue is growing 32.6%"

**Seller's position:** "Revenue grew from EUR 321K to EUR 426K -- a 32.6% increase. This is a high-growth business. You should be paying a growth premium."

**Counter:**

The 32.6% growth is real and acknowledged. However:

- Year 1 to Year 2 growth in any business is largely ramp-up, not organic growth. The business opened in January 2024. Revenue in Year 1 reflects a partial ramp (first-year businesses typically achieve 60-75% of steady-state revenue). The Year 2 figure (EUR 426K) may be closer to steady state.
- The quarterly data confirms this: Q1 2025 (EUR 112.5K), Q2 (EUR 112.3K), Q3 (EUR 85.6K), Q4 (EUR 115.6K). Revenue has been flat between Q1 and Q4 at approximately EUR 113K/quarter. **There is no sequential growth within 2025.** The 32.6% YoY figure reflects Year 1 ramp, not ongoing momentum.
- July 2025 was EUR 22K -- a 54% drop from the March peak of EUR 47.5K. Significant seasonality exists.
- Growth without profitability is not a value driver. A business growing at 32.6% with 0% net margin is growing its losses, not its value.

**Script:**

> "We respect the revenue trajectory. But when we look at the quarterly pattern within 2025, revenue has been flat at approximately EUR 113K per quarter. The YoY growth is a Year 1 ramp effect, not a sustained growth trend. And growth at sub-9% EBITDA margin means you're growing revenue without proportionate profit. That's a cost structure problem, not a growth story."

### Argument 2: "We invested EUR 858K"

**Seller's position:** "We put EUR 858K into this business. You're offering less than 25% of what we invested."

**Counter:**

This is the most emotionally charged argument and the most analytically irrelevant. Investment cost is not a valuation input under any recognized methodology (IVS, ASA, NACVA). The appropriate response is:

> "We have enormous respect for the investment you made. Building what you built from scratch took vision, capital, and persistence. But in any transaction, value is determined by what the business produces, not what it cost to build. If a company invested EUR 10M in a factory that produces EUR 0 profit, the factory is not worth EUR 10M. Your EUR 858K built something real -- EUR 426K in revenue and a trained team. The market values that at what it earns, adjusted for risk. And at EUR 36.5K EBITDA, the math leads to a different place than EUR 858K."

**Do not argue about the EUR 858K. Acknowledge it. Redirect to earnings.**

### Argument 3: "The equipment alone is worth EUR 120K"

**Seller's position:** "The Candela laser cost EUR 65K and the T-Shape EUR 55K. Equipment alone justifies a higher price."

**Counter:**

- Carisma's own assessment values the Candela at EUR 50K and the T-Shape at EUR 15K -- a total of EUR 65K, not EUR 120K
- The T-Shape has depreciated significantly. The EUR 55K original cost was for a device that is now 2+ years into its useful life with newer models available
- These assets are already generating revenue captured in the EUR 426K top line. They are not additive to the EBITDA valuation -- they are already embedded in it
- Portable equipment (the only items with independent value) is worth EUR 65K. Fit-out is worth zero without the lease

> "We agree the Candela has value -- we estimate EUR 50K. The T-Shape has depreciated to approximately EUR 15K. But these devices are already producing revenue that's captured in your EUR 426K top line. We can't count them twice -- once in the EBITDA multiple and again as a separate asset. They're already in the price."

### Argument 4: "Local clientele is 78%"

**Seller's position:** "78% of our clients are local, not hotel guests. This proves the business has a standalone customer base independent of the hotel."

**Counter:**

This is actually a strong point for the seller, and we should acknowledge it. 78% local clientele significantly reduces hotel-dependency risk. However:

> "We agree that the 78% local base is a genuine strength. It reduces our concern about hotel relationship risk. But it also means only 22% of your revenue comes from the hotel channel, which raises the question: what is the premium value of a hotel location if the business is primarily serving local walk-ins? A standalone spa serving 78% local clients could operate from lower-cost premises. The hotel premium in the lease -- which you're paying EUR 72K per year for -- is partially misaligned with your actual client mix."

### Argument 5: "You'll make EUR 150K+ EBITDA once you optimize"

**Seller's position:** "You and I both know you'll fix the staffing, add the clinic, and generate EUR 150K+ in EBITDA. The business is worth what you'll make from it."

**Counter:**

This is the turnaround premium argument. It is intellectually coherent but commercially wrong:

> "If that were true, every unprofitable business would be priced at what a better operator could do with it. That's not how markets work. The turnaround risk sits with the buyer. We bear the redundancy costs, the integration friction, the revenue disruption, and the execution risk. That's why buyers get a discount -- because we're the ones taking the risk of creating the value. If the EBITDA improvement were guaranteed, you'd have done it yourself and sold a EUR 150K EBITDA business for EUR 600K. The fact that you haven't tells us it's harder than it looks."
