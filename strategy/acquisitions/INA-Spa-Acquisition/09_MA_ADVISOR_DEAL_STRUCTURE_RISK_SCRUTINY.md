## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY -- TOP 10 CRITICAL FINDINGS

After reviewing every document in this deal file, reading the full Fortina lease contract clause by clause, stress-testing the financial model, and examining the gap between what has been analysed and what remains unknown, here are the ten findings that matter most.

### The Ten Things That Keep Me Up at Night

**1. THE LEASE IS NOT 13 YEARS -- IT IS 9-10 YEARS.** The financial model uses 13 years of cash flows. The lease commenced July 2020. We are now in Year 6. Remaining term is approximately 9-10 years, expiring around 2035. The model overstates the runway by 3-4 years, which inflates NPV by approximately 15-20%. This is not a minor modelling error -- it materially changes every return metric.

**2. THE 5% TURNOVER FEE APPLIES TO ALL SALES -- NOT JUST SPA.** The lease (Clause 3.1) states the 5% fee is calculated on "the sales generated." There is no carve-out for different business lines. The financial model assumes 0.5% effective rate on clinic and slimming revenue (declaring only 10% to the landlord). This "declare only 10% of clinic/slim revenue" strategy described in the model parameters is legally and commercially dangerous. If the landlord audits (Clause 3.6 grants this right with 2 days' notice), discovery of deliberate underreporting could constitute a material breach triggering lease termination under Clause 9.1.3. You would be risking the entire EUR 350K investment to save EUR 18-27K per year.

**3. THE LEASE ASSIGNMENT CLAUSE IS WORSE THAN "DIFFICULT" -- IT IS AN ABSOLUTE VETO.** Clause 5.26 states consent may be withheld "in their absolute discretion." This is not "not to be unreasonably withheld" -- it is an absolute right. The landlord (Edward Zammit Tabona / Fortina Developments Limited) can say no for any reason or no reason. A Change of Control is explicitly deemed an assignment. There is no legal remedy if consent is refused. This single clause means Carisma could spend EUR 50K+ on due diligence and legal fees and walk away with nothing.

**4. THE COMPLEX SALE TERMINATION CLAUSE IS A TICKING TIME BOMB.** Clause 9.3 allows the landlord to terminate if the Complex is sold OR leased to a third party. The payout is EUR 150K + depreciated investment. For Carisma as a NEW assignee, the critical question is: does the investment reimbursement cover Carisma's incremental EUR 80K clinic capex, or only the original lessee's investment? The contract says "reimburse the investment effected by the Lessee" -- if an assignee is not the original "Lessee" for these purposes, Carisma's EUR 80K clinic build-out may not be reimbursable. This is an unresolved legal question that could mean EUR 80K of capex evaporates if the Complex is sold.

**5. EBITDA OF EUR 36.5K MAY ITSELF BE OVERSTATED.** No audited financial statements exist. Only management accounts have been provided. 24 months of bank statements have NOT been reviewed. VAT returns have NOT been cross-referenced. Without independent verification, the EUR 426K revenue figure is an assertion, not a fact. In my experience, unaudited management accounts in small spa businesses overstate revenue by 5-15% and understate costs by similar margins. If actual EBITDA is EUR 25-30K (a 20-30% haircut), the business is barely cash-flow positive even before depreciation.

**6. 109 OF 136 DUE DILIGENCE ITEMS REMAIN OUTSTANDING.** The DD checklist shows that 80% of requested items have not been received or verified. Of 30 items rated CRITICAL, only a handful are resolved. The deal is being priced and structured based on incomplete information. You are making a EUR 350K investment decision with less than 20% of the data you need.

**7. THE FINANCIAL MODEL CONTAINS STRUCTURAL OPTIMISM.** The model assumes: (a) spa revenue of EUR 440K in Year 1 (higher than current EUR 426K despite transition disruption), (b) clinic revenue of EUR 100K in Year 1 (aggressive for a new launch), (c) slimming revenue of EUR 80K in Year 1 (an entirely new service line with zero current revenue), (d) wage-to-revenue ratio declining from 49% to 38% within 3 years, (e) 5% tax rate (correct for Malta imputation but only if structured properly), and (f) zero terminal value (conservative, but realistic given lease expiry). Revenue growing immediately post-acquisition is not what happens in the real world. Post-acquisition disruption typically reduces revenue by 5-15% in Year 1 before recovery.

**8. THE "DECLARE ONLY 10% OF CLINIC/SLIM REVENUE" STRATEGY IS A DEAL-KILLER RISK.** This is embedded in the model parameters and saves EUR 18-27K/year on turnover fees. The lease says "5% fee on sales generated." Operating a clinic and slimming business from the premises and declaring only 10% of their revenue to the landlord is, at minimum, a breach of the spirit of the lease. At maximum, it is fraud. The landlord has the contractual right to inspect accounts with 2 days' notice (Clause 3.6). If discovered: lease termination for breach (Clause 9.1.3), 14 days' notice, loss of entire investment. This "saving" of EUR 18-27K/year risks EUR 350K+ of capital. The expected value of this strategy is deeply negative.

**9. THE HOTEL PARTNERSHIP IS NOT CONTRACTUAL.** 22% of revenue (EUR 93K) comes from Barcelo Hotel referrals. No formal hotel partnership agreement has been produced. If the hotel changes management, brands (it is referred to as Barcelo, Numi, and Fortina across various documents -- suggesting instability), or simply decides to build its own in-house spa, this revenue stream disappears. The financial model treats hotel revenue as stable and growing. It should be treated as at-risk.

**10. THE SELLER IS RATIONALLY BETTER OFF NOT SELLING AT EUR 200K.** The seller invested EUR 857K. At EUR 200K, they absorb a EUR 657K loss. The Complex sale termination clause (9.3) would pay them approximately EUR 593K (EUR 150K + depreciated investment of EUR 443K). Simply continuing to operate and waiting for the Complex to be sold is a better financial outcome than accepting EUR 200K. This means the seller's negotiation floor is not EUR 200K -- it is closer to EUR 300K (the point at which selling becomes preferable to holding and hoping). Offering EUR 200K may not just be rejected -- it may be perceived as an insult that destroys the negotiation entirely.

### Overall Deal Assessment

**CONDITIONAL BUY at EUR 200-220K -- but ONLY after lease assignment is confirmed in writing with no rent premium, the 5% turnover fee applicability to clinic revenue is resolved with the landlord, and at least the 14 CRITICAL due diligence items are satisfied.**

Without lease confirmation: **PASS.** Do not spend another euro on this deal until the landlord has said yes in writing.

The short answer: it is directionally correct, but the range should be wider and the conditions more stringent.

**What the numbers say:**

**The convergence zone for what the seller is actually selling: EUR 175-220K.**

Everything above EUR 220K is paying for value that Carisma will create through clinic buildout, wage restructuring, and marketing optimization. The seller has no legitimate claim to that value.

### 2.2 What Changed from EUR 340K to EUR 200-220K?

The February 22 reports recommended EUR 340K based on EBITDA of EUR 72K. The March 6 data room revealed actual EBITDA of EUR 36.5K -- exactly half. The math is mechanical:

- EUR 72K x 4.7x = EUR 340K (old target)
- EUR 36.5K x 4.7x = EUR 172K (same multiple, real earnings)
- EUR 36.5K x 5.5-6.0x = EUR 200-219K (generous multiple, real earnings)

The repricing is justified. The old target was built on a foundation that does not exist.

### 2.3 Will EUR 200-220K Insult the Seller?

Yes, probably. And that is a problem.

The seller's emotional anchor is EUR 857K invested. Their rational anchor is the EUR 593K Complex sale termination payout. Their advisory's likely opening is EUR 420K with a floor around EUR 300K.

Offering EUR 200K represents a 77% discount to sunk cost and a 66% discount to the termination payout alternative. Even though the offer is analytically sound, the perception gap may be too wide to bridge.

**My recommendation:** Open at EUR 180K (establishing an aggressive anchor), move to EUR 200K with earnout (EUR 220K total), and be prepared to reach EUR 240K maximum (EUR 200K cash + EUR 20K earnout + EUR 20K seller note, per the term sheet structure). This still represents 6.6x actual EBITDA -- generous by any standard.

### 2.4 Is 5.5-6.0x EBITDA the Right Multiple?

For a single-location spa with 8.6% EBITDA margins, loss-making after depreciation, absolute-discretion lease assignment, Complex sale termination risk, and only 9-10 years of lease remaining, 5.5-6.0x is at the high end of fair. Comparable multiples for similar businesses:

- UK single-location spas (2023-2025): 3.0-5.0x EBITDA
- European leisure/wellness (sub-EUR 500K EBITDA): 3.5-5.5x EBITDA
- Malta market (micro-cap, illiquid): typically 2.5-4.5x EBITDA with significant negotiation

At 6.0x, Carisma is already paying a premium that reflects the lease, location, equipment, and client base. Going above 6.0x would require the seller to demonstrate earnings power that does not yet exist.

### 2.5 Is the EBITDA of EUR 36.5K Even Real?

I do not know, and neither does anyone else, because:

- No audited financial statements have been provided
- No bank statements have been reviewed (24 months outstanding)
- No VAT returns have been cross-referenced
- No POS/booking system reports have been independently verified
- The only source is seller-provided management accounts

In my 30 years of doing deals, management accounts in small businesses are accurate plus or minus 15% at best. The EUR 36.5K could be EUR 30K. It could be EUR 42K. Until bank statements are in hand, treat it as an estimate.

**Recommendation:** Build a EUR 5K EBITDA haircut into the model (use EUR 31.5K as the conservative anchor) until bank statements confirm the reported figures.

## 3. LEASE RISK ASSESSMENT -- DEEP DIVE

The Fortina lease is a 34-page contract signed January 10, 2020, between Fortina Developments Limited (Lessor, signed by Edward Zammit Tabona) and Well Being Services Limited (Lessee, signed by John Muscat). I have read every clause. Here are the findings that matter.

### 3.1 Assignment -- Clause 5.26 (DEAL-CRITICAL)

**Exact language:** "Not to assign or attempt to assign their interest in this Lease in respect of the whole or any part of the Premises without the prior written consent of the Lessor. The Lessor shall be entitled to withhold their consent in their absolute discretion. If the Lessee are a company (other than a company listed on a recognized stock exchange) a Change of Control of the Lessee shall be deemed to be an assignment without the prior written consent of the Lessor."

**What this means:**
- Whether Carisma buys assets or shares, landlord consent is required
- The landlord can refuse for ANY reason or NO reason
- There is no legal mechanism to force consent
- The landlord can use the consent process to extract concessions: higher rent, personal guarantees, new deposit, consent fee
- If the landlord says no, there is no deal at any price

**What previous analyses got right:** Identifying this as a deal-killer.

**What previous analyses missed:** The landlord's incentive structure. The Zammit Tabona family owns the entire Fortina Complex -- hotel, apartments, offices, retail, spa. They want a profitable, well-operated spa that enhances the hotel's value proposition. Carisma, as an established multi-brand wellness operator, is arguably a MORE attractive tenant than Well Being Services Limited, a single-asset company that loses money. The landlord consent process should be framed as an upgrade, not a transfer.

**Recommended approach:**
1. Approach the landlord BEFORE making any offer to the seller
2. Present Carisma's credentials, financial strength, and plans
3. Ask for consent in principle, with terms to be documented
4. If the landlord refuses or demands onerous terms, walk away before spending money on DD

### 3.2 Complex Sale Termination -- Clause 9.3 (HIGH RISK)

**Exact language:** "The Lessor shall also be entitled to terminate this Lease either: (a) if the Complex is sold to a third party or (b) if the Complex is leased to a third party, after the third party expresses the desire to terminate this Lease Agreement, and this by giving the six (6) month notice to the Lessee."

**Termination payout:** EUR 150K one-time payment + reimbursement of investment at depreciated value (0% in first 2 years, then 10% per year).

**Current depreciation status (for original lessee):**
- Investment: EUR 738K fit-out
- Years since investment: approximately 4-6 years (depending on when fit-out was completed)
- Depreciation: 0% years 1-2, then 10%/year for years 3-6 = approximately 20-40% depreciated
- Remaining value: EUR 443K-590K
- Total payout: EUR 593K-740K

**Critical unresolved question:** If Carisma invests EUR 80K in clinic capex as a new assignee, does that investment qualify for reimbursement under this clause? The contract says "reimburse the investment effected by the Lessee." If the assignment creates a new lessee relationship, Carisma's EUR 80K should qualify. If the contract is interpreted as referring only to the original lessee's investment at commencement, it may not. This must be resolved by legal counsel before committing capex.

**Probability assessment:** The Fortina Complex is a major mixed-use development in one of Malta's most valuable seafront locations. Part 2 of the Schedule describes a state-of-the-art complex with residential, hotel, office, retail, and restaurant components. The Zammit Tabona family has held this property for decades. A sale is not imminent based on public information, but:
- The Complex is a trophy asset that would attract significant institutional interest
- The Zammit Tabona family may seek liquidity for generational reasons
- A change in hotel brand/operator (already happened -- from Fortina to Barcelo to potentially "Numi") suggests ongoing restructuring
- The schedule mentions a "Lido across Tigne Seafront road" as an extension -- suggesting ongoing development activity

**Probability of Complex sale in next 10 years:** 15-25% (my estimate). This is non-trivial and should be modelled.

### 3.3 Rent Structure -- Clause 3.1 (SIGNIFICANT)

The rent has two components:

**Component 1: Fixed rent (escalating 3% annually)**

| Lease Year | Calendar Year (est.) | Fixed Rent (EUR) |
|---|---|---|
| Year 6 (current) | 2025/26 | 65,563 |
| Year 7 | 2026/27 | 67,530 |
| Year 10 | 2029/30 | 73,792 |
| Year 13 | 2032/33 | 80,635 |
| Year 15 | 2034/35 | 85,546 |

**Component 2: 5% turnover fee on sales (minimum EUR 350K)**

The lease states: "five per cent (5%) fee calculated on the sales generated. The latter fee shall always be calculated on a minimum of three hundred fifty thousand Euro (EUR 350,000) sales or whichever amount is greatest."

**Critical finding -- NO carve-out for different business lines:** The 5% applies to ALL sales generated at the premises. The financial model's assumption of 0.5% effective rate on clinic and slimming revenue (by "declaring only 10%") has no basis in the contract language. Sales are sales. If Carisma generates EUR 700K of total revenue, the turnover fee is 5% x EUR 700K = EUR 35K.

**Total rent projection (honest calculation):**

| Year | Fixed Rent | Revenue (est.) | 5% Turnover Fee | Total Rent | Rent % of Revenue |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Yr 1 (Carisma) | 67,530 | 500,000 | 25,000 | 92,530 | 18.5% |
| Yr 3 | 71,643 | 620,000 | 31,000 | 102,643 | 16.6% |
| Yr 5 | 76,006 | 700,000 | 35,000 | 111,006 | 15.9% |
| Yr 10 | 85,546 | 800,000 | 40,000 | 125,546 | 15.7% |

The landlord effectively owns a 5% revenue royalty. Every euro of growth Carisma creates, the landlord takes 5 cents. Over 10 years, cumulative rent could exceed EUR 1.1 million.

### 3.4 Permitted Use -- Definitions Section

**Exact language:** "Permitted Use: To be used as a Spa or any related use."

"Any related use" is ambiguous. Laser hair removal and body contouring (already operating) arguably fall within "related use." Medical injectables (Botox, fillers), IV therapy, and prescription skincare are more aggressive interpretations. If the landlord takes a narrow view, the entire clinic thesis collapses.

**Recommendation:** Obtain explicit landlord written confirmation that medical aesthetics, injectables, IV therapy, and slimming consultations are permitted uses. This must be part of the assignment consent letter. Do not rely on the ambiguity.

### 3.5 Accounts Audit Right -- Clause 3.6

**Exact language:** "The Lessor reserves the right to demand to the Lessee to check at any point in time throughout this Agreement, with a two (2) day prior notice, the accounts of the Lessee."

This is directly relevant to the "declare only 10% of clinic revenue" strategy. The landlord can audit with 48 hours' notice. If they find systematic underreporting, the consequences could include: back-payment of underpaid turnover fees, interest at 8% (Clause 3.4), and potential lease termination for breach.

### 3.6 Security Deposit -- Clause 3.5

The lessee must deposit 3 months' rent as a banker's guarantee. At current rates, this is approximately EUR 20-22K. It is unclear whether Carisma must provide a new guarantee or whether the existing guarantee transfers. This is an additional cost that should be in the model.

### 3.7 Subletting -- Clause 5.27

Subletting requires prior written landlord consent. The hair salon sublease opportunity (EUR 20K/year potential) is contingent on this consent. Do not model sublease income until confirmed.

### 3.8 Alterations -- Clause 5.9

Any alterations require prior written consent of the Lessor with plans submitted in advance. For non-structural internal alterations, consent "shall not be unreasonably withheld." For structural alterations, consent "may otherwise be withheld or granted subject to such conditions as the Lessor may in their sole discretion consider appropriate."

Clinic room conversion will involve some modifications (plumbing, electrical, partition changes). These must be approved. Budget time (4-6 weeks for approval) and possible conditions from the landlord.

### 3.9 Reinstatement -- Clause 5.34

On lease expiry: surrender premises in "good and substantial condition," replace any broken fixtures, make good any damage from removal of lessee's fixtures, and pay for any outstanding works. Unless the Lessor requires removal, internal partitions, fixtures, and fittings are to remain, floors, ceilings/soffit left serviceable, walls plastered and in good state.

This means at lease end, the EUR 80K clinic build-out stays with the landlord. Carisma recovers nothing unless it removes portable equipment.

### 3.10 No Renewal Right -- Clause 2.A.3

"The Term may not be extended except with the consent of both parties in writing."

There is no automatic renewal right. No option to extend. No right of first refusal. At the end of the 15-year term (approximately 2035), the landlord has no obligation to continue the tenancy. Carisma could invest EUR 350K+, build a profitable business, and be evicted at lease end with zero compensation.

## 8. DEAL STRUCTURE RECOMMENDATIONS

### 8.1 Asset Purchase vs. Share Purchase

**Asset purchase is correct.** Reasons:

- Avoids inheriting unknown liabilities of Well Being Services Limited
- Allows cherry-picking of assets (take the Candela laser, leave any problematic contracts)
- Enables purchase price allocation optimization (allocating more to equipment reduces stamp duty)
- Simpler tax treatment (Malta's TOGC provisions apply)
- Avoids inheriting any tax disputes, regulatory issues, or litigation

The one disadvantage: lease assignment requires landlord consent (but so does a change of control in a share purchase under Clause 5.26). There is no structural advantage to share purchase for lease purposes.

### 8.2 Recommended Price Structure

| Cash at close | EUR 180-200K | Base consideration |

| **Total headline** | **EUR 220-240K** | |

The earnout protects against revenue overstatement. The seller note keeps the seller engaged during transition and provides a security mechanism (secured against portable equipment).

### 8.3 Critical Protection Mechanisms

**Must be in the APA:**

1. **Lease assignment as condition precedent** -- Deal does not close until landlord consent is obtained in writing with no rent increase above the contractual schedule.

2. **Landlord confirmation of permitted use** -- Medical aesthetics, injectables, and IV therapy must be explicitly confirmed as permitted.

9. **Prepaid liability deduction** -- All outstanding gift cards, vouchers, and prepaid packages must be quantified and deducted from the purchase price (or covered by the seller).

10. **MAC clause** -- If EBITDA falls below EUR 2K/month for 3 consecutive months between signing and closing, Carisma can renegotiate or walk.

## 10. DEAL-BREAKER SCENARIOS

### Walk Away If:

1. **Landlord refuses lease assignment** -- No deal at any price. Do not restructure as a management agreement or sublease -- these create additional complexity and risk.

2. **Landlord restricts medical aesthetics** -- Without the clinic, the deal does not generate adequate returns. The spa alone produces EUR 36.5K EBITDA, which at EUR 200K purchase price delivers approximately 12% IRR over 10 years (spa only, no clinic). This is below the 18% hurdle rate.

9. **The seller demands more than EUR 260K all-in** -- Above this price, even with the clinic, the bear case IRR drops below 10%. This is not a risk-adjusted return worthy of a concentrated, illiquid, single-location investment.

## 11. NEGOTIATION STRATEGY CRITIQUE

### 11.1 What the Current Strategy Gets Right

- **Data-driven pricing** -- The repricing from EUR 340K to EUR 200-220K following the data room is exactly correct. Good discipline.
- **Multiple valuation methodologies** -- Football field approach showing convergence across EBITDA, DCF, revenue, and replacement cost methods.

### 11.2 What the Current Strategy Gets Wrong

1. **Opening at EUR 160K may be too aggressive.** The M&A Advisor Deep Dive recommends opening at EUR 160K (4.4x EBITDA). This is analytically defensible but may be perceived as insulting given the seller's EUR 857K investment and EUR 593K termination alternative. The seller may refuse to counter, ending negotiations before they start. Better to open at EUR 180K with clear analytical justification.

2. **The "declare only 10% of clinic revenue" assumption is built into the return projections.** This is not a negotiation strategy problem -- it is a structural integrity problem. The entire return profile of the deal is partly built on a lease compliance gamble. Remove this assumption, model full 5% on all revenue, and see if the deal still works. (Spoiler: it does, but returns are 2-3% lower.)

3. **Insufficient attention to the landlord as the third party in this deal.** The negotiation strategy focuses on buyer-seller dynamics. But the landlord is arguably the most important party. The landlord holds absolute veto power, can terminate the lease, audits accounts, and approves alterations. The landlord strategy should precede the seller strategy.

4. **Timeline pressure not adequately managed.** The 45-day exclusivity period in the term sheet may not be enough. Landlord consent alone could take 4-8 weeks. Legal DD, equipment inspection, regulatory inquiries, and employment contract review add another 4-6 weeks. The total pre-close process is realistically 3-4 months, not 45 days. Request 90-day exclusivity.

5. **Underestimation of seller's walk-away option.** The strategy treats EUR 250K as the absolute walk-away for Carisma. But the Monte Carlo simulation shows that at EUR 250K, there is still a 31% probability of positive NPV (conditional on lease success). The question is not "what is the maximum analytically defensible price?" but "what price maximizes the probability of closing a deal that works?" That price may be EUR 240-250K with strong protections.

6. **No specific plan for the landlord meeting.** Before any offer to the seller, Carisma should have an informal meeting with the Zammit Tabona family or their representative. Present Carisma's credentials, express interest in the spa, and gauge receptivity to an assignment. This intelligence shapes everything.
