## THE TWO DEAL-BREAKERS

Everything in this document serves two objectives:

1. **SUBLEASE RIGHTS** -- Mert must be allowed to sublease portions of the spa premises. Current Clause 5.27 requires landlord consent with no reasonableness standard. The hotel can refuse for any reason.

2. **HAIR SALON CONVERSION** -- Mert needs to convert/remove the existing hair salon within the spa to repurpose that space. Clause 5.9 requires landlord consent. Non-structural internal alterations cannot be unreasonably withheld. Structural or external changes are at the landlord's sole discretion.

If either fails, the deal dies.

# PART 1: HOW TO GET THE HOTEL TO AGREE TO SUBLEASE RIGHTS

## 1.1 The Legal Starting Position

Clause 5.27 is a restrictive alienation clause. It prohibits the lessee from sub-letting, sharing possession, or granting rights over any part of the premises without "prior written consent of the Lessor." Critically, unlike Clause 5.9 (alterations), there is NO "shall not be unreasonably withheld" qualifier. This means the hotel has absolute discretion to refuse.

This is the hardest ask in the entire negotiation. You are asking the landlord to give up a contractual right they deliberately reserved.

## 1.2 Why the Hotel Should Say Yes: The Value Proposition

The M&A advisor / seller must build a case that granting sublease rights is in the hotel's financial interest. Here are the arguments, ranked by persuasive power:

### ARGUMENT 1: The 5% Turnover Fee Makes the Hotel a Silent Beneficiary (STRONGEST)

The lease includes a 5% turnover fee on all sales above the EUR 350,000 minimum base. Every euro of additional revenue that Carisma generates -- including revenue from sub-tenants operating within the spa premises -- increases the hotel's income.

**The script:** "Carisma plans to bring in specialist operators for certain treatments -- medical aesthetics practitioners, for example. These sub-tenants will generate revenue that flows through the spa's books. Your 5% turnover fee applies to ALL sales on the premises. More operators means more revenue, which means a larger turnover fee for you. At our projected revenue levels, your annual turnover fee increases from roughly EUR 4,000 today to EUR 30,000-48,000 per year. Over the remaining 9 years, that is an additional EUR 234,000-396,000 in your pocket. You are being paid handsomely for granting this consent."

**The math to show:**

| Scenario | Annual Revenue | 5% Turnover Fee (on excess above EUR 350K) | Hotel's Annual Gain vs. Today |
|----------|---------------:|-------------------------------------------:|------------------------------:|
| INA Today | EUR 430K | EUR 4,000 | -- |
| Carisma Base (with sub-tenants) | EUR 961K | EUR 30,550 | +EUR 26,550 |
| Carisma Bull (with sub-tenants) | EUR 1,321K | EUR 48,550 | +EUR 44,550 |

**9-year cumulative hotel windfall from approving subleasing: EUR 239,000-401,000.**

### ARGUMENT 2: Quality and Guest Experience Enhancement

**The script:** "Carisma operates over 50 hotel spas across multiple properties. What we have learned is that specialisation drives quality. Rather than having one operator try to do everything -- massage, hair, nails, aesthetics, slimming -- we bring in best-in-class operators for each discipline. The hotel's guests get a superior experience. That drives hotel reviews, repeat bookings, and RevPAR. Think of it like a food court model in a premium setting -- each operator is the best at what they do, and the overall offering is far stronger than any single operator could deliver."

### ARGUMENT 3: Risk Diversification for the Landlord

**The script:** "Right now, you have one tenant responsible for the entire spa revenue. If that one operator struggles, your turnover fee drops to zero and you are left with a single distressed tenant. With sub-tenants, you have multiple revenue streams within the same premises. If one sub-tenant underperforms, the others continue generating revenue. Your turnover fee is more stable and diversified. This is the same principle as a shopping mall -- multiple tenants are more resilient than one."

### ARGUMENT 4: Higher Premises Utilisation

**The script:** "The hair salon today generates approximately EUR 3,000-5,000 per month. That is EUR 720-1,200 per square meter per year. We plan to convert that space into a medical aesthetics clinic generating EUR 8,700-12,700 per square meter per year -- a 7-12x increase. Whether we operate that ourselves or bring in a specialist sub-tenant, the premises become dramatically more productive. That higher utilisation benefits you through the turnover fee and increases the value of your property."

### ARGUMENT 5: The Hotel Retains Full Control Through Approval Rights

**The script:** "We are not asking for unrestricted subleasing. We are proposing that subletting be permitted subject to your prior written approval of each sub-tenant. You would approve the identity of the sub-tenant, the permitted use, and the quality standards. You retain full veto power over anyone we bring in. All we are asking is that you do not withhold approval unreasonably -- the same standard that already applies to alterations under Clause 5.9."

## 1.3 Concessions Mert Can Offer to Get Sublease Rights

These are tradeable items. The M&A advisor should NOT offer them proactively. They should be held in reserve and deployed only if the hotel resists. Start with the value proposition. Only concede if needed.

### CONCESSION 1: Approval Rights Over Sub-Tenants (LOW COST, HIGH PERCEIVED VALUE)

"We will not sublease to anyone without your prior written approval. You approve the identity, the use, and the quality standards. If you object to a specific sub-tenant, we accept your decision."

**Why this works:** The hotel's real fear is losing control of who operates in their building. Giving them approval rights costs Mert nothing (he would choose quality operators anyway) but addresses the landlord's core anxiety.

### CONCESSION 2: Revenue Transparency (LOW COST)

"We will provide quarterly revenue reports for the entire spa operation, including all sub-tenant revenue, so you can verify the turnover fee calculations."

**Why this works:** Landlords fear subleasing because it creates opacity. They worry sub-tenants will hide revenue. Offering transparency costs nothing and builds trust.

### CONCESSION 3: Quality Standards Covenant (LOW COST)

"We will ensure all sub-tenants comply with the same quality standards as the main operation. We will include this in our sub-lease agreements and you can inspect at any time."

### CONCESSION 4: Revenue Floor Guarantee (MODERATE COST)

"We will guarantee a minimum annual turnover of EUR 600,000 for the purposes of the 5% turnover fee calculation, regardless of actual sales. This means your minimum annual turnover fee is EUR 12,500 -- compared to approximately EUR 4,000 today."

**Why this works:** Removes the hotel's downside risk entirely. Even if the sub-tenant model fails, the hotel is guaranteed 3x their current turnover fee income. Deploy this only if the hotel is fixated on risk.

**Cost to Mert:** If revenue drops below EUR 600K, Mert pays the difference. At EUR 500K revenue, cost is EUR 5,000 extra per year. At EUR 400K revenue, cost is EUR 10,000 extra per year. This is manageable insurance.

### CONCESSION 5: Modest Rent Increase (HIGH COST -- LAST RESORT)

"We would consider a small adjustment to the fixed rent base -- say EUR 2,000 per year above the current schedule -- in exchange for the subletting right being formally added to the lease."

**Why this is last resort:** EUR 2,000/year over 9 years = EUR 18,000. That is real money for a right that should be obtainable through the other concessions. Only offer this if the hotel is genuinely resistant and you have exhausted other options.

### CONCESSION 6: Extended Lease Commitment (MODERATE COST, HIGH VALUE)

"In exchange for sublease rights, we would be willing to extend the lease commitment by agreeing to waive our termination right under the 'di rispetto' period for the next 3-5 years."

**Why this works:** The hotel values tenant stability. If Mert commits to staying longer, the hotel gets guaranteed rent income. This is particularly powerful because Mert plans to stay anyway (he is investing EUR 250K+ in the business), so the cost of this concession is nearly zero.

## 1.4 Creative Structures That Make the Hotel Comfortable

### STRUCTURE A: Management Agreement Instead of Sub-Lease

Rather than a traditional sublease, structure the arrangement as a "management agreement" or "concession agreement" where the specialist operator provides services under Carisma's brand and license, rather than as an independent tenant. All revenue flows through Carisma's books (where the 5% turnover fee applies), and the specialist is paid a fixed fee or revenue share.

**Legal position:** This may not technically constitute "subletting" under Clause 5.27 because the specialist is not in exclusive possession of any part of the premises. They are a contractor operating under Carisma's supervision.

**Script:** "We are not proposing to sublease in the traditional sense. We bring in specialist practitioners who operate under our brand, under our supervision, using our booking system. Revenue flows through our books. The practitioner does not have a separate lease with you -- they have a service agreement with us. This is no different from a restaurant hiring a pastry chef from an external catering company."

**Risk:** The landlord's lawyers may see through this and argue it is a disguised sublease. However, if the specialist does not have exclusive possession and operates under Carisma's direction, the argument has merit under Maltese law.

### STRUCTURE B: Profit-Sharing With the Hotel

"We propose a revenue share on all sub-tenant activities. For every sub-tenant we bring in, you receive an additional 2% of that sub-tenant's revenue on top of the existing 5% turnover fee. This means you earn 7% on sub-tenant revenue."

**Why this works:** The hotel becomes a direct economic beneficiary of subleasing. Their incentive flips from "why should I allow this?" to "I want more sub-tenants."

**Cost to Mert:** At EUR 200K sub-tenant revenue, cost is EUR 4,000/year. Manageable.

### STRUCTURE C: Joint Venture Model

"We form a simple partnership for the clinic component where the hotel holds a small equity stake (5-10%) in the sub-operation. This aligns interests completely."

**Why this works:** The hotel becomes an investor, not just a landlord. Their motivation shifts entirely to supporting the operation's success.

**Risk:** Complexity. The hotel may not want the administrative burden or liability exposure of a JV. Only propose this if the hotel is sophisticated and interested in upside.

## 1.5 Psychological Persuasion Tactics for Landlord Negotiations

People are 2x more motivated to avoid losses than to capture equivalent gains. Frame the current situation as the hotel losing money.

**Script:** "Right now, your spa premises are generating roughly EUR 430K in annual revenue. Your turnover fee is about EUR 4,000 per year. The hair salon space alone is generating EUR 36,000-60,000 per year -- that is EUR 720-1,200 per square meter. Every month that space stays as a hair salon, you are leaving EUR 1,500-2,000 in turnover fees on the table. That is money that is being lost right now."

### TACTIC 2: RECIPROCITY (Cialdini Principle)

Give before you ask. The advisor should offer something valuable to the hotel before requesting sublease rights.

**Script:** "Before we discuss the subletting question, I want you to know that Carisma has already committed to investing EUR 150,000 in upgrading the spa premises. That investment improves the value of your building at no cost to you. We are giving you a better tenant, a better premises, and more income. Against that backdrop, we would like to discuss the flexibility to bring in specialist operators."

### TACTIC 3: SOCIAL PROOF (Cialdini Principle)

Show that other hotels grant sublease rights as standard practice.

**Script:** "In most hotel-spa arrangements we operate, the management agreement includes the right to bring in specialist practitioners. ### TACTIC 4: ANCHORING HIGH (Tversky & Kahneman)

Before asking for sublease rights, anchor the conversation to a much larger ask. Then "retreat" to the sublease right, making it seem like a modest request.

**Script:** "Ideally, we would like to discuss an expansion of the spa premises to include additional treatment rooms on the ground floor [large ask]. We understand that may not be possible right now. What IS possible, and what would make a significant difference, is the flexibility to bring in specialist operators for specific disciplines within the existing footprint [the real ask]."

### TACTIC 5: SCARCITY AND URGENCY

Create a sense that this opportunity is time-limited.

**Script:** "Carisma is evaluating several locations in Malta for their next spa operation. The Fortina is our first choice because of the existing infrastructure. But if we cannot get the operational flexibility we need, we have alternatives. We would rather invest here -- but we need to know that the partnership works both ways."

### TACTIC 6: THE LABELLED OBJECTION (Chris Voss)

Name the hotel's likely objection before they raise it. This disarms the objection.

**Script:** "I know you might be thinking: 'If we allow subletting, we lose control of who is in our building.' I completely understand that concern. ### TACTIC 7: FUTURE-PACING (NLP Technique)

Paint a vivid picture of the future that includes sublease rights, making the hotel visualise the positive outcome.

**Script:** "Imagine this: 18 months from now, the spa is generating over a million euros in revenue. ## 1.6 Real-World Case Studies: Tenants Winning Sublease Rights

### CASE 1: WeWork and the Sublease Revolution (2010-2019)

WeWork's entire business model was built on taking restrictive commercial leases and negotiating sublease rights. Their approach: demonstrate to landlords that subleasing would increase occupancy rates and overall building revenue. WeWork offered landlords percentage-of-revenue participation and quality control covenants. By 2019, landlords competed to give WeWork sublease rights because the model demonstrably increased property values by 15-25%.

**Lesson for today:** Frame subleasing as an operational model that increases the property's value, not as a right being taken from the landlord.

### CASE 2: Marriott International -- Hotel Spa Operator Model

Marriott operates spas in its hotels through third-party management companies (e.g., Steiner Leisure, WTS International). These operators routinely sub-contract specialist treatments (aesthetics, wellness, fitness) to independent practitioners. The hotel's concern is quality control, which is addressed through brand standards and operator approval.

**Lesson for today:** The hotel-spa industry standard is to allow specialist sub-operators. Present this as normal business practice, not an unusual request.

### CASE 3: Selfridges Birmingham -- Department Store Concession Model

Selfridges allows "concession" operators within its department store under strict brand standards. Each concession operates semi-independently but under Selfridges' umbrella. Revenue is tracked through a unified POS, and Selfridges takes a percentage. The landlord (Hammerson) benefits from the higher overall revenue and foot traffic.

**Lesson for today:** The "concession within a concession" model is established in retail. Apply the same logic: specialist operators within the spa, all under Carisma's brand umbrella, with revenue flowing through a single POS for turnover fee calculation.

### CASE 4: Regent Street Crown Estate Negotiations (London)

The Crown Estate, which owns Regent Street in London, historically had extremely restrictive alienation clauses. In the 2000s, tenants successfully negotiated qualified subletting rights by offering: (a) landlord approval over sub-tenants, (b) sublease rent not to exceed head lease rent (prevents rack-renting), (c) tenant remaining liable under the head lease (no release). This framework is now standard in UK commercial leases.

**Lesson for today:** Offer the hotel all three protections: approval rights, revenue transparency, and Carisma remaining liable under the head lease regardless of any subletting.

### CASE 5: Dubai Mall -- Multi-Operator Wellness Zones

Dubai Mall's wellness zone operates as a single leased premises with multiple specialist operators (spa, salon, aesthetics, wellness). The head tenant (a management company) subleases to specialists and guarantees the landlord (Emaar) a minimum revenue commitment. Emaar collects a percentage of all revenue.

**Lesson for today:** Revenue guarantee + percentage rent = landlord incentive to approve subleasing.

# PART 2: HOW TO GET PERMISSION TO CHANGE THE HAIR SALON

## 2.1 The Legal Starting Position

Clause 5.9 (Alterations) provides that "consent shall not be unreasonably withheld in the case of non-structural internal alterations only." Structural changes or external changes are at the Lessor's "sole discretion."

This is MUCH stronger ground than the sublease clause. The key question is: is the hair salon conversion a non-structural internal alteration?

## 2.2 Why the Hair Salon Conversion Is Non-Structural

The conversion plan (per the INA Med Spa Conversion Plan) involves:

1. **Removing hair salon fixtures** -- wash stations, styling chairs, mirrors, chemical storage. These are trade fixtures, not structural elements. They are attached to the building for the purpose of the business and can be removed without affecting the building's structural integrity.

2. **Installing partition walls** -- to create treatment rooms within the open salon space. Partition walls are lightweight, non-load-bearing dividers. They do not affect the building's structure. Installing partitions is the textbook definition of a non-structural internal alteration.

3. **Upgrading plumbing for clinical sinks** -- the hair salon already has plumbing for wash stations. Redirecting existing plumbing to clinical sinks is a plumbing modification, not a structural change.

4. **Upgrading electrical circuits** -- adding dedicated circuits for medical equipment. Electrical work is an internal alteration, not a structural change.

5. **Installing ventilation for laser extraction** -- adding mechanical extraction. HVAC modifications are internal alterations unless they penetrate external walls. If extraction vents through an existing building duct or internal route, this is non-structural.

6. **New flooring** -- replacing salon flooring with sealed vinyl or epoxy. Flooring replacement is an internal alteration.

**Legal conclusion:** Every element of the hair salon conversion falls within "non-structural internal alteration." Consent cannot be unreasonably withheld.

### What Would Be "Unreasonable" Withholding?

Under Maltese commercial lease law (and consistent with UK/EU precedent which Maltese courts often follow), a landlord unreasonably withholds consent if:

- The alteration does not diminish the property's value
- The alteration does not affect the building's structure
- The alteration is within the permitted use
- The alteration improves the premises
- The landlord refuses without giving reasons
- The landlord refuses because of collateral demands (e.g., "I'll approve only if you increase rent")

If the hotel refuses consent for the hair salon conversion without a legitimate building-related reason, Mert has grounds to proceed regardless or to seek a court declaration that consent was unreasonably withheld.

## 2.3 How to Frame the Hair Salon Conversion

### FRAME 1: It Is an Upgrade, Not a Change

**Script:** "We are not removing anything from the spa. We are upgrading an underutilised area. The hair salon generates EUR 3,000-5,000 per month. After conversion, that same space will generate EUR 36,000-53,000 per month. That is a premium medical aesthetics clinic that dramatically increases the hotel's appeal and your turnover fee income. This is not a change -- it is an improvement."

### FRAME 2: It Is Consistent with Permitted Use

**Script:** "The lease permits use as 'a Spa or any related use.' Medical aesthetics is the fastest-growing segment of the spa and wellness industry. Every major hotel spa in Europe now includes some form of medical aesthetics -- injectables, laser treatments, skin rejuvenation. Converting the hair salon into a treatment clinic is not changing the use -- it is evolving it in line with industry trends. It is squarely within 'Spa or any related use.'"

### FRAME 3: It Enhances the Hotel's Competitive Position

**Script:** "The Fortina competes with the Hilton, Corinthia, Excelsior, and AX Hotels for wellness-focused guests. All of these hotels have or are developing medical aesthetics offerings. A hair salon does not differentiate the Fortina. A medical aesthetics and longevity clinic does. This conversion puts the Fortina at the leading edge of Malta's wellness hospitality market."

### FRAME 4: The Work Is Minimal and Non-Disruptive

**Script:** "The actual construction work is straightforward: remove salon fixtures, install lightweight partition walls, upgrade plumbing and electrics. Estimated construction period: 4-6 weeks. No structural work, no external changes, no disruption to hotel operations. We will coordinate the schedule to minimise any impact on hotel guests."

### FRAME 5: The Hotel Benefits Through the Turnover Fee

**Script:** "Converting the hair salon into a clinic adds EUR 382,000-576,000 in annual revenue to the premises. At 5%, that is EUR 19,000-29,000 in additional annual turnover fees for you. Over the remaining lease, that is EUR 171,000-261,000 in additional income. This conversion pays for itself -- for you -- within the first year."

## 2.5 If the Hotel Pushes Back: Escalation Strategies

### ESCALATION 1: Request Written Reasons

If the hotel says no, request written reasons. Under the principle that consent shall not be unreasonably withheld, the landlord must provide legitimate reasons.

**Script:** "We understand you have concerns. Could you put your reasons in writing so our lawyers can review the position? We want to work with you, and understanding your specific concerns will help us address them."

**Why this works:** Landlords who are withholding unreasonably often cannot articulate a legitimate reason. Asking for written reasons forces them to either find a legitimate basis or realise they do not have one.

### ESCALATION 2: Offer to Reinstate at Lease End

"We will include a reinstatement covenant: at the end of the lease, if you wish, we will restore the premises to their current layout at our cost."

**Why this works:** Removes the landlord's concern about permanent changes. Cost to Mert: minimal. By lease end (9 years away), the value of restoring a hair salon is negligible, and the landlord will almost certainly prefer the clinic layout.

### ESCALATION 3: Independent Survey

"We will commission an independent building surveyor to confirm that all proposed works are non-structural internal alterations. If the surveyor confirms this, we believe consent should follow."

**Why this works:** Removes subjectivity. An independent expert confirms the works are within the contractual threshold.

### ESCALATION 4: Legal Position (Nuclear Option -- Use Sparingly)

"Our lawyers have reviewed the lease. The proposed works are non-structural internal alterations. Under Clause 5.9, consent for such works cannot be unreasonably withheld. We respectfully request your consent. If consent is withheld without reasonable grounds, we reserve the right to seek a court declaration."

**When to use:** Only if all other escalation steps have failed. This is adversarial and will damage the relationship. However, Mert's legal position is strong.

## 3.2 Top 10 Things NOT TO SAY

### 1. DO NOT Reveal Mert's Deal-Breakers

NEVER say: "Subleasing is a deal-breaker" or "If we cannot change the hair salon, the deal is off." This hands the hotel leverage to extract concessions.

INSTEAD say: "Subleasing and the clinic conversion are important parts of Carisma's operational plan. We would love to discuss how to make these work for everyone."

### 2. DO NOT Mention the EUR 250,000 Purchase Price

The hotel does not need to know what Mert is paying for the business. If they learn the price, they may: (a) demand a share, (b) perceive the buyer as having deep pockets, or (c) anchor their expectations for any concession payments.

If asked directly: "The purchase price is a private matter between buyer and seller. What matters for your relationship is the quality of the operator and the revenue plan."

### 3. DO NOT Criticize the Current Operation

NEVER say: "The spa has been underperforming" or "John has not maximised the space." This insults the seller (who is your ally in this meeting) and suggests to the hotel that the premises may be troubled.

INSTEAD say: "The spa has a solid foundation. Carisma sees significant opportunity to build on what has been established."

### 4. DO NOT Use Legal Language or Threaten Legal Action

NEVER say: "The lease requires you to consent" or "You cannot unreasonably withhold consent." This creates an adversarial dynamic in a meeting that should be collaborative.

INSTEAD say: "We believe the works fall within the lease terms, and we would welcome the opportunity to work with you on the details."

### 5. DO NOT Offer Concessions Proactively

NEVER say: "We are willing to increase the rent if you allow subleasing." This signals desperation and trains the hotel to ask for concessions on every request.

INSTEAD: Let the hotel state their concerns first. Then address each concern specifically. Only offer concessions in response to specific objections.

### 6. DO NOT Discuss Detailed Renovation Plans

NEVER show detailed floor plans, equipment lists, or construction specifications. This gives the hotel ammunition to raise objections to specific details.

INSTEAD say: "We plan to upgrade the hair salon into a medical clinic. The works are straightforward internal alterations. We will submit detailed plans for your review at the appropriate time."

### 7. DO NOT Agree to Any Terms on the Spot

NEVER say: "That sounds reasonable, we agree." Any terms discussed today should be documented and reviewed by Mert and his lawyers before commitment.

INSTEAD say: "That is helpful. Let us take that back to Carisma and come back to you with a formal proposal."

### 8. DO NOT Mention Lease Weaknesses or Termination Rights

NEVER reference Clause 9.3 (hotel's termination right) or discuss the lease's remaining term as if it is a concern. This reminds the hotel of their power.

### 9. DO NOT Discuss Staff Changes or Redundancies

The hotel may have relationships with current spa staff. Any mention of restructuring could alarm them and create unnecessary resistance.

### 10. DO NOT Rush or Pressure for Immediate Answers

NEVER say: "We need an answer today" or "The deal depends on your decision this week." Patience signals confidence and strength.

INSTEAD say: "We understand this requires careful consideration. We are happy to provide any additional information you need and meet again whenever suits you."

# PART 4: CONTINGENCY PLANS

## 4.1 What If the Hotel Says No to Subleasing?

### OPTION A: The Management Agreement Workaround

Structure all specialist operations as management agreements / service contracts rather than subleases. The specialist practitioner is engaged as a contractor, not a sub-tenant. They operate under Carisma's brand, use Carisma's booking system, and all revenue flows through Carisma's accounts.

**Legal risk:** The landlord could argue this is a disguised sublease. However, if the specialist does not have exclusive possession of any part of the premises and operates under Carisma's direction and control, the arrangement is legally distinct from a sublease.

**Practical implementation:** Draft service agreements with practitioners that explicitly state: (a) Carisma retains possession and control of the premises at all times, (b) the practitioner provides services as an independent contractor, (c) the practitioner has no right of exclusive occupation, (d) Carisma can terminate the arrangement at any time.

### OPTION B: Employee Model

Hire specialist practitioners as Carisma employees rather than sub-tenants. A nurse injector, for example, can be employed directly by Carisma on a part-time or full-time basis. No sublease is required because there is no separate operator -- it is all Carisma.

**Limitation:** This increases Carisma's employment obligations and reduces flexibility. However, it completely avoids the sublease restriction.

### OPTION C: Escalate to Formal Negotiation

If the hotel's refusal appears unreasonable, engage a commercial lease specialist solicitor to open formal negotiations. Present a detailed proposal with all the concessions outlined in Section 1.3, and request a formal written response.

### OPTION D: Lease Amendment

Negotiate a formal amendment to Clause 5.27 that adds a reasonableness standard: "Consent shall not be unreasonably withheld where the sub-tenant's use is consistent with the Permitted Use and where the Lessee provides the Lessor with reasonable details of the proposed sub-tenant."

### OPTION E: Walk Away

If the hotel is implacably opposed to any form of specialist operation and the management agreement workaround is not viable, the deal may not deliver sufficient value. Mert's entire upside case (the EUR 273K-456K EBITDA projections) depends on the medical aesthetics clinic, which may require specialist practitioners. Without operational flexibility, the business remains a EUR 39K EBITDA spa -- which does not justify EUR 250K.

**Walk-away threshold:** If NONE of the workarounds (management agreement, employee model, or negotiated amendment) are achievable, reassess whether the deal works at EUR 250K as a spa-only operation.

## 4.2 What If the Hotel Says No to the Hair Salon Change?

### OPTION A: Argue Unreasonable Withholding

The hair salon conversion is a non-structural internal alteration. Clause 5.9 says consent "shall not be unreasonably withheld." If the hotel refuses without legitimate building-related reasons, Mert has legal grounds to:

1. Request written reasons for refusal
2. Commission an independent survey confirming works are non-structural
3. Engage a solicitor to formally assert the right under Clause 5.9
4. As a last resort, seek a court declaration

**Timeline for legal route:** 3-6 months in Maltese courts. This is a delay, not a deal-killer.

### OPTION B: Phased Approach

"We will start with the elements that require no consent (removing trade fixtures, changing the salon's service offering) and seek consent for the partition walls and plumbing changes separately."

Removing hair salon furniture and changing the service offering (from hair services to spa treatments) does not require physical alterations to the building. Mert can repurpose the space for different treatments using portable equipment while formal consent for construction is obtained.

### OPTION C: Offer the Hotel a Larger Turnover Fee on Clinic Revenue

"We will agree to a 7% turnover fee on all revenue generated from the converted space, rather than the standard 5%. In exchange, we request consent for the conversion."

**Cost to Mert:** At EUR 438K clinic revenue, additional 2% = EUR 8,760/year. Over 9 years = EUR 79K. This is significant but may be worth it to unlock EUR 273K+ annual EBITDA.

### OPTION D: Propose a Trial Period

"Allow us to convert the space on a 12-month trial basis. If at the end of 12 months you are not satisfied with the result, we will reinstate the hair salon at our cost."

**Why this works:** Low-risk for the hotel. They can see the results before committing permanently.

**Relevance:** Directly parallel situation. Medical wellness operator within a hospitality property, needing conversion permission and specialist operators.

## 5.2 Proven Negotiation Frameworks

### FRAMEWORK 1: BATNA ANALYSIS (Fisher & Ury, "Getting to Yes")

**Your BATNA (Best Alternative to No Agreement):**
If the hotel refuses both sublease rights and hair salon conversion, Mert's BATNA is:
- Walk away from the deal (EUR 0 cost, EUR 0 revenue)
- Find another location (6-12 months, EUR 300K-500K buildout cost)
- Operate INA as a spa-only business (EUR 39K EBITDA -- does not justify EUR 250K price)

**The Hotel's BATNA:**
If the hotel refuses and the deal falls through:
- The current tenant (John Muscat) continues operating at EUR 430K revenue (turnover fee: EUR 4K/year)
- Or John Muscat exits and the hotel must find a new tenant (time, cost, vacancy risk)
- The hotel forfeits EUR 26K-44K per year in additional turnover fees that Carisma would generate

**ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement):**
The ZOPA exists because both parties benefit from Carisma's entry. The hotel's gain (EUR 26K-44K/year in additional turnover fees) exceeds any reasonable concession Mert would need to make (EUR 2K-5K/year in rent increase or other concessions).

### FRAMEWORK 2: INTEREST-BASED NEGOTIATION (Fisher & Ury)

**Positions vs. Interests:**

| Party | Position | Underlying Interest |
|-------|----------|-------------------|
| Hotel | "We do not allow subletting" | Control over who operates in their building; maintain quality standards; avoid liability |
| Mert | "I need sublease rights" | Operational flexibility to bring in specialist practitioners; maximise revenue; execute clinic plan |
| Hotel | "No alterations" | Protect the building; maintain architectural integrity; avoid devaluation |
| Mert | "I need to convert the hair salon" | Repurpose underutilised space; build medical aesthetics clinic; generate EUR 438K+ revenue |

**Interest-Based Solutions:**
- **Hotel's interest in control:** Addressed by giving them approval rights over sub-tenants
- **Hotel's interest in quality:** Addressed by quality standards covenant and brand standards
- **Hotel's interest in revenue:** Addressed by the 5% turnover fee on higher revenue
- **Mert's interest in flexibility:** Addressed by management agreement structure (avoids triggering sublease clause)
- **Mert's interest in clinic:** Addressed by framing conversion as non-structural internal alteration (consent cannot be unreasonably withheld)

### FRAMEWORK 3: THE NEGOTIATION DIAMOND (Shell International Model)

Four dimensions of negotiation value:

1. **Economics:** The hotel earns EUR 26K-44K more per year with Carisma. This is the strongest lever.
2. **Relationship:** Present Carisma as a long-term partner, not a transactional tenant. Build personal rapport with the hotel's decision-maker.
3. **Contract:** Use the existing lease's language strategically (Clause 5.9 reasonableness standard for alterations; permitted use as "Spa or any related use").
4. **BATNA/Power:** Subtly signal that Carisma has alternatives (other locations in Malta). The hotel should feel that they need this deal to happen.

### FRAMEWORK 4: ANCHORING AND ADJUSTMENT (Tversky & Kahneman)

**First Number Wins.** The first concrete proposal shapes the entire negotiation.

**Application:** Before the hotel names any conditions for consent, the M&A advisor should anchor the conversation:
- "Carisma is proposing to invest EUR 150,000 in your premises and grow revenue to over EUR 1 million. The sublease flexibility and the hair salon conversion are part of that investment plan. We see these as routine operational matters, not major lease changes."

This frames the requests as minor (routine operational matters) rather than major (lease modifications). If the hotel anchors first with "we want a 50% rent increase," the negotiation starts from a worse position.

### FRAMEWORK 5: LOGROLLING (Raiffa, "The Art and Science of Negotiation")

Trade items of different value to each party. Identify what the hotel values most and what Mert values most, then exchange.

**What the hotel likely values most:**
1. Tenant stability (long commitment)
2. Revenue growth (higher turnover fees)
3. Control (approval over sub-tenants)
4. Building quality (no damage to property)

**What Mert values most:**
1. Sublease rights (operational flexibility)
2. Hair salon conversion (clinic buildout)
3. Lease term extension (reduces expiry risk)
4. Reasonable rent (cost management)

**Logrolling trades:**
- Mert gives: extended commitment (waive di rispetto termination right for 5 years) + landlord approval over sub-tenants + quality standards covenant + revenue transparency
- Hotel gives: sublease rights (with approval) + consent for hair salon conversion + written confirmation of "Spa or any related use" including medical aesthetics

Both parties get what they value most. Neither party gives up anything that materially hurts them.

### FRAMEWORK 6: CHRIS VOSS TACTICAL EMPATHY (Applied to Landlord)

**Technique 1: Mirror**
Hotel says: "We are concerned about losing control of who operates in our building."
Response: "Losing control?" [pause, let them elaborate]

**Technique 2: Label**
"It seems like maintaining the Fortina's reputation and guest experience is really important to you."

**Technique 3: Accusation Audit**
"You might be thinking that we are trying to take advantage of the lease terms or bring in operators who do not meet your standards. I understand that concern completely, which is why we are offering you full approval rights."

**Technique 4: Calibrated Questions**
- "How would you like us to handle the approval process for any specialist practitioners?"
- "What would you need to see from Carisma to feel comfortable with the clinic conversion?"
- "What concerns would you want addressed before giving consent?"

These questions make the hotel feel in control while steering the conversation toward "how do we make this work" rather than "should we do this."

### FRAMEWORK 7: COMMITMENT AND CONSISTENCY (Cialdini)

Get the hotel to agree to small, progressive commitments that build toward the larger ask.

**Sequence:**
1. "Do you agree that a more professional operator benefits the hotel?" (Easy yes)
2. "Do you agree that higher spa revenue means higher turnover fees for you?" (Easy yes)
3. "Do you agree that medical aesthetics is a growing part of the spa industry?" (Easy yes)
4. "Do you agree that specialist practitioners deliver better results than generalists?" (Likely yes)
5. "Given all of that, would you be open to discussing how Carisma can bring in specialist practitioners under your approval?" (Natural progression)

Each "yes" makes the final "yes" more psychologically consistent.
