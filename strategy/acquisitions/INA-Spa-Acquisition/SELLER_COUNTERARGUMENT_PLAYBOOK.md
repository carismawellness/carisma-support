# SELLER COUNTERARGUMENT PLAYBOOK
## INA Spa & Wellness Acquisition -- Buyer's Negotiation Field Guide

**Prepared for:** Mert Gulen, Carisma Wellness Group
**Date:** March 6, 2026
**Classification:** STRICTLY CONFIDENTIAL -- Buyer Eyes Only
**Purpose:** Read this in the car on the way to the meeting. Every seller argument you will hear is in here, with the exact words to say back.

---

## HOW TO USE THIS DOCUMENT

Each section follows the same structure:

1. **What they will say** -- their exact argument, word for word
2. **Why it sounds compelling** -- acknowledge the logic so you do not dismiss it too fast
3. **Why it is wrong** -- the data-driven rebuttal
4. **Your script** -- exact words to say, conversational but firm
5. **Supporting data point** -- the killer fact that ends the exchange

Read the scripts out loud once before the meeting. They need to sound like you, not like a document.

---

## OPENING STATEMENT

Before they make any arguments, you set the frame. Whoever frames the conversation first controls what "fair" means for the rest of the meeting.

### Your Opening Script

> "Thank you for making time for this. I want to say upfront -- I respect what you have built here. Building a spa from zero inside a hotel, getting it to EUR 426K in revenue in two years, training a team of 13 people -- that is real work and it shows. I am not here to diminish that.
>
> What I want to do today is have an honest conversation about where we both see value and find a structure that works. I have been through the data room carefully. My family's investment group has reviewed everything. There are things that genuinely impressed us -- the local client base, the street-level access, the fact that laser and T-Shape are already running. And there are things that concern us -- the cost structure, the lease terms, and where EBITDA sits today.
>
> I am a serious buyer. I am not here to waste your time. I want to find a deal that lets you walk away feeling good about what you built, and lets me build the next chapter. So let us talk openly."

### Why This Opening Works

- It leads with genuine respect (disarms defensiveness)
- It signals you have done real diligence (you know the numbers)
- It names specific positives (local clients, laser equipment -- shows you read the data)
- It names concerns without being aggressive (cost structure, lease, EBITDA)
- It positions you as collaborative, not adversarial
- It introduces "my family's investment group" early (sets up the Higher Authority frame)

## BODY LANGUAGE AND TONE NOTES

Read this section once before walking in. Then forget the words and just remember the feeling.

**Posture:** Lean back slightly. Do not lean forward eagerly. You are evaluating, not pursuing.

**Eye contact:** Steady when they speak. Look at your notes briefly when delivering a difficult number. This signals you are reading data, not making it up.

**Voice:** Slightly slower than your natural pace. Lower your volume by 10% when delivering the hardest points. The quieter you get, the more seriously they listen.

**Silence:** After delivering any rebuttal, stop talking. Count to four in your head. Let them process. The first person to speak after a hard number usually concedes something.

**Never do these:**
- Do not cross your arms (defensive)
- Do not smile when delivering a low number (condescending)
- Do not check your phone (disrespectful)
- Do not say "to be honest" or "frankly" -- it implies you have been dishonest until now
- Do not say "with all due respect" -- it always precedes disrespect

**Always do these:**
- Nod when they make a valid point (acknowledgment is not agreement)
- Take notes visibly (signals you value what they say)
- Use their name occasionally ("I hear you, Petra")
- Mirror their energy -- if they get heated, you get calmer

## COUNTERARGUMENT 1: "Revenue is EUR 426K and growing 33% -- this is a growth business"

### What they will say

> "We did EUR 426,000 in 2025, up 33% from EUR 321,000 in 2024. This is a business with strong momentum. You are buying into a growth trajectory, not a static asset. At this rate, we will be at EUR 550-600K within two years."

### Why it sounds compelling

Revenue growth of 33% year-over-year is genuinely impressive in any context. It suggests market demand is proven, the location works, and the business has not yet hit its ceiling. Growth businesses command premium valuations in every industry.

### Why it is wrong

The 33% growth is a ramp-up illusion, not a sustainable trend. The spa opened in January 2024. Year 1 revenue (EUR 321K) was generated by a brand-new business that started from zero -- Q1 2024 would have been near-zero as the spa was literally just opening its doors. Comparing Year 2 to that artificially low base flatters the growth rate enormously.

The monthly data tells the real story. H2 2025 growth has already flattened:
- July 2025: -17% YoY decline (worst month at EUR 22K)
- September 2025: -3% YoY
- December 2025: -3% YoY
- H2 2025 annualized run-rate: EUR 382K (below the full-year EUR 426K)

The business is approaching its natural ceiling under current operations. The "33% growth" will not repeat.

### Your script

> "The revenue growth is real and I give you credit for it. But I have to be careful about what that 33% actually represents. Your spa opened in January 2024. The first few months of any new business are ramp-up -- you were close to zero in early 2024. So the Year 1 base was artificially low.
>
> When I look at the monthly data more carefully, the picture changes. Your H2 2025 shows flat-to-negative growth in several months -- July was actually down 17% year-over-year. If I annualize the second half of 2025, the run-rate is closer to EUR 382K, not EUR 426K. The growth is decelerating, which is completely normal for a two-year-old business that has filled its initial capacity.
>
> I am not saying the revenue is bad -- EUR 426K is genuinely strong for a 7-room spa. But I cannot price the business as if 33% growth will continue, because the data says it will not."

### Supporting data point

**H2 2025 annualized run-rate is EUR 382K, not EUR 426K. July 2025 revenue was EUR 22,047 -- a 17% year-over-year decline. The growth story peaked in March 2025 (+148% YoY) and has been decelerating every month since.**

## COUNTERARGUMENT 2: "We invested EUR 858K -- you are offering less than 25%"

### What they will say

> "We put EUR 858,000 into this business. The fit-out alone was EUR 738,000. We bought the Candela laser for EUR 65,000. The T-Shape machine was EUR 55,000. You are offering us less than a quarter of what we invested. That is not a fair deal."

### Why it sounds compelling

EUR 858K is a real number backed by real invoices. The emotional weight is heavy -- this person made thousands of individual decisions, each feeling justified, that added up to nearly a million euros. Offering 25 cents on the dollar feels like robbery.

### Why it is wrong

Investment cost is not business value. These are fundamentally different concepts that the seller is conflating. The market does not price businesses based on what was spent building them -- it prices them based on what they earn. If someone spent EUR 2 million building a restaurant that earns zero profit, the restaurant is not worth EUR 2 million.

More critically, EUR 738K of the EUR 858K investment is in leasehold improvements -- tiled walls, plumbing, HVAC, hammam infrastructure. These are embedded in the landlord's building. If the lease terminates, these assets have near-zero salvage value. You cannot remove a hammam. The portable assets -- laser, T-Shape, treatment beds, reception equipment -- are worth EUR 80-120K at auction.

### Your script

> "I understand the weight of that number. EUR 858K is a significant commitment, and I want you to know I am not dismissing the effort and capital you put into this place. What you built here is genuinely impressive.
>
> But I have to separate what was spent from what the business produces. These are two different questions. The market does not price businesses on historical investment -- it prices them on current earnings. If it were the other way around, every business would be worth exactly what was spent building it, and there would be no risk in entrepreneurship.
>
> The harder reality is that EUR 738K of your investment is in the fit-out -- the walls, the plumbing, the hammam, the sauna. Those are embedded in the landlord's building. If the lease ends, those assets stay with the building. The portable equipment -- the laser, the T-Shape, the beds -- those have real value, but at current market rates we are talking EUR 80-120K, not EUR 300K.
>
> I know this is a difficult number to hear. But what I am buying is the income stream, the client relationships, and the right to operate in this location -- not the construction cost of the build-out."

### Supporting data point

**EUR 738K of the EUR 858K investment is in non-portable leasehold improvements embedded in the landlord's property. Portable asset auction value: EUR 80-120K. The business itself generates EUR 36,538 EBITDA and loses EUR 18,978 after depreciation. Sunk cost is not recoverable through sale price -- it is recoverable through operating profits, which this business has not yet generated.**

## COUNTERARGUMENT 3: "The equipment alone is worth EUR 300K+"

### What they will say

> "Just look at the equipment. The Candela laser cost EUR 65,000. The T-Shape was EUR 55,000. The full fit-out was EUR 738,000. Even at depreciated values, the equipment and infrastructure are worth well over EUR 300,000. You cannot even buy a Candela laser and set up a single treatment room for what you are offering."

### Why it sounds compelling

Equipment replacement cost is real. A brand-new Candela laser does cost EUR 60-70K. Fitting out 7 treatment rooms, a hammam, sauna, and ice plunge from scratch would cost hundreds of thousands. The seller is correct that replicating this from zero is expensive.

### Why it is wrong

There are three separate errors in this argument:

1. **Purchase cost is not current value.** The fit-out is 2+ years old. Standard depreciation on spa equipment is 8-10 years. T-Shape machines depreciate rapidly in the secondary market -- a EUR 55K machine has a fair market value of EUR 12-18K. The Candela holds value better at EUR 40-50K.

2. **Leasehold improvements are not portable.** The hammam, sauna, ice plunge, tiled treatment rooms, plumbing, and HVAC are built into the landlord's building. You cannot put them on a truck. If the lease ends, they belong to the building. Their value to the buyer exists only for the remaining lease term.

3. **Equipment value is already embedded in the offer price.** When we value the business, we are valuing everything -- the equipment, the lease, the client base, the revenue stream. The equipment is not on top of the business value -- it is part of it.

### Your script

> "The equipment is genuinely good -- I was impressed when I toured the facility. The Candela is a quality machine and the wet-area infrastructure is well done.
>
> But I need to separate replacement cost from fair market value. The T-Shape machine, for example, cost EUR 55K new. In the secondary market today, that is a EUR 12-18K asset -- these devices depreciate quickly. The Candela holds value better at around EUR 40-50K. So the key equipment is worth EUR 52-68K at market rates, not EUR 120K at purchase price.
>
> The bigger issue is the fit-out. EUR 738K was spent, and I do not doubt that. But that is embedded in the hotel's building. The hammam, the sauna, the plumbing -- none of that comes with me if the lease ends. It is a leasehold improvement, and its value only exists for the remaining lease term.
>
> When I put a price on the business, the equipment value is already in there. I am not offering EUR 200K and saying the equipment is worth zero on top. The equipment, the lease, the clients, the revenue -- it is all in the number."

### Supporting data point

**Portable asset FMV: Candela laser EUR 40-50K, T-Shape EUR 12-18K, treatment beds and reception equipment EUR 28-52K. Total portable assets: EUR 80-120K. The remaining EUR 738K in leasehold improvements has zero salvage value outside the lease -- it stays with the building.**

## COUNTERARGUMENT 4: "The location inside Fortina is premium -- you cannot get this elsewhere"

### What they will say

> "This is a spa inside a hotel in Sliema with 13 years of lease runway. You cannot find this anywhere else in Malta. Hotel spa partnerships are exclusive, relationship-driven, and nearly impossible to negotiate from scratch. The location alone is worth a significant premium."

### Why it sounds compelling

This is the seller's strongest argument and it contains genuine truth. Hotel spa concessions in Malta are rare. The Fortina location in Sliema is premium. Negotiating a new 13-year lease from scratch would be difficult, possibly impossible. The street-level access that drives 78% local clientele is genuinely unusual.

### Why it is wrong

The lease has significant structural problems that reduce its value:

1. **Assignment at landlord's absolute discretion.** The landlord can refuse the transfer for any reason or no reason. If they say no, the entire deal is worthless. If they say yes with conditions, they could demand higher rent, personal guarantees, or other terms that change the economics.

2. **Complex sale termination right.** If the Fortina Complex is sold to a third party, the landlord can terminate the lease entirely. Carisma would receive EUR 150K plus depreciated fit-out value -- but would lose the business, the clients, and all post-acquisition investment.

3. **Remaining term is approximately 10 years, not 13.** The lease commenced mid-2020. We are in Year 6. The actual remaining runway is 9-10 years.

4. **Rent escalates every year.** Fixed rent grows 3% annually (reaching EUR 85.5K by Year 15), plus 5% of all revenue above EUR 350K. The landlord effectively has a 5% equity stake in the top line. By Year 10, total rent exceeds EUR 100K per year.

5. **Cumulative rent over the remaining term: approximately EUR 1 million.** This is not a cheap location over time.

### Your script

> "I agree with you -- the location is the most valuable part of this business. Street-level access with 78% local clientele inside a hotel in Sliema is genuinely hard to find. I would not be at the table if I did not value that.
>
> But the lease comes with significant structural risk that I have to price in. Three things concern me.
>
> First, assignment is at the landlord's absolute discretion. They can say no for any reason. Until I have written consent with terms preserved, this deal has an existential risk attached to it.
>
> Second, if the Fortina Complex is ever sold, the landlord can terminate the lease entirely. I would get EUR 150K plus depreciated fit-out value, but I lose the business. That is a real risk I am absorbing.
>
> Third, the lease is not 13 years anymore. It commenced in 2020. We are in Year 6. The actual remaining term is about 10 years. And the rent escalates every year -- 3% on the fixed component plus 5% of revenue. By Year 10, total rent exceeds EUR 100K annually. Over the remaining term, I will pay approximately one million euros in rent.
>
> The location is premium. But the lease terms reduce the premium significantly. I am pricing the location at what the lease actually delivers, not what it looks like on the surface."

### Supporting data point

**Lease assignment requires landlord consent at absolute discretion. Complex sale triggers termination right. Remaining term is approximately 10 years, not 13. Cumulative rent over remaining term: approximately EUR 1,032,000. The landlord captures 5% of every euro of revenue growth through the turnover fee.**

## COUNTERARGUMENT 5: "We have built a client base of 7,474 appointments"

### What they will say

> "We had 7,474 appointments in 2025. That is a real, proven client base. These people know us, trust us, and come back. You are buying that relationship, and it has taken us two years to build."

### Why it sounds compelling

Client acquisition is expensive and time-consuming. 7,474 appointments represent real demand validation. The 78% local client base means these are repeat visitors, not one-time tourists. There is genuine goodwill embedded in those relationships.

### Why it is wrong

Three problems with this argument:

1. **Appointments are not clients.** 7,474 appointments does not mean 7,474 unique clients. If the average client visits 4-6 times per year (typical for a spa), the actual unique client base is 1,200-1,800 people. Still valuable, but a different number.

2. **Revenue per appointment is low.** EUR 426K divided by 7,474 appointments equals EUR 57 per appointment. Industry benchmark for premium Mediterranean spas is EUR 75-120. This suggests heavy discounting, a high proportion of low-value services (manicures, pedicures), or both. The client base exists but is undermonetized.

3. **Client loyalty follows therapists, not brands.** In the spa industry, clients develop relationships with their therapist, not the brand. If key therapists leave post-acquisition, their clients leave with them. The "client base" is partially embedded in the staff, which makes it fragile.

### Your script

> "The appointment volume is impressive and I take it seriously. 78% local clientele is exactly the kind of foundation I want to build on.
>
> Two things I want to flag, though. First, EUR 57 per appointment is below industry benchmarks -- premium spas in the Mediterranean typically see EUR 75-120. That tells me there is either heavy discounting or a high proportion of lower-value services. It is actually a positive signal for me -- it means there is pricing optimization potential -- but it also means the current revenue per visit is not where it should be.
>
> Second, and this is something I know from running spas -- clients follow therapists. If I lose two or three key therapists post-acquisition, their clients go with them. The client base is real, but it is partially embedded in people, not in a database. That makes it more fragile than a subscription business or a SaaS product.
>
> I value the client relationships. They are already in my price. But I cannot treat 7,474 appointments as though each one is a guaranteed recurring revenue unit."

### Supporting data point

**Revenue per appointment: EUR 57. Industry benchmark: EUR 75-120 (32-110% uplift potential). Estimated unique clients: 1,200-1,800 (not 7,474). Client retention risk: therapist departure causes client attrition -- goodwill is partially embedded in employees, not the brand.**

## COUNTERARGUMENT 6: "The clinic opportunity (laser, T-Shape) adds huge upside"

### What they will say

> "We already have a Candela laser generating 16.4% of treatments and a T-Shape machine at 7.2%. That is 23.6% of our business already in medical aesthetics. With the right operator, this becomes a full clinic doing EUR 200-300K in additional revenue. That upside should be reflected in the price."

### Why it sounds compelling

The existing equipment is real and already generating revenue. The clinic conversion opportunity is genuinely valuable -- Carisma's own projections show Year 3 EBITDA of EUR 200K+ with a full clinic operation. The seller is correct that the infrastructure reduces Carisma's clinic capex by EUR 70-100K versus a greenfield build.

### Why it is wrong

This is the most important conceptual point in the entire negotiation: **the buyer keeps the turnaround value they create.**

The seller did not build a clinic. The seller has a laser machine that therapists use for basic treatments. Converting this into a medical aesthetics operation requires:
- A licensed nurse practitioner (EUR 66K/year salary)
- Medical protocols and compliance systems
- Clinic-specific marketing and patient acquisition
- Treatment room conversion and additional devices (EUR 30K)
- Regulatory approvals for expanded procedures

All of that is Carisma's execution, Carisma's investment, and Carisma's risk. The seller is asking to be paid for value that the buyer will create. That is not how acquisitions work.

### Your script

> "The fact that the laser and T-Shape are already on-site is genuinely valuable to me, and it is part of why I am interested in this deal rather than building from scratch. I give you full credit for that.
>
> But there is a distinction I need to make. You have equipment that therapists use for basic laser treatments. Converting that into a medical aesthetics clinic -- with a nurse practitioner, medical protocols, regulatory compliance, a different patient acquisition model -- that is a completely different business. That conversion requires my capital, my team, my expertise, and my risk.
>
> If the clinic succeeds and generates EUR 200K in additional revenue, that is value I created. If it fails, I absorb the loss. The standard principle in acquisitions is that the buyer keeps the value they create through their own execution. I am not paying you for results I have not achieved yet.
>
> What I am paying for is the platform -- the equipment being there, the fact that I do not need to buy a laser from zero. That is real and it is in the price. But the clinic upside is mine to earn, not yours to sell."

### Supporting data point

**Clinic conversion requires: nurse practitioner (EUR 66K/year), EUR 30K additional devices, EUR 20K marketing launch, EUR 40K working capital = EUR 80-100K additional investment by Carisma. The seller built a laser treatment room. The buyer builds a clinic. The turnaround premium belongs to the buyer, not the seller.**

## COUNTERARGUMENT 7: "We can just keep running it ourselves if you will not pay fair value"

### What they will say

> "If we cannot get a fair price, we will just keep operating. The business covers its costs. We are not under pressure to sell. We will wait for the right buyer."

### Why it sounds compelling

A seller who can walk away has leverage. If they are genuinely comfortable holding the business indefinitely, the buyer cannot use time pressure. The business does cover its operating costs (EBITDA is positive at EUR 36.5K), so it is not bleeding cash in the way a loss-making business would.

### Why it is wrong

This is a bluff, and the data proves it.

1. **The business loses money after depreciation.** PBT is negative EUR 18,978. The EUR 36.5K EBITDA is consumed by depreciation on the EUR 738K fit-out. In economic terms, the owner is not covering the wear-and-tear on their own infrastructure.

2. **Rent escalates every year.** Fixed rent grows 3% annually. The turnover fee adds 5% on top. Without revenue growth to match, margins compress further each year. By Year 10, total rent exceeds EUR 100K.

3. **The owner invested EUR 858K and earns zero return.** Every month they hold this business, they are earning 0% on nearly a million euros of capital. That capital could be in a bank account earning 3-4% (EUR 25-34K/year) with zero effort. The opportunity cost of holding is real.

4. **The "only 2 buyers" comment is already known.** There are not five buyers waiting in the wings. There are two. If one walks, the seller negotiates with one buyer -- which is not a negotiation, it is a take-it-or-leave-it.

5. **The business needs investment they are not making.** Repairs and maintenance spending is EUR 2,117 -- 0.5% of revenue. That is deferred maintenance. The facility is deteriorating, and the longer they hold, the more capital it needs.

### Your script

> "I understand that holding is always an option, and I respect your right to do that.
>
> But I want to make sure we are both looking at what holding actually means financially. The business generates EUR 36.5K in EBITDA, but after depreciation, the pre-tax result is negative EUR 19K. You are not building equity by holding -- you are watching the fit-out depreciate while rent escalates 3% every year.
>
> Your EUR 858K is currently earning a zero percent return. In a savings account, that capital generates EUR 25-30K per year with no risk. Every month you hold, the opportunity cost is real.
>
> I also notice repairs and maintenance is at EUR 2,100 for the year -- half a percent of revenue. That tells me maintenance is being deferred. The longer the business sits, the more capital it will need just to maintain the current standard.
>
> I am not saying this to pressure you. I am saying it because holding is not free, and the numbers suggest it gets harder, not easier, from here."

### Supporting data point

**PBT: negative EUR 18,978. Annual opportunity cost of EUR 858K at 3.5% risk-free: EUR 30,000. Rent escalates 3% annually. Repairs and maintenance spend: EUR 2,117 (0.5% of revenue -- indicating deferred maintenance). The longer they hold, the worse the economics become.**

## COUNTERARGUMENT 8: "Other buyers would pay more"

### What they will say

> "We have other interested parties. If you will not pay a fair price, we will talk to them. There is competition for this asset."

### Why it sounds compelling

Competition drives prices up. If a second buyer is willing to pay EUR 350K, then EUR 200K is not the market price. The threat of losing the deal to a competitor creates urgency.

### Why it is wrong

1. **The seller already revealed there are only 2 buyers.** This was disclosed in the initial meeting. Two buyers in a market of 550,000 people is not competitive tension -- it is limited demand.

2. **If a better offer existed, the seller would have taken it.** The fact that they are sitting in this meeting, negotiating with you, means the other buyer either has not made an offer or has made one the seller does not prefer.

3. **The other buyer faces the same economics.** Any rational buyer will look at the same data room, see the same EUR 36.5K EBITDA, the same escalating rent, the same lease assignment risk, and arrive at a similar valuation range. The data does not change because a different person reads it.

4. **Strategic buyers in Malta are not interchangeable.** Carisma brings existing spa operations, a nurse practitioner network, marketing infrastructure, and multi-brand integration capability. The other buyer may not have these -- which means their ability to turn the business around is lower, which means they should rationally pay less, not more.

### Your script

> "I welcome competition -- it keeps everyone honest. If there is a buyer who values this business more highly than I do, you should absolutely talk to them.
>
> What I would ask is this: if you do receive a competing offer, give me the opportunity to see it and respond before you make a decision. That is a fair process for everyone.
>
> What I would not do is change my price based on a number I have not seen on paper. I have done my analysis. I know what this business earns, what the lease terms are, and what it will cost me to bring it to profitability. My offer reflects that analysis. Another buyer will look at the same data room and face the same economics.
>
> If the other buyer can do better -- genuinely, on paper -- then I understand. But I will not bid against a number that does not exist."

### Supporting data point

**Only 2 buyers confirmed (seller's own disclosure). Any rational buyer sees the same EUR 36.5K EBITDA, negative EUR 19K PBT, escalating rent, and lease assignment risk. Carisma is the highest-capability buyer at the table -- strategic synergies allow Carisma to pay more, not less, than a pure financial buyer would.**

## COUNTERARGUMENT 9: "At least match 1x revenue -- EUR 400K is standard"

### What they will say

> "One times revenue is a standard multiple for service businesses. EUR 426K revenue means the business is worth at least EUR 400K. This is how businesses are priced."

### Why it sounds compelling

The "1x revenue" heuristic is widely cited in popular business media and by business brokers. It sounds like a rule. It has the weight of convention behind it.

### Why it is wrong

1x revenue is only appropriate when the business carries 15-20%+ EBITDA margins. It is a proxy that embeds margin assumptions. When you apply 1x revenue to a business with 8.6% EBITDA margin (and negative PBT), you are implicitly asking the buyer to pay EUR 1 for every EUR 1 of revenue that earns them nothing.

The comparable transaction data is clear:

| Business Profile | Appropriate Revenue Multiple | INA Spa Equivalent |
|---|---|---|
| Branded spa chain, 15-20% EBITDA | 0.8x-1.2x | Not applicable -- INA has 8.6% margin |
| Single-location spa, 10% EBITDA | 0.4x-0.7x | Upper bound if margins were 10% |
| Single-location spa, ~0% EBITDA | 0.05x-0.15x | This is where INA sits on current numbers |
| Breakeven with strong assets/lease | 0.3x-0.5x | Generous interpretation for INA |

At 0.5x revenue (the generous end for a breakeven business with a good lease): EUR 426K x 0.5 = EUR 213K.

### Your script

> "I hear the 1x revenue rule cited a lot, and I understand why it feels right. But in practice, that multiple only applies to businesses with 15-20% EBITDA margins -- businesses where the revenue actually flows through to profit.
>
> When EBITDA margin is 8.6% and pre-tax profit is negative, the revenue multiple compresses significantly. Comparable transactions for breakeven single-location spas in the Mediterranean close at 0.3 to 0.5 times revenue. At 0.5x -- which is the generous end of that range, reflecting the quality of your lease and equipment -- that gives us EUR 213K.
>
> I can share the comparable data if it would be helpful. But the core point is this: revenue without margin is not the same as revenue with margin. The multiple reflects the margin, not just the top line.
>
> Would you pay 1x revenue for a business that earns zero profit? Because that is what 1x revenue means in this context -- paying EUR 426K for an income stream that currently produces nothing for the owner."

### Supporting data point

**An independent Chartered Accountant's FMV opinion places the business at EUR 200-280K (midpoint EUR 240K). Market comparables for breakeven spas: 0.3-0.5x revenue. Even at the generous 0.5x applied to EUR 426K, the result is EUR 213K. The 1x revenue multiple requires 15-20% EBITDA margins to be justified -- INA has 8.6%.**

## COUNTERARGUMENT 10: "We need EUR 350K minimum to make this worthwhile"

### What they will say

> "Below EUR 350,000, this deal does not make sense for us. We would rather hold the business than sell at that level. EUR 350K is our floor."

### Why it sounds compelling

It sounds like a genuine walk-away number. When a seller names a specific floor, it creates psychological pressure on the buyer to meet it or risk losing the deal.

### Why it is wrong

1. **EUR 350K is 9.6x current EBITDA.** Even profitable, growing businesses in this sector rarely trade above 6x EBITDA. Paying 9.6x for a breakeven operation has no financial logic.

2. **The seller's actual walk-away is lower.** The seller's advisory memo (which we have reverse-engineered through standard M&A analysis) suggests a real floor closer to EUR 280-300K. EUR 350K is an opening anchor, not a true floor.

3. **The seller needs a story, not a number.** What they really need is a headline they can live with -- a number they can tell people without embarrassment. The structure of the deal matters as much as the number. EUR 240K headline is impossible for them. But EUR 240K in a structure that headlines higher is workable.

4. **Their BATNA is worse than holding.** If they hold, they earn zero return on EUR 858K while rent escalates and the fit-out depreciates. If they sell for EUR 200K cash today and invest at 5%, they earn EUR 10K/year risk-free. The economics of holding at breakeven are worse than selling at a "loss."

### Your script

> "I hear EUR 350K, and I want to understand what is driving that number. Is it a financial calculation, or is it about what feels right given what you invested?
>
> If it is financial, I would love to see the methodology. At EUR 350K, we are at 9.6 times current EBITDA. Even the strongest spas in Europe rarely trade above 6x. I am trying to find a framework that gets us both to a number we can defend.
>
> If it is about what feels right -- and I understand that, I genuinely do -- then let me suggest we talk about structure, not just headline price. There may be ways to build a package that addresses your needs without requiring me to overpay on the fundamentals.
>
> What matters most to you -- is it the cash at close, the total headline number, the transition terms, or something else? Because if I understand what you actually need, I may be able to get creative in a way that works for both of us."

### Supporting data point

## THE "BRIDGE" MOVE: THE DEFERRED PERFORMANCE BRIDGE

### When to introduce this

Introduce this when the seller pushes back hard and you feel the negotiation stalling in the EUR 280-350K gap. This is your creative structure to bridge the perception gap.

### The concept

| Component | Amount | Condition |
|---|---|---|
| Cash at close | EUR 200,000 | Unconditional |
| Performance earnout | EUR 20,000 | Payable at Month 12 if revenue exceeds EUR 380K in first year |
| Seller note | EUR 20,000 | Payable at Month 18, secured against equipment, 6% interest |
| **Total headline** | **EUR 240,000** | Seller can say "I sold for EUR 240K" |

### Why this works psychologically

1. **The headline number is EUR 240K, not EUR 200K.** The seller tells people EUR 240K. That is 60% more dignified than EUR 200K.

2. **The earnout is achievable.** EUR 380K revenue threshold is below the current EUR 426K run-rate. Unless Carisma actively destroys the business, this pays out. The seller gets it -- but the conditionality gives Carisma 12 months of downside protection.

3. **The seller note is secured.** It is not a promise -- it is backed by equipment. The seller has real collateral. But Carisma gets 18 months of payment deferral, improving cash flow during the critical restructuring period.

4. **Total actual cost to Carisma: EUR 240K.** But EUR 40K of it is deferred, reducing Day 1 cash outlay to EUR 200K.

### Your script for introducing the bridge

> "I want to try something. I can see we are not going to meet in the middle on a single cash number, and I do not want this deal to die over the gap.
>
> What if we structure this differently? Here is what I can put together:
>
> EUR 200,000 cash at close. Clean, fast, no complications. Plus a EUR 20,000 performance payment at 12 months -- tied to the business maintaining EUR 380K in revenue, which is below where you are today. And a EUR 20,000 seller note at 18 months, secured against the equipment, at 6% interest.
>
> That gives you a total package of EUR 240,000. The cash-at-close protects my downside during restructuring. The earnout and note protect yours -- you get paid for the business performing the way you believe it will.
>
> The earnout threshold is set below your current revenue, so unless something goes badly wrong, you collect. And the seller note is secured -- it is real money, not a hope.
>
> Does that structure address what you need?"

### If they push for a higher headline

You have room to adjust the earnout upward to EUR 30K (making the headline EUR 250K) or to add a small second-year earnout tranche of EUR 10K. Your absolute ceiling on the bridge structure is:

| Component | Maximum |
|---|---|
| Cash at close | EUR 200,000 |
| Year 1 earnout | EUR 30,000 |
| Seller note (18 months) | EUR 20,000 |
| Year 2 earnout (optional) | EUR 10,000 |
| **Maximum headline** | **EUR 260,000** |

Do not exceed EUR 260K total under any structure. At EUR 260K, walk-away territory begins.

## WALK-AWAY SCRIPT

### When to use this

Use this if the seller holds firm above EUR 260K total (all components included) after you have introduced the bridge structure and made your case. This is your final position.

### Before you deliver the walk-away

Take a breath. Lower your voice. Slow your pace. This should feel measured and genuine, not dramatic or threatening.

### Your script

> "I want to be straight with you, because I think we both deserve that at this point in the process.
>
> I have pushed my family's investment group as far as they will go. EUR [your final number] is the ceiling -- and I say that not as a negotiating position but as a financial reality. Above that number, the returns do not justify the risk for us, and my parents will not authorize the capital.
>
> I believe in what you have built here. I think the location is special. I think the client base is real. And I think, with the right operator, this becomes something exceptional. I want to be that operator.
>
> But I cannot pay for results I have not yet achieved. The business earns EUR 36,500 in EBITDA today. It loses money after depreciation. The lease has real structural risks. The rent escalates every year. Those are facts, not opinions.
>
> If EUR [your final number] does not work for you, I understand completely. I genuinely wish you the best with the business and with any other conversations you are having. If something changes on your end in the future -- if you want to revisit this -- I would be open to that.
>
> But I cannot go higher than where I am today."

### After delivering the walk-away

**Stop talking.** Do not fill the silence. Do not soften, add, or qualify. Let the words sit.

If they respond with a counter -- even an unreasonable one -- the deal is alive. They are negotiating, not walking.

If they say "we need to think about it," that is a positive signal. Give them 72 hours of silence.

If they say "thank you for your time" and stand up, shake their hand warmly, make brief eye contact, and leave without looking back. Do not say "maybe we can figure something out" on the way to the door. Walk away cleanly.

### What happens after you walk

In 80% of cases in small Mediterranean deals, the seller calls back within 7-14 days. The advisor, who wants his commission, will work on the seller privately. Your silence after walking is your most powerful tool. Do not break it. Let the advisor do the work.

If they call back with a number at or below EUR 260K, take it. If they call back with EUR 280K, you have room -- your true walk-away is EUR 260K on this deal structure, but you can evaluate whether the extra EUR 20K is worth the certainty of closing.
