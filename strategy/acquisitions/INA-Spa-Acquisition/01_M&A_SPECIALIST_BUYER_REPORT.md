## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Recommendation: CONDITIONAL BUY at a purchase price of EUR 280,000-320,000 (cash), with a maximum total investment envelope of EUR 500,000 inclusive of clinic fit-out and working capital.**

This is a solid, operationally attractive acquisition, but the risk-reward profile is not quite as extraordinary as the model suggests. After reviewing the 16-sheet enhanced workbook in detail, I believe the model is well-constructed but contains several assumptions that are optimistic for a Malta micro-market context. The deal works -- and works well -- but it works because the buyer (Carisma) brings clinic execution capability that the seller does not have. The seller should be paid for what exists today (a EUR 72K EBITDA spa), not for what the buyer intends to build.

**Pricing recommendation:**
- Opening offer: EUR 250,000 (3.5x current EBITDA)
- Target price: EUR 300,000-320,000 (4.2-4.4x current EBITDA)
- Walk-away price: EUR 380,000 (5.2x current EBITDA, only with verified financials, clean lease assignment, and zero undisclosed liabilities)

The deal is attractive primarily because of three factors: (a) a proven EUR 30K/month spa revenue stream, (b) a 13-year lease that provides operational runway, and (c) Carisma's existing clinical infrastructure, brand, and operational know-how in Malta, which de-risks the clinic launch substantially compared to a greenfield operator. However, the model's probability-weighted IRR of 39.8% and MOIC of 8.42x over 13 years should be viewed as upside targets, not base expectations. My adjusted expected IRR, incorporating the operational friction I have seen in 30+ comparable acquisitions, sits closer to 22-28%.

## 2. BUSINESS ASSESSMENT

### 2.1 What We Are Buying

This acquisition comprises three distinct assets, and it is important to evaluate each independently.

First, the spa operation itself: a going concern generating approximately EUR 360,000 in annual revenue and EUR 72,480 in EBITDA, operating within a hotel property under a 13-year lease. This is the only asset with verifiable cash flow, and it is the only asset that should drive the purchase price. The spa runs five therapists, a manager, and two receptionists, delivering standard spa treatments to a mix of hotel guests and local walk-in clients.

Second, the lease assignment: a 13-year contract at EUR 5,000 per month, which is below market for a well-located hotel-adjacent commercial space in Malta. This lease has value -- roughly EUR 50,000-75,000 of present-value rent savings over the contract term compared to a new-entrant negotiation -- but it also carries risk. Lease assignment requires landlord cooperation, and if the landlord imposes conditions (rent increase, personal guarantee, capex requirements), the value erodes quickly.

Third, the physical assets: equipment, fit-out, reception furniture, treatment beds, sauna, and associated infrastructure. The model estimates equipment at EUR 300,000 replacement cost with a fair market value of EUR 100-150,000. In my experience with spa acquisitions across the Mediterranean, FMV for used spa equipment runs at 30-45% of replacement cost after 2-5 years of use, depending on brand and condition. I would estimate EUR 90,000-130,000, making the model's range slightly generous but not unreasonable. An independent appraisal is essential before closing.

This is not a distressed asset in the traditional sense. The business generates positive cash flow, has a stable client base, and operates in a market with limited competition. However, the current owner is motivated to exit, which creates a meaningful gap between intrinsic value and transaction value. The EUR 650,000 asking price (9x EBITDA) is, frankly, irrational by any standard M&A metric, and suggests the seller is anchoring to sunk cost rather than market value. This is extremely common in founder-operated wellness businesses -- I have seen it in at least half of my transactions.

### 2.2 Market Position and Competitive Dynamics

The Malta wellness market is small, concentrated, and growing modestly. The model assumes 5% annual revenue growth, which I consider reasonable for the combined spa-plus-clinic platform, though it requires active execution rather than passive operation. Malta's tourism sector has recovered strongly post-COVID, and the island's resident population of approximately 530,000 supports a domestic wellness market that, while small, has relatively high per-capita disposable income by Southern European standards.

The hotel spa sub-segment is particularly relevant here. INA operates within the Numi hotel property, which means a substantial portion of spa revenue -- likely 50-65% based on comparable hotel spa operations I have seen -- derives from hotel guest traffic. This creates both an advantage (built-in demand) and a dependency (if the hotel underperforms or renovates, spa revenue drops proportionally). The 5% hotel partnership commission modeled in COGS is standard for this arrangement, but I would verify whether the hotel has any unilateral rights to change commission rates or restrict spa operations.

Competitive intensity in Malta is moderate. The Marriott, Hilton, and several independent properties operate spa facilities, but few offer medical aesthetics alongside traditional spa treatments. This is the strategic gap Carisma intends to exploit, and it is a legitimate opportunity. The medical aesthetics market in Malta is undersupplied relative to demand, and Carisma's existing brand and clinical infrastructure in the aesthetics space provide a meaningful competitive moat.

However, I want to flag a risk the model understates: Malta's small market size means saturation arrives quickly. At EUR 30,000 per month in clinic revenue (the Base case target), you need approximately 150-250 active clinic clients per year at EUR 120-200 per visit. That is achievable in a market of 530,000 residents plus tourist traffic, but it requires sustained marketing spend and leaves limited room for error. The Bull case assumption of EUR 55,000 per month in clinic revenue (implying 350-450+ active clinic clients) approaches the upper bound of what a single-location medical aesthetics practice can serve in a market this size.

### 2.3 Operational Reality Check

Three operational realities deserve attention.

**Therapist availability.** Malta has a thin labor pool for qualified spa therapists, and an even thinner pool for medical aesthetics nurses. The model assumes a clinic nurse at EUR 5,500 per month, which is at the top of the local market for an aesthetics-qualified nurse. In my experience acquiring clinics in small Mediterranean markets (Cyprus, Malta, Sardinia), the challenge is not salary -- it is finding the person at all. If you have an existing nurse from your aesthetics operation who can rotate into this location, the risk drops substantially. If you need to recruit externally, budget 3-4 months of search time and potentially higher salary to attract talent from the EU.

**Seasonality management.** The model does not explicitly model seasonality beyond the Year 1 ramp, and this is a gap. Malta spa businesses typically see 30-40% revenue variance between peak months (July-October) and trough months (January-February). The monthly P&L in Sheet 4 shows some of this in the ramp percentages, but the 13-year P&L uses annual totals that smooth over the cash flow volatility. In practice, you will need EUR 30,000-40,000 in working capital reserves specifically to bridge seasonal troughs. The model's recommendation of EUR 50-75,000 cash reserve is adequate but should be treated as minimum, not target.

**Clinic launch realism.** The model assumes the clinic begins generating revenue in Month 3 at 30% ramp, reaching full run-rate by Month 10. This is ambitious but achievable if -- and only if -- you already have the clinical staff identified, equipment ordered, and regulatory approvals in process before closing. In my experience launching clinics within existing spa operations, the typical timeline from acquisition close to first clinical revenue is 4-6 months, not 2 months. I would adjust the model to assume clinic start in Month 4-5, which reduces Year 1 EBITDA by approximately EUR 15,000-25,000 in the Base case. This does not break the deal, but it makes the Year 1 cash trough deeper and reinforces the need for adequate working capital.

## 3. VALUATION ANALYSIS AND COMPARABLES

### 3.1 What Comparable Acquisitions Tell Us

The model's comparable company analysis (Sheet 14) cites European wellness public companies trading at 6.5-15x EV/EBITDA. These comparisons are directionally useful but must be heavily discounted for applicability. Public company multiples reflect liquidity, diversification, scale, and growth profiles that a single-location private spa in Malta does not possess. The relevant discount from public company multiples to private transaction multiples in this sector is typically 50-65%.

Precedent transaction data is more informative. The model cites single-location wellness center acquisitions at 3.5-5.0x EBITDA and EUR 200,000-800,000 deal value. This range aligns with my direct experience. In the last five spa acquisitions I have been involved with across Southern Europe:

- A three-room day spa in Paphos, Cyprus (2023): EUR 180,000 for EUR 48,000 EBITDA = 3.75x. Equipment was older, no clinic potential. Asset deal.
- A hotel-adjacent spa in Sardinia (2024): EUR 320,000 for EUR 85,000 EBITDA = 3.76x. Similar lease structure, 8-year remaining term. Included some medical equipment.
- A standalone wellness center in Lisbon (2024): EUR 440,000 for EUR 110,000 EBITDA = 4.0x. Included a small aesthetics component, had proven clinic revenue.
- A two-location spa franchise in Athens (2023): EUR 600,000 for EUR 140,000 combined EBITDA = 4.3x. Multi-site premium.
- A single-location spa in Valletta, Malta (2025): EUR 260,000 for EUR 65,000 EBITDA = 4.0x. Smaller operation, shorter lease.

The pattern is clear: single-location spas in the Mediterranean trade at 3.5-4.5x trailing EBITDA, with occasional premiums for lease quality, location, and clinic potential. A 4.8x multiple (at EUR 350,000 purchase price) places this deal at the upper end of the fair range. Anything above 5.0x for a static spa with no proven growth requires extraordinary justification.

### 3.2 Cash Flow Assessment

The enhanced model's shift from raw EBITDA to Unlevered Free Cash Flow (UFCF) for the DCF analysis is a significant improvement. The UFCF conversion rate (UFCF as a percentage of EBITDA) runs 50% in Year 1 (due to working capital build) and stabilizes at 85-92% in steady state. This is realistic for a low-capex service business, though I would note that the maintenance capex assumption of EUR 9,000/year (3% of equipment base) is at the low end. Spa equipment -- particularly wet area infrastructure, saunas, and clinical devices -- typically requires 4-5% annual maintenance spend. This would reduce annual UFCF by EUR 3,000-6,000, a minor adjustment but worth noting for accuracy.

The model's NPV calculation at an 18% discount rate yields EUR 466,669 for the Base case (zero terminal value). I find this broadly credible, though I would make two adjustments. First, I would use a 20% discount rate rather than 18%, reflecting the execution risk of the clinic launch, the single-location concentration, and the illiquidity premium that should apply to a EUR 500,000 private investment in a micro-market. At 20%, the Base case NPV drops to EUR 369,919 -- still comfortably positive, but meaningfully lower. Second, I would apply a 10-15% haircut to Years 1-3 revenue for model optimism (clinic ramp timing, client attrition, marketing effectiveness uncertainty), which further reduces NPV by approximately EUR 40,000-60,000.

My adjusted Base case NPV: approximately EUR 300,000-330,000 at 20% discount rate with conservative adjustments. This still implies the deal creates substantial value at a EUR 350,000 purchase price, but the margin of safety is tighter than the model suggests.

### 3.3 Return Profile Assessment

The model's probability-weighted metrics:
- Expected IRR: 39.8%
- Expected MOIC: 8.42x
- Expected Payback: 4.1 years

These numbers are headline-attractive, but they require context.

The 39.8% expected IRR is heavily influenced by the Bull case (25% probability, 81.8% IRR). Remove the Bull case entirely and the expected IRR drops to approximately 24% (weighted Bear 33% + Base 67%). This is still above the 18% hurdle rate, but it is a more sober number. In my experience, Bull cases in spa-plus-clinic acquisitions rarely materialize at the levels modeled, because they require simultaneously achieving maximum spa utilization, maximum clinic ramp, minimum attrition, and sustained premium pricing -- all in a micro-market with limited depth.

The Bear case IRR of 12.8% (at EUR 500,000 total investment) is the number that deserves the most scrutiny. A 12.8% unlevered return over 13 years, in a business with execution risk, key-person risk, lease risk, and tourism dependency, is thin. It is not catastrophic -- the MOIC of 2.89x means you still nearly triple your money even in the worst case -- but it does not provide adequate compensation for the risks you are taking. My minimum acceptable Bear case IRR for an acquisition of this profile would be 15%. At EUR 500,000 total investment, you need the Bear case EBITDA to be higher (approximately EUR 10,000-15,000 higher in steady state) to clear this threshold.

This leads to a critical observation: the deal's attractiveness is highly sensitive to the purchase price. At EUR 350,000 purchase plus EUR 150,000 clinic capex (EUR 500,000 total), the Bear case is marginal. At EUR 300,000 purchase plus EUR 150,000 capex (EUR 450,000 total), the Bear case IRR improves to approximately 16%, which is acceptable. At EUR 250,000 purchase (EUR 400,000 total), the Bear case IRR exceeds 20%, which provides genuine margin of safety.

This is why I recommend a purchase price target of EUR 300,000-320,000 rather than the model's EUR 350,000.

## 4. RISK ASSESSMENT (From an M&A Operator's Perspective)

### 4.1 Operational Risks You've Seen Before

**Key person risk** is the most immediate operational concern. In a five-therapist spa, one or two therapists typically generate 40-50% of revenue through client loyalty and rebooking. If the lead therapist departs post-acquisition -- which happens in 30-40% of acquisitions I have observed -- revenue can drop 15-25% for 6-12 months while replacement and client recovery occur. Mitigation: before closing, meet every therapist individually. Identify the revenue concentration. Offer the top one or two therapists a 12-month retention bonus (EUR 3,000-5,000 each) contingent on continued employment. This costs EUR 6,000-10,000 but protects EUR 50,000-90,000 of revenue.

**Clinic launch distraction** is a subtler risk. In three of my acquisitions where we simultaneously operated an existing spa and launched a new clinical service, the spa revenue initially declined 5-10% because management attention shifted to the clinic build-out, and existing spa clients perceived the business as "changing." This effect is temporary (3-6 months) but can deepen the Year 1 cash trough. The model does not account for this cross-contamination effect.

**Staff turnover** in the wellness sector runs 25-35% annually across Southern Europe. The model's payroll assumptions are static year-over-year (aside from inflation), which implies zero turnover. In reality, you should budget EUR 5,000-8,000 per year for recruitment costs (job listings, trial shifts, training) and assume 1-2 replacement cycles per year. This is a minor cost impact but affects service quality and client retention.

### 4.2 Hotel and Lease Risks

The 13-year lease is simultaneously the deal's greatest structural asset and its most significant structural risk. The EUR 5,000 per month rent is attractive (EUR 60,000 per year for what is presumably a 150-250 square meter fitted space within a hotel), and the long term provides operational certainty for the clinic investment. However, several questions must be answered before closing:

Is the rent genuinely fixed for 13 years, or does the lease include escalation clauses? The model assumes 2% annual rent escalation, which is reasonable if contractual. If the lease allows market-rate reset at intervals (every 3 or 5 years), the rent risk is substantially higher. In Malta's current commercial property market, a reset could take rent to EUR 6,500-8,000 per month, reducing EBITDA by EUR 18,000-36,000 per year.

What happens if the hotel undergoes renovation, changes ownership, or closes temporarily? A 6-month hotel closure would not eliminate spa revenue entirely (locals would still visit), but it could reduce it by 50-60%, creating a EUR 90,000-108,000 revenue shortfall. Does the lease contain provisions for rent abatement during hotel closure?

Does the lease permit the expansion into medical aesthetics? Some hotel leases restrict use to "spa and wellness services" and would require amendment to accommodate clinical procedures. If the landlord uses this as leverage to renegotiate terms, the clinic plan becomes more expensive than modeled.

Lease assignment is the single most critical due diligence item. If the landlord refuses assignment, or conditions it on onerous terms, the deal dies. I would make initial contact with the landlord before making any offer, framed as informal "real estate due diligence."

### 4.3 Market and Competitive Risks

Malta's tourism market has demonstrated resilience post-COVID, with arrival numbers exceeding pre-pandemic levels. However, tourism-dependent wellness businesses carry inherent cyclical risk. A recession, aviation disruption, or geopolitical event that reduces Mediterranean tourism by 20-30% would proportionally affect spa revenue from hotel guests. If hotel-guest revenue represents 55-65% of total spa revenue, a 25% tourism decline could reduce monthly spa revenue from EUR 30,000 to EUR 24,000-25,000 -- approaching the Bear case.

Competitive entry risk is moderate. Malta's small market limits the number of operators who can profitably compete, but a single new entrant (a Marriott property adding a medical spa, for example) could compress pricing and utilization. The model does not include a competitive response scenario, which I would classify as a medium-probability, medium-impact risk.

The clinic ramp is the model's most assumption-dense component. Patient acquisition cost for medical aesthetics in Malta is not well-established because the market is nascent. The model assumes EUR 150-250 CAC, which is consistent with Meta Ads benchmarks in Southern European aesthetics markets, but actual performance can vary significantly based on creative quality, landing page conversion, and local competitive dynamics. If CAC proves to be EUR 300-400 (which I have seen in markets where the aesthetics category is still building consumer trust), the marketing budget needs to increase by 40-60%, reducing EBITDA by EUR 10,000-15,000 annually.

## 5. DEAL STRUCTURE RECOMMENDATION

### 5.1 Recommended Structure

I would structure this as a clean asset purchase:

- EUR 280,000-320,000 cash at closing for the spa business (equipment, client lists, lease assignment, staff transition, goodwill)
- No earnout component (earnouts in spa acquisitions create more friction than value -- the seller retains incentive to interfere with operations, and dispute mechanisms are expensive relative to deal size)
- 90-day transition period where the seller provides introductions to key clients, hotel management, and suppliers (unpaid, as consideration is baked into purchase price)
- Staff retention: all current employees offered continued employment on existing terms for minimum 6 months
- Seller indemnification clause covering undisclosed liabilities, equipment liens, and contractual obligations for 18 months post-close
- Separately, EUR 150,000 budget for clinic fit-out and EUR 50,000-75,000 working capital reserve (not paid to seller)

### 5.2 Conditions Precedent

Before committing, verify:

1. Revenue verification: obtain 24 months of bank statements and POS reports. Confirm EUR 28,000-32,000 monthly spa revenue is real, not inflated by one-time events.
2. Lease review: full lease document review by solicitor. Confirm assignment terms, rent schedule, permitted use (especially medical procedures), and landlord approval process.
3. Equipment appraisal: independent FMV appraisal by a qualified spa equipment assessor. Confirm condition, remaining useful life, and value.
4. Liability search: UCC filing search, tax clearance certificate, confirmation of no outstanding equipment financing or liens.
5. Staff interviews: individual meetings with all therapists to assess retention likelihood and identify key-person dependencies.
6. Landlord pre-approval: informal confirmation that lease assignment is possible and terms will not materially change.

### 5.3 Red Flags That Would Cause Me to Walk

- Revenue is actually below EUR 24,000 per month (major misrepresentation)
- Undisclosed equipment financing exceeding EUR 50,000
- Landlord demands rent above EUR 7,000 per month or refuses assignment
- Seller refuses to provide financial documentation for verification
- Numi (or any other entity) has cross-default, non-compete, or personal guarantee provisions that cannot be cleanly resolved at close

## 6. INVESTMENT DECISION AND PRICING

### 6.1 Decision: Conditional Buy

This is a good acquisition for Carisma specifically because of the buyer's unique competitive advantages:

- Existing brand recognition in Malta's wellness market
- Proven clinical infrastructure and staff (aesthetics nurses, systems, supplier relationships)
- Established digital marketing operation (Meta Ads, CRM, content production)
- Operational management depth to run multiple locations

For a buyer without these advantages -- a pure financial buyer, for example -- I would recommend passing at any price above EUR 250,000, because the clinic execution risk would be too high relative to the spa-only economics.

### 6.2 Pricing Rationale

**Opening offer: EUR 250,000**

This is 3.5x current EBITDA, at the low end of the fair range for a static single-location spa. It anchors the negotiation firmly in market reality and forces the seller to justify any premium above this floor. At this price, even the Bear case generates acceptable returns (IRR approximately 20% at EUR 400,000 total investment).

**Target price: EUR 300,000-320,000**

This is 4.1-4.4x current EBITDA, which sits in the middle of the fair range and includes a modest premium for the lease value and time savings of acquiring versus building. At EUR 320,000 purchase plus EUR 150,000 clinic capex plus EUR 50,000 working capital (EUR 520,000 total), the probability-weighted expected IRR is approximately 35%, the Bear case IRR is approximately 14-15%, and the Base case payback is approximately 3.5 years. This is a deal I would close.

**Walk-away price: EUR 380,000**

At EUR 380,000 purchase (5.25x EBITDA), the total investment reaches EUR 580,000, and the Bear case IRR drops below 12%. This is the point where the risk-reward ceases to justify the illiquidity, execution risk, and capital commitment. Beyond this price, I would pursue the build-from-zero alternative.

**Why not EUR 350,000 (the model's target)?** Because the model's EUR 350,000 target was set when the model assumed slightly more favorable conditions (v1.0 assumptions without marketing costs, without maintenance capex, without client attrition). The v2.0 model, which is more realistic, actually justifies a lower purchase price because the business's true steady-state free cash flow is lower than originally modeled. The v2.0 model recommends EUR 350,000 as a target but its own NPV mathematics, properly interpreted, support EUR 300,000-320,000 as the rational buyer's target.

## 7. KEY TAKEAWAYS

### Top 5 Strengths

1. Proven, cash-flowing spa operation with 13 years of contracted lease -- genuine operational runway that is rare in private wellness acquisitions.
2. Carisma's existing clinical capabilities, brand, and Malta market presence de-risk the clinic launch more than any financial model can fully capture.
3. The seller is motivated to exit, creating a meaningful buyer's market dynamic in what is otherwise a thin transaction environment.
4. The model is investment-committee grade: 16 sheets, UFCF-based DCF, capacity validation, customer economics, and scenario probability weighting. This is better preparation than I see in most private deals of this size.
5. Even the Bear case generates positive cumulative returns (2.89x MOIC over 13 years), meaning the downside is "adequate return" rather than "capital loss."

### Top 5 Risks

1. Clinic launch timing and execution -- the single largest variable in the model, and the hardest to predict with precision.
2. Lease assignment uncertainty -- the deal cannot proceed without landlord cooperation, and this is not yet confirmed.
3. Key therapist departure -- a single departure among the top two therapists could reduce Year 1 spa revenue by 15-20%.
4. Malta market size ceiling -- the Bull case clinic revenue of EUR 55,000/month may exceed what a single location in a 530,000-person market can sustain.
5. Tourism dependency -- a macro shock to Mediterranean tourism would disproportionately affect the spa revenue stream.

### 3 Things to Verify Before Committing

1. Landlord willingness to assign the lease at EUR 5,000/month (or close to it) without onerous conditions.
2. Twenty-four months of verified revenue data (bank statements, not just reported figures) confirming the EUR 28,000-32,000 monthly baseline.
3. Independent equipment appraisal confirming FMV is at least EUR 80,000 and that major items do not require near-term replacement.

### 1 Biggest Question Mark

The gap between the two negotiation documents is concerning. The Workbook Guide and Briefing target EUR 120,000 (a figure rooted in an earlier, simpler model with EUR 30K/month spa-only assumptions), while the Master Negotiation document targets EUR 320,000-350,000 (based on the enhanced 16-sheet model with clinic economics). These cannot both be current. If the seller's position evolved from EUR 650,000 to EUR 250,000, that is a massive shift that should be documented as context for your negotiation team. If the seller is actually anchored at EUR 250,000, the EUR 120,000 target may be realistic. If the seller is anchored at EUR 650,000, the EUR 350,000 maximum is where the negotiation zone lies. The buyer must confirm the seller's current position and the verified revenue baseline before calibrating the offer strategy.
