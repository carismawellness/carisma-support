## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### The Verdict

This is a **conditionally attractive acquisition at EUR 200,000-220,000** -- and a value-destroying mistake at any price above EUR 260,000.

The financial model projects a base-case MOIC of 2.95x and IRR of 35-40% at EUR 220,000. These returns are real, but they are entirely contingent on four assumptions that have not been validated:

1. The landlord consents to lease assignment (probability: 70-75%)
2. The medical aesthetics clinic launches successfully (probability: 55-65%)
3. Wages can be reduced from 49.2% to 38% of revenue within 18 months (probability: 40-50%)
4. Revenue grows at 5% annually in a market with no proven growth trajectory beyond Year 1 ramp (probability: 50-60%)

When I stack these probabilities -- and I use "stack" deliberately, because these are not independent events -- the joint probability of the base case materializing as modeled is approximately **8-12%**. The Monte Carlo simulation confirms this: at EUR 220,000 purchase price, the unconditional probability of achieving a positive NPV at an 18% hurdle rate is 40.2%. In plain language, there is a 60% chance this investment underperforms a risk-adjusted benchmark.

That does not mean the deal is bad. It means the deal is a calculated bet. The question is whether the price adequately compensates for that calculation. At EUR 200-220K, it does. At EUR 280K+, it does not.

### What The Spreadsheet Gets Right

The financial model is sophisticated. The 13-year DCF with zero terminal value is methodologically conservative. The three-scenario framework (Bear 25%/Base 50%/Bull 25%) is appropriate. The 18% discount rate for a single-asset, lease-dependent micro-business is defensible (I would argue for 20%, but 18% is within range). The rent escalation is correctly modeled from the Fortina contract. The 5% Malta tax rate under imputation is accurate.

### What The Spreadsheet Misses

The model has seven structural blind spots that a financial analyst would not catch but that determine whether this deal creates or destroys value:

| # | Blind Spot | Impact | Section |
|---|-----------|--------|---------|
| 1 | **Lease assignment is binary, not probabilistic** -- the model treats it as a 20% haircut; in reality, the landlord says yes or no, and if no, the deal is worth exactly EUR 0 | Existential | 8.1 |
| 2 | **Clinic revenue cannibalization is not modeled** -- 23.6% of current spa revenue is already laser/T-Shape; launching a "clinic" partly cannibalizes the spa line | EUR 50-70K NPV overstatement | 4.3 |
| 3 | **The 5% turnover fee routing assumption is legally untested** -- the model routes clinic/slimming revenue through separate entities at 0.5% effective turnover fee; the landlord's reaction to this is unknown | EUR 80-120K risk | 4.5 |
| 4 | **Wage reduction from 49% to 38% requires terminating 3-4 people under Malta TUPE** -- the model treats this as a smooth glide path; the reality is labor tribunal risk, key-person flight, and morale damage | EUR 30-50K execution cost | 6 |
| 5 | **Seasonality creates 3 months of negative cash flow annually** -- July 2025 revenue was EUR 22K against monthly wages of EUR 17.5K + EUR 6K rent; the working capital requirement is undermodeled | EUR 20-30K additional WC | 7.3 |
| 6 | **No competitor response is modeled** -- INA Spa is not the only spa in Sliema; a pricing war, poaching of therapists, or landlord offering space to a competing operator is not considered | Strategic risk | 10 |
| 7 | **Complex sale termination payout accrues to ORIGINAL lessee's investment, not Carisma's** -- if the Complex sells after Carisma invests EUR 80-100K in clinic capex, recovery of that capex is uncertain | EUR 80-100K at risk | 9.4 |

### The Bottom Line

**Proceed -- but only with the following guardrails:**

- Purchase price: EUR 200,000-220,000 maximum (cash at close)
- Total investment envelope: EUR 350,000 (purchase + clinic capex + working capital)
- Condition precedent: Written landlord consent for lease assignment AND confirmation that medical aesthetics is permitted use
- Walk-away: EUR 260,000 under any structure
- Do not sign an LOI until landlord consent is obtained

## 2. MARKET & COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

### 2.1 Malta Wellness Market -- The Uncomfortable Truth

The Maltese wellness market is a EUR 45-55 million market serving a population of 535,000. It is not growing at the rate the financial model implies.

**Market reality check:**

| Metric | Model Assumption | Market Reality | Gap |
|--------|-----------------|----------------|-----|
| Spa market growth rate | 5% annual (implied by revenue projections) | 2-3% real growth (tourism-driven, population-constrained) | 2-3pp optimistic |
| Medical aesthetics growth | 20% Year 1, scaling to 15% | 12-18% in EU markets; Malta is smaller, less mature | Plausible but at top of range |
| Addressable population | Not explicitly modeled | ~120,000 women aged 25-55 in Malta; ~45,000 in Sliema/surrounding | Small TAM |
| Penetration rate required | Not modeled | At EUR 500K spa revenue, implies ~8,800 appointments from ~3,000-4,000 unique local clients | 2.5-3.5% of addressable population |

The penetration math is critical and has been entirely absent from every analysis conducted to date. INA Spa currently books 5,827 local appointments annually. If we assume the average local client visits 2-3 times per year, that implies 1,940-2,914 unique local clients. In a Sliema catchment of approximately 45,000 women aged 25-55, that represents 4.3-6.5% penetration.

**The question no one has asked:** Can penetration grow to 8-10% (required for the EUR 500K+ revenue projections), or is this market ceiling-constrained? Malta's small geography means clients have alternatives. A 30-minute drive covers the entire island. INA Spa is not competing only with spas in Sliema -- it is competing with every spa in Malta.

**Assessment:** The 5% annual revenue growth assumption for the spa line is optimistic. I would model 3% real growth after Year 2, with the Year 1-2 uplift coming primarily from pricing optimization (revenue per appointment from EUR 57 to EUR 70), not volume growth. The market is too small and too competitive for sustained 5% volume growth from a single location.

### 2.2 Medical Aesthetics in Malta -- Growth Potential vs. Regulatory Reality

The medical aesthetics thesis is the core of this deal's value. Without the clinic, the spa alone generates EUR 55-60K EBITDA under Carisma management -- a 12-15% IRR at EUR 200K purchase price. Acceptable but not compelling. The clinic is what transforms the deal from "acceptable" to "excellent."

**Market opportunity -- what is real:**

Malta's medical aesthetics market is genuinely underserved relative to comparable European markets. Laser treatments, injectables, and body contouring are growing at 15-20% annually across Southern Europe. Malta's relatively affluent, appearance-conscious population and limited supply of medical aesthetic providers create a genuine opportunity.

**Market opportunity -- what is not real:**

The Clinic Expansion Business Plan projects Year 1 clinic revenue of EUR 65-150K (conservative to aggressive) growing to EUR 300K by Year 3. The Med Spa Conversion Plan projects EUR 194K in Year 1 growing to EUR 636K by Year 3. These projections span a 3:1 range, which tells me nobody actually knows what clinic revenue will be. When your low estimate is EUR 65K and your high estimate is EUR 194K in the same year, you are guessing, not forecasting.

**My estimate:** Year 1 incremental clinic revenue (net of cannibalization from existing laser/T-Shape) is EUR 50-80K. Year 3 incremental is EUR 150-250K. The model uses EUR 100K growing to EUR 300K -- the midpoint of my range, which I consider plausible but not conservative.

**Regulatory friction -- the missing timeline:**

No analysis has adequately modeled the regulatory timeline for clinic expansion:

| Step | Estimated Duration | Dependency |
|------|-------------------|------------|
| Landlord consent for medical use | 4-12 weeks | Binary gate -- cannot proceed without |
| Medical Director recruitment | 8-16 weeks | EUR 1,500-3,000/month recurring cost |
| Malta Medicines Authority registration | 6-12 weeks | Requires Medical Director in place first |
| Environmental Health inspection | 4-8 weeks | Requires physical modifications complete |
| Treatment room conversion | 4-8 weeks | Can run parallel with above |
| Staff recruitment and training | 8-12 weeks | Nurse practitioner, aesthetician |
| Marketing and patient pipeline | 8-16 weeks | Must start during build phase |
| **Total critical path** | **6-12 months** | Sequential dependencies |

The financial model assumes clinic revenue begins in Month 1. In reality, meaningful clinic revenue is unlikely before Month 6-9, and stabilization at run-rate occurs at Month 12-18. This timing gap costs EUR 40-60K in delayed revenue versus the model.

### 2.3 Slimming Market in Malta -- An Unexamined Thesis

The model includes a slimming business line starting at EUR 80K Year 1, growing 25% annually to a EUR 300K cap. This line has received almost no analytical scrutiny.

**Critical questions that remain unanswered:**

1. What specific slimming services will be offered? The model allocates EUR 30K CAPEX but specifies no equipment, no treatment protocol, and no staffing plan.
2. Who is the target customer? Is this different from the spa client or the aesthetics client? If it is the same client, this is cross-sell revenue, not incremental market.
3. What is the competitive landscape for slimming services in Malta? Carisma already operates Carisma Slimming as a separate brand. Is this cannibalization of an existing Carisma revenue stream?
4. A 25% growth rate for a slimming line with EUR 30K CAPEX is aggressive. What justifies it?

**Assessment:** The slimming line contributes EUR 200-400K to the 13-year NPV in the financial model. I consider this line to be the least credible of the three revenue streams. It should be treated as pure optionality -- valued at zero in the base case and included only in the Bull scenario. See Section 5 for the full feasibility assessment.

## 3. OPERATIONAL PERFORMANCE AUDIT

### 3.1 Revenue Quality Assessment

The verified revenue of EUR 426,018 (2025) is higher than initial estimates, but the quality of that revenue matters more than the quantity.

**Revenue quality indicators:**

| Indicator | Actual | Benchmark | Assessment |
|-----------|--------|-----------|------------|
| Revenue per appointment | EUR 57 | EUR 75-120 (premium Mediterranean spa) | **31-53% below benchmark** |
| Revenue per treatment room | EUR 60,860 | EUR 80,000-120,000 | **24-49% below benchmark** |
| Revenue per employee | EUR 32,770 | EUR 40,000-55,000 | **18-40% below benchmark** |
| Utilization rate | 45-53% (est.) | 65-75% | **12-22pp below benchmark** |
| Repeat booking rate | Unknown | 40-60% (industry) | **Critical data gap** |
| Average client lifetime value | Unknown | EUR 400-800 (industry) | **Critical data gap** |

Every single revenue quality metric is below benchmark. This is simultaneously a risk and an opportunity. The risk: current revenue may be fragile, dependent on heavy discounting or a few productive therapists. The opportunity: even modest improvements in pricing and utilization could drive meaningful revenue uplift without additional marketing spend.

**The EUR 57 average revenue problem:** At EUR 57 per appointment, INA Spa is underpricing its services by 25-40% relative to comparable Mediterranean spas. This suggests either:
- Heavy discounting to build volume (common in Year 1-2)
- A treatment mix skewed toward low-value services (mani/pedi, basic massage)
- Failure to upsell premium treatments (facials, packages)
- Below-market pricing for laser and T-Shape services specifically

The data room does not provide pricing data per treatment category, which is a significant gap. If laser treatments are priced at EUR 80-100 instead of the industry standard EUR 150-250, there is a massive revenue uplift opportunity from repricing alone.

### 3.2 Cost Structure Decomposition

The P&L reveals two dominant cost items that together consume 66% of revenue:

**Wages: EUR 209,574 (49.2%)**

This is the single most important number in the entire data room. It defines the deal. At 49.2% of revenue, the business is structurally unprofitable. No amount of revenue growth solves a wage problem of this magnitude -- if wages grow proportionally with revenue (as they tend to in labor-intensive service businesses), EBITDA margin remains stuck at 8-9%.

The 13 employees break down as:
- 1 Manager
- 1 Senior Therapist
- 7 Therapists
- 2 Receptionists
- 2 Cleaners

Average compensation: EUR 16,121 per year (EUR 1,343/month). This is approximately Malta's minimum wage. There is almost no room to reduce per-head costs. The fix must come from headcount reduction.

**Rent: EUR 71,689 (16.8%)**

The rent is a dual-component liability that escalates mechanically:
- Fixed base: EUR 60,000 (Year 3 of lease), escalating at approximately 3% per annum
- Turnover fee: 5% of actual revenue or EUR 350,000 minimum threshold, whichever is greater

At EUR 426K revenue, the turnover fee is approximately EUR 21,300. The total rent of EUR 71,689 is already 16.8% of revenue. By Year 10, fixed rent alone reaches EUR 73,792 before the turnover fee. If revenue grows to EUR 700K (with clinic), total rent reaches EUR 108,792 (EUR 73,792 + EUR 35,000 turnover fee).

**The rent escalation trap:** The landlord effectively holds a 5% equity stake in every euro of revenue growth. If Carisma successfully grows the business from EUR 426K to EUR 800K, the landlord captures an additional EUR 18,700 per year in turnover fees. Over 10 years, that is EUR 187K flowing to the landlord from Carisma's operational improvements. This is a structural drag on returns that no operational improvement can eliminate.

### 3.3 Deferred Maintenance Risk

The P&L shows Repairs & Maintenance at EUR 2,117 (0.5% of revenue). This is dangerously low for a facility with:
- Wet areas (sauna, steam room, hammam, hydrotherapy)
- 7 treatment rooms with specialized plumbing and HVAC
- A Candela laser and T-Shape machine requiring regular calibration and servicing
- A fit-out that is only 2 years old but cost EUR 738K

Industry benchmark for spa maintenance is 2-4% of revenue (EUR 8,500-17,000). The EUR 2,117 actual suggests either:
1. The seller has been deferring maintenance to inflate EBITDA (most likely)
2. Warranty coverage has been handling costs (partially possible for 2-year-old equipment)
3. There is a ticking time bomb of deferred maintenance that the buyer will inherit

**My estimate of true maintenance costs:** EUR 10,000-15,000 per year. If I adjust EBITDA for normalized maintenance, EBITDA drops from EUR 36,538 to EUR 23,538-28,538. This is a material adjustment that further compresses fair value.

**Wet area lifecycle costs:**

| Equipment | Estimated Replacement Cost | Typical Lifespan | Annual Provision |
|-----------|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Sauna unit | EUR 25,000-35,000 | 8-12 years | EUR 2,500-4,375 |
| Steam room | EUR 20,000-30,000 | 8-10 years | EUR 2,500-3,750 |
| Hammam | EUR 30,000-50,000 | 10-15 years | EUR 2,000-5,000 |
| Hydrotherapy | EUR 15,000-25,000 | 8-12 years | EUR 1,250-3,125 |
| **Total** | **EUR 90,000-140,000** | | **EUR 8,250-16,250** |

These replacement costs are not discretionary. They are eventual certainties. The financial model allocates EUR 12,000/year for maintenance capex, which is at the low end of what is required.

### 3.4 Insurance Adequacy

Insurance costs of EUR 3,358 (0.8% of revenue) are notably low for a business that:
- Operates a Class IIb medical device (Candela laser)
- Performs body contouring treatments (T-Shape)
- Has 13 employees
- Operates wet areas with inherent slip/fall risk
- Is planning to expand into medical aesthetics

Industry benchmark for spa insurance (public liability + professional indemnity + employer liability + equipment) is 1.5-2.5% of revenue (EUR 6,400-10,650). The EUR 3,358 suggests either inadequate coverage or material uninsured risks.

**If Carisma expands into injectables and medical aesthetics, professional indemnity insurance alone could cost EUR 5,000-8,000 per year.** This is not reflected in any current projection.

## 4. CLINIC BUILDOUT FEASIBILITY ASSESSMENT

### 4.1 The Clinic Is Partially Built -- But That Cuts Both Ways

The data room reveals that 23.6% of current treatments are already clinical/aesthetic services (Laser 16.4% + T-Shape 7.2%). This fundamentally changes the clinic thesis:

**The positive spin (as presented in existing analysis):**
- Equipment already on-site (Candela laser EUR 65K cost, T-Shape EUR 55K cost)
- Clinical protocols and patient base already exist
- Reduces clinic CAPEX from EUR 150K to EUR 80K
- Clinic launch is "expansion, not greenfield"

**The scrutiny that has been missing:**

1. **Cannibalization is real and unquantified.** If EUR 100K of current spa revenue comes from laser and T-Shape, then the "clinic" does not add EUR 100K in Year 1 -- it adds EUR 100K minus whatever laser/T-Shape revenue migrates from the "spa" line to the "clinic" line. The model treats spa revenue as flat-to-growing AND adds clinic revenue on top. In reality, when you rebrand laser treatments from "spa services" to "clinic services," you are relabeling, not growing.

   **Quantified impact:** The financial model projects EUR 100K clinic Year 1 revenue. But approximately EUR 70-100K of laser/T-Shape revenue is already in the EUR 440K spa projection. The true incremental clinic revenue in Year 1 is EUR 0-30K, not EUR 100K. This is a EUR 70-100K overstatement in Year 1, compounding in subsequent years.

2. **The Candela laser utilization question.** The laser currently generates approximately EUR 70K in annual revenue at EUR 120-180 per treatment. That implies 390-580 laser treatments per year, or 1.5-2.3 per working day. A single Candela unit can perform 6-8 treatments per day. Current utilization is 19-38%.

   **The opportunity is real** -- doubling laser treatments from current levels is physically achievable. But it requires a fundamentally different marketing approach, a medical director to oversee protocols, and likely nurse practitioners to perform treatments. These costs (EUR 3,000-5,500/month) are modeled, but the ramp-up timing is not.

3. **Treatment room availability.** The Med Spa Conversion Plan proposes converting the Hair Salon (~25-30 sqm) and Nail Salon (~20-25 sqm) into clinical rooms. But this means:
   - Losing hair salon sublease income (estimated EUR 20K/year)
   - Losing nail salon revenue (part of the current EUR 426K)
   - Requiring landlord approval for alterations (Clause 5.9: written consent required)
   - Construction downtime for conversion (4-8 weeks, during which those rooms generate zero revenue)

   The alternative Clinic Expansion Business Plan proposes converting 2 existing treatment rooms for EUR 80K. This is more conservative but means operating the spa with 5 rooms instead of 7 during build-out, reducing spa capacity by 29%.

### 4.2 Regulatory Pathway -- The Critical Chain

Every analysis mentions the need for a Medical Director and MMA registration, but none provides a realistic critical-path timeline or cost estimate:

**Step 1: Medical Director Recruitment (Weeks 1-16)**

Finding a qualified medical professional willing to serve as Medical Director for a hotel spa clinic in Malta is not trivial. Requirements:
- Licensed medical practitioner registered with the Malta Medical Council
- Willing to serve part-time (2-4 hours/week minimum for oversight)
- Comfortable with aesthetic protocols (laser, injectables, body contouring)
- Available in Malta (small talent pool)

Cost: EUR 1,500-3,000/month. This is a recurring operational cost that begins before the clinic generates any revenue and continues indefinitely.

**Step 2: Malta Medicines Authority Registration (Weeks 8-20)**

The Candela laser is a Class IIb medical device under EU MDR 2017/745. Operating it requires:
- An authorized operator license
- A Responsible Person designation
- Compliance with the EU Medical Device Regulation

**Critical question that no one has addressed:** The current operator license is presumably held by Well Being Services Limited (the seller's company). If Carisma acquires assets (not shares), the operator license does not transfer. Carisma must apply for a new license. During the application period (6-12 weeks), the laser cannot legally operate. That is EUR 5,000-10,000 per month in lost laser revenue.

If Carisma acquires shares (buying the company), the license may transfer, but this triggers different tax, liability, and due diligence considerations. The deal structure has regulatory implications that have not been analyzed.

**Step 3: Landlord Confirmation of Permitted Use (Binary Gate)**

The lease permits "Spa or any related use." Medical aesthetics may or may not qualify as "related use." The landlord has absolute discretion on this interpretation. If the landlord says medical aesthetics is not permitted, the entire clinic thesis collapses.

**My assessment of probability:**
- Laser treatments (already being performed): 90% likely to be accepted as "related use"
- Body contouring (already being performed): 85% likely
- Injectables (Botox, fillers -- new): 60-70% likely; requires medical supervision, changes the risk profile of the premises
- IV therapy (new): 50-60% likely; introduces medical waste, clinical protocols
- Medical consultations: 70-80% likely

The clinic plan assumes all services are permitted. The reality is that injectables and IV therapy carry meaningful probability of landlord objection.

### 4.5 The Turnover Fee Routing Assumption -- A Structural Risk

The financial model contains a critical assumption that has not been stress-tested: clinic and slimming revenue routed through separate entities to attract only a 0.5% effective turnover fee to the landlord, versus the full 5% that applies to spa revenue.

**From the model code:**
```
Spa turnover fee: 5% of spa revenue
Clinic turnover fee: 0.5% of clinic revenue (via separate entity)
Slimming turnover fee: 0.5% of slimming revenue (via separate entity)
```

**The problem:**

The Fortina lease defines the turnover fee as 5% of "actual sales." If Carisma operates a clinic and slimming service from the same leased premises, the landlord will almost certainly argue that ALL revenue generated from the premises constitutes "actual sales" subject to the 5% fee.

The argument that routing clinic revenue through a "separate entity" reduces the turnover fee from 5% to 0.5% is:
1. Legally untested under Maltese commercial law
2. Aggressive from a landlord-relationship perspective (the landlord will view this as evasion)
3. Potentially a breach of the permitted use clause (operating a medical clinic under a spa lease through a shell entity)
4. A material red flag during the lease assignment negotiation

**Quantified impact:** If the landlord enforces the full 5% on all revenue:

| Year | Total Revenue (Projected) | 5% Turnover Fee | Model's 0.5% on Clinic/Slim | Additional Cost |
|------|--------------------------|-----------------|------------------------------|-----------------|
| 3 | EUR 620,000 | EUR 31,000 | EUR 900 | EUR 30,100 |
| 5 | EUR 740,000 | EUR 37,000 | EUR 1,500 | EUR 35,500 |
| 10 | EUR 900,000 | EUR 45,000 | EUR 2,250 | EUR 42,750 |

Over 10 years, the 5% vs. 0.5% delta amounts to approximately EUR 350,000 in additional rent. This is the single largest unaddressed assumption in the financial model.

**My recommendation:** Model the full 5% on all revenue generated from the premises. If Carisma can successfully negotiate a lower rate for non-spa services, treat that as upside. Do not base the investment case on an untested legal structure.

## 5. SLIMMING CLINIC FEASIBILITY ASSESSMENT

### 5.1 The Slimming Line Is Underanalyzed

The financial model includes a slimming business line with the following assumptions:
- Year 1 revenue: EUR 80,000
- Growth rate: 25% annually
- Revenue cap: EUR 300,000
- CAPEX: EUR 30,000
- Operating margin: 30% (implied by model)

This line has received less analytical scrutiny than any other component of the deal, despite contributing EUR 200-400K to the 13-year NPV.

### 5.2 Critical Feasibility Questions

**Question 1: What treatments are included?**

The model does not specify. Slimming services in Malta typically include:
- CoolSculpting/cryolipolysis (requires EUR 30,000-80,000 device)
- Body wraps and detox (low-tech, EUR 2,000-5,000 setup)
- Nutritional counseling (requires dietitian -- EUR 25,000-35,000/year salary)
- EMS (electrical muscle stimulation -- EUR 10,000-20,000 device)
- Weight management programs (primarily labor cost)

At EUR 30,000 CAPEX, Carisma can afford EMS + body wraps + basic counseling. They cannot afford CoolSculpting or any advanced body contouring technology. Yet the model projects EUR 80K Year 1 revenue from this EUR 30K investment -- a 267% revenue-to-capex ratio that is extremely aggressive.

**Question 2: Is this cannibalization of Carisma Slimming?**

Carisma already operates Carisma Slimming as a separate brand. If INA Spa also offers slimming services, the buyer must answer: is this new demand or is this redirecting existing Carisma Slimming clients to a different location? If 50% of slimming revenue is cannibalized from existing Carisma operations, the incremental value of the slimming line halves.

### 6.2 Malta Employment Law -- What The Model Glosses Over

The Staffing Restructuring Plan correctly identifies Malta's TUPE regulations (S.L. 452.85) as the governing framework. Under TUPE:

1. **All 13 employees automatically transfer** to Carisma on their existing terms
2. **Dismissal because of the transfer is automatically unfair** -- unless the employer can demonstrate "economic, technical or organizational" (ETO) reasons
3. **Notice periods are mandatory** -- based on length of service (1 week per year for first 6 months, then scaled)
4. **Redundancy is possible** but requires genuine role elimination, not simply cost-cutting

**The legal exposure is low but real.** The Staffing Restructuring Plan estimates maximum redundancy exposure at EUR 8-10K for all 13 staff. This is likely correct for direct redundancy payments. But it omits:

- **Industrial Tribunal risk:** If terminated employees file unfair dismissal claims, legal costs run EUR 2,000-5,000 per case, with potential awards of EUR 3,000-12,000 per employee
- **Key-person departure risk:** The Senior Therapist and 2-3 top-performing therapists likely generate 40-50% of revenue. If they interpret restructuring as hostility and leave voluntarily, revenue drops immediately
- **Morale cost:** Reducing from 13 to 9-10 staff in a small team environment damages team cohesion. In a service business where client experience depends on staff attitude, this is a real economic cost
- **Timing:** TUPE prohibits changes to employment terms for a "reasonable period" post-transfer (typically interpreted as 6-12 months in Malta). Aggressive restructuring in the first 6 months risks legal challenge

### 6.3 The Realistic Restructuring Timeline

The model assumes wages decline from 49.2% to 38% over 18 months via a smooth glide path. Reality:

| Phase | Period | Action | Wage Impact | Risk |
|-------|--------|--------|------------|------|
| Stabilize | Months 1-3 | No changes. Assess staff, build trust, understand who does what | 0% reduction | Low |
| Natural attrition | Months 3-6 | Do not replace departing staff (if any). Cross-train remaining team | -3-5% (if attrition occurs) | Low |
| Voluntary restructuring | Months 6-9 | Offer voluntary separation packages to underperforming staff | -5-10% (if accepted) | Moderate |
| Forced restructuring | Months 9-18 | Redundancies if targets not met. Formal process required | -10-15% | High |
| Revenue growth | Ongoing | Growing denominator reduces ratio even without headcount changes | -3-5% (denominator effect) | Low |

**My estimate of achievable wage ratio by Month 18:** 42-45% (not 38%)

The gap between the model's 38% target and my 42-45% estimate is significant. At EUR 440K revenue:
- 38% wages = EUR 167,200 (model target)
- 42% wages = EUR 184,800
- 45% wages = EUR 198,000

The EUR 17,600-30,800 annual difference between the model and reality reduces EBITDA by the same amount -- from the model's projected EUR 95K+ to EUR 65-77K in Year 2.

### 6.4 The Key-Person Problem

No analysis has adequately addressed key-person dependency. In a 7-room spa with 13 staff:

- **The Manager** runs day-to-day operations. Losing the manager immediately post-acquisition creates operational chaos
- **The Senior Therapist** likely drives the highest rebooking rates. Clients book with therapists, not spas. If she leaves, her clients leave
- **The top 2-3 therapists** together likely generate 40-50% of rebookings (standard Pareto distribution in spa businesses)

**The critical metric no one has measured:** What is the rebooking rate by therapist? If the top 2 therapists have 60% rebooking rates and the bottom 5 have 15%, then losing those 2 therapists collapses 25-30% of revenue.

**My recommendation:** Before making any offer, Carisma must meet the Manager and Senior Therapist individually. Assess their willingness to stay under new ownership. If either expresses intent to leave, reduce the purchase price by EUR 20-30K to reflect the client relationship risk.

## 8. INTEGRATION RISK ASSESSMENT

### 8.1 The Lease Assignment -- A Binary Existential Risk

I have reviewed the full Fortina lease contract (34 pages). The assignment clause (5.26) is unambiguous:

> Assignment requires the lessor's written consent, which may be withheld at the lessor's **absolute discretion**. A change of control of the lessee entity shall be deemed an assignment.

This is not a "risk factor." This is a binary gate. If the landlord says no, the deal is worth EUR 0 to Carisma. There is no mitigation, no workaround, and no appeal. The landlord can refuse for any reason or no reason.

**What the model does:** Applies a 20% probability of lease failure and reduces expected NPV accordingly. At EUR 220K, the Monte Carlo shows conditional P(NPV>0) of 51.5% given lease success, versus unconditional P(NPV>0) of 40.2%.

**What the model gets wrong:** You do not invest EUR 350,000 in a deal with a 20% chance of total failure unless the expected return from the 80% success scenario is extraordinary. The model's base case delivers excellent returns IF the lease transfers. But the 20% failure case does not result in a 20% loss -- it results in near-total loss. Carisma would retain the option to negotiate a new lease directly with the landlord, but at terms that are likely worse than the current lease.

**The correct approach:** Do not invest a single euro until the landlord has provided written consent. Not verbal. Not "in principle." Written confirmation, with the existing lease terms preserved. This is the #1 condition precedent, and it is non-negotiable.

**The landlord's leverage:** The landlord knows that the lease cannot be assigned without consent. This gives the landlord leverage to:
- Demand higher rent as a condition of consent
- Require a personal guarantee from Carisma's directors
- Shorten the lease term
- Demand a larger security deposit
- Impose additional restrictions on use

Each of these outcomes degrades the deal economics. If the landlord demands rent 10% above the current schedule, that alone adds EUR 100K+ to the cumulative rent over the remaining term.

### 12.4 Post-Acquisition Priorities

**Months 1-3: Stabilize**
- Do nothing to staff, pricing, or operations
- Build trust with team, clients, and landlord
- Complete clinic regulatory pathway (Medical Director, MMA registration)
- Conduct detailed physical inspection of all equipment and wet areas
- Implement Carisma booking system and financial reporting

**Months 3-6: Optimize**
- Review pricing (target EUR 65-70 average revenue per appointment, up from EUR 57)
- Begin natural attrition -- do not replace departing staff
- Cross-train existing team for multi-service capability
- Launch clinic marketing (build pipeline before treatments begin)
- Establish Carisma brand presence (signage, marketing materials, social media transition)

**Months 6-12: Transform**
- Launch clinic services (laser optimization, add injectables if permitted)
- Implement efficiency improvements (scheduling optimization, product standardization)
- Consider voluntary restructuring if wage ratio remains above 44%
- Evaluate slimming service introduction (pilot basis, EUR 10K initial investment)
- First formal performance review against 100-Day Plan KPIs

**Months 12-18: Scale**
- Clinic at steady-state operations
- Wage ratio target: 42-44% (not 38% -- be realistic)
- Total revenue target: EUR 520,000-600,000 (spa + clinic)
- EBITDA target: EUR 80,000-100,000
- Decide on slimming service expansion based on pilot results

### 12.5 What Not To Do

| Action | Why To Avoid It |
|--------|----------------|
| **Restructure staff in Month 1-3** | TUPE protection period; destroys trust; triggers tribunal risk |
| **Cut prices to drive volume** | Revenue per appointment is already 31% below benchmark; cut further and EBITDA collapses |
| **Invest in slimming before clinic is proven** | Sequence the bets; clinic first, slimming only if clinic succeeds |
| **Reveal walk-away price to advisor** | Advisor's incentive is to close at the highest price; never disclose ceiling |
| **Route clinic revenue through separate entity without landlord discussion** | Creates lease breach risk; landlord could terminate for cause |
| **Sign LOI before landlord consent** | LOI creates momentum and expectations that are hard to reverse |
| **Assume 32.6% growth continues** | This is a Year 1 ramp effect, not organic growth |
| **Model wage ratio at 38% for Year 1** | 42-44% is realistic; 38% is aspirational |

**All nine conditions must be in the "Go" column.** A single "No-Go" on any of the top three items (price, landlord consent, permitted use) kills the deal.

### 13.3 My Final Verdict

**CONDITIONAL GO at EUR 200,000-220,000 with the conditions precedent enumerated above.**

This acquisition is worth pursuing because:
1. The local client base (78%, 5,827 appointments) is a genuine asset
2. The clinic equipment is already in place, reducing CAPEX by EUR 70-100K
3. The location (Sliema, street-level access) is strategically valuable
4. At EUR 200-220K, the downside is bounded (EUR 80-120K maximum loss in disaster scenario) while the upside is substantial (MOIC 2.5-4.5x in base case)
5. The deal price is validated by six independent valuation methodologies converging on EUR 183-220K

This acquisition is NOT worth pursuing at EUR 280K+ because:
1. Expected NPV turns negative above EUR 280K
2. The spa-only IRR drops below 15% (unacceptable for a single-asset, lease-dependent investment)
3. The deal becomes entirely dependent on clinic success -- speculation, not investment
4. The seller's BATNA is EUR 80-120K (portable assets at auction) -- Carisma has significantly more leverage than any analysis has acknowledged

### 13.4 The BCG Partner's Final Observation

In thirty years of advising on acquisitions, I have learned one thing above all else: **the price you pay is the only variable you fully control.** You cannot control the landlord. You cannot control the staff. You cannot control the market. You cannot control regulatory timelines. You can control the check you write.

At EUR 200-220K, this deal provides sufficient margin of safety to absorb the operational risks, regulatory delays, and market uncertainties that will inevitably materialize. At EUR 280K+, the margin of safety evaporates, and you are betting that everything goes right. In thirty years, everything has never gone right.

The seller invested EUR 857,884 and built a business that earns EUR 36,538. That is a 4.3% annual return on invested capital. The gap between what he spent and what the business produces is his loss, not Carisma's obligation. Carisma's job is not to make the seller whole. Carisma's job is to acquire a platform for value creation at a price that protects capital in the downside and generates exceptional returns in the base case.

That price is EUR 200,000-220,000. Not a euro more.
