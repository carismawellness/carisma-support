# Tactical Memo: INA Spa Acquisition — Handling the 1x Revenue Anchor

**From:** Senior Advisor
**To:** Carisma Wellness Group
**Re:** INA Spa — Valuation Gap Strategy

---

## 1. How Many Times I've Seen the "1x Revenue" Argument

Dozens of times. Every single time, it comes from the same place: a seller who built something over years, poured money into it, and is now watching someone offer less than it "should" be worth. The emotional logic is real even when the financial logic isn't.

**Deal 1 — A day spa in Valletta, 2019.** Seller had EUR 350K in revenue, EUR 15K EBITDA. Wanted EUR 350K. Justified it by saying "salons in London sell for 1x revenue." We pointed out that London salons run 20-30% EBITDA margins and Malta leases don't compare. Closed at EUR 190K plus a EUR 20K consultancy arrangement for the first year so the seller could tell people "I got EUR 210K." She needed the headline, not the structure. We gave her the headline.

**Deal 2 — A wellness clinic in Greece, 2021.** EUR 600K revenue, roughly breakeven, six years left on lease. Seller wanted EUR 500K. We spent two months trying to get her to EUR 320K. She wouldn't move. Deal died. Six months later she called back. We closed at EUR 290K. The business had declined during the negotiation. That silence is sometimes the most powerful tool you have.

**Deal 3 — A hotel spa in Croatia, 2022.** EUR 480K revenue, EBITDA of roughly EUR 30K after real add-backs. Seller anchored EUR 480K. Buyer was at EUR 250K. We closed at EUR 310K with a EUR 60K deferred payment tied to Year 2 revenue exceeding EUR 420K. The seller believed in his business enough to take the earnout. The buyer paid EUR 310K in cash and got comfortable with the deferred piece because the threshold was realistic. Seller ended up collecting EUR 370K total. Everyone walked away with a version of a win.

The pattern is always the same: sellers anchor on revenue because it's the number they know. They built that revenue. They are proud of that revenue. Your job is not to argue with their revenue. Your job is to redirect the conversation entirely.

---

## 2. The Exact Conversation

You sit across from the seller and you say something like this:

"Look, I want to be straight with you because I think you deserve that. The EUR 400K you're generating is real. That's not in question. But when someone buys a business, they're not buying last year's sales. They're buying future cash. They're buying what lands in their pocket after they pay rent, staff, supplies, and the hotel. Right now, what lands in your pocket is close to zero. You've worked hard for that revenue and ended up at breakeven. That's not your failure — that's the cost structure. Fourteen people for seven rooms is a lot of overhead. The hotel taking 5% off the top with no maintenance contribution — that's a real drag. So when I stand here and offer EUR 220K, I'm not saying your revenue isn't real. I'm saying what a buyer can afford to pay for a business that generates zero profit is limited by math, not opinion."

Then you pause. You let it sit. You don't rush to fill the silence.

If they push back — and they will — you continue:

"I've done this enough times to know that the hardest thing in selling a business is separating what you put in from what it's worth today. You put in EUR 800K in fit-out. That's real money. But fit-out doesn't transfer at cost. It transfers at what a buyer can recover through future earnings. At breakeven, recovery time is infinite. That's the problem we're both trying to solve."

---

## 3. The Restaurant Test

Tell them this:

"Imagine I own a restaurant. Last year I did EUR 400K in food sales. But my rent, staff, food costs and everything else also came to EUR 400K. I made nothing. Would you pay EUR 400K to buy that restaurant? You'd be paying EUR 400K for a job that pays you zero. The price of any business is always about the profit, not the sales. Sales just tell you how busy it is. Profit tells you what it's worth."

That's it. Thirty seconds. Most sellers have run something with thin margins. They understand this immediately when it's framed around their own experience.

---

## 4. If the Seller Holds at EUR 400K

You do not walk immediately. That's amateur. You go quiet.

You say: "I understand. Let me go back to my principal and see if there's a structure that works for both sides. I can't promise anything."

Then you wait two weeks. You don't call. You let the seller wonder.

In those two weeks, one of three things happens. They call you, which means their position has softened. They don't call, which means you call them at two weeks with a revised structure (see below). Or they find another buyer, which means the market has spoken and this deal was never yours.

The EUR 140K gap is too large to close through pure negotiation. You need structure. Walking is only the play if the deal genuinely doesn't work at any price — and with 13 years of lease and EUR 400K revenue, there's value here, it's just not where the seller thinks it is.

---

## 5. The Bridge Deal

Here is a structure that gives the seller a EUR 400K headline while keeping buyer economic exposure at EUR 265K.

**Headline Price:** EUR 400K

**Structure:**

- EUR 220K cash at closing. This is your anchor. Non-negotiable.
- EUR 45K deferred payment at Month 18, contingent on the business maintaining EUR 380K+ annualized revenue in the first 12 months post-close. If revenue falls below EUR 380K, this tranche is reduced proportionally. If it hits EUR 400K+, the full EUR 45K is paid.
- EUR 60K seller note at 3% over 36 months. Monthly payments of approximately EUR 1,750. Secured against the lease rights only, not Carisma's other assets. Note is subordinated to any future working capital facility.
- EUR 75K conditional earnout tied to EBITDA performance in Years 2 and 3. Specifically: EUR 25K per year for each year in which EBITDA exceeds EUR 50K. If the business never reaches EUR 50K EBITDA under new management, this tranche is never paid.

**Buyer's Real Exposure:**

- Day 1 cash out: EUR 220K
- Committed exposure (cash + note): EUR 265K
- Maximum theoretical exposure: EUR 400K, only if the seller's own revenue and profit claims prove accurate

**Why this works:** The seller gets to say "I sold for EUR 400K." They get that headline. In reality, they're collecting EUR 220K on day one, EUR 45K if the business holds, EUR 60K over three years, and EUR 75K only if the buyer turns it profitable. The seller's confidence in the business is the test. If they believe in EUR 400K revenue and future profitability, they should take this structure comfortably. If they balk, they're telling you something important about what they actually believe.

---

## 6. Honest Assessment

EUR 400K is achievable as a headline through structure. It is not achievable as cash consideration. Any buyer paying EUR 400K cash for a breakeven business with 14 staff on a revenue-share lease would be making an error.

The real number where this deal lives is EUR 260-280K in committed buyer exposure with structured upside to EUR 350-375K if the business performs. That range is defensible, financeable, and gives the seller meaningful participation in the upside they believe exists.

If the seller genuinely will not move below EUR 350K cash at closing, this deal does not work. Full stop. There is no debt service on EUR 350K when EBITDA is zero. The buyer would be underwater before the ink dries.

The question you need answered before your next meeting is this: does the seller need EUR 400K, or do they need to feel like they got EUR 400K? Those are two very different problems. The first one might kill the deal. The second one is solvable in an afternoon.

My read on this situation — two serious buyers, 13 years on the lease, a seller who has already had one conversation at EUR 200K without walking — is that the seller is not at EUR 400K. They're at EUR 340-360K and they're waiting to be convinced to accept less. The structure above gives them the conviction they need. Present it as your final position. Tell them it's fair. Mean it. And give them a week to decide.

---

*The deals that close at the right price are rarely the ones where both sides felt great on day one. They're the ones where both sides came to terms with reality together. That's your job here.*
