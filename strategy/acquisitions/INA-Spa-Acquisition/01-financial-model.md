### Framework: Embedded vs. Moveable Assets

Before the table: **a critical structural distinction** that most buyers overlook.

**Embedded/Non-transferable assets** (structural fit-out) have zero resale value to Carisma as a buyer. They exist in the property regardless of who occupies it. Carisma cannot take plumbing, tiled steam rooms, or custom woodwork with them if the deal falls through. These assets benefit Carisma by eliminating the capital expenditure required to replicate them — but they do not represent transferable value in a distressed sale. They are properly valued as "avoided capex" (see Section 5), not as an asset purchase. They are properly valued as "avoided capex", not as an asset purchase.

**Moveable assets** (equipment, furniture, devices) have genuine fair market value. They can be sold, relocated, or retained. These are what the buyer is actually acquiring in terms of physical assets.

## Section 3: Lease Value Analysis

### Framework

A lease signed at below-market rates is a financial asset. The holder of a 13-year lease at €5,000/month in a market where equivalent space costs €12,000/month is effectively receiving a €7,000/month subsidy. The NPV of that subsidy stream is real, transferable value.

This analysis uses:
- Assumed current market rate for comparable Malta wellness/commercial space: **€11,000/month** (midpoint of stated €10,000–€14,000 range)
- Discount rate: **10%** (appropriate for Malta commercial real estate, risk-adjusted)
- Remaining lease term: **13 years (156 months)**
- NPV formula: PV of annuity = A × [(1 - (1+r)^-n) / r], monthly compounding

### Analytical Notes on Lease Premium

**Low rent scenario (€5,000):** The lease is worth €509,000 in NPV terms on rent savings alone. This is a substantial hidden asset. If the owner does not understand this, it is a significant information asymmetry in favor of Carisma. Do not educate the seller.

**Mid rent scenario (€8,000):** The lease carries €254,000 in value. Even at this moderate savings level, the lease premium exceeds the distressed EBITDA value of the business itself.

**High rent scenario (€12,000):** The lease is at or above market. There is no lease premium — in fact, it represents a liability burden. Carisma would be assuming an above-market rent obligation. In this scenario, the acquisition rationale shifts almost entirely to (a) avoided fit-out capex, (b) Carisma's operational ability to extract materially higher revenue than current €22,500/month, and (c) strategic expansion of the medical aesthetics brand.

**Critical warning:** Verify that the lease is assignable. Many commercial leases in Malta require landlord consent for assignment or subletting. If the landlord must approve a transfer and can charge a premium for consent, the NPV figures above must be reduced by the consent cost. Obtain the full lease document before any price discussion.

## Section 4: Business Valuation — Distressed Asset EBITDA Multiple

### Methodology Notes

EBITDA multiple valuation is only meaningful when EBITDA is positive. When the underlying business is loss-making, the EBITDA multiple approach yields zero or negative enterprise value — which is analytically correct. A buyer purchasing a loss-making business with no clear path to profitability is not acquiring an asset; they are assuming a liability, and they should be compensated for that in the form of a lower purchase price, deferred consideration, or liability protections.

For distressed wellness/spa assets in a secondary European market:
- **1.0x EBITDA:** Reflects maximum risk, no management, declining trend, no brand premium
- **1.5x EBITDA:** Reflects partial stabilization assumption — buyer has a credible turnaround plan
- **2.0x EBITDA:** Reflects a business with clear operational issues but demonstrated peak performance upside (justified here given the €40K–€50K/month peak revenue evidence)

## Section 7: Key Financial Risks

### Risk 1: Hidden Liabilities — Unpaid Creditors and Arrears

Two years of declining management under Numi creates significant risk of unpaid supplier invoices (product suppliers, laundry services, maintenance contractors), utility arrears, and possibly outstanding VAT returns to the Malta Tax and Customs Administration (MTCA). These liabilities do not transfer automatically to a lease assignee, but they can create friction, legal complications, and time delays if suppliers pursue the business entity. Request a full creditor schedule and aged payables list. Ask for the last 4 VAT return submissions and payment confirmations. If any VAT is outstanding, the seller must settle before completion.

### Risk 2: Lease Assignability and Landlord Consent

This is the single highest-risk item. Many commercial leases in Malta, particularly those for hospitality or wellness spaces, contain assignment restrictions requiring landlord consent. The landlord may:
- Require a new personal or corporate guarantee
- Charge a consent fee (sometimes 3–6 months' rent)
- Use the assignment as an opportunity to renegotiate the rent to market rates
- Simply refuse if their interests are better served by a lease break

If the landlord can renegotiate on assignment, the entire lease premium value evaporates. This must be confirmed before any commercial terms are agreed. The lease document review is not optional.

### Risk 3: Equipment Loans and Chattel Mortgages

Medical aesthetics devices (laser, HIFU, IPL, RF equipment) are frequently financed through equipment leasing arrangements or chattel mortgages. A €200,000 capex line on aesthetics devices in 2022/2023 may still carry €80,000–€120,000 in outstanding finance. If Carisma acquires these devices without confirming they are unencumbered, they may be acquiring equipment that a finance company holds a security interest over. The finance company can repossess. Request certificates of title and any outstanding finance agreements for all equipment over €5,000 in value.

### Risk 4: Staff Liabilities — Back-Pay and Redundancy Obligations

The staff situation is unknown. Under Malta employment law (Employment and Industrial Relations Act), employees are entitled to notice periods, severance pay on redundancy, and accrued vacation pay. If the current operator (the entity that employed staff) has not paid full entitlements, and if Carisma acquires the business as a going concern (TUPE-equivalent transfer), it may inherit employment liabilities. Even in a pure lease assignment, if Carisma retains any current staff, it should conduct individual employment status checks. Worst case: two years of accrued entitlements unpaid across 8–12 staff members could represent €30,000–€80,000 in hidden liability.

### Risk 5: Regulatory and Licensing Compliance

Converting hair salon and nail areas into a medical aesthetics clinic requires Maltese Health Authority licensing. The Malta Medicines Authority and the Superintendent of Public Health regulate premises where Class III medical devices are used (lasers above certain thresholds, IPL, etc.). The cost and timeline for this licensing is a real constraint. If the conversion requires significant regulatory approval, Carisma should model a 6–9 month delay in the medical aesthetics revenue stream. Additionally, the existing spa must be compliant with Health Department inspections — two years of deteriorated management may mean compliance gaps that require remediation cost before re-opening.

### Risk 6: Deferred Maintenance and Infrastructure Condition

Hydrotherapy and steam infrastructure is maintenance-intensive. Pumps, jets, heating systems, water treatment, and steam generators require regular servicing. Two years of poor management suggests these systems may have deferred maintenance or damage that is not visible without inspection. Failure in water heating or hydrotherapy systems could require €20,000–€80,000 in emergency remediation before the spa is operational. Commission a full mechanical and engineering survey before acquisition. This cost should be factored into any price negotiation.

### Risk 7: Working Capital Requirement to Bridge to Profitability

A distressed takeover is not a day-one cash generator. Carisma must model the working capital requirement for the first 90–180 days: lease payments, staff costs for new hires and training, product restocking, marketing launch spend, and any remediation/fit-out costs for the medical aesthetics conversion. Conservative working capital requirement: **€60,000–€120,000** over the first 6 months before the business reaches EBITDA breakeven under Carisma management. This is cash that must be available at signing. Add this to the acquisition price to understand the true total cash outlay.

## Section 8: Due Diligence Financial Checklist

The following documents must be received, reviewed, and independently verified before any binding commercial terms are agreed. No exceptions.

### Financials — Business Performance

1. **24-month P&L statements** (month-by-month): Revenue by category (spa treatments, retail, medical aesthetics, hair, nails), broken down by revenue stream. Aggregate figures are insufficient — demand line-item detail.

2. **24-month bank statements** for all business bank accounts: Cross-reference against the P&L. Discrepancies between declared revenue and bank deposits suggest either unrecorded cash revenue or misreported figures. Both are red flags requiring explanation.

3. **Current balance sheet** and most recent audited accounts: Identifies all assets, liabilities, loans, and shareholder balances. If the business is an SRL (limited liability company), audited accounts are legally required in Malta for companies meeting certain thresholds.

4. **VAT returns — last 8 quarters**: Confirm all filings are submitted and paid. Request MTCA confirmation of compliance. Outstanding VAT is a priority creditor in insolvency and can become a Carisma liability depending on deal structure.

5. **Corporation tax returns and payment receipts — last 3 years**: Confirm tax compliance. Malta Corporation Tax is 35% with refund mechanisms; confirm no outstanding assessments.

6. **Aged creditors schedule**: Full list of outstanding supplier payables with aging (0–30, 31–60, 61–90, 90+ days). Any creditor outstanding more than 90 days indicates payment difficulty.

7. **Payroll records — last 24 months**: Confirm number of employees, salary levels, employment contracts, and that all Jobsplus filings and social security (NI) payments are current. Request NI payment receipts from the SISA (Social Insurance) authority.

### Lease and Property

8. **Full lease agreement — original signed document**: Review for: term, break clauses, rent review mechanisms (CPI-linked?), assignment and subletting clauses, permitted use restrictions (critical if adding medical aesthetics), landlord consent requirements, and personal guarantee obligations. Have a Maltese property lawyer review this document, not a general commercial lawyer.

9. **Lease payment history — last 24 months**: Confirm rent has been paid in full and on time. Any arrears with the landlord must be resolved before assignment. Request landlord confirmation in writing.

10. **Landlord's written consent to assignment in principle**: Before finalizing any deal price, obtain informal landlord confirmation that they are willing to engage on assignment. Without this, the entire transaction is contingent on a third party's approval that has not yet been obtained.

### Equipment and Assets

11. **Fixed asset register with purchase dates, purchase prices, and current book values**: Cross-reference against your independent equipment valuation. Identify any assets that appear on the register but cannot be physically located.

12. **Equipment finance and leasing agreements**: Full schedule of any outstanding loans, finance leases, or hire purchase agreements. Include lender names, outstanding balances, monthly payments, and security arrangements. Confirm which assets are unencumbered.

13. **Service and maintenance records for all major equipment**: Hydrotherapy systems, steam generators, HVAC, medical devices. Lapsed servicing voids manufacturer warranties and indicates deferred maintenance costs.

14. **Equipment calibration certificates for medical devices**: Laser and other Class III medical devices require periodic calibration and safety certification. Confirm all medical devices have valid calibration records. Non-calibrated devices cannot legally be used and may require expensive recommissioning.

### Regulatory and Compliance

15. **Malta Health Authority operating license(s)**: Confirm the spa holds a valid operating license. Confirm its transferability on change of operator.

16. **Business registration documents**: Warrant number, MBR (Malta Business Registry) registration, company structure, shareholder list, directorship, and any charges registered against the company.

17. **Insurance policies — current and lapsed**: Public liability, employers' liability, property/equipment. Confirm coverage has been maintained. Gaps in coverage during the period of decline are a risk indicator.

### Operational

18. **List of all current staff with employment contracts, salary, start date, and notice periods**: Enables accurate calculation of potential TUPE/transfer liabilities and redundancy costs.

19. **All supplier contracts and pricing agreements**: Identify any minimum purchase commitments, exclusivity arrangements, or auto-renewing contracts that would bind a new operator.

20. **Client database and booking system data**: Revenue quality check. Verify that stated revenue figures are supported by actual bookings and client records, not aggregated figures that could be manipulated.

## Final Recommendation

**The opportunity is real, but the rent figure determines everything.**

Before any commercial discussion, Carisma must obtain the actual rent figure. This single variable swings the fair value of the acquisition from €80,000 to €712,000. Entering a negotiation without knowing the rent is entering blind.

Once the rent is known:
- If rent is below €8,000/month: This is a genuine asset acquisition with lease premium value. Carisma should move quickly and negotiate a price in the €100,000–€200,000 range with strong liability protections.
- If rent is €8,000–€12,000/month: The acquisition rationale is primarily strategic (space, avoided capex, medical aesthetics expansion). The price should reflect the liability being assumed, not the asset value. Target €30,000–€80,000 with robust due diligence protections.
