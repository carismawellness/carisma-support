# SECTION 1: Pre-Meeting Mindset & Positioning

## 1.1 — The Power Reversal: Behavioral Guidelines

He called you. This single fact changes everything. In M&A, the initiating party almost always concedes more — not because of weakness per se, but because initiation reveals motivation, and motivation is leverage.

Here are five specific behavioral guidelines for carrying this advantage into the room:

**Guideline 1: Arrive Unhurried, Leave First**

Walk in without visible urgency. Do not check your watch. Do not have your phone on the table. But set a natural endpoint: "I have another meeting at 3, so I want to make sure we use our time well." This communicates that your calendar is full — that you are someone with options and competing demands. When the meeting ends, you leave on your schedule, not when the conversation has exhausted itself. An exhausted conversation is a negotiation that ran away from you.

**Guideline 2: Speak Slowly, Pause Deliberately**

Anxious people talk fast. Powerful people pause. When he asks you a direct question — particularly one about money or your interest level — do not answer immediately. Take a breath. Look slightly downward as if genuinely thinking. Then answer. This is not a performance; it is a signal that you are choosing your words because they matter, not because you are eager to please.

**Guideline 3: Use the Late-Night FM DJ Voice (Voss)**

Chris Voss describes a specific tone for high-stakes moments: calm, slow, slightly downward-inflecting. Not cold — warm, but measured. This voice says "I'm comfortable here. Nothing you say surprises me. I've seen this before." Reserve it specifically for when he talks about money or makes a counteroffer. The contrast with his emotional energy will be striking.

**Guideline 4: Do Not Jump to Fill Silence**

If he says something uncomfortable — a high number, an accusation, a challenge — resist the human instinct to fill the silence. Nod slightly. Maintain eye contact. Let the silence breathe for three to five full seconds before responding. In a negotiation, silence almost always benefits the listener. The speaker, feeling the pressure of silence, will often add more, soften their position, or qualify their statement. Give him that opportunity.

**Guideline 5: Respond to Proposals With Questions, Not Reactions**

Whatever number or demand he puts on the table, your first response should never be a counter-proposal. It should be a calibrated question. "How did you arrive at that?" or "What does that number need to accomplish for you?" This keeps you in discovery mode longer, extracts his reasoning, and signals that you evaluate proposals carefully rather than react emotionally.

## 1.2 — The Three Frames: How to Communicate Them Without Stating Them

**Frame 1: "I am evaluating an interesting opportunity — I have not decided to buy."**

How to communicate this subtly: In your opening remarks, use language of exploration, not acquisition. Say things like "I wanted to come understand the situation better" rather than "I'm here to talk about buying." Reference the mutual contact naturally — "When [name] mentioned this to me, I was curious to learn more." The word "curious" is doing heavy lifting here. Curiosity signals interest without commitment. You are an investigator, not a suitor.

Throughout the meeting, introduce conditionals: "If the numbers work..." or "Depending on what I find in due diligence..." These phrases keep you in the evaluator's seat. He is the one seeking approval; you are the one deciding whether to grant it.

**Frame 2: "My time is valuable. This must work for my business, not just his exit needs."**

How to communicate this subtly: Early in the conversation, briefly reference your current operational pressures — not as complaints, but as context. "We're expanding the aesthetics side, which is actually what got me interested in this conversation. We need space, and we're evaluating a few options." This establishes that you have needs of your own, that this acquisition must serve your strategy, and that you are not doing him a favor by showing up.

You reinforce this frame whenever you ask questions about the space's practical fitness: "How is the ventilation in the treatment rooms? We have specific equipment requirements for medical aesthetics." These questions signal that your needs are the filter through which this deal will be evaluated — not his asking price.

**Frame 3: "I am a fair buyer. I will not overpay, but I will pay fairly. If we can't align on fair value, I move on — calmly."**

How to communicate this subtly: This frame is best established early with a simple, honest statement that preempts defensiveness: "I want to be straightforward with you from the start — I'm not here to lowball you or waste your time. I'll tell you exactly how I think about value, and if we can find a number that's fair to both of us, I'd like to move quickly. If we can't, I'd rather know now than have either of us spend months on something that won't close."

This lands as integrity. It is also extraordinarily clever, because it frames low offers as "fair value" and frames the conversation as a shared quest for fairness — not an adversarial negotiation over price.

## 1.3 — BATNA Asymmetry: How to Surface It Without Stating It

Your BATNA is real: you can find alternative commercial space in St. Julian's or Sliema. This is not a bluff. His BATNA is catastrophic: he holds a 13-year lease obligation on a bleeding asset with no visible exit.

You never say any of this directly. That would be cruel and would shut him down. Instead, you surface it structurally through the conversation.

**Method 1: The Casual Reference to Alternatives**

Early in the meeting, mention without emphasis: "We've been looking at a few different options for expansion. A couple of spaces have come up in Sliema, one in Valletta. Nothing that's quite right yet, but we're not in a rush." This plants the seed that you have alternatives and no timeline pressure. One sentence. Drop it. Move on. Do not elaborate.

**Method 2: The Empathic Question About His Ongoing Situation**

Later, after he has opened up: "I'm curious — how are you managing the fixed costs in the meantime? Is the business covering them, or..." Let him finish the sentence. When he acknowledges he is losing money monthly, nod slowly and say nothing immediately. Let that reality sit in the room. Then: "That's a difficult position to be in." Full stop. You are not twisting the knife — you are simply acknowledging what is true. But the acknowledgment makes the truth more vivid for him.

**Method 3: The Timeline Contrast**

At some point in Act 2 or 3, when the conversation gets to numbers: "The reason I'm being deliberate about the number is that this has to make sense for us long-term. We're not under pressure to move on any particular timeline — so I'd rather take the time to get it right than rush into something." 

You have just told him, without saying it, that you have all the time in the world. He does not.

# SECTION 2: Meeting Structure — The Four Acts

## ACT 1: Rapport and Intelligence Gathering (First 20-30 Minutes)

**Objective:** Make him feel heard and comfortable. Extract maximum intelligence before revealing any position. Never give him information he can use against you before you have information you can use.

### The Opening Move

When you sit down, resist the urge to get to business quickly. Spend the first five minutes in genuine human connection. Ask about him. How did he end up in the spa business? Was the Malta operation always part of the plan? This is not wasted time — it is intelligence gathering of the most valuable kind: who is this person, what does he care about, what does he regret?

### Chris Voss Techniques for This Phase

*Mirroring:* When he says something significant, repeat the last two to three words as a slight upward-inflecting question. He says "It's been really frustrating, especially since the Numi situation fell apart..." You say: "...since it fell apart?" Then wait. He will explain. People cannot resist explaining themselves to someone who seems genuinely curious.

*Labeling:* Identify and name his emotional state. "It sounds like this situation has taken a real toll on you." Or: "It seems like you put a tremendous amount of yourself into building this place." Labels, done right, make people feel deeply understood — and people who feel understood open up.

*Calibrated Questions:* Every question you ask should be open-ended and start with "what" or "how." Never "why" — why questions sound accusatory. "What's been the shift since the Numi partnership ended?" is better than "Why did the Numi partnership fail?"

### The 10 Intelligence-Extraction Questions

**Question 1: "What's the backstory on the space? How did INA come to be?"**

*Intelligence extracted:* His emotional investment and identity tied to the business. How much pride is wrapped up in this. Whether he built it from scratch or inherited a concept.

*Follow-up if he opens up:* "That sounds like an enormous undertaking. What was the original vision for it?" (Deepens rapport, extracts the gap between vision and reality.)

*Follow-up if he deflects:* "I ask because understanding what was built here helps me see what the real asset is — beyond just the physical space." (Reframes the question as being in his interest to answer.)

---

**Question 2: "What's been the biggest challenge with the space since the partnership with Numi ended?"**

*Intelligence extracted:* His pain level. The operational impact of the Numi departure. Whether there are ongoing contractual complications. How long he has been in distress.

*Follow-up if he opens up:* "How long ago did that situation fully resolve itself?" (Establishes the timeline of his pain. The longer he has been bleeding, the more urgent his need to exit.)

*Follow-up if he deflects:* "I ask because lease spaces like this can sometimes carry over complications from prior arrangements — I just want to make sure there's nothing that would complicate a clean transfer." (Frames it as due diligence, not nosiness.)

---

**Question 3: "What does the current revenue picture look like on a monthly basis?"**

*Intelligence extracted:* Actual top-line numbers. Gap between his stated figure and reality. Whether he is forthcoming or evasive with financial information.

*Follow-up if he opens up:* "And what's driving most of that — is it regular clients, walk-ins, hotel guests?" (Helps you understand the sustainability and transferability of the revenue base.)

*Follow-up if he gives a vague answer:* "I ask because when I put numbers together, the current run rate is the foundation — I want to make sure I'm working from real figures, not assumptions." (Frames accuracy as being in his interest.)

---

**Question 4: "To understand the full picture of the fixed obligations — what does the lease cost per month?"**

*Intelligence extracted:* THE MOST IMPORTANT NUMBER. Monthly rent determines whether the deal is viable at any price. High rent + low revenue = unprofitable operation no matter what you pay to acquire it.

*Follow-up if he answers directly:* "And how long is left on the term?" (Establish the full lease liability he is trying to exit — and that you would be assuming.)

*Follow-up if he hesitates:* "It's a standard question in any space evaluation — I need to make sure the fixed cost structure makes sense for how we'd run it." (Normalizes the question.)

---

**Question 5: "What would your ideal outcome from this conversation look like — what would 'success' look like for you personally?"**

*Intelligence extracted:* His must-haves versus nice-to-haves. Whether he primarily needs money, or whether he primarily needs to exit the lease obligation. Sometimes sellers care more about escaping an obligation than about the sale price — and knowing this is a significant negotiating advantage.

*Follow-up if he focuses on money:* "And is there a timeline by which you'd want to have that resolved?" (Reveals urgency.)

*Follow-up if he focuses on exiting the lease:* "Understood — so the primary thing is getting this off your plate, and the financial component is secondary?" (Clarify the hierarchy of his needs. If exit from obligation is primary, you can potentially negotiate a lower price in exchange for taking on the lease.)

---

**Question 6: "What's your ideal timeframe for resolving this situation?"**

*Intelligence extracted:* His urgency level. The more specific his timeline, the more pressure he is under. "As soon as possible" is very different from "within the year."

*Follow-up if he names a specific month:* "What's driving that timing?" (There is usually a specific catalyst — a personal financial deadline, a lease review date, a personal situation. Finding the catalyst tells you how hard his deadline actually is.)

*Follow-up if he is vague:* "I ask because my process involves some diligence steps that take time — I want to make sure our timelines are compatible." (Sets the expectation that you will be thorough, not rushed.)

---

**Question 7: "Have you had other conversations with parties who might be interested?"**

*Intelligence extracted:* Your competitive position. Whether there are real competing buyers (rare in a distressed situation like this, but possible). Even the absence of other conversations is informative — it confirms you are his primary option.

*If he says yes:* "And have those gone anywhere?" — Do not react. Probe calmly. If there were serious buyers, he would likely already have a deal. The fact that he is talking to you suggests either those conversations stalled or they were not serious.

*If he says no:* "Okay. That's helpful context." Leave it there. The information is noted.

---

**Question 8: "How is the Numi situation fully resolved at this point? Any ongoing contractual connections there?"**

*Intelligence extracted:* Legal clarity on whether a prior partner has any residual claims on the business, equipment, or lease. A messy Numi departure could mean there are unresolved contracts, disputes, or equipment ownership questions that would complicate a clean acquisition.

*Follow-up:* "And all the equipment that was brought in — is it clearly owned by INA, or is any of it on a lease or financing arrangement?" (This opens the equipment ownership conversation naturally.)

---

**Question 9: "What's the relationship like with the building owner? Is that a straightforward relationship?"**

*Intelligence extracted:* Landlord consent risk. In Malta, commercial lease assignments typically require landlord approval. If the landlord is hostile, demanding, or likely to extract a significant premium for consent, the deal economics change. This question also reveals whether the seller has thought through the complexity of a lease assignment.

*Follow-up:* "Would you expect any complications there, or is it more of a formality?" (His answer tells you how much of a friction point this is likely to be.)

---

**Question 10: "The hair salon area and the nail space — what's the current situation with those? Are they operational?"**

*Intelligence extracted:* The utilization and condition of the most obvious value-add opportunities. If those spaces are non-operational and in poor condition, they depress the value of the overall acquisition. But they also give you genuine upside arguments when you eventually discuss your strategy — which can be used to justify a lower purchase price ("we're inheriting underperforming assets that need investment").

*Follow-up:* "What would it take to bring those up to operating standard, in your estimate?" (Make him calculate the investment required — not you. His own estimate, even if low, becomes a useful reference point in the price conversation.)

## ACT 2: Reframing the Value Conversation (20-30 Minutes)

**Objective:** Move the conversation from sunk cost to forward-looking value. This is the most critical intellectual work of the meeting.

### The Sunk Cost Redirect Script

He will say it. It is almost guaranteed. Some version of: "I put over a million euros into this place. I can't just walk away for nothing."

This moment is the entire hinge of the negotiation. Handle it wrong, and you will spend the next two hours fighting about a number that has no bearing on the deal's economics. Handle it right, and you fundamentally reframe what a "fair" price means.

Here is the complete script:

---

*He says:* "Look, I need you to understand — I've invested over a million euros in this space. The fit-out alone was enormous. The equipment. The branding. Everything."

*You say:*

"I hear you. And I want to acknowledge that directly, because I know what I'm going to walk you through today might feel like it doesn't fully honor what you've built here. And that would be a hard thing to sit with after everything you've put in.

So let me be upfront about something before we get into numbers.

The way I think about value in any business I look at — and this isn't specific to you, it's how every serious buyer approaches it — is forward-looking. Not backward-looking. The investment you made was real. The commitment was real. The vision behind it was real. And none of that disappears just because the market moved in a difficult direction.

But here's the honest truth, and I think you already know this: what a business is worth today isn't determined by what it cost to build. It's determined by what it's generating now, what it can generate with the right operator, and what the fixed obligations look like against that.

I'm not saying this to be unkind. I'm saying it because if we're going to find a number that makes sense — one that you can actually do a deal on — it has to be grounded in what the business is worth to me as an operator going forward. Not what it cost to create.

Now — with that said — I do think there's a real deal here. I think the space has genuine potential. And I want to show you how I'm looking at it."

---

*If he pushes back:* "I understand that framework intellectually, but emotionally it's very hard to—"

*You respond:* "Of course it is. That's completely fair. This is not just a financial transaction for you — it's something you built. I get that. And the right deal here isn't one where you feel taken advantage of. It's one where you feel like you made a sensible decision given the circumstances. That's what I want us to get to."

*Then pause. Let him settle. Then:* "Can I show you the numbers I've been working from, and you tell me where you think I'm off?"

This last line is critical. You are inviting him to participate in the analysis — which moves the conversation from adversarial to collaborative.

---

### The Equipment Valuation Conversation

He will price equipment at purchase cost. A laser that cost €200,000 new three years ago is not worth €200,000. It is worth its depreciated market value — likely 30-50% of that, depending on model, condition, and demand in the secondary market.

Here is the complete script for this specific sub-conversation:

---

*When equipment comes up:*

"Equipment is an interesting one, and I want to handle it properly — meaning in a way that's defensible for both of us.

The challenge with pricing equipment is that purchase cost and market value are two very different things. Not because the equipment isn't good — but because commercial spa and medical aesthetics equipment depreciates quickly, and the secondary market reflects that. A laser that cost €200,000 might be worth €60,000-80,000 in a properly conducted appraisal, because that's what a buyer in the open market would pay for it.

I don't want to just assert that number to you — that wouldn't be fair. What I'd like to propose is that we bring in a qualified equipment appraiser to do a formal assessment. We'd split the cost, or I'd cover it entirely. The result gives us both a number we can defend. If it comes in higher than what I'm expecting, I'll incorporate it. If it's in the range I'm thinking, then we both have independent confirmation of value.

Does that seem like a reasonable way to handle it?"

---

*If he says "but that laser cost me €200,000":*

"I completely understand that. And for what it's worth, I believe you — a good system at that specification would have cost that. The question isn't what it cost; the question is what a qualified buyer in today's market would pay for it. Those are two different things, and they're different for a specific reason: equipment like this has a defined useful life, and the market reflects that. The appraiser will tell us both exactly where it sits."

*Key move:* You have proposed a neutral third party. This is from Fisher and Ury — when two parties disagree on a fact, introduce an objective standard. The appraiser is not your advocate; he is a neutral standard. This makes it very difficult for the seller to reject the outcome without looking unreasonable.

---

### The Business Valuation Framework Conversation

At some point in Act 2, you need to introduce the concept of EBITDA-based valuation without using jargon that makes his eyes glaze over.

*Script:*

"The way businesses in hospitality and wellness are typically valued — and this is pretty standard across the industry — is based on earnings. Specifically, what the business generates after you strip out the costs of running it. The reasoning is simple: a buyer is paying for future cash flow. So the value is a multiple of what you can reasonably expect that cash flow to be.

In a stable, well-run spa operation, you might see multiples of three to five times annual earnings. In a distressed situation — where revenue has been declining, operations are in transition, and there's uncertainty about the forward trajectory — multiples compress. Buyers are being compensated for the risk they're taking on.

The reason I walk you through this isn't to lecture you — it's because when I give you my number later, I want you to understand exactly how I got there. Not as a take-it-or-leave-it, but as a starting point for a real conversation."

## ACT 3: The Offer — Anchoring and Negotiation (20-30 Minutes)

**Objective:** Drop your anchor, justify it, and begin the concession dance toward your target.

### 3.1 — The Anchor Drop

Never ask "what are you looking for?" You drop the anchor. This is non-negotiable. Whoever sets the first number establishes the gravitational field of the negotiation. Research consistently shows that first anchors have disproportionate influence on final outcomes, even when they are known to be extreme.

Here is the complete anchor-drop script:

---

*Set up the framework first:*

"Based on everything I've been able to assess — the current revenue run rate, the operational situation, the equipment, and what the space would need to be fully functional for our purposes — I want to give you a number. And I want to walk you through exactly how I got there, because I think that's more useful than just throwing a figure out.

Current monthly revenue is approximately €20,000-22,000. On an annual basis, that's roughly €240,000-260,000. After rent, staff, consumables, and basic overhead, I'm estimating the business is operating at roughly breakeven or a small loss at current volumes. That means the EBITDA is essentially zero or negative.

In a distressed acquisition where EBITDA is minimal, the value framework shifts to asset value — primarily the lease assignment and the equipment. The lease has significant years remaining, which has value, but that value is qualified by the rent obligation and the landlord consent requirement. The equipment value I'd want confirmed by a third-party appraisal.

Taking all of that into account — and recognizing that we'd be investing substantially to bring the underutilized areas up to operating standard — I'd be looking at this in the range of €40,000 to €50,000 as a starting point."

*Then stop talking. Put the number on the table. Look at him calmly. Do not qualify it further. Do not apologize for it. Do not immediately pivot to "but obviously there's room to move." Silence.*

The silence after the anchor is where most buyers lose their nerve and give ground before the seller has even responded. Hold it.

---

### 3.2 — Handling His Reactions

**Reaction A: He laughs or is visibly offended**

*He says:* "That's nowhere near what this is worth. Come on. The fit-out alone cost more than that."

*You respond* (in the late-night DJ voice, calm, unhurried):

"I hear you. And I expected that reaction — which is exactly why I walked you through the framework before giving you the number. I'm not suggesting the fit-out isn't valuable. What I'm saying is that in a business acquisition, value is determined by earnings potential and asset realization, not construction cost. Those are genuinely different things.

But I'm not asking you to accept my number. I'm asking you: where do you think I'm wrong in the analysis? Because if there's something I'm missing — revenue I don't have visibility on, equipment that's been recently serviced, a landlord arrangement that makes the lease more transferable — I want to understand that. Show me what I'm not seeing."

*The move:* You have not moved off your number. You have not defended it emotionally. You have invited him to show you data that would justify a higher number. This is brilliant because: (a) it signals you are data-driven, not arbitrary; (b) it puts the burden of proof on him; (c) it keeps the conversation collaborative rather than adversarial.

---

**Reaction B: He counters with a high number (e.g., €600,000-800,000)**

*He says:* "I've had independent people look at this and value the lease and equipment at €700,000."

*You respond:*

"I'd love to see that analysis. Genuinely — if there's a valuation that shows me something I'm not seeing, I want to understand it. What's it based on?"

*Let him explain. Then:*

"So the lease value they're attributing — is that net of the rent obligation? Because a 13-year lease that costs €X per month isn't pure value — it's a liability if the revenue doesn't cover it. The value of the lease is the surplus it generates over market rent, if any. And on the equipment — is that at purchase cost or at depreciated market rate?"

*The move:* You are not rejecting his number. You are examining its methodology. Almost certainly, any informal valuation he has received was done by someone using purchase cost for equipment and face value for the lease — neither of which reflects market reality. Asking him to explain the methodology reveals its weaknesses without you having to argue about it directly.

---

**Reaction C: He goes quiet or says "I need to think about it"**

*He says:* "That's... lower than I was expecting. I need to think about it."

*You respond:* "Of course. Take the time you need." 

*Then after five seconds of silence:* "Can I ask you something while we're here? When you picture what a deal would look like — not specifically a number, but what it would need to accomplish for you — what's the most important thing? Is it the total amount? How it's structured? Getting it done within a certain timeframe?"

*The move:* His "I need to think about it" is not a rejection. It is shock. The question you ask redirects the conversation to his underlying needs, which gives you information about how to restructure the offer in a way that might satisfy him even at a number close to yours.

---

### 3.3 — The Ackermann Concession Sequence

The Ackermann method (Voss/FBI) prescribes structured concessions that signal you are approaching your limit. The math: start at 65% of your target, then move to 85%, 95%, and finally 100%.

If your target is €150,000, the Ackermann sequence is:

| Move | Percentage | Amount | Conditional On |
|---|---|---|---|
| Anchor | Starting position | €40,000-50,000 | Nothing — this is your opening anchor |
| Move 1 | 65% of target | €97,500 | "If the equipment appraisal supports a higher value than I estimated, AND we get written confirmation the landlord will approve the assignment without additional cost, I could stretch to..." |
| Move 2 | 85% of target | €127,500 | "If you can provide three years of financial records confirming revenue trajectory, and the rent figure you've given me is confirmed in writing, I could come to..." |
| Move 3 | 95% of target | €142,500 | "We're getting close. I think I can get to..." |
| Final | 100% with precise odd number | €148,750 | "This is my best number. I want to be straightforward with you — this is where I land when I've done all the math." |

**Critical notes on the Ackermann method:**

First, each concession is smaller than the last. The gap between moves decreases: €57,500 → €30,000 → €15,000 → €6,250. The narrowing gaps signal to him that you are genuinely approaching your ceiling.

Second, the final number is deliberately non-round (€148,750 rather than €150,000). Non-round numbers signal precision — they suggest you have done specific calculations to arrive there, rather than picked a round number arbitrarily. Voss calls this "throwing in a piece of furniture" — the odd number makes it feel final.

Third, every concession is conditional. You are not simply moving up; you are moving up in exchange for something — information, documentation, agreement on a term. Each conditional extracts value.

**Concession scripts:**

*Move 1 script:*
"I've been sitting with the numbers since we talked about the equipment and the lease terms. If we can get an independent equipment appraisal within the next two weeks, and if the landlord confirms in writing that the assignment is straightforward, I could get to €97,500. That's a meaningful move from where I started, and it's contingent on those two things being resolved in the way we've discussed."

*Move 2 script:*
"I've looked at this again with my team. If you can share three years of financial statements — and I understand the numbers have been difficult, I'm not expecting anything you don't have — and if the rent figure is confirmed in the lease document, I could come to €127,500. I want to make sure I'm not building my model on assumptions that turn out to be wrong."

*Move 3 script:*
"We're close. I think we can get this done. I can get to €142,500. At this point I need to ask you directly: is there something structurally in the deal — payment terms, timing, how we handle the handover — that would make the difference between yes and no? Because I'd rather find that solution than let this fall apart over something we could solve."

*Final move script:*
"I've pushed this as far as I can justify to myself and my partners. €148,750. That's my best number, arrived at honestly. I want to do this deal. If you can get there, let's do it."

## ACT 4: Closing or Graceful Exit (10-15 Minutes)

### The Trial Close

When the price gap has narrowed to a workable range — when you sense he is tired of the back-and-forth and is orienting toward yes — deploy the trial close.

*Script:*

"Let me ask you something directly. It sounds like if we could land somewhere around [TARGET RANGE], we'd have the foundation of a deal. Am I reading that right?"

*Critical note:* The trial close is phrased as a question, not a statement. It is also calibrated to his emotional state — "it sounds like" is a soft label. You are testing without committing. If he says yes, you have a deal in principle. If he says no, you have more information about what the gap is.

*If he says yes:* "Good. Let me tell you what I'd need to move to the next step — I'd want to do a formal due diligence review: financials, lease documents, equipment list, staff arrangements. Nothing invasive. Standard process. Can we set a timeline for that?"

*If he hedges:* "What would need to be different for you to feel good about it?" (Back to calibrated questions.)

---

### The Graceful Exit

If you have not reached agreement and the meeting is ending without resolution:

*Script (use the late-night DJ voice for this):*

"I think we've covered a lot of important ground today. I'm genuinely interested in this space — I wouldn't be here if I weren't. And I want to be honest with you: I can't get to a number that would make you whole on your investment. What I can do is offer a fair price for what the business is generating today and what it represents strategically for us.

I'm going to take some time to review everything we've discussed. I know you need to think about it too. The one thing I'd offer for you to consider in the meantime — not as pressure, just as context — is that every month that goes by with the space in its current situation has a cost. And the calculation that I'm doing in the meantime, that other operators in this space are also doing. Space in Malta is getting harder to find, and options like this don't stay available forever.

I'll reach out in a few days. If something shifts in your thinking before then, you have my number."

*What this script does:*
- "I'm genuinely interested" — reassures him you have not walked away
- "I can't make you whole" — honest, and disarms a future demand
- "Every month that goes by has a cost" — plants the loss aversion seed without being crude
- "Other operators are also doing this calculation" — Cialdini's social proof, without a bluff
- "You have my number" — you leave the initiative with him, which subtly reinforces that he needs you

---

### The Deliberately Slow Close

Even if he accepts your number in this meeting, do not close today. Here is why.

First, any capitulation in the first meeting almost always means you anchored too high. If he says yes immediately, your anchor was not low enough. Slowing down gives you time to reconsider whether you have left money on the table.

Second, he will feel buyer's remorse if you close too quickly. Sellers who feel railroaded develop second thoughts, find reasons to reopen, bring in advisors who reverse course. A close that took three weeks and felt earned will stick; a close that took ninety minutes will fray.

Third, the due diligence process itself is a negotiating tool. Things you discover in due diligence — deferred maintenance, undisclosed liabilities, revenue that was overstated — all become legitimate grounds for price adjustment. The only way to use due diligence as a negotiating lever is to not have already closed.

Fourth, introducing your professional advisor (accountant or solicitor) into the next meeting shifts the dynamic. You are no longer two people negotiating; you are a principal and their team evaluating a transaction. This is more formal, more serious, and more difficult for him to emotionally override.

*Script if he accepts too quickly:*

"I'm glad we're in the same territory. Before we shake hands on anything, I want to make sure we do this properly — for both our sakes. I'll have my solicitor reach out to set up a formal due diligence period. It's standard process, and it protects you as much as it protects me. Once that's complete, if everything is as we've discussed, we can move to formal documents."

You have not rejected his acceptance. You have institutionalized it. The deal feels alive; you have just introduced a buffer that gives you time and leverage.

# SECTION 3: Psychological Levers — Full Playbook

## 3.1 — Loss Aversion (Kahneman)

Kahneman's research shows that the pain of loss is approximately twice as powerful as the pleasure of equivalent gain. He is already losing. The question is whether that loss is vivid and visceral to him — or whether he is numbing himself to it.

**The "Cost of Waiting" Framing**

You do not tell him how much he is losing. You ask him to calculate it himself. Calculations you make feel like attacks; calculations he makes feel like realizations.

*Script:*

"I'm curious about something — what does the current situation cost you on a monthly basis, all in? If you factor in the rent, the staffing, the utilities, and compare it to what the revenue is covering..."

Let him do the math. If the rent is €8,000/month and the business is generating €20,000 while costing €22,000+ to operate, he is bleeding €2,000-€5,000+ per month. Over twelve months, that is €24,000-€60,000. He already knows this. But saying it out loud, doing the math in front of you, makes it real in a different way.

Then, softly: "And over the remaining term of the lease — what does that add up to, if things don't change?"

Do not answer the question. Let him sit with it. This is not cruelty; it is honesty. The most compassionate thing you can do for him is help him see that the status quo is not a neutral position — it is an active and accelerating loss.

---

## 3.2 — Social Proof and Scarcity (Cialdini)

Cialdini established that people evaluate options partly based on what others think of them. If you are interested, implying that others are also interested — or that you have alternatives — creates urgency without explicit pressure.

**Script for implying alternatives:**

"I'll be transparent with you — we're not exclusively focused on INA. There are a couple of other spaces we're evaluating in the region, one of which we have a meeting about next week. I mention that not as pressure — it's just the reality that we need to make a decision within a reasonable timeframe, and we'll do that based on whichever opportunity makes the most sense."

*Key notes:* This is not a bluff if it is true (and you should make it true — genuinely look at one other space). It should be delivered in a matter-of-fact tone, not as a threat. You are informing, not warning. The information lands as social proof (others value space in this area) and scarcity (you may not be available indefinitely).

---

## 3.3 — Strategic Silence

Research in negotiation psychology shows that most people become deeply uncomfortable with silence after approximately four to six seconds and will begin talking to fill it. The person who talks to fill silence almost always gives ground — they explain, qualify, or soften their position without any pressure from the other side.

**How to hold silence:**

After dropping a number or asking a key question, stop. Breathe slowly. Maintain eye contact or look slightly down at your notepad. Do not look at your phone. Do not pick up your coffee. Do not arrange papers. Just be still.

Seven seconds is the inflection point. Most people break before seven seconds. If you can hold silence for seven seconds after delivering a number or a key observation, you will find that the other person almost always responds — and that response is almost always more revealing than whatever you would have said.

**What to do if he tries to make you fill the silence:**

He might look at you expectantly. He might say "so... what do you think?" or "well?" In that case: "I'm just taking a moment to think about what you've said." Then silence again. This is not rudeness; it is deliberateness. And deliberateness signals power.

---

## 3.4 — The Landlord's Consent Variable

Any assignment of a commercial lease in Malta will require landlord consent. This is a legitimate variable that creates genuine uncertainty — and genuine uncertainty is a legitimate reason to be cautious about price.

*Script for introducing this variable:*

"One thing I'll need to get comfortable with before we can finalize anything is the lease assignment process. My solicitor will need to review the original lease to understand the consent provisions — some leases have language that makes assignment straightforward, others create friction or give the landlord leverage to renegotiate terms. Do you know offhand what the lease says about that?"

*What this does:*

First, it is a legitimate concern — you genuinely need this information. Second, it introduces an external variable that is outside your control, which you can use to justify caution about price. Third, it subtly reminds him that the lease is not simply his to transfer — it requires a third party's cooperation. And that third party is not his ally.

If the landlord is known to be cooperative, this variable relaxes. If the landlord is unknown or potentially difficult, you have a real reason to compress your offer: "I need to build in some cushion for uncertainty around the lease transfer."

# SECTION 5: Red Lines and Deal-Breakers

### Red Line 1: Rent Above €10,000/Month Combined With Any Meaningful Asking Price

**Trigger condition:** Monthly rent is confirmed at €10,000 or above, AND the seller is anchored above €250,000.

**How to identify it in the meeting:** The rent figure comes out in Act 1. At €10,000/month, the annual fixed rent obligation is €120,000. Against revenue of €240,000-260,000, the margin available to cover all other costs AND service any acquisition price is extremely thin.

**How to exit gracefully:** "I appreciate the time we've spent today, and I don't want to waste yours. With a rent obligation at that level, the numbers become very tight for us given current revenue. I'd need to see a clear path to significantly higher revenue before the acquisition could make sense. That's not impossible — but it's a bigger bet than I'm comfortable making right now. Let me sit with it."

This leaves the door open (you may revisit if rent is negotiated down or revenue improves) without committing to a dead deal.

---

### Red Line 2: Undisclosed Debts or Liens on Equipment

**Trigger condition:** During due diligence, equipment is found to be subject to financing arrangements, leases, or the business has outstanding debts not disclosed in the meeting.

**How to identify it in the meeting:** Ask directly in Act 2 about financing arrangements on equipment and any liabilities attached to the business. Watch for hesitation, vagueness, or sudden subject-changing.

**How to exit gracefully:** "Before we go further, I need everything that's financially attached to the business to be disclosed. If there are obligations I don't know about, it changes the analysis significantly. Can we make sure my solicitor gets a complete picture of any outstanding financing on the equipment and any creditor obligations? If everything comes back clean, great. If there are items we haven't discussed, I'd rather know now than after we've invested time on both sides."

---

### Red Line 3: Landlord Consent Is Uncertain, Costly, or Involves Renegotiated Rent

**Trigger condition:** The landlord is known to be uncooperative, has previously refused assignments, or is likely to use consent as leverage to renegotiate rent upward.

**How to identify it in the meeting:** Ask about the landlord relationship in Act 2. If the seller is vague, nervous, or says something like "the landlord is a bit difficult" or "we've had some tension" — this is a significant flag.

**How to exit gracefully:** "I want to be straightforward — any deal structure here is contingent on landlord consent to the lease assignment on terms that are workable for us. If that's uncertain, I'd need that resolved before I could put any formal offer together. It's not something I can price around, because the lease is the core asset. Can you have a preliminary conversation with the landlord to gauge their openness before we invest more time?"

This puts the burden on him to solve the landlord relationship. If he cannot, the deal is not viable and you have not wasted significant resources finding that out.

---

### Red Line 4: Revenue Is Lower Than Represented

**Trigger condition:** Financial documentation reveals that actual monthly revenue is materially below the €20,000-25,000 cited in the meeting, or reveals that the peak revenue was a one-time event rather than a sustainable level.

**How to identify it in the meeting:** Ask for documentation in Act 2. His willingness or reluctance to provide actual financial records tells you something. A seller with clean books who has nothing to hide will offer them quickly.

**How to exit gracefully:** "The numbers I've been working from are the figures we discussed. If the documentation shows something different, I'd need to revisit everything from the beginning — the valuation methodology, the offer, all of it. I'd rather pause now and wait for documentation than build on assumptions that turn out to be wrong."

---

### Red Line 5: Legal Complications From the Numi Partnership

**Trigger condition:** There are unresolved contractual disputes, IP claims, or equipment ownership disputes arising from the Numi departure that would attach to any buyer.

**How to identify it in the meeting:** Ask directly in Act 1 about how the Numi situation was resolved. Probe for whether there are any ongoing disputes, whether Numi made claims on any equipment or IP, and whether there is written documentation of a clean separation.

**How to exit gracefully:** "Any residual legal entanglement from a prior partnership is something my solicitor would flag immediately as a deal-stopper. If there's any uncertainty about how the Numi situation is fully resolved, I'd need written confirmation of a clean separation before we proceed. That's non-negotiable — not because I doubt you, but because it's what my legal team will require regardless."

---

### Red Line 6: The Owner Expects a Price Above €300,000 and Will Not Move

**Trigger condition:** After two to three concession moves in the Ackermann sequence, the seller remains anchored above €300,000 and shows no meaningful movement.

**How to identify it in the meeting:** You will see it in the concession dance. If his moves are large and yours are getting smaller, and the gap is not closing proportionally, he has a floor he will not cross in this meeting.

**How to exit gracefully:** "I want to be honest with you — I think we have a genuine difference of view on where the value sits right now. I'm not going to try to convince you overnight of something you've thought hard about. What I'd suggest is that we both take a step back, and I'll have my accountant prepare a formal analysis I can share with you — it might be more useful than me describing it verbally. If after that we're still far apart, then we'll know, and we can make a clear decision from there."

This is not a hard exit. It introduces a new process (the accountant's analysis) that (a) slows things down, (b) introduces a third party whose numbers carry more authority than yours, and (c) gives him a reason to keep the door open.

# SECTION 6: Post-Meeting Protocol

## 1. Follow-Up Timing

Wait 24-48 hours before any follow-up communication. The exact timing matters.

If you follow up within hours: you signal that the meeting was on your mind all day, that you are eager, and that you are perhaps second-guessing yourself. This transfers power.

If you follow up after 48+ hours: you signal that you have a busy life, that this is one of several things you are evaluating, and that you are deliberate rather than reactive.

When you do follow up: a brief, warm email. Not a letter. Not a long message. Two to three sentences maximum.

*Follow-up email script:*

"Good to meet yesterday. I've been sitting with everything we discussed — want to make sure I'm working from the right numbers before I go further. I'll have my accountant pull together a formal view and I'll be in touch by [specific date]. In the meantime, if anything comes to mind from your side, you have my details."

*What this email signals:* You are busy (you have been "sitting with it," not obsessing over it). You are methodical (you are getting professional input before proceeding). You have a process (you will be in touch by a specific date — not "soon").

---

## 2. The Due Diligence Request

Send a formal due diligence request via email within 72 hours of the meeting if the conversation ended positively. Frame it as "standard process."

*Email framing script:*

"Following on from our conversation, I'd like to move to a brief due diligence phase before we formalize anything. This is standard process on my end and shouldn't be onerous. I'll send through a list of documents my team typically reviews — financials, the lease, equipment list, staffing arrangements. Nothing sensitive or unusual, just the basics that any buyer would need to confirm the numbers we discussed.

My solicitor will be in touch separately regarding the lease documents."

*The formal list to request:*

- Three years of P&L statements (or as much as is available)
- Most recent twelve months of bank statements
- Current lease agreement (full document)
- Equipment inventory with original purchase records
- Any financing or leasing agreements on equipment
- Staff contracts or employment summary
- Any outstanding supplier invoices or debts
- Written confirmation of Numi contract termination
- Correspondence or confirmation from the landlord regarding the current lease

---

## 3. The Second Meeting Setup

The second meeting should involve your accountant or financial advisor. Their presence serves multiple functions: it slows the process (which benefits you), it adds professional credibility to your analysis, and it introduces a third party whose conclusions carry weight independent of you.

*How to propose the second meeting:*

"I'd like our next conversation to include my accountant, who will have reviewed the documents by then. It's more efficient — rather than me translating their analysis to you, you can hear it directly. Does that work for you?"

This is framed as efficiency but functions as institutionalization. The deal is now a professional process, not a conversation between two people.

---

## 4. Using Silence in Written Communication

Keep all written communication brief. Short emails from busy people; long emails from anxious people. This applies to every communication in this process.

A good rule: if your email is more than five sentences, cut it in half. Every unnecessary sentence signals that you are trying harder than the situation requires.

Do not answer every question immediately. If he sends a detailed email with several questions, respond to the most important one and let the others wait for the next conversation. This is not rudeness; it is selectivity. Selective responsiveness signals that your time is finite and valuable.

# SECTION 7: Script Summary — Pre-Meeting Cheat Sheet

Review this in the ten minutes before you walk in.

---

**THE 3 MINDSET FRAMES**

1. "I am evaluating an interesting opportunity. I have not decided to buy."
2. "This must work for my business. His exit needs are not my problem."
3. "I am fair. I will not overpay. If we can't find fair value, I move on — calmly."

---

**THE 5 MOST IMPORTANT QUESTIONS IN ORDER**

1. "What's been the biggest challenge with this space since the Numi situation ended?" — *Unlocks his pain story*
2. "What does the monthly picture look like right now — is it covering its costs?" — *Establishes the bleeding*
3. "What would a successful resolution look like for you personally?" — *Reveals his true priority: money or exit*
4. "What's your ideal timeframe for resolving this?" — *Calibrates his urgency*
5. "Have you had other conversations with interested parties?" — *Maps your competitive position*

---

**THE ANCHOR AND HOW TO SAY IT**

"Based on the current revenue, the operational situation, the equipment, and what we'd need to invest to bring the underutilized spaces up — I'd be looking at this in the range of €40,000 to €50,000. Let me walk you through how I got there..."

*Then stop. Hold silence. Do not qualify. Do not apologize.*

---

**THE 4 CONCESSION MOVES**

(Using €150,000 target)

- Move 1: "If we get an independent equipment appraisal and written landlord confirmation — €97,500."
- Move 2: "With three years of financial documentation reviewed and confirmed — €127,500."
- Move 3: "We're very close. I can get to €142,500. What would make the difference for you?"
- Final: "This is my best number, arrived at honestly: €148,750."

Each move is smaller. Each is conditional. The final number is non-round.

---

**THE 3 POWER PHRASES**

1. *Label his pain:* "It sounds like this situation has taken a real toll on you..." [then silence]
2. *Mirror his key words:* He says "I've had so much invested in this place." You say: "...so much invested." [then silence]
3. *Calibrated question when pushed:* "How am I supposed to make that work at the current revenue level?" [not aggressive — genuinely curious, then silence]

---

**IF THINGS GO BADLY — THE EXIT LINE**

*In the late-night DJ voice, calm and warm:*

"I think we see the value differently right now. I'm not going to try to push you somewhere you're not comfortable. What I'd suggest is that we both take some time — I'll have my team put together a more formal analysis and share it with you. If after that we're still far apart, we'll know. But I don't think we're done yet."

*Leave. Do not rush. Do not apologize. Follow up in 48 hours.*

---

**IF THINGS GO WELL — THE TRIAL CLOSE**

"It sounds like if we could land somewhere around [TARGET], we'd have the foundation of something here. Am I reading that right?"

*If yes:* "Good. Let me outline what the next steps look like on my end — there's a standard diligence process, and then we'd move to formal documents. I'd want my solicitor to reach out within the week."

*Do not shake hands on a final number today. Move to process.*

---

**ONE LAST THING TO REMEMBER**

He reached out to you. Every concession you make is a gift. Every gift should buy you something in return. Information. Documentation. Agreement on terms. Timeline. Never give something for nothing. And never, ever let eagerness leak into the room. The moment he senses your excitement, the price goes up.

Walk in calm. Walk out in control. Let the deal come to you.
