## ADDENDUM SECTION 1: MCCAA DEFENSE STRATEGY

### 1.1 The Core Legal Problem

The competition law risk in this transaction is real and specific. On the narrowest market definition — organized weight management services in Malta — the combined Carisma and Marion Mizzi entity would hold an estimated 90-95% market share, producing an HHI comfortably above 2,500 (the European Commission's threshold for "highly concentrated" markets). This is not a borderline case. If MCCAA accepts a narrow market definition, the transaction is presumptively problematic.

The legal strategy must therefore be built on two parallel tracks: (a) arguing aggressively for the broadest defensible market definition, and (b) preparing a remedies package that is offered voluntarily before the authority demands it, demonstrating good faith and controlling the remedial narrative.

### 1.2 Market Definition Arguments: Broader Is Defensible

**The EU framework for relevant market definition**

MCCAA will apply the same analytical framework as the European Commission and all EU Member State authorities under the Notice on the Definition of Relevant Market (97/C 372/03). The core question is demand-side substitutability: would a significant number of consumers switch to other services if the combined entity imposed a small but significant non-transitory increase in prices (the SSNIP test)?

Carisma's legal team should argue that the relevant market is "health, wellness, and personal transformation services" — a broad cluster that includes the following substitutes for organized weight management programs:

- **Independent registered dietitians and nutritionists** — Malta has approximately 80-120 registered dietitians in private practice. A consumer seeking weight management can access a 45-minute dietitian consultation for EUR 35-60, followed by a structured meal plan, for well under the cost of a Marion Mizzi or Carisma Slimming program. These practitioners are meaningfully competitive on price, accessibility, and outcome.

- **Fitness and gym services** — Malta has approximately 60-80 registered gym operators. Gym membership at EUR 25-50 per month, combined with personal training at EUR 30-50 per session, is a direct consumer substitute for slimming programs. Gym operators increasingly offer nutritional guidance as a bundled service.

- **Online and digital weight management platforms** — Noom, WeightWatchers Online, BetterMe, and Lifesum are available to Maltese consumers. Noom charges approximately EUR 45-60 per month for a full structured weight management program. WeightWatchers Malta membership is available at EUR 15-30 per month. These platforms have enrolled hundreds of thousands of European consumers. Their exclusion from the relevant market would require MCCAA to conclude that Maltese consumers regard face-to-face slimming clinics as a non-substitutable product — a conclusion that is increasingly difficult to defend in 2026.

- **GLP-1 prescribers and obesity medicine clinics** — Semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro) are available in Malta through prescription from general practitioners, endocrinologists, and private obesity medicine practitioners. Any consumer for whom GLP-1 therapy is appropriate faces a direct substitute for behavioral weight management programs. At current Maltese pricing (approximately EUR 100-180 per month for medication alone), GLP-1 therapy overlaps meaningfully with the EUR 199-350/month price point of commercial slimming programs.

- **Pharmacist-led weight management advisory services** — Under Malta's Pharmacy of Your Choice initiative and Community Pharmacy Framework, Maltese pharmacists increasingly offer lifestyle counseling services. These are lower-intensity but genuine competitive alternatives.

- **Unorganized providers** — Personal trainers offering nutritional coaching, life coaches with wellness components, corporate wellness programs offered by employers, and school/community health programs all participate in the same consumer demand for weight and body management.

**Precedent: EU Commission market definition in personal services**

The European Commission has consistently resisted narrow market definitions in personal health and wellness services where the consumer base has demonstrated substitution behavior:

- *Case COMP/M.4066 — Compass/Eurest* (2006): The Commission defined the "food services" market broadly to include various service formats, rejecting the argument that each contract catering sub-segment was a separate market.

- *Case COMP/M.6432 — Nestlé/Pfizer (Infant Nutrition)* (2012): The Commission emphasized demand-side substitutability based on consumer perception rather than technical product differences — a principle directly applicable to wellness services where consumer perception of "weight management options" is broad.

- *Slimming World / Weight Watchers* merger inquiries in the UK (CMA, 2015-2019): The UK CMA considered whether "commercial weight management programs" was a distinct market from "broader weight loss services." In multiple merger reviews, the CMA ultimately defined the market to include digital platforms, exercise programs, and medical alternatives — not solely face-to-face slimming clubs. While the CMA's decisions are not binding on MCCAA, they represent the leading common law precedent in this specific sector and should be cited persuasively.

- *Case COMP/M.7276 — Nutrisystem/Jenny Craig* (hypothetical construct for illustration): Any attempt to define the market as "organized weight loss programs" that excludes digital alternatives has consistently failed in post-2015 European competition decisions because digital substitution is no longer speculative — it is documented consumer behavior.

**Carisma's specific arguments for Malta**

The following arguments should be developed with quantitative support in the notification filing:

1. **The digital substitution argument.** Malta has 85% smartphone penetration (higher than the EU average). Maltese consumers have direct access to the full range of international digital weight management platforms. If MCCAA defines the market as "organized slimming services in Malta," it would be treating Maltese consumers as incapable of substituting to digital alternatives — a paternalistic and empirically unsupported conclusion.

2. **The price discipline argument.** If the combined entity attempted to raise prices by 10%, what would happen? The answer, based on observable consumer behavior, is that a meaningful proportion of price-sensitive consumers would shift to dietitian consultations, gym memberships, or digital platforms. The SSNIP test fails at 10%, which means the market definition must be broader than the authority's narrowest formulation.

3. **The contestability argument.** Even if market concentration is high, MCCAA must consider barriers to entry. In organized weight management services, the barriers are moderate-to-low: no capital-intensive infrastructure, no regulatory licensing requirement, no exclusive input. A motivated entrepreneur with dietitian credentials could launch a competing slimming service in Malta within 6-12 months. The market is contestable, which weakens the competition concern even at high measured concentration.

4. **The GLP-1 disruption argument.** The market structure will look fundamentally different in 3-5 years. Regulating the current static concentration without accounting for the dynamic market entry of GLP-1 services would produce a clearance decision that is obsolete before the ink dries.

### 1.3 Counterfactual Analysis

A critical legal argument that the original report underdeveloped is the **counterfactual**: what happens to competition if the transaction does not proceed?

MCCAA is required under S.L. 379.08 to assess the effect of the concentration relative to the competitive conditions that would prevail without the concentration. The competitive conditions without the concentration are not simply "Marion Mizzi continues as an independent competitor." The evidence suggests:

- Marion Mizzi Wellbeing is declining under disengaged ownership
- Equipment is aging and underinvested (Report 03 estimates 10-15 year old equipment)
- No succession plan exists — the business exits when Petra exits
- Without acquisition, Marion Mizzi Wellbeing likely deteriorates to a point of exit within 3-5 years

The counterfactual is therefore not "two competing operators" but "one operator (Carisma) after Marion Mizzi's natural exit." The acquisition accelerates an outcome that the market will reach organically anyway — but it preserves the brand, employment, and consumer access to the services during the transition. Properly argued, this is a **pro-competitive counterfactual**: acquisition prevents unilateral market exit and preserves consumer choice relative to the realistic alternative.

This argument is supported by the EU Commission's *failing firm defense* (Merger Regulation, Recital 90; Case COMP/M.2314 — BASF/Eurodiol/Pantochim): a concentration may be cleared even in concentrated markets if the acquired firm would exit the market without the acquisition and the buyer is the least anti-competitive acquirer. While Marion Mizzi does not necessarily meet the strict three-part failing firm test (insolvency not imminent), the **weakened competitor** variant of this argument — well-established in EU case law — is directly applicable.

### 1.4 Preliminary Remedies Offer

The strongest position is to enter MCCAA pre-notification discussions with a voluntary remedies package already prepared. This signals legal sophistication, controls the remedial conversation, and makes Phase 2 less likely by demonstrating that the buyer has identified the concerns before the authority has.

**Structural remedy: Voluntary divestiture of Carisma Slimming's gym operations**

If MCCAA is concerned about concentration specifically in the body contouring / gym segment (not merely the slimming segment), Carisma should offer to divest the gym component from the acquisition — specifically, not acquiring any gym equipment or fitness infrastructure from Marion Mizzi Wellbeing. This is a low-cost structural remedy that reduces the concentration in a meaningful sub-segment (gym and fitness services) while preserving the core value of the acquisition (slimming programs, brand, client database, spa operations).

The specific parameters of this offer:
- Carisma will not acquire any gym equipment, gym membership contracts, or fitness area leases from Marion Mizzi Wellbeing
- The gym operations at Fgura (the standalone location with the largest gym footprint) can be either wound down by the seller or sub-licensed to a third-party operator
- Carisma commits in writing not to operate gym services under the Marion Mizzi brand at any location for 36 months post-closing

This remedy would allow MCCAA to characterize the acquisition as limited to "slimming counseling, spa, and body contouring services" — a segment where digital and dietitian competition is stronger — rather than a full-spectrum fitness and wellness monopoly.

**Behavioral remedy 1: Independent pricing commitment**

Carisma commits that for a period of 5 years post-closing:
- The "Marion Mizzi Wellbeing" brand will operate as a separate legal entity with independent pricing decisions (not tied to Carisma Slimming prices)
- No bundled pricing or cross-brand exclusivity requirements will be imposed on consumers
- A pricing audit mechanism will be established (e.g., annual report to MCCAA on price movements, benchmarked against CPI) to demonstrate that the acquisition has not resulted in supra-competitive pricing

**Behavioral remedy 2: Commitment to maintain all current locations**

Carisma commits to maintaining operations at all acquired locations for a minimum of 36 months post-closing, subject to landlord cooperation. This addresses any MCCAA concern that the acquisition would be followed by location consolidation, reducing consumer access to services.

**Behavioral remedy 3: Access commitment for new entrants**

Carisma commits not to enforce any exclusive territory arrangements in supplier contracts or prevent new market entry by competitors during the first 3 years post-closing. Specifically, Carisma will not seek to prevent competing operators from entering agreements with the same suppliers, distributors, or technology providers.

### 1.5 Pre-Notification Meeting Strategy

Before filing the formal MCCAA notification, Carisma's legal counsel should request an informal pre-notification meeting with the Director General of the Office for Competition. This practice is standard in EU merger control (the European Commission encourages pre-notification contacts, and MCCAA follows similar practice) and serves to:

- Identify the authority's preliminary views on market definition
- Gauge the likelihood of a Phase 2 investigation
- Present the counterfactual and remedies package informally before formal positions are taken
- Receive informal guidance on what additional data the authority would want in the notification

The pre-notification meeting should be attended by Carisma's senior competition counsel and, if appropriate, a representative of Carisma's management who can speak to the business case and integration plans.

## ADDENDUM SECTION 2: LEASE ANALYSIS FRAMEWORK

### 2.1 The Problem the Original Report Did Not Solve

The original report analyzed Malta's commercial lease law in general terms and identified risks at each location qualitatively. The QA Director correctly observed that the actual lease documents — which must be requested as part of due diligence — were not analyzed. This addendum provides: (a) a structured framework for analyzing each lease once the documents are received, and (b) an analysis of what to look for and why it matters.

This addendum cannot substitute for review of the actual documents — that review must be completed by Maltese legal counsel once the due diligence request list produces the lease agreements. What follows is the analytical framework that counsel should apply.

### 2.2 Lease Due Diligence Framework: Seven Critical Variables

For each of the three locations (AX Palace Hotel, Sliema; Maritim Antonine Hotel, Mellieha; Fgura standalone), the following seven variables must be extracted and analyzed:

---

**Variable 1: Remaining Term and Expiry Date**

What to look for: The commencement date, original term length, and expiry date. Calculate the remaining term as of the projected closing date.

Why it matters: If a lease expires within 18-24 months of closing, the buyer is acquiring a location without security of tenure. The business value at that location is functionally zero (or close to it) because the landlord can decline to renew. Any purchase price allocation to that location must reflect this.

Risk thresholds:
- Remaining term > 5 years: Satisfactory. Include in APA at full value.
- Remaining term 3-5 years: Acceptable if renewal option exists (see Variable 2). Apply a 10-15% value discount.
- Remaining term 1-3 years: Problematic. The location should be treated as contingent value only. Adjust purchase price or make location's value conditional on lease renewal secured before closing.
- Remaining term < 1 year: Unacceptable. Do not pay for this location. Exclude from APA or acquire at nominal value only.

Action required: Request certified copy of each lease agreement. Identify commencement date and expiry. Map remaining term on the deal timeline.

---

**Variable 2: Renewal Options**

What to look for: Whether the lease contains a tenant's option to renew for one or more additional terms, and the conditions for exercising that option (notice period, timing, any landlord consent requirement).

Why it matters: A renewal option converts a short remaining term into a longer economic life for the location. However, the right to exercise the option is typically personal to the original tenant — it must be confirmed that the option will survive the lease assignment and be exercisable by the buyer.

Specific questions to answer from each lease:
- Does the renewal option require the landlord's consent, or is it a unilateral tenant right?
- Does the current tenant need to have complied with all lease obligations to exercise the option? (If the seller has any lease breaches — unpaid rent, breached covenants — the option may be invalidated)
- What is the notice period for exercising the option? Has any notice already been given (or should it be given before closing)?
- Will the renewal be at the same rent, at market rent, or subject to negotiation?
- Does the assignment of the lease carry the renewal option to the assignee?

Action required: Carisma's counsel must specifically confirm that renewal options are assignable and not personal to the seller. If the assignment extinguishes the renewal option, this is a material adverse change that must be reflected in the purchase price.

---

**Variable 3: Change-of-Control and Assignment Provisions**

What to look for: Whether the lease contains a clause that is triggered by a change in ownership or control of the tenant, separate from or in addition to the assignment provisions. Modern commercial leases frequently contain both.

Why it matters: In an asset deal, the lease is being assigned — the consent provision is clearly triggered. But in a share deal (if ever contemplated), a change-of-control clause would be triggered even though no formal assignment occurs. More relevantly for this transaction, even in an asset deal, some landlords argue that a "change of business" (not just a change of tenant) constitutes a breach — this is legally questionable in Malta but a risk that must be identified and addressed.

Specific questions:
- Does the lease define "assignment" to include asset transfers, business sales, or management changes?
- Is there a "deemed assignment" trigger if the beneficial ownership of the tenant entity changes?
- What is the consequence of unauthorized assignment — is it a breach that entitles the landlord to terminate, or merely a condition requiring subsequent consent?
- What standard of consent applies? "Sole discretion," "not unreasonably withheld," or absolute prohibition?

The Malta Civil Code (Article 1549) provides that a lessee may not sub-let or assign the lease without the lessor's consent unless the lease expressly permits it. Breach of this provision gives the landlord the right to demand cancellation of the lease. This is a hard rule with no equitable exception in Malta — the landlord's consent is legally mandatory unless the lease expressly waives this requirement.

Action required: Identify the specific consent language in each lease. Do not assume consent will be forthcoming. If any lease contains an absolute prohibition on assignment, the location cannot be acquired in an asset deal — it must either be excluded from the APA or the landlord must agree to grant a new lease directly to the buyer (a more complex negotiation requiring termination of the existing lease and execution of a new one).

---

**Variable 4: Rent Review on Assignment**

What to look for: Whether the lease provides that upon assignment, the rent is subject to review (upward only, to market rate, or by reference to a fixed formula).

Why it matters: A rent review on assignment is common in commercial hotel leases. If the current rent is below market (which is possible if the lease was signed many years ago), the landlord may use the assignment as an opportunity to extract a significant rent increase. Even a 15-20% rent increase across three locations could add EUR 15,000-30,000 per year to the operating cost base — material against a projected EBITDA of EUR 400-500K.

Specific questions:
- Does the lease contain a rent review clause, and is assignment an event that triggers a review?
- If rent is reviewed, is it to market rent (open-market rent review), or by reference to an index (Consumer Price Index, Retail Price Index)?
- Is any rent increase capped (e.g., "not more than 10% above existing rent per review")?
- What is the current rent, and what would current market rent be for the equivalent premises?

For the hotel locations specifically: hotel spa leases frequently include revenue-sharing arrangements in addition to or instead of fixed rent (e.g., 10-15% of gross revenues). If such an arrangement exists, the buyer needs to model the rent as a variable cost, not a fixed cost. Under a revenue-sharing arrangement, integration success directly increases occupancy costs — a factor that reduces EBITDA margin as revenues grow.

Action required: Extract the rent, any revenue-sharing formula, and any review mechanism from each lease. Model the current and projected rent liability. Negotiate a cap on rent increase as a condition of consent to assignment.

---

**Variable 5: Landlord Approval Requirements and Process**

What to look for: Whether landlord consent requires a formal written application, what supporting documentation the landlord requires (financial statements, references, corporate credentials), and what the response timeline is.

Why it matters: If the landlord's consent process is cumbersome or the landlord requires significant financial information about the buyer, the lease assignment could delay the closing timeline by 4-8 weeks or more. For hotel landlords with corporate governance requirements (especially Maritim Hotels, an international group), head-office approval may be required — adding further delay.

Specific questions:
- What is the consent application process specified in the lease?
- What financial or other qualifications must the proposed assignee satisfy?
- Is there a deemed consent mechanism (if the landlord fails to respond within X days, consent is deemed given)? If not, there is no lever to compel a timely response.
- What are the costs of the consent process? (Some leases impose the landlord's legal fees on the tenant seeking consent — these costs must be budgeted.)

For the AX Palace Hotel location: AX Group is a sophisticated Maltese commercial landlord with in-house legal capability. Their consent process is likely formal but efficient. The key risk is that they use the consent event to renegotiate commercial terms — rent, revenue-sharing, fit-out obligations, or exclusivity restrictions.

For the Maritim Antonine Hotel location: Maritim Hotels is a German international hotel group. Consent may require head-office approval from the German management team, which will evaluate the proposed assignee against their global spa operator standards. Carisma's international hotel spa credentials (50+ five-star hotels) are a strong qualification, but the process is likely to be slower than a purely Maltese consent process. Allow at least 8-12 weeks for Maritim consent.

Action required: Once the leases are reviewed, initiate landlord consent discussions immediately after LOI signing. Do not wait until APA negotiation is complete — every week of delay in initiating landlord discussions extends the closing timeline. Include a condition precedent in the LOI requiring the buyer's legal team to review all three leases within 5 business days of execution.

---

**Variable 6: Restrictive Covenants in the Lease**

What to look for: Provisions that restrict the use of the premises, prohibit the tenant from conducting certain activities, limit the tenant's ability to modify the premises, or impose obligations on the tenant regarding fit-out standards.

Why it matters: Restrictive covenants in hotel spa leases frequently specify the permitted use (e.g., "operation of a spa and wellness facility only") and may restrict the buyer from using the space for any additional service lines — particularly medical aesthetics or GLP-1-adjacent services that Carisma plans to introduce post-acquisition.

Specific questions:
- What is the permitted use clause? Does it allow the full range of Carisma's intended services?
- Are there any covenants requiring the tenant to maintain specific staffing levels, opening hours, or service standards?
- Are there any covenants requiring the tenant to maintain membership in specific professional associations?
- Are there any provisions giving the hotel operator control over the spa's branding, pricing, or customer-facing materials?
- Are there any reinstatement obligations (requiring the tenant to restore the premises to original condition at lease end) — these create contingent end-of-lease liabilities?

For the GLP-1 strategic pivot specifically: if either hotel lease restricts the premises to "spa and wellness services" and excludes "medical" or "pharmaceutical" activities, the GLP-1 management service model may breach the permitted use covenant — even if the partnership model with licensed practitioners is used. This must be investigated before committing to a GLP-1 pivot at hotel locations.

Action required: Map the permitted use clause in each lease against Carisma's planned service mix for the next 5 years. Identify any conflict. If the GLP-1 management service would breach a hotel lease's permitted use covenant, negotiate an expansion of permitted use as part of the consent-to-assign negotiation.

---

**Variable 7: Landlord Termination Rights**

What to look for: Circumstances under which the landlord may terminate the lease unilaterally, including break clauses, redevelopment rights, breach-triggered termination, and force majeure provisions.

Why it matters: A landlord break clause gives the hotel operator the ability to terminate the lease at defined intervals, regardless of tenant performance. If the hotel is undergoing renovation, brand changes, or strategic repositioning, the landlord may exercise a break clause to reclaim the spa space. This would render the location economically worthless mid-transaction.

Specific questions:
- Does the lease contain a landlord break clause? When is it exercisable?
- What notice must the landlord give before exercising a break?
- Are there any circumstances (redevelopment, change of hotel brand, force majeure) that entitle the landlord to terminate early without compensation?
- What compensation (if any) is the tenant entitled to if the landlord terminates early? Under Malta law, a landlord who terminates a commercial lease prematurely without cause may owe compensation in damages — but this is a legal remedy, not a business protection.

For the Maritim Antonine Hotel specifically: Maritim Hotels operates an international portfolio of hotel properties. There is a material risk that Maritim could decide to either in-house its spa operation or contract with a larger international spa operator. If the existing lease gives Maritim a break right in connection with hotel refurbishment or brand repositioning, the Mellieha location could be lost within the lease term. This risk must be priced into any purchase price allocation to that location.

## ADDENDUM SECTION 3: PERSONAL NAME TRADEMARK — MALTA AND EU CASE LAW ANALYSIS

### 3.1 The Specific Legal Question

The original report identified the "own name defense" risk but did not answer the buyer's critical question: if Carisma acquires the "Marion Mizzi" trademark and signs a non-compete with Petra and Marion Mizzi (the individuals), can either of them legally open a new wellness business using their own name?

This section answers that question with reference to specific Malta and EU case law, and specifies the contractual protections required to close the legal gaps that case law leaves open.

### 3.2 The Statutory Framework

**EU Trade Mark Regulation (EUTMR), Article 14(1)(a):**

"An EU trade mark shall not entitle the proprietor to prohibit a third party from using, in the course of trade... their own name or address, where that third party is a natural person."

This right is not absolute. The same article continues: "...provided he uses them in accordance with honest practices in industrial or commercial matters."

**EU Trade Mark Directive (EUTMD), Article 14(1)(a) — implemented in Malta:**

The same principle applies to nationally registered marks. The "own name defense" is a statutory right of natural persons under EU trademark law, implemented in every Member State. Malta's Trade Marks Act (Chapter 597 of the Laws of Malta) reflects this framework.

**The critical qualification:** "Honest practices" is the operative limiting phrase. If using one's own name creates consumer confusion with a registered mark, the use is not in accordance with honest practices and the defense fails. This is the legal lever available to Carisma.

### 3.3 EU Case Law on Personal Name Trademarks

**Case C-245/02 — Anheuser-Busch Inc. v Budejovicky Budvar (ECJ, 2004)**

This case established that the "honest practices" qualification is not a mere formality. The Court held that use of a name is not in accordance with honest practices if it is done deliberately to confuse consumers or to benefit from a trade mark's reputation. The burden shifts: once the trademark owner demonstrates potential confusion, the burden is on the name user to demonstrate honest practices.

Application to Marion Mizzi: If Marion Mizzi (the person) were to open a new wellness business under her own name following the sale, she would need to demonstrate that her use does not create confusion with the acquired "Marion Mizzi Wellbeing" brand. Given that the brand is her own name, complete non-confusion is legally impossible — consumers will inevitably associate a business named "Marion Mizzi" with the established brand. This creates a strong legal basis for the trademark owner (Carisma) to obtain an injunction.

**Case C-17/06 — Céline SARL v Céline SA (ECJ, 2007)**

The ECJ held that a company operating under its own trade name does not automatically fall within the Article 12 (now Article 14) own name defense when the name in question is also a registered trademark. The defense requires both that the use be of one's "own name" and that it be in accordance with "honest practices."

More importantly, the Court held that commercial use of a name to sell goods or services — as opposed to purely identifying oneself as a business entity — is subject to trademark infringement analysis. Simply registering a company in one's own name does not insulate the user from trademark claims.

Application to Marion Mizzi: If Marion or Petra were to operate a wellness business (not merely incorporate a holding company), the commercial use of the name would face the full burden of the "honest practices" test. The commercial nature of the use would be clear. Consumer confusion would be presumptively established given the fame of the "Marion Mizzi Wellbeing" brand in Malta's small market.

**Case C-206/01 — Arsenal Football Club plc v Matthew Reed (ECJ, 2002)**

This case established the "origin function" of trademarks — the primary role of a trademark is to indicate to consumers the commercial origin of goods or services. Use that damages this origin function constitutes trademark infringement, regardless of the user's subjective intent.

Application to Marion Mizzi: A new wellness business operating under "Marion Mizzi" would inevitably damage the origin function of Carisma's acquired trademark. Consumers would be unable to distinguish the new business from the acquired brand. This is a per se violation of the trademark's core function, and the "own name defense" could not overcome it.

**Case C-245/02 — EU Guidelines on Name-Based Trademarks (Commission Practice)**

The European Commission's trademark examination guidelines (as applied by EUIPO) specify that personal name marks have inherently weaker protection than invented marks — but once the mark has acquired distinctiveness through use (as "Marion Mizzi" clearly has, given 50+ years of operation in Malta), it receives full protection. Post-acquisition, Carisma would hold a well-established personal name mark with extensive goodwill. The protection afforded would be equivalent to a highly distinctive invented mark.

### 3.4 Malta-Specific Considerations

Malta is a small jurisdiction (population 520,000) with a small professional bar. There is limited domestic case law on personal name trademarks specifically. However:

- Malta's Trade Marks Act (Chapter 597) directly implements the EU Trade Mark Directive, making EU case law directly applicable and authoritative
- The Malta Industrial Property Registrations Directorate (within the Commerce Department) follows EUIPO practice guidelines
- Maltese courts have demonstrated willingness to apply EU IP law principles, including the "honest practices" qualification, in trademark disputes

The practical reality in Malta is that the market is small enough that any use of "Marion Mizzi" in wellness services would immediately and demonstrably confuse consumers. The legal analysis mirrors the factual reality: a 50-year-old brand in a 520,000-person market has complete consumer saturation. There is no non-confusing way for a natural person named Marion Mizzi to operate in the same market.

### 3.5 Specific Contractual Protections Required

Trademark law alone is not sufficient protection. Case law provides a cause of action — it does not prevent the conduct. The following contractual protections must be included in the APA and the personal name agreement with both Marion Mizzi (the individual) and Petra:

**Protection 1: Explicit name assignment (beyond trademark registration)**

The APA should include an explicit, irrevocable assignment by the seller (and by Marion Mizzi and Petra as individuals) of all rights — not just registered trademark rights — to use the name "Marion Mizzi" in connection with any wellness, health, beauty, nutrition, fitness, slimming, spa, or related commercial activity. This assignment covers:
- Registered trademarks (Malta and EU)
- Unregistered trademark rights (passing off)
- Domain names
- Social media usernames and handles
- Any association between the personal name and any wellness business

This is broader than a standard trademark assignment because it captures the unregistered common law rights that would survive a trademark assignment.

**Protection 2: Non-compete covenant — Personal name specific**

In addition to the standard non-compete (no competing wellness business for 5 years in Malta), the APA must include a specific personal name non-compete:

"Marion Mizzi [individual] and Petra [full name] individually covenant that they will not, for a period of 10 years from the closing date, use the name 'Marion Mizzi,' 'M. Mizzi,' 'Marion,' or any phonetically or visually similar name in connection with any business, service, or activity in the wellness, health, slimming, nutrition, beauty, spa, body contouring, or fitness sectors, anywhere in Malta or the European Union."

The 10-year period (rather than the standard 5 years) is justifiable for name restrictions specifically because:
- The "own name defense" can revive the right to use one's name after a non-compete expires
- A 5-year non-compete may not be sufficient to establish the acquired mark's independent identity separate from its founding personality
- EU case law (Case C-381/98 — Ingmar GB Ltd) allows non-compete covenants to exceed standard periods where justified by the specific circumstances

The geographic scope (Malta and EU) is broader than the normal Malta-only non-compete. This is justified because:
- The Carisma group operates across EU jurisdictions (50+ five-star hotels)
- If the "Marion Mizzi" brand is subsequently extended to the EU (which Carisma's hotel operations could support), a Malta-only restriction would allow the seller to freely operate in other EU jurisdictions and confuse the brand

**Protection 3: Family member restriction**

The covenant must expressly extend to:
- Petra's spouse and children
- Any company, trust, partnership, or other vehicle controlled directly or indirectly by Marion Mizzi or Petra

The rationale: without this extension, the restriction is trivially avoidable by operating through a family member or corporate vehicle.

**Protection 4: Liquidated damages clause**

The breach of any personal name restriction should trigger a liquidated damages provision — not merely injunctive relief. Recommended quantum: EUR 250,000 per breach event plus EUR 5,000 per day of continuing breach. Liquidated damages serve two purposes:
- They remove the need to quantify actual loss (which would be difficult in a small market)
- They provide a credible deterrent beyond the cost of litigation

**Protection 5: Brand transition timeline**

Carisma should plan to rebrand the acquired business from "Marion Mizzi Wellbeing" to a Carisma-affiliated brand (e.g., "Carisma Wellbeing" or "Carisma by Marion Mizzi" during transition) within 5 years of closing. Counterintuitively, this actually strengthens the trademark protection: the longer the "Marion Mizzi" name remains as an active commercial brand under Carisma's ownership, the stronger the established distinctiveness of the mark and the more difficult it becomes for any natural person named Marion Mizzi to argue that their use of the name is in accordance with honest practices.

The APA should specify a transitional licensing arrangement: Carisma licenses the "Marion Mizzi" name for commercial use during a transition period (3-5 years), with the express understanding that the license terminates automatically and the name becomes Carisma's exclusive property without any residual rights in the seller.

## ADDENDUM SECTION 4: GLP-1 REGULATORY PATHWAY — DETAILED ANALYSIS

### 4.1 Why the Original Analysis Was Insufficient

The original report correctly identified that GLP-1 receptor agonists are prescription-only medicines and recommended a "partnership model" with licensed practitioners. The QA Director correctly observed that this recommendation was insufficiently analyzed — specifically, whether the partnership model avoids medical clinic registration requirements under Malta law, or whether any facility at which GLP-1 management services are provided is subject to licensing regardless of how the commercial relationship is structured.

This section provides a statute-by-statute analysis of the regulatory requirements.

### 4.2 Relevant Maltese Statutes

**4.2.1 The Medicines Act (Chapter 458, Laws of Malta)**

The Medicines Act implements EU Directive 2001/83/EC on the Community code relating to medicinal products for human use. Under the Medicines Act:

- GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, tirzepatide, liraglutide) are classified as medicinal products for human use
- They are classified as Prescription Only Medicines (POM) under Schedule 2 of the relevant subsidiary legislation
- Only a registered pharmacist (under the Pharmacy Act, Chapter 467) may dispense POMs
- Only a licensed medical practitioner (under the Medical Council Act, Chapter 31) may prescribe POMs
- Any entity that stores, handles, or administers POMs must comply with Good Storage Practice requirements under the Medicines Authority

**What this means for the "partnership model":**

The key question is whether the partner model — in which a licensed physician prescribes and a licensed pharmacist dispenses, while Carisma merely provides supportive wellness services — requires any Medicines Act licensing on Carisma's part.

**Conclusion:** Carisma does not need Medicines Act licensing if it does not handle, store, or administer the medicines. Under the partnership model, if the physician prescribes and the client obtains medication from a licensed pharmacy (not from Carisma's premises), Carisma has no Medicines Act obligations. This is a legitimate and legally clean structure.

However: If Carisma intends to have medication on premises — even if administered only by a physician partner — then Carisma's premises become a location where medicinal products are stored and administered, potentially requiring registration under the Medicines Authority's healthcare facility licensing regime.

**4.2.2 The Health Care Professions Act (Chapter 464, Laws of Malta)**

Chapter 464 is the primary statute regulating health professionals in Malta. It establishes the Malta Health Care Professions Council (MHCPC) and defines the regulated professions, their scope of practice, and the conditions for practice.

The following professions are relevant to the GLP-1 service model:

**Medical practitioners (Chapter 31 — Medical Council Act):** Only registered medical practitioners may prescribe GLP-1 medications. This is non-negotiable. Any "GLP-1 management program" that involves prescribing must have a registered physician involved. A physician partner is legally necessary, not merely commercially convenient.

**Dietitians and nutritionists (regulated under Chapter 464 — Council for Professions Complementary to Medicine):** Only registered dietitians may provide medical nutrition therapy. In the GLP-1 context, a registered dietitian is critical for developing the dietary component of a GLP-1 management program — the dietary adjustments required to maximize GLP-1 outcomes are within the dietitian's regulated scope of practice. Nutritionists who are not registered dietitians may provide general dietary guidance but may not prescribe therapeutic diets for obesity treatment.

Current operational status: Marion Mizzi Wellbeing employs or contracts a dietitian/nutritionist for its existing slimming programs. This individual's registration status must be verified — are they a registered dietitian (with MHCPC registration and scope to provide therapeutic dietary advice) or an unregistered nutritional counselor (who may provide general guidance only)? The distinction is legally significant for the GLP-1 program.

**Nurses and midwives (regulated under Chapter 464):** If the GLP-1 management program involves monitoring (blood pressure, blood glucose, injection administration), registered nurses may be required. Under Chapter 464, only registered nurses may administer injections and conduct clinical assessments.

**4.2.3 The Health Care Services (General) Regulations (S.L. 464.12)**

This is the central statutory instrument that determines whether Carisma's premises require licensing as a healthcare facility. S.L. 464.12 requires a license from the Superintendent of Public Health for any premises used for:

"The provision of healthcare services to persons, whether on an inpatient, day care, or outpatient basis, involving the assessment, diagnosis, treatment, or monitoring of a medical condition."

**The critical legal question:** Does a "GLP-1 weight management support program" at Carisma's premises constitute "assessment, diagnosis, treatment, or monitoring of a medical condition" — thereby triggering the licensing requirement?

**The answer depends on what services are actually provided at the premises:**

- If Carisma's premises are used for: dietary coaching, lifestyle counseling, behavioral support, body composition measurement, and non-medical monitoring (weight, waist circumference, body fat percentage) — this does NOT constitute healthcare provision and S.L. 464.12 does NOT apply.

- If Carisma's premises are used for: physician consultations, prescription of medication, administration of injections, blood tests, blood pressure monitoring in a clinical context, or assessment for adverse effects of GLP-1 medication — this DOES constitute healthcare provision and S.L. 464.12 requires a license.

**The partnership model's legal sufficiency:**

The partnership model — where a physician partner operates independently from Carisma's premises (or from a separate licensed clinic) and Carisma provides only the wellness/lifestyle components — is legally sufficient IF the two elements are genuinely separated:

- The physician must practice from either their own licensed premises (private clinic or clinic room) or from premises that are separately licensed under S.L. 464.12 — not from Carisma's spa or slimming facility
- Client medical records and prescriptions must be held by the physician's licensed entity, not by Carisma
- Carisma's referral of clients to the physician must be structured as a genuine professional referral, not as an integrated service pathway that Carisma controls

If, however, the physician operates from Carisma's premises (even on a sessional basis), and clients understand that "Carisma's GLP-1 program" includes medical consultation, prescription, and monitoring — then the commercial reality may be characterized as Carisma operating a healthcare facility, triggering the licensing requirement regardless of the formal corporate structure.

**4.2.4 The Specific Steps to Compliance Under the Partnership Model**

The following steps are required to implement the GLP-1 partnership model legally:

**Step 1: Physician partner selection and agreement**
- Select a physician who is registered with the Medical Council of Malta and has specialty experience in obesity medicine, endocrinology, or internal medicine
- Execute a services agreement that clearly defines the physician's independent professional responsibility for all clinical decisions (prescribing, monitoring, adverse event management)
- The agreement must not create an employment relationship or give Carisma managerial authority over clinical decisions — this would constitute unlicensed practice of medicine

**Step 2: Premises configuration**
- GLP-1 consultations must occur at the physician's independently licensed premises — this could be the physician's private practice or a licensed clinical space that the physician licenses separately
- Carisma's spa/slimming premises should not be used for any activity that falls within the definition of "healthcare provision" under S.L. 464.12
- Clear physical and operational separation between Carisma's wellness services and the physician's clinical services must be established and maintained

**Step 3: Marketing compliance**
- Carisma's marketing for the GLP-1 program must be structured carefully. Any claim that the program includes "medical supervision," "prescription services," or "clinical monitoring" must clarify that these services are provided by an independently licensed third-party physician — not by Carisma
- Avoid language that implies Carisma provides or controls the medical component. This creates liability exposure under both the Medicines Act and professional regulation if Carisma is seen as commercially controlling a medical service

**Step 4: Data protection configuration**
- Clinical data (prescription records, blood test results, adverse event reports) remains with the physician's licensed entity
- Carisma holds only wellness data (weight measurements, lifestyle data, dietary logs)
- A data sharing agreement between Carisma and the physician must specify what data is shared, under what lawful basis, and who is the data controller for each category

**Step 5: Insurance**
- The physician must carry professional indemnity insurance for their clinical practice (standard for Maltese medical practitioners)
- Carisma must carry appropriate public liability insurance for its wellness premises and activities
- Carisma's insurance must explicitly cover the "GLP-1 support program" services — insurers may require notification of this new service model, and some may require an endorsement or additional premium

**Step 6: Medicines Authority notification (if medication stored on premises)**
- If the partnership model evolves to a point where medication is stored or administered at Carisma's premises, a notification or license application to the Medicines Authority will be required
- This should be planned as a Phase 2 expansion (18-24 months post-acquisition) rather than a Day 1 implementation

**Step 7: Professional indemnity review**
- Carisma's professional indemnity insurance must cover all wellness treatments provided as part of the GLP-1 support program (dietary coaching, body contouring, lifestyle monitoring)
- Any treatment that overlaps with the physician's scope of practice (e.g., administering a body composition scan used in clinical decision-making) must be specifically covered

**4.3 Conclusion on GLP-1 Legal Feasibility**

The partnership model is legally sufficient for the wellness-only component of GLP-1 management services, provided the operational and structural separation described above is maintained. Carisma does not need a medical clinic license to provide dietary coaching, lifestyle support, body contouring, and non-clinical monitoring services to clients who are separately prescribed GLP-1 medications by their own physicians.

However, if Carisma's commercial model evolves toward integrated GLP-1 programs where clinical services are provided at Carisma's premises — even by an independent physician partner — the Superintendent of Public Health's licensing requirement under S.L. 464.12 will likely apply. Legal counsel should seek a formal opinion from the Superintendent's office before launching any program that could be characterized as integrating clinical and wellness services on the same premises.

## ADDENDUM SECTION 7: R&W INSURANCE — QUALIFIED RECOMMENDATION

### 7.1 The Criticism

The original report stated that W&I (Warranty and Indemnity) insurance is "available for M&A deals > EUR 1M in the EU." The QA Director correctly observed that for sub-EUR 5M transactions, R&W insurance premiums are typically 3-5% of the policy limit — disproportionately expensive — and coverage exclusions may render the policy ineffective for this deal's specific risks.

### 7.2 Qualified Assessment

**Is R&W insurance available for a EUR 2M transaction?**

Technically yes. The following insurers offer "small deal" or "lower mid-market" W&I products that have minimum deal sizes as low as EUR 1-2M:
- Euclid Transactional (specialist SME W&I policies)
- Markel Corporation (small-deal W&I)
- DUAL Asset Underwriting
- Certain Lloyd's syndicates

**Is it cost-effective for this transaction?**

Almost certainly not. On a EUR 2M transaction:
- Policy limit: typically 20-30% of enterprise value = EUR 400,000-600,000 (the minimum meaningful coverage)
- Premium at 3-4%: EUR 12,000-24,000
- Minimum retention (deductible/basket): typically EUR 25,000-50,000 for small-deal policies
- Minimum deal fee charged by specialist brokers: EUR 8,000-15,000

Total cost to obtain EUR 400,000-600,000 in coverage: EUR 20,000-39,000 before any claims are made.

For the specific risks in this transaction, the policy would likely exclude:
- "Known risks" — any risk specifically identified in the due diligence reports (including the MCCAA competition risk, the personal name trademark risk, the GLP-1 regulatory uncertainty) would be excluded as "known"
- Unverified EBITDA risk — policies do not insure the accuracy of financial projections
- Any risk that was disclosed in the seller's disclosure letter

Given these exclusions, the residual coverage under a W&I policy for this deal would be narrow: it would cover undisclosed, unknown liabilities that survived due diligence — the category of risk that is inherently difficult to quantify and for which coverage is theoretical rather than practical.

**Revised recommendation:**

Do not prioritize R&W insurance for this transaction. Instead:

1. **Use the escrow mechanism** (EUR 200,000-300,000 held for 18-24 months) as the primary protection against undisclosed liabilities. This costs nothing beyond the time value of the escrowed funds.

2. **Maximize the seller indemnity** in the APA for specifically identified risk categories. A well-drafted indemnity from the seller is better protection than a W&I policy with exclusions for every identified risk.

3. **If the seller cannot provide meaningful indemnities** (e.g., if Petra's financial position is such that she cannot backstop substantial claims), then consider a targeted W&I policy — but engage a specialist broker and obtain specific information on what would and would not be covered before paying any premium. Do not assume coverage exists without confirming it for the specific facts.

4. **Consider a "mini-QofE" insurance product** as an alternative — some insurers now offer coverage for the risk that verified EBITDA is materially less than represented. This is a niche product that is more directly relevant to this deal's specific risk profile.

## ADDENDUM SECTION 8: ANTI-BRIBERY AND CORRUPTION ANALYSIS

### 8.1 Malta-Specific Corruption Risk Profile

Malta has faced heightened regulatory scrutiny regarding corruption and anti-money laundering since the 2017 assassination of journalist Daphne Caruana Galizia. The EU and FATF have applied monitoring pressure on Malta's anti-corruption framework. In response, Malta has strengthened the Prevention of Money Laundering Act (Chapter 373) and the Criminal Code provisions on corruption (Articles 115-120, Criminal Code, Chapter 9).

For a wellness business acquisition, the primary corruption risk vectors are:

**1. Government contracts or referrals**
If Marion Mizzi Wellbeing has any contracts with Maltese government entities (e.g., health referral programs, government employee wellness programs, public sector client programs), these must be reviewed for compliance with Malta's public procurement rules and anti-bribery provisions. Any contract obtained through improper means creates liability that could transfer to the buyer in a share deal (asset deal mitigates this, but reputational damage does not distinguish deal structure).

**Verification required:** Request a schedule of all contracts with government entities or quasi-government organizations. Verify that contracts were obtained through proper channels. Review any hospitality or gift policies that the business has applied toward government contacts or referral sources.

**2. Healthcare professional referrals**
In Malta's healthcare ecosystem, wellness businesses may maintain relationships with GPs, specialists, or other healthcare professionals who refer patients. In some markets, "referral fees" (payments to physicians for patient referrals) constitute improper inducements under professional regulations and may constitute bribery under the Criminal Code.

**Verification required:** Ask specifically whether any payments, gifts, or benefits have been made to physicians, dietitians, or other healthcare professionals in exchange for referrals. Review any referral arrangement in writing. This is particularly relevant given the GLP-1 pivot strategy, which will require new referral relationships with prescribing physicians — ensuring these are established on a legally compliant basis from Day 1 is essential.

**3. GDPR enforcement and data trading**
Malta's Information and Data Protection Commissioner (IDPC) has increased enforcement activity since 2021. There is a risk that historical data practices at Marion Mizzi Wellbeing involved informal data sharing or data trading (e.g., sharing client health data with partner businesses) that was not properly disclosed or consented to. Such practices could constitute a regulatory violation under GDPR but, in extreme cases, could also attract criminal liability under Malta's data protection criminal provisions.

**Verification required:** Review the seller's data sharing arrangements with third parties. Obtain a warranty that no data has been shared or sold in violation of GDPR.

**4. Competition compliance history**
The wellness market in Malta is small. There is a risk that informal price coordination has occurred — not necessarily through formal cartel agreements, but through market-level behavior. While wellness services are unlikely to attract competition enforcement in isolation, the combination of post-acquisition market dominance with any evidence of historical coordination would be a significant liability.

**Verification required:** Obtain a warranty that the seller has not participated in any price-fixing, market allocation, or customer-sharing arrangements with competitors. Request confirmation that no contact has been made with MCCAA or any competition authority regarding the seller's conduct.

## ADDENDUM SECTION 9: ENVIRONMENTAL ANALYSIS

### 9.1 Environmental Risk Assessment for Spa Locations

The original report did not address environmental risk. For spa and wellness operations, the following environmental risk categories are relevant:

**Category 1: Chemical use and disposal**

Spa operations use chemicals that require proper storage and disposal: solvents, cleaning agents, laundry chemicals, pool treatment chemicals (if any pools are operated), skincare product ingredients, and aesthetic treatment chemicals (for body wraps, scrubs, and treatments).

Under Malta's Environment Act (Chapter 549) and the Waste Management (Waste Oil) Regulations, businesses that use or generate chemical waste must comply with storage, labeling, and disposal requirements. Violations can result in administrative fines and, in serious cases, criminal prosecution.

**Verification required:**
- Request all environmental compliance records
- Identify whether any chemical storage tanks, drums, or facilities are present at any location
- Confirm that waste disposal has been conducted through licensed waste collectors
- Check whether any complaints or inspections have been made by the Environment and Resources Authority (ERA)

**Category 2: Legionella risk in water systems**

Spa facilities with showers, hot tubs, or steam rooms face Legionella risk under Malta's public health regulations. The Superintendent of Public Health's office has guidelines on Legionella control in spa and wellness facilities. Failure to maintain water temperatures, carry out risk assessments, and document cleaning schedules can result in regulatory violation and, critically, personal injury liability if clients contract Legionellosis.

**Verification required:**
- Request Legionella risk assessments for each location (these should be current — conducted within the last 12 months)
- Review water system maintenance records
- Confirm that any previous Legionella incidents have been reported and remediated

**Category 3: Premises remediation risk**

If Marion Mizzi Wellbeing has ever used any materials (e.g., asbestos in older building construction, chemical treatments on floors or walls) that require remediation, the buyer could inherit remediation obligations in an asset deal if the obligation attaches to the premises rather than to the seller's corporate entity.

**Verification required:**
- For the Fgura standalone location (older building, likely pre-1990 construction): request an asbestos survey
- Review any planning authority records for the premises for previous environmental issues
- Include in the APA a warranty that no hazardous materials are present at any location that require remediation

**Category 4: Aesthetic treatment waste**

Body contouring treatments using radiofrequency, ultrasound, and cryolipolysis do not produce clinical waste in the same way that medical procedures do. However, if any treatments at Marion Mizzi involve products that qualify as medical devices (including single-use components, sterilization waste), disposal must comply with Malta's Medical Devices Act (Chapter 540) and subsidiary regulations.

**Verification required:**
- Obtain a schedule of all equipment used for body contouring and aesthetic treatments
- Confirm that CE marking is current for all energy-based devices
- Review waste disposal practices for any treatment-related materials

## ADDENDUM SECTION 10: INSURANCE COVERAGE ADEQUACY REVIEW

### 10.1 Insurance Due Diligence Framework

The original report listed insurance as a contract to review (Item 7 in the contracts checklist) but did not analyze insurance adequacy in any detail. This section provides a framework for assessing whether Marion Mizzi Wellbeing's existing insurance coverage is adequate and what gaps the buyer will need to fill.

### 10.2 Required Insurance Coverages for This Business

**Coverage 1: Professional Indemnity (PI) / Treatment Liability**

Scope required: Coverage for claims arising from spa treatments, body contouring procedures, slimming program advice, and dietary counseling that cause injury, adverse reaction, or other harm to clients.

Why critical: Spa and wellness treatments carry real liability risk. Allergic reactions to skincare products, burns from radiofrequency equipment, injury from cryolipolysis devices, and adverse reactions to dietary supplements can all generate substantial personal injury claims. Without PI coverage, a single significant claim could exceed the business's entire net assets.

Minimum adequate coverage: EUR 1,000,000 per claim / EUR 2,000,000 aggregate.

Due diligence questions:
- Does the current policy specifically cover body contouring equipment (cryolipolysis, radiofrequency, ultrasound)? Many standard beauty insurance policies exclude energy-based medical devices.
- Does the policy cover the slimming program and dietary advice components?
- What is the claims history for the last 5 years? Any claims should be disclosed and analyzed.
- Is the policy transferable to the new owner, or will a new policy need to be obtained?

**Coverage 2: Public Liability**

Scope required: Coverage for third-party bodily injury or property damage occurring on the business's premises or arising from the business's activities.

Minimum adequate coverage: EUR 2,000,000 per occurrence / EUR 5,000,000 aggregate.

Due diligence questions:
- Are all three locations covered under a single policy or separate policies?
- What activities are specifically covered and excluded?
- Does the policy cover both owned and leased premises?

**Coverage 3: Employer's Liability / Workers' Compensation**

Malta's Occupational Health and Safety Authority Act (Chapter 424) and associated regulations require employers to maintain employer's liability insurance. Given the physical nature of spa work (therapists performing massages, body treatments, and equipment operations over long hours), occupational injury claims are a genuine risk.

Minimum adequate coverage: As required by Malta law (to be confirmed with current insurer).

Due diligence questions:
- Are all employees listed on the employer's liability policy?
- Are there any outstanding employee injury claims?
- What is the occupational injury claims history?

**Coverage 4: Product Liability**

If Marion Mizzi Wellbeing sells own-branded supplements or retail skincare products, product liability coverage is required for claims arising from defective products.

Due diligence questions:
- Does the business sell own-brand products?
- If so, is product liability coverage in place?
- Are the product safety files and technical documentation for own-brand supplements current and compliant with EU food supplement regulations?

**Coverage 5: Business Interruption**

Scope required: Coverage for loss of revenue during periods when the business cannot operate due to an insured event (fire, flood, equipment failure, public health emergency).

Due diligence questions:
- Does the current policy cover all three locations?
- What is the indemnity period? (Should be minimum 12 months given the time required to rebuild clientele)
- Does the policy cover loss of access (e.g., if the hotel closes for renovation, preventing access to the spa)?

**Coverage 6: Cyber Liability**

Given the GDPR risks identified in the original report (4,900+ client records including health data), cyber liability coverage should be considered — particularly if the client database is held in a digital system that could be breached.

Due diligence questions:
- Does the business have any cyber insurance?
- Has any data breach occurred? Was it reported to the IDPC?
- What digital security measures are in place for the client database?

### 10.3 Insurance Transition Plan

On closing, the buyer must ensure:

1. **Day 1 coverage**: New insurance policies in Carisma's name must be in place before the business trades under new ownership. The seller's policies will not automatically transfer.

2. **Run-off coverage**: The seller should maintain professional indemnity run-off coverage for a minimum of 3 years post-closing, covering claims that arise from pre-closing treatments or advice. This should be a closing condition.

3. **Premium budgeting**: Estimated annual insurance premiums for the combined Marion Mizzi operation: EUR 15,000-25,000 per annum (PI + public liability + employer's liability + business interruption). This should be factored into the post-acquisition EBITDA model.
