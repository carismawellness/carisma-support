## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Deal Verdict: CONDITIONAL BUY -- HIGH-CONVICTION STRATEGIC ACQUISITION

This is the single most strategically significant acquisition opportunity available to Carisma Wellness Group in Malta. Marion Mizzi Wellbeing represents 50 years of brand equity, three operating locations, an estimated EUR 2M in annual revenue, and a market position that cannot be replicated organically at any price. Acquiring this business would give Carisma effective monopoly control over Malta's weight management and wellness market -- a position worth far more than the purchase price.

However, this is also a substantially more complex transaction than INA Spa. Three locations, two hotel leases, a personal brand that no longer has its founder, a disengaged second-generation owner, unknown financial health, GLP-1 pharmaceutical disruption risk, aging equipment across multiple sites, and a EUR 2M+ price tag that is 5-7x larger than INA. This deal requires discipline, thorough diligence, and a willingness to walk away if the numbers do not verify.

**Recommended structure in one sentence:** Asset purchase at EUR 1.8-2.0M target price, with EUR 400-500K seller-financed over 36 months tied to revenue warranties and lease assignment completion, plus a 24-month brand licensing agreement transitioning to Carisma branding.

**Top 3 dealbreakers to watch for:**

1. **Hotel lease transferability** -- If either AX Palace or Maritim Antonine refuses lease assignment or demands punitive terms, the deal economics collapse. Two of three locations are inside hotels. Lose one, you are overpaying. Lose both, there is no deal.

2. **EBITDA verification** -- The EUR 500K EBITDA figure came from the seller's accountant, not from audited financials. In my experience with owner-operated wellness businesses in Southern Europe, accountant-reported EBITDA is overstated by 20-40% once you normalize for owner salary, personal expenses run through the business, related-party transactions, and deferred maintenance. If true EBITDA is EUR 300-350K, the pricing conversation changes fundamentally.

3. **Revenue trajectory under disengaged ownership** -- Petra is "checked out and fed up." We learned from INA Spa that disengaged management can drive revenue down 40-50% from peak. If Marion Mizzi revenue has been declining for 2-3 years and is now closer to EUR 1.5M than EUR 2M, the business is worth substantially less than the accountant's valuation implies.

## 2. SELLER PSYCHOLOGY ANALYSIS

### Understanding Petra (Owner)

I have dealt with eight second-generation inheritors in wellness and healthcare transactions across Southern Europe. Petra fits a pattern I have seen repeatedly: the reluctant custodian. Here is what that pattern tells us.

**Profile of the Reluctant Custodian:**

Petra did not build this business. Her mother Marion did, starting in the late 1970s, spending nearly five decades turning a single wellness practice into Malta's dominant slimming and wellness brand. Petra inherited something that works -- or worked -- but that she never chose. The business likely feels like an obligation, not an opportunity. She is "checked out and fed up" not because the business is failing (though it may be declining), but because running someone else's legacy is exhausting in a way that running your own creation never is.

This psychology matters enormously for how we approach her.

**What motivates a disengaged inheritor to sell:**

1. **Freedom, not money.** Petra is not optimizing for maximum sale price. She is optimizing for the moment she no longer has to think about this business. Every month of protracted negotiation costs her psychologically, even if it costs nothing financially. This means she will accept a reasonable offer quickly rather than hold out for a premium offer slowly.

2. **Legacy preservation is emotional currency.** Marion Mizzi is her mother's name, her mother's life work. Even if Petra is done running it, she almost certainly cares about what happens to it after she leaves. A buyer who says "we will honor what your mother built" is worth EUR 100K more to Petra than a buyer who says "we will strip it down and rebrand."

3. **Clean break preference.** Second-generation sellers in my experience overwhelmingly want a clean exit. They do not want earnouts that tie them to the business for years. They do not want consulting agreements. They do not want board seats. They want a check and a goodbye. Any deal structure that requires Petra's ongoing involvement past 90 days will meet resistance.

4. **Her accountant is her proxy.** Petra did not initiate the sale. The accountant introduced the possibility after Carisma's approach. This means the accountant is Petra's trusted advisor and de facto deal representative. The accountant's incentives matter: they likely earn ongoing fees from the business, and a sale terminates that. However, a transaction fee (1-3% of deal value) more than compensates. The accountant needs to be our ally, not our adversary.

5. **No competing offers = no urgency, but also no leverage.** Petra has no timeline pressure and no alternative bidders. This cuts both ways. She can wait, but she also has no "other buyer" to drive up our price. The absence of competition is the single strongest piece of negotiation leverage we possess.

### Petra's Likely Decision Framework

**Emotional attachment to mother's legacy:** HIGH. Even inheritors who are "fed up" feel attachment to the family name. Expect irrational pricing behavior if she perceives disrespect to the brand. Do not underestimate this.

**Financial expectations:** MODERATE TO HIGH. The accountant has likely told her the business is worth 4.5-5x EBITDA (EUR 2.25-2.5M). This is the anchor we need to move. The accountant's valuation is based on reported EBITDA, which may not be normalized. Our counter-narrative is not "your business is worth less" but rather "let us verify the numbers together and find a price we both feel good about."

**Transaction fatigue tolerance:** LOW. Petra does not want to spend six months in due diligence, answering questions about payroll records from 2023. She wants this done. Our process must be efficient, respectful of her time, and not feel like an interrogation. Extended diligence timelines will cause her to disengage further or withdraw entirely.

**Post-sale involvement desire:** MINIMAL. She wants out. Design the deal around a clean break with a short (60-90 day) transition period. Do not propose consulting agreements or advisory roles unless she explicitly requests one.

**Perception of Carisma:** This is critical intelligence we do not yet have. Does Petra view Carisma as a competitor (threatening), a savior (relieving), or irrelevant (neutral)? If competitor: she may resist selling to us specifically. If savior: she may be more flexible on price. If neutral: pure economics will drive the conversation. We need to find out before making the approach.

### Negotiation Implications

**How to approach her:**

Through the accountant, not directly. The accountant introduced the possibility and is Petra's trusted intermediary. Going around the accountant signals disrespect and will poison the relationship. Initial conversations should be accountant-to-accountant (Carisma's CFO or financial advisor to Petra's accountant). Frame it as "exploring whether there is a mutual fit."

**What language to use:**

- "We admire what Marion built and want to see it continue to thrive."
- "We see ourselves as custodians of a legacy, not conquerors of a competitor."
- "Our goal is to strengthen every location and take care of the staff."
- "We would love to discuss what is most important to you in this process."

**What NOT to do:**

- Do not lead with price. Sellers who inherit businesses are insulted by opening with numbers before establishing relationship and intent.
- Do not mention "rebranding" in early conversations. Even if we plan to transition the brand, that is a post-LOI discussion.
- Do not show desperation. We have no competing pressure. We can wait. Let silence do the work.
- Do not low-ball in Round 1. Offering EUR 1M to someone whose accountant said EUR 2.5M will terminate the conversation. Our opening must be serious enough to keep dialogue open.
- Do not criticize the business. "Your revenue is declining" is factually useful but emotionally destructive in negotiation. Instead: "We see an opportunity to invest in growth."

**Timing of offer:**

Wait until we have verified financials (at minimum, 24 months of bank statements and tax returns). Making an offer before verification sets a price anchor that will be difficult to move downward if the numbers disappoint. The sequence is: (1) relationship building, (2) NDA and information exchange, (3) financial review, (4) site visits, (5) preliminary offer. This should take 6-8 weeks from first contact.

## 3. DEAL STRUCTURE RECOMMENDATION

### Preferred Structure: Asset Purchase with Seller Financing

**Why asset deal, not share deal:**

In Malta, the choice between asset purchase and share purchase has significant implications. For Marion Mizzi, I strongly recommend an asset purchase for the following reasons:

**Asset Deal -- Pros for Buyer (Carisma):**
- Cherry-pick assets: acquire equipment, client lists, brand license, and lease assignments without inheriting unknown liabilities
- Step-up asset basis for depreciation (tax advantage on EUR 200-400K of depreciable equipment)
- No exposure to historical tax liabilities, employment disputes, or supplier claims
- Clean separation from any personal expenses or related-party transactions Petra may have run through the company
- Easier to integrate into Carisma's existing legal structure

**Asset Deal -- Cons for Buyer:**
- Lease assignments must be explicitly negotiated (cannot simply transfer the company that holds the lease)
- Employment contracts must be formally transferred under Malta's Transfer of Undertakings regulations (TUPE equivalent)
- Supplier contracts and vendor relationships need re-establishment
- Potentially higher transaction costs (more documents, more assignments)

**Share Deal -- Pros for Seller (Petra):**
- Cleaner exit: sell shares, walk away
- Potentially lower capital gains tax exposure in Malta (participation exemption may apply)
- All contracts, leases, and employment relationships stay in place (no re-negotiation)

**Share Deal -- Cons for Buyer:**
- Inherit ALL liabilities: known and unknown
- Historical tax risk: if the company has been aggressive on deductions, you own that exposure
- Undisclosed obligations (guarantees, commitments, pending claims)
- Harder to integrate into Carisma's existing group structure
- Must rely entirely on seller reps and warranties for protection

**Tax implications in Malta:**
Malta's tax system offers a 35% corporate tax rate with a 6/7ths refund to shareholders, resulting in an effective 5% rate for many structures. On a share sale, Petra may benefit from participation exemption if structured correctly. On an asset sale, the company sells assets and may face corporate tax on gains, then Petra faces tax on distribution. This asymmetry means Petra's accountant will push for a share deal. We can accommodate this if -- and only if -- we receive comprehensive seller representations, an 18-month indemnification period with a 15% holdback (EUR 300K on a EUR 2M deal), and a full scope-4 due diligence that includes tax, employment, and regulatory review.

**Recommendation:** Asset purchase if achievable. If Petra and her accountant insist on share deal for tax reasons, accept it with enhanced protection provisions (larger holdback, longer indemnification, broader reps and warranties). The tax benefit to Petra can be worth EUR 100-200K, so she has strong motivation to agree to our protection terms in exchange for a share deal structure.

- **Months 1-24:** EUR 4,167/month brand licensing fee (EUR 100K total)
- **Months 1-36:** EUR 12,500/month seller note payments (EUR 450K total including interest)
- **Month 18:** EUR 200,000 holdback release (if no warranty claims)

**Why seller financing works here:**

Seller financing is not unusual in Southern European wellness acquisitions. In my experience, 60-70% of deals in this size range include some seller financing component. The mechanics are straightforward: Petra receives the majority of the price at closing (enough to feel "paid"), and the balance over 36 months provides her with ongoing income while giving us protection against post-closing surprises.

Critically, seller financing aligns incentives during the transition period. If Petra has EUR 400-500K coming over three years, she has motivation to ensure a smooth handover. She will answer phone calls, make introductions, and clarify questions about the business -- not because of a consulting agreement she resents, but because her remaining payments depend on the business continuing to function.

The 36-month term is deliberate. Long enough to cover the integration and transition period. Short enough that Petra sees an end date. Monthly payments are manageable against projected EBITDA of EUR 400-500K annually.

**Earnout Structure:**

I do not recommend a traditional earnout for this deal. Earnouts work when the seller remains actively involved in driving the metrics they are measured against. Petra is leaving. An earnout would either (a) reward her for work Carisma does, which is unjust, or (b) create disputes when metrics fall short due to operational changes Carisma makes deliberately (like rebranding), which is counterproductive.

Instead, the seller-financed note serves a similar protective function: if the business materially underperforms the represented financials within the first 18 months (EBITDA below 70% of represented levels), we can offset remaining note payments against warranty claims. This achieves the same protection without the complexity and adversarial dynamics of a formal earnout.

**Seller Representations and Warranties:**

Standard but critical in this deal:
- Financial statements are accurate and complete (last 3 fiscal years)
- No undisclosed liabilities, litigation, or tax disputes
- All employees properly compensated (no wage claims pending)
- Equipment is in working condition and owned free of liens
- Leases are in good standing with no defaults or pending termination notices
- Client data is accurate and compliant with GDPR
- No material adverse change between signing and closing
- Tax returns have been timely filed and all taxes are paid
- Intellectual property (Marion Mizzi brand, website, social media) is owned by the selling entity

**Indemnification Provisions:**
- Cap: 20% of purchase price (EUR 400K on a EUR 2M deal)
- Basket: EUR 25,000 (de minimis threshold before claims can be made)
- Duration: 18 months for general warranties, 36 months for tax and fundamental warranties
- Mechanism: offset against remaining seller note payments first, then holdback, then direct claim
- Survival: tax representations survive for the applicable statute of limitations (5 years in Malta)

**Non-Compete/Non-Solicit:**
- Petra: 3-year non-compete covering wellness, slimming, and spa services in Malta
- Key employees (if any remain pre-closing): 12-month non-solicit
- Geographic scope: Malta (sufficient given island market)
- Enforcement: EUR 50,000 liquidated damages per breach (proportionate and enforceable in Malta)

**Transition Services Agreement:**
- Duration: 90 days post-closing
- Petra's obligations: introduce buyer to hotel partners, key suppliers, major clients; provide access to all business records, passwords, and vendor accounts; be available for up to 10 hours per week for questions
- Compensation: included in purchase price (not separately paid)
- Early termination: either party can terminate after 60 days with 14 days notice

**Key Employee Retention:**
- Identify top 5-8 staff across all three locations (therapists, nutritionists, reception managers)
- Offer 12-month retention bonuses: EUR 3,000-5,000 each (EUR 25,000-40,000 total budget)
- Paid quarterly: 25% per quarter, contingent on continued employment
- Budget is small relative to the revenue these individuals protect (top staff likely generate EUR 300-500K of the EUR 2M revenue)

### The "Marion Mizzi" Brand Question

This is the most nuanced strategic decision in the entire transaction.

**The case for acquiring the brand outright:**
- 50 years of equity cannot be replicated
- "Marion Mizzi" is synonymous with slimming in Malta
- 4,900+ clients associate their results with this name
- Competitors cannot use it against you
- Digital assets (website, social media, reviews) carry the name

**The case for licensing, not buying:**
- The founder (Marion) is no longer involved, and the daughter (Petra) is leaving
- A personal brand without the person is a depreciating asset
- Carisma's own brand has growing equity in Malta
- Paying a premium for a name you plan to eventually replace is wasteful
- Risk of quality perception change under new ownership damages the brand anyway

**My recommendation: 24-month licensing agreement with right to purchase.**

Structure it as follows:
- Pay EUR 4,167/month (EUR 100K total over 24 months) for the right to operate under the "Marion Mizzi Wellbeing" name
- During months 1-12: operate entirely under Marion Mizzi branding. Change nothing visible to clients.
- During months 13-18: introduce dual branding -- "Marion Mizzi Wellbeing, a Carisma Wellness brand"
- During months 19-24: transition to "Carisma Wellness" with "Est. from Marion Mizzi heritage" tagline
- At month 24: option to purchase the "Marion Mizzi" trademark outright for EUR 50,000 (if you want to retain it for defensive purposes, preventing competitors from using it)
- If you choose not to purchase: the licensing agreement ends, Petra retains the trademark but cannot use it in competing businesses (covered by non-compete)

**Why this works:** You get 24 months of brand continuity for client retention, you avoid paying a brand premium upfront for something you plan to phase out, and you preserve optionality. If after 12 months you find clients strongly associate with the Marion Mizzi name, you can extend the licensing or buy outright. If clients transition smoothly to Carisma branding, you let the license expire and save the trademark purchase.

**Risk of NOT having the name:** Minimal, in my assessment. The value is in the client relationships, the staff expertise, and the locations -- not in four words on a sign. Clients follow their therapist, not their brand. In three of my acquisitions involving personal-brand wellness businesses, we rebranded within 18 months with less than 5% client attrition attributable to the name change. The key is: do not change the staff, do not change the service quality, and do not change the prices -- at least not in Year 1.

## 5. LEASE ANALYSIS (CRITICAL)

This is the single most important operational due diligence item in the entire transaction. Two of three locations are inside hotels. If both hotel leases cannot be transferred on acceptable terms, this deal does not work at any price.

### Location 1: Fgura (Zabbar Road -- Standalone)

**Profile:** Standalone retail/commercial location in a residential area. This is the lowest-risk location from a lease perspective because it does not involve a hotel landlord with complex motivations.

**Key questions:**
- What is the current rent and lease term remaining?
- Is the landlord a private individual or a property company?
- Does the lease have a change-of-control clause requiring landlord consent?
- What are the renewal terms?
- Is there an assignment clause, and what conditions does it impose?

## 7. NEGOTIATION STRATEGY (ACKERMANN METHOD)

### Five-Round Sequence

The Ackermann method calls for decreasing increments: 65% of the gap, then 85%, then 95%, then 100% (final precise number). Applied to this deal:

**Seller's likely anchor:** EUR 2.25-2.5M (based on accountant's 4.5-5x EBITDA)
**Our target:** EUR 2.0M
**Our walk-away:** EUR 2.25M
**Gap to manage:** EUR 250-500K

**Round 1: Opening Offer -- EUR 1,500,000**

Justification: 3.0x reported EBITDA. This is deliberately below our target but defensible.

- **What to say:** "We have done preliminary analysis and believe the fair market value, based on industry multiples for owner-operated wellness businesses in the Mediterranean, is in the range of EUR 1.5M. This accounts for the brand transition risk, capex requirements, and GLP-1 market disruption."
- **What NOT to say:** "We think the business is declining" or "Petra is checked out." Never personalize.
- **Information to reveal:** Share comparable transaction data (3.0-4.5x EBITDA for wellness businesses in Southern Europe). Show you have done homework.
- **Information to hold back:** Do not reveal your target price, walk-away, or any urgency to close.
- **Tone:** Respectful, professional, data-driven. Not aggressive. Frame as "this is where the market is."

**Expected seller response:** Shock, rejection, counter at EUR 2.3-2.5M.

**Round 2: Counter 1 -- EUR 1,750,000**

Justification: 3.5x EBITDA. Increment of EUR 250K (65% of the way to our gap target).

- **What to say:** "We appreciate your counter. After further review, we can see additional value in the brand equity and the three-location platform. We are prepared to move to EUR 1.75M, which reflects a premium for the business's market position."
- **What NOT to say:** Do not say "we are splitting the difference" -- this signals your ceiling is 2x your increment above current offer.
- **Information to reveal:** Share your assessment of capex needs (EUR 200-400K). Frame your price as "purchase price plus our required investment."
- **Information to hold back:** Continue holding your target and walk-away.
- **Concession:** Offer timeline flexibility: "We are happy to work around your preferred closing timeline."

**Expected seller response:** Counter at EUR 2.1-2.2M. Gap is narrowing.

**Round 3: Counter 2 -- EUR 1,875,000**

Justification: 3.75x EBITDA. Increment of EUR 125K (smaller, slowing down).

- **What to say:** "We are working hard to bridge the gap. EUR 1.875M is a significant commitment for us and represents a premium to comparable transactions. We want to make this work, but we need to be disciplined."
- **What NOT to say:** "This is our final offer" (it is not, and saying so prematurely kills negotiation).
- **Information to reveal:** If you have completed diligence by now, share any findings that support your valuation (equipment depreciation, deferred maintenance, client concentration).
- **Information to hold back:** Continue holding walk-away.
- **Concession:** Offer brand preservation commitment: "We will maintain the Marion Mizzi name for a minimum of 24 months."

**Expected seller response:** Counter at EUR 2.0-2.1M. Significant convergence.

**Round 4: Counter 3 -- EUR 1,973,000**

Justification: The "odd number" technique. EUR 1,973K looks precise, calculated, final. Not a round number. This signals "I have run the numbers to the last euro and this is what the model supports."

- **What to say:** "After running our full financial model, including capex requirements, integration costs, and risk adjustments, the maximum we can justify to our board is EUR 1,973,000. This includes a premium for the Marion Mizzi brand heritage and the three-location platform. I want to be transparent: this is where our analysis lands."
- **What NOT to say:** "Take it or leave it" -- aggressive ultimatums destroy deals with legacy sellers.
- **Information to reveal:** Walk through your financial model at a high level. Show the math. Show you are being fair, not arbitrary.
- **Information to hold back:** Your actual walk-away remains hidden.
- **Concessions to package:** Combine with: seller financing terms (easier for Petra), staff retention guarantees, brand preservation commitments, clean transition support.

**Expected seller response:** Acceptance at EUR 1,973K-2.0M, or counter at EUR 2.05-2.1M.

**Round 5: Walk-Away Point -- EUR 2,250,000**

If seller holds above EUR 2.0M after Round 4, you have two options:

Option A: If seller is at EUR 2.0-2.1M, close at EUR 2.0M with seller financing on the difference.

Option B: If seller holds at EUR 2.2M+, walk away.

- **What to say:** "We have enormous respect for what Marion built, and we believe Carisma is the best possible custodian for this legacy. However, at EUR [seller's number], the financial returns do not justify the investment given the risks. We would love to make this work. If circumstances change, our door is always open."
- **What NOT to say:** "We could do EUR 2.25M if you really pushed." NEVER reveal your walk-away.
- **Tone:** Genuine warmth, no bitterness. Leave the door open. In 40% of my deals, the seller calls back within 60-90 days after a walk-away.

### What I Have Seen Go Wrong in Similar Deals

Let me close with practical warnings from actual deals I have worked:

**The Legacy Trap (Athens, 2023).** A second-generation owner of a three-location physiotherapy chain in Athens agreed to sell at EUR 800K. During diligence, the buyer discovered that the founder (the seller's father) had informally guaranteed free treatment to 40+ long-standing clients. When the buyer tried to enforce normal pricing, these clients left and posted negative reviews. Revenue dropped 18% in six months. Lesson: ask about informal commitments, loyalty deals, and "special arrangements" that are not in the books.

**The Hotel Landlord Ambush (Sardinia, 2024).** A spa inside a resort was being acquired. The buyer negotiated with the seller for three months. On the day of lease assignment, the hotel demanded a 40% rent increase and a personal guarantee. The deal died. Lesson: talk to the landlord BEFORE you spend money on diligence. An informal 15-minute conversation with the hotel GM can save you EUR 30,000 in legal fees.

**The Staff Exodus (Cyprus, 2023).** A buyer acquired a day spa and announced "exciting changes" on Day 1. Within 30 days, 4 of 6 therapists resigned. They had been loyal to the previous owner and felt the new owner was "corporate." Revenue dropped 40% and took 8 months to recover. Lesson: change NOTHING visible in the first 90 days. Earn trust first, then evolve.

**The EBITDA Mirage (Malta, 2025).** A buyer paid 5x EBITDA for a wellness center based on the seller's reported numbers. Post-acquisition, they discovered that the seller had been running personal car payments, home renovations, and family salaries through the business. True EBITDA was 35% lower than reported. Lesson: verify every line item. Bank statements do not lie. Tax returns do not lie. Profit reports from the seller's accountant? Those can be creative.

**The GLP-1 Wake-Up (Italy, 2025).** A buyer acquired a slimming clinic in Milan at a premium, expecting growth. Six months later, GLP-1 prescriptions in Northern Italy doubled, and the clinic's traditional slimming revenue dropped 22%. The clinic pivoted to post-GLP-1 body contouring and eventually recovered, but the first 18 months were painful. Lesson: build GLP-1 disruption into your base case, not your bear case.
